Techniques Mixing Compression

The Complete Guide to Sidechain Compression

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated June 2025 8 min read Intermediate

Sidechain compression is one of the most powerful and widely-used techniques in modern music production. At its core, it lets one audio signal control the compression applied to another — unlocking everything from subtle glue and mix cohesion to the iconic pumping sound of electronic dance music.

In this guide, you'll learn exactly how sidechain compression works, the different ways to set it up in any DAW, and creative applications that go well beyond the basics.

What you'll learn: How sidechaining works technically, how to set it up step-by-step, the kick-bass relationship, ghost kick techniques, creative sidechaining, and common mistakes to avoid.

How Sidechain Compression Works

A standard compressor reduces the gain of a signal when it exceeds a set threshold. The signal being compressed and the signal being analyzed for compression are the same — that's standard gain reduction. In a sidechain configuration, these two signals are different.

The compressor still acts on its input signal (the track it's on), but the trigger signal — the one analyzed to decide when and how much to compress — comes from an external source fed into the compressor's sidechain input.

In practice: place a compressor on your bass track, then route your kick drum into that compressor's sidechain. Every time the kick hits, the compressor on the bass ducks the bass volume momentarily. The kick punches through cleanly, and when it finishes, the bass returns to full volume. Your low end becomes clean, rhythmically punchy, and full-sounding simultaneously.

The Kick–Bass Relationship

The kick drum and bass guitar (or 808) occupy the same frequency real estate — roughly 60–200Hz. They fight for space. Sidechain compression is the standard professional solution.

Parameter Typical Starting Point Purpose
Ratio 4:1 – 8:1 Controls amount of ducking
Attack 0–5ms How fast compression kicks in
Release 80–200ms Sets the pumping rhythm
Threshold −20 to −10dB How sensitive the trigger is
Makeup Gain 2–6dB Restore lost volume

Setting Up Sidechain in Major DAWs

Ableton Live

In Ableton, use a Compressor on your bass track and enable the sidechain button (the triangle icon in the compressor). Set the Audio From dropdown to your kick track. Ableton routes the kick's signal into the compressor's key input.

Logic Pro

In Logic, insert a compressor on the bass channel. Open the compressor's side panel and set the Side Chain source to the kick drum channel. Logic handles the internal routing automatically.

FL Studio

FL Studio uses a slightly different approach. Place a Peak Controller on the kick channel, then link its output to the volume knob of the bass mixer channel using right-click → "Link to controller." Adjust the peak controller's volume and tension settings to shape the ducking behavior.

Common Mistake: Setting a very fast release causes the bass to snap back too suddenly, which can sound unnatural. Try matching your release time to the tempo: at 120 BPM, a 16th-note interval is ~125ms — a great starting point for a musical release.

The Ghost Kick Technique

You don't need the kick drum to actually be audible to use it as a sidechain trigger. This is where the "ghost kick" technique comes in — invaluable for producers who want the pumping effect on non-percussive elements without introducing actual kick drum sounds into the mix.

Create a new MIDI or audio track with a kick drum sample. Mute the track's output in your mixer so it produces no sound in the final mix. Route this silent kick to the sidechain input of your target compressor. The compressor responds to the ghost kick's level, creating ducking even though the kick is never heard.

This technique is widely used in:

  • Atmospheric pads that pulse in time with an implied beat
  • Vocal chops that duck with invisible groove
  • Reverb and delay returns that clear space rhythmically
  • Full-mix sidechain on the master bus (use with extreme care)

Creative Sidechain Applications

Beyond kick-bass management, sidechain compression opens an entire universe of creative possibilities.

Reverb and Delay Ducking

Place a compressor on your reverb return track. Sidechain it with the dry vocal. When the vocalist sings, the reverb ducks, keeping the vocals clean and intelligible. When the vocalist finishes a phrase, the reverb blooms back up beautifully — a technique used on virtually every major pop and R&B record.

Electronic Pumping Effect

The signature "pumping" sound of house, techno, and EDM is nothing more than a high-ratio sidechain compressor on pads, chords, or the full mix, triggered by the kick drum. Set Ratio to 10:1 or higher, Attack near 0ms, and dial in the Release to match your tempo for that rhythmic breathing effect.

Multiband Sidechaining

Some plugins allow frequency-specific sidechaining. Instead of ducking the entire bass when the kick hits, you only duck the 60–120Hz region — the exact frequency range where the two instruments clash. The bass's upper harmonics remain unaffected, preserving presence and warmth while clearing muddiness.

"Sidechain compression is less about loudness and more about making space. The kick doesn't win because it's louder — it wins because everything around it momentarily steps aside."

Recommended Plugins for Sidechaining

While every stock DAW compressor can sidechain, several dedicated plugins offer more control, visual feedback, or unique characteristics:

  • Xfer LFOTool — Volume automation-based sidechaining with precise shape control
  • Nicky Romero Kickstart — One-knob pumping, instant results
  • Waves RVox — Reverb + gate/ducking in one, great for vocal chains
  • FabFilter Pro-C 2 — Transparent, surgical sidechain compression with excellent metering
  • Tokyo Dawn Kotelnikov — Free, pristine dynamic range control with sidechain support

Common Sidechain Mistakes

  • Too fast a release: The bass snaps back audibly, sounding unnatural. Slow it down until the return is smooth.
  • Too aggressive a ratio: Heavy sidechaining on every element makes the mix fatiguing. Use it purposefully.
  • Ignoring phase: Heavy sidechain on the master bus can cause phase cancellation issues. Check in mono.
  • No makeup gain: You've compressed the bass — restore some of that volume so your levels are consistent before/after bypass.
