Compression Ratio Explained: What It Does and How to Set It

Ratio is the one compression control that determines how hard your compressor fights back. Here's what every setting actually means — and how to pick the right one for every source.

Quick Answer: Compression ratio is the relationship between input gain above the threshold and the resulting output gain. A 4:1 ratio means for every 4 dB the signal rises above the threshold, the output only rises 1 dB. Low ratios (1.5:1–3:1) are gentle and transparent. High ratios (8:1–∞:1) are aggressive and limiting. Most mixing tasks fall between 2:1 and 6:1.

What Is Compression Ratio?

When a compressor's threshold is crossed, it doesn't just stop the signal — it controls how much the output can rise relative to the input. That relationship is the ratio.

If your ratio is 4:1, and the input signal rises 8 dB above the threshold, the output only rises 2 dB above it. The compressor absorbed 6 dB of that excess — that 6 dB is your gain reduction (GR), visible on the GR meter.

If your ratio is 2:1, the same 8 dB of excess becomes 4 dB of output — gentler, less reduction, more of the original dynamic is preserved.

If your ratio is ∞:1 (infinity to one), no signal can rise above the threshold at all. That's a limiter.

The Ratio Scale: From Gentle to Brick Wall

Compressor ratios run on a continuous scale. Here's how each zone behaves in practice:

1.1:1 to 1.5:1 — Barely There

At these ratios the compressor is barely doing anything perceptible. You might see 1–2 dB of gain reduction on peaks. This territory is used in mastering to apply the most subtle density and cohesion, or in long-form bus compression over an entire session. Transients are essentially untouched.

2:1 — Gentle and Transparent

This is the go-to ratio for bus compression and any source where you want control without character. Acoustic guitar, piano, strings, room mics on drums — anything where the natural dynamics are an asset. At 2:1, 10 dB over the threshold becomes 5 dB out. You're taking the edge off the loudest peaks without squashing the feel of the performance.

3:1 to 4:1 — The Bread and Butter Zone

The most used range in mixing. Vocals, lead synths, bass — anything that needs to sit consistently in a mix without constant level automation. At 4:1, 10 dB over becomes 2.5 dB out. You can hear the compression working, but it still sounds natural. The FabFilter Pro-C 2 defaults to 4:1 for good reason — it solves 80% of mixing problems without second-guessing.

6:1 to 8:1 — Heavy Control

At this range you're making deliberate sonic decisions. Snare drums, rap vocals, DI bass, drum room mics being crushed for effect. The compressor is doing significant work — 10 dB over becomes less than 2 dB out at 6:1. Transients start to get affected and the compression becomes part of the character of the sound, not just transparent gain management.

10:1 to 20:1 — Near-Limiting

These ratios are used for aggressive parallel compression, heavily processed lead vocals, or specific mixing effects where you deliberately want to hear the compressor working. Pumping and breathing are expected at these settings. This is where 1176-style "all buttons in" mode lives — four simultaneous ratios creating a unique harmonic character.

∞:1 — Limiting

Infinity to one means the output cannot rise above the threshold no matter how loud the input is. This is the definition of a limiter. Used on master buses to hit loudness targets, on individual tracks to prevent clipping, and as a safety net in any live or broadcast context. The FabFilter Pro-L 2 and iZotope's limiter modules operate in this zone.

Ratio vs. Threshold — The Critical Relationship

Ratio alone doesn't tell you how much compression you'll hear. The threshold determines when the compressor kicks in, and the ratio determines how hard it clamps down when it does. They work together.

Example: A 10:1 ratio with a threshold at -5 dBFS will barely compress most signals — only the loudest peaks will trigger it. A 2:1 ratio with a threshold at -30 dBFS will compress almost continuously, smoothing out the entire dynamic range of a track.

The number you actually care about — and should monitor — is the gain reduction meter. If you're seeing 6–8 dB of GR on a vocal, that's working compression. If you're seeing 20+ dB of GR on a mix bus, something is wrong with either your threshold or your ratio setting.

Hard Knee vs. Soft Knee

The knee setting changes how the ratio is applied around the threshold — and it's closely related to how your ratio choice sounds in practice.

With a hard knee, the full ratio engages the instant the signal crosses the threshold. It's precise and punchy — great for transient control on drums and for limiting. You hear the compressor "snap on."

With a soft knee, compression gradually ramps up as the signal approaches and passes through the threshold. The effective ratio starts low and increases toward the set ratio over a dB range around the threshold. This sounds much more natural and is preferred for vocals, acoustic instruments, and bus processing where you don't want the compressor to announce itself.

Soft knee effectively makes even a 6:1 ratio feel gentler on transients — it's one of the reasons the LA-2A style optical compressors sound musical even at high ratios. Hard knee at 6:1 sounds decidedly more aggressive than soft knee at 6:1.

Ratio and Attack Time — Working Together

Ratio and attack time interact directly. A fast attack with a high ratio will clamp down on transients immediately and aggressively — you lose punch and snap. A slow attack with a high ratio lets transients pass before the compressor engages — the ratio is working mainly on the sustain of the note.

This is the classic trick for drums: use 6:1–8:1 ratio with a slow attack (30–50ms) so the initial transient punch passes through, then the compressor controls the body and tail. You get the crack of the snare with the sustain tamed.

For vocals, a medium attack (10–30ms) with 3:1–4:1 lets the consonants and initial breath of each syllable stay natural while the compressor catches loud vowel peaks before they jump out of the mix.

Exercises

Beginner — Hear the Ratio

Load a vocal or drum loop into your DAW. Insert a compressor (any will do — stock plugins are fine). Set the threshold so you're seeing about 6 dB of gain reduction on the loudest peaks. Now sweep the ratio from 1.5:1 to 10:1 in steps — don't touch the threshold. Listen carefully. At 2:1 it barely changes the feel. At 8:1 you'll hear the dynamic life being squeezed out. Find the point where the vocal sits in the mix without losing its expressiveness — that's your sweet spot for this source.

Intermediate — Ratio vs. Threshold Tradeoff

On a snare drum track, set ratio to 4:1 and lower the threshold until you see 8 dB of GR. Listen to what happens to the transient attack and the body of the snare. Now raise the threshold (less compression) and increase the ratio to 8:1 — aim for the same 8 dB of GR. You'll notice the two settings can produce similar average gain reduction but feel completely different. The high ratio/higher threshold setting has more dynamic variation between hits. The low ratio/lower threshold setting is more uniformly compressed. Both have uses. Learn what each one does to the groove.

Advanced — Parallel Ratio Blending

Set up a parallel compression chain on your drum bus. On the wet (parallel) channel, push the ratio to 10:1 or higher and lower the threshold until you're seeing 15–20 dB of GR — an intentionally crushed version of the drums. On the dry channel, keep the original uncompressed signal. Blend the two together with the wet fader. Start at zero and slowly bring it up until you hear the crushed drums adding density and sustain under the transients of the dry signal. This is the New York parallel compression technique — and it works precisely because the ratio on the parallel channel is aggressive enough to sustain everything between hits.

FAQ

What does compression ratio mean?

Compression ratio is the relationship between how much the input signal exceeds the threshold versus how much the output is allowed to rise. A 4:1 ratio means for every 4 dB the signal goes above the threshold, only 1 dB comes out.

What is a good compression ratio for vocals?

Most vocals respond well to ratios between 2:1 and 4:1. This tames dynamic peaks while preserving the natural expressiveness and breath of the performance. Heavy-processed or rap vocals can use 6:1 to 8:1.

What compression ratio for drums?

Drum bus compression typically uses 2:1 to 4:1 for glue. Individual snare and kick can use 4:1 to 8:1 for punch and control. Parallel drum compression often goes as high as 10:1 or infinity:1.

What is the difference between 2:1 and 4:1 compression?

At 2:1, a signal 10 dB above the threshold comes out 5 dB above it — gentle and transparent. At 4:1, the same input comes out just 2.5 dB above — noticeably more controlled. 4:1 is about twice as aggressive as 2:1.

What ratio is limiting?

A limiter uses an infinity:1 ratio (or very high ratios like 20:1 or 100:1). This means no signal can exceed the threshold — it's a hard ceiling on the output level.

What compression ratio for bass guitar?

Bass guitar typically uses 4:1 to 6:1 to even out the dynamic differences between notes and finger pressure. This keeps the bass sitting consistently in the mix without squashing its tone.

Does higher ratio mean more compression?

Yes. A higher ratio means the compressor clamps down harder on signals above the threshold. Combined with a lower threshold, a high ratio will produce much more gain reduction than a low ratio at the same threshold.

What is soft knee vs hard knee compression?

Hard knee means the compressor switches on at full ratio the moment the signal hits the threshold. Soft knee gradually ramps up compression around the threshold — it sounds more natural and is often preferred for vocals and acoustic sources.

What compression ratio should I use for mastering?

Mastering compression uses very gentle ratios — typically 1.5:1 to 2:1. The goal is cohesion and loudness control, not heavy dynamic reduction. Heavy ratios on a master crush transients and kill dynamics.

Can you use too much compression ratio?

Yes. Too high a ratio squashes transients, kills dynamics, causes pumping, and makes the audio sound lifeless. The ratio should be set to achieve gain reduction that serves the mix — usually 3–6 dB GR is enough for most sources.
