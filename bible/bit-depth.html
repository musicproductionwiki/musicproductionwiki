Bit Depth

/bɪt dɛpθ/

Quick Answer Definition How It Works Parameters Quick Reference History How To Use It In The Wild Types Mistakes Related Further Reading FAQ

Bit Depth is the number of bits used to represent each audio sample, directly determining a recording's dynamic range and noise floor. Each additional bit adds approximately 6 dB of dynamic range.

01 Definition

Every time you track a vocal at 16-bit out of habit, you're leaving dynamic range on the table that no plugin in the world can put back.

Bit depth is the number of binary digits (bits) assigned to represent the amplitude of each discrete audio sample in a digital recording. When an analog-to-digital converter (ADC) measures an incoming voltage at a given moment in time, it must express that measurement as a finite integer. The more bits available, the more granular that integer can be, and the closer the digital representation comes to the continuous variation of the original analog signal. A 16-bit system can express 65,536 discrete amplitude levels; a 24-bit system can express 16,777,216. That difference is not cosmetic — it is the difference between a professional recording chain and a consumer one.

The most practical consequence of bit depth is dynamic range. A rule of thumb universally applied in digital audio engineering states that each bit contributes approximately 6.02 dB of dynamic range, derived from the signal-to-noise ratio formula for a full-scale sine wave: SNR ≈ 6.02N + 1.76 dB, where N is the number of bits. This means 16-bit audio offers roughly 96 dB of dynamic range, 24-bit offers approximately 144 dB, and 32-bit integer reaches 192 dB — exceeding the physical dynamic range of any microphone or preamp ever built. In practice, 24-bit has become the universal recording standard because its theoretical noise floor sits some 30–40 dB below the self-noise of the finest analog hardware available.

Bit depth should not be confused with sample rate. Sample rate (measured in kHz) determines how many amplitude snapshots are taken per second, governing frequency response and Nyquist bandwidth. Bit depth, by contrast, determines the resolution of each snapshot's vertical amplitude measurement. You can have 192 kHz audio at 16-bit — thousands of samples per second, each measured crudely — or 44.1 kHz audio at 32-bit float, where each of those fewer snapshots is captured with extraordinary precision. In modern production, these are independent variables that must each be set deliberately, and conflating them is a foundational mistake that leads to poorly-architected sessions.

In the internal processing domain of digital audio workstations, bit depth takes on a different character. Most modern DAWs process audio internally at 32-bit or 64-bit floating-point precision, regardless of the bit depth at which audio was recorded or at which the session is configured to export. Floating-point arithmetic allows numbers to exceed the 0 dBFS boundary without clipping, which means internal summing, gain adjustments, and plugin chains can operate with effectively unlimited headroom. This internal float environment is one reason why gain staging in the box, while still important for analog-modeled plugins, is more forgiving than gain staging in an analog console chain where true clipping is immediate and destructive.

Understanding bit depth is essential for every stage of the production chain: tracking, mixing, bouncing, and final delivery. Decisions about bit depth at each stage cascade forward; recording at 24-bit preserves maximum dynamic information from the source, mixing at 32-bit float provides computational safety, and exporting at 16-bit for CD or streaming requires dithering — the deliberate addition of low-level noise to mask quantization distortion when reducing bit depth. Each of these decisions reflects a fluency with digital audio architecture that separates engineers who merely operate equipment from those who understand it.

02 How It Works

At the hardware level, bit depth is implemented by an analog-to-digital converter. When an incoming analog signal — say, a microphone's voltage output after preamplification — reaches the ADC, the converter samples the instantaneous voltage at a rate determined by the sample rate clock, then quantizes that voltage: it rounds the continuous value to the nearest of the available discrete steps. The number of available steps is 2^N, where N is the bit depth. In a 24-bit converter, the ADC chooses among 16,777,216 possible amplitude values. The gap between adjacent values — the quantization step size — represents the fundamental resolution limit of the system. Any signal component falling between two steps is rounded to the nearer one, and this rounding error manifests as quantization noise.

Quantization noise behaves differently from analog noise. In well-behaved converters with sufficient bit depth, it is statistically spread across the audio spectrum and sits far below audibility. However, when bit depth is too low for the signal — particularly for low-level signals close to the noise floor, or when very small gain changes are applied in a low-bit-depth processing environment — quantization distortion becomes audible as a harsh, grainy distortion that is spectrally correlated with the signal itself. This is most often heard as a kind of crackling or «dirty» quality on reverb tails, piano decay, or quiet passages. Dithering addresses this by injecting a precisely shaped noise signal before quantization, which randomizes the error and converts correlated distortion into uncorrelated, spectrally shaped noise that is both less audible and more musically benign.

Floating-point representation introduces a fundamentally different architecture. Rather than expressing a sample value as a fixed integer, floating-point encodes a number as a mantissa (the significant digits) and an exponent (the scale factor). 32-bit float, the standard for DAW internal processing, uses 24 bits for the mantissa and 8 bits for the exponent. This allows the format to represent an enormous range of values — from extremely quiet to far above 0 dBFS — without clipping. The relative resolution is consistent across that range. A signal at -60 dBFS has the same number of significant bits as a signal at 0 dBFS. This is profoundly useful for processing: a plugin that applies heavy compression followed by makeup gain can do so without incurring the rounding errors that integer arithmetic at the same nominal bit depth would introduce. 64-bit double-precision float, used in some DAWs for mix bus summing, extends this further but at the cost of higher CPU demand.

The digital-to-analog conversion process at the output stage mirrors the ADC in reverse. A DAC reconstructs an analog voltage from the digital samples, using reconstruction filtering (a low-pass filter at the Nyquist frequency) to smooth the staircase approximation of the digital waveform into a continuous voltage suitable for driving amplifiers and speakers. The quality of this reconstruction, again, is constrained by both the sample rate (governing temporal resolution) and the bit depth (governing amplitude precision). High-quality DACs in professional converters — units like the Prism Sound ADA-8XR or the Lavry Gold — achieve measured performance within a few dB of 24-bit theoretical limits, making further increases in bit depth for output delivery essentially academic.

In summary, bit depth governs the vertical resolution of the digital audio grid. The higher the bit depth, the finer the amplitude steps, the lower the quantization noise floor, and the wider the usable dynamic range of the recorded or processed signal. Every downstream process — mixing, bouncing, streaming encoding — operates within the constraints established at capture, which is why tracking at 24-bit is a non-negotiable baseline for any serious production regardless of the final delivery format.

03 The Parameters

Every bit depth — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these and you can work with any implementation.

BIT DEPTH
Number of bits per sample — sets dynamic range ceiling

The core parameter itself. 16-bit yields ~96 dB, 24-bit yields ~144 dB, 32-bit integer yields ~192 dB. For all recording and mixing sessions, 24-bit is the minimum professional standard; 32-bit float is preferred for any session involving heavy processing or multiple gain stage passes.

DYNAMIC RANGE
Usable amplitude range from noise floor to full-scale peak

Calculated as approximately 6.02 × N + 1.76 dB, where N equals bit depth. This is the span between the quietest representable signal and 0 dBFS. A 24-bit recording offers 144 dB of theoretical dynamic range — more than the ear's 120 dB limit and far more than any microphone's 130 dB maximum, so 24-bit is the practical ceiling of meaningful resolution.

NOISE FLOOR
Level of quantization noise relative to full scale

The quantization noise floor sits at approximately −96 dBFS for 16-bit, −144 dBFS for 24-bit, and −192 dBFS for 32-bit integer. In real-world sessions, the microphone and preamp self-noise (typically −120 to −130 dBFS equivalent) will dominate long before 24-bit quantization noise becomes audible, making 24-bit more than sufficient for any capture chain in existence.

DITHER
Noise added before bit-depth reduction to mask quantization distortion

When reducing bit depth — from 24-bit session to 16-bit for CD export, for example — dithering adds a low-level noise signal (typically shaped with a noise-shaping curve like POW-r or MBIT+) before truncation. This randomizes the rounding error, converting correlated harmonic distortion into uncorrelated broadband noise that is less audible and more perceptually benign. Always apply dither as the absolute last process in a mastering chain.

FLOATING-POINT PRECISION
Internal processing format — 32-bit or 64-bit float

Most DAWs process audio at 32-bit or 64-bit floating-point internally, independent of session bit depth. Floating-point encoding stores a mantissa and exponent, allowing values beyond 0 dBFS without clipping and providing consistent relative resolution across a massive dynamic range. This is why internal summing buses can accept signals far above full scale during mixing before a final limiter is applied.

TRUNCATION
Direct bit-depth reduction without dithering — produces correlated distortion

Truncation is the act of discarding the lower-order bits when reducing bit depth, without first applying dither. The resulting quantization error is correlated with the signal, producing harmonic distortion most audible on decaying sounds — reverb tails, piano notes, sustained synthesizer pads. Truncation without dither is an error in most contexts; the only deliberate exception is when a specific lo-fi aesthetic is being pursued.

05 History & Origin

The theoretical framework for digital audio quantization was laid by Harry Nyquist in his 1928 paper on signal transmission, and independently formalized by Claude Shannon in his 1948 information theory work. Shannon's sampling theorem established that the number of discrete levels used to represent a sample — what we now call bit depth — directly determines the signal-to-noise ratio of the digital representation. However, these remained mathematical abstractions until the semiconductor industry made high-speed, high-resolution ADC circuits manufacturable at scale.

The first commercially deployed 16-bit digital audio systems appeared in the late 1970s. Sony and Philips, collaborating on what would become the Compact Disc standard, settled on 16-bit / 44.1 kHz in 1980 after a series of listening tests and engineering compromises. The choice of 16-bit was partly dictated by the ADC technology available at the time — achieving genuinely accurate 16-bit conversion was itself a significant engineering challenge. Early professional digital recorders, including the Sony PCM-1600 (used to produce some of the first CD releases in 1982) and the Mitsubishi X-80 32-track digital recorder (used on landmark sessions at Record Plant and The Village studios in Los Angeles), operated at 16-bit and demonstrated that the format was viable for professional use, even if engineers of the era frequently complained about a harshness in the high frequencies and a «digital» quality in the low-level detail.

The move toward 20-bit and then 24-bit resolution began in earnest in the late 1980s and accelerated through the 1990s. The introduction of the AES/EBU digital audio interface standard in 1985 paved the way for 20-bit and 24-bit data transmission. Digidesign's Pro Tools systems, beginning with the original Sound Designer software in 1984 and evolving through the 24-bit capable Pro Tools 24 system in 1997, brought high-resolution digital audio into studio workflows that had previously relied on 16-bit DAT tape backup. Simultaneously, high-end outboard converter manufacturers — Apogee Electronics (founded 1985), Prism Sound (founded 1987), and later Lavry Engineering — competed on the quality of their 20-bit and 24-bit ADC/DAC implementations, establishing a market for converter quality as distinct from mere bit-depth specification.

The adoption of floating-point arithmetic in DAW processing emerged from the workstation computing world. Digidesign's earliest TDM (Time Division Multiplexing) DSP chips processed at fixed-point precision, and engineers working on complex Pro Tools sessions in the 1990s were acutely aware of the accumulating rounding errors in long DSP chains. Steinberg's Cubase VST, introduced in 1996, moved audio processing onto the host CPU and used floating-point math natively, offering a meaningful architectural advantage. By the mid-2000s, 32-bit float internal processing was universal across all major DAW platforms. Ableton Live, Logic Pro, and later versions of Pro Tools all standardized on 32-bit float or 64-bit double-precision for summing and plugin processing, effectively solving the problem of in-box processing noise for all practical purposes and allowing producers to focus on creative decisions rather than precision management.

06 How Producers Use It

In tracking sessions, the practical application of bit depth is straightforward: set your audio interface or standalone converter to 24-bit and leave it there. Whether you are recording drums through a Neve console into an API 2500 and then to Pro Tools, or plugging a condenser microphone directly into a Focusrite Scarlett Solo on a bedroom desk, 24-bit capture ensures the noise floor of your digital recording is well below the noise floor of your analog chain. The only legitimate reason to track at 16-bit in 2026 is storage constraint on embedded systems, and even that is increasingly rare. Some interfaces, including the Universal Audio Apollo range and the RME Fireface series, advertise dynamic range specifications of 118–120 dB on their converters — still comfortably within the 144 dB theoretical ceiling of 24-bit, so the format presents no limiting factor whatsoever.

For electronic music producers working entirely in-the-box, bit depth decisions shift to the export and bounce stage. During production, the DAW's 32-bit float internal engine handles all processing; the bit depth setting in session preferences mainly governs how audio files are written to disk when recording audio clips or bouncing stems. Most producers working in Ableton Live, FL Studio, or Logic Pro should bounce stems and project renders at 24-bit WAV or AIFF, which preserves the full dynamic range of the mix for mastering. Sending a 24-bit stereo mix to a mastering engineer gives them the maximum working resolution. Sending 16-bit material pre-mastering is a workflow error that discards dynamic information that cannot be recovered.

Mixing engineers encounter bit depth most directly when dealing with session file transfers and stem exports. A common real-world scenario: a producer delivers 32-bit float WAV stems (many DAWs export this format by default when «32-bit float» is selected in export preferences), and the mixing engineer imports them into a 24-bit session. The 32-bit float files contain values that may exceed 0 dBFS — signals that are technically clipped at the 24-bit boundary but that the float file encodes faithfully. A mixing engineer who understands this can apply gain reduction before the tracks hit any channel processing, preserving the transient information. An engineer who does not understand it may experience mysterious clipping on what appear to be well-leveled tracks.

At the mastering stage, bit depth decisions become critical and irreversible. The mastering engineer works in a 32-bit or 64-bit float environment, applies processing, and must make a final decision about the delivery format. For CD, the output must be 16-bit / 44.1 kHz; for hi-res streaming platforms like Tidal Masters or Apple Music Lossless, 24-bit / 44.1–96 kHz files are accepted. The conversion from 24-bit or 32-bit float down to 16-bit requires dithering — typically using a noise-shaping algorithm like POW-r Type 3, iZotope MBIT+, or the dithering built into T-RackS or WaveLab. Applying dither twice (at an intermediate stage and again at the final bounce) stacks dither noise and should be avoided; dither is a once-only process applied as the absolute last step in the signal chain.

AbletonSet session bit depth to 24-bit in Preferences → Audio. When exporting, choose 24-bit WAV for stems and project renders. For final 16-bit delivery, use the built-in dither option in the Export Audio/Video dialog (Triangular, Rectangular, or POW-r modes available). Ableton's internal engine runs at 32-bit float regardless of session setting.
FL StudioFL Studio's mixer and plugin engine operates at 32-bit float internally. When exporting via File → Export → WAV, select 32-bit float for stem exports delivered to a mixing engineer, or 24-bit for general archive bounces. For 16-bit CD export, enable dithering in the export dialog; FL's built-in dither is functional, though third-party mastering plugins like iZotope Ozone are preferred for critical work.
Logic ProLogic processes internally at 64-bit double-precision float — the highest native precision of any major DAW. Set project sample rate and bit depth in File → Project Settings → Audio. For mixing output, bounce at 24-bit; for mastering delivery, Logic's built-in dither (accessed in the Bounce dialog under «Dithering») offers POW-r 1, 2, and 3 modes. POW-r 3 is appropriate for most program material.
Pro ToolsPro Tools sessions are created with a fixed bit depth (16-bit or 24-bit) chosen at session creation — this governs how audio files are written to disk when recording. Always create sessions at 24-bit. Internal mixing on HDX hardware operates at 48-bit fixed-point on DSP chips, which provides headroom analogous to floating-point. When bouncing to disk, select 24-bit for mix deliverables and apply dither via a dedicated plugin (Maxim or POW-r Dither) inserted last on the Master Fader for 16-bit exports.
ReaperReaper defaults to 64-bit float internal processing and allows per-track bit depth configuration unusual among DAWs. In Preferences → Audio → Recording, set default recording format to 24-bit WAV or 32-bit float WAV. For export, use File → Render and select appropriate bit depth; for 16-bit delivery, enable dither via the «Dither» checkbox in the Render dialog, which applies triangular dither. For critical mastering exports, insert a dedicated dither plugin (ReaEQ-based noise shaping or a third-party mastering suite) on the master before the final render.

07 In the Wild

Abstract knowledge becomes practical when you can hear it in music you know. These tracks demonstrate bit depth used intentionally, at specific moments, for specific purposes.

Daft Punk — "Get Lucky" (2013)
0:00–0:45 (intro guitar and bass) · Produced by Daft Punk, co-produced by Nile Rodgers

Recorded at 24-bit through vintage Neve 8078 console at Electric Lady Studios in New York. The dynamic detail in Nile Rodgers' clean Stratocaster — the pick attack transients, the string squeak, the breath between chord stabs — illustrates exactly what 24-bit capture preserves that 16-bit would attenuate. Listen on a high-resolution playback system and focus on the space between the guitar attacks; the noise floor is essentially absent beneath them, a signature of competent 24-bit capture with quality preamps.

Kendrick Lamar — "HUMBLE." (2017)
0:00–0:12 (opening piano stab and vocal entrance) · Produced by Mike WiLL Made-It

The opening piano stab on HUMBLE. is a masterclass in dynamic range exploitation. The transient is allowed to fully extend without brick-wall limiting at the tracking stage, preserving the full amplitude envelope of the attack. The rapid decay that follows reveals an extremely low noise floor — the silence between the stab and the vocal is clean to a degree that is only possible with 24-bit recording and careful gain staging. The vocal then enters at a level that would clip a 16-bit system recorded at the same reference level, illustrating the headroom advantage of 24-bit.

Billie Eilish — "bad guy" (2019)
0:45–1:10 (drop section, bass and processed vocal) · Produced by FINNEAS

Recorded largely at FINNEAS's home studio using an Apogee Duet interface (24-bit, 44.1 kHz) and an Audio-Technica AT2020 microphone in the early sessions. The production demonstrates that 24-bit capture quality is achievable on modest hardware when gain staging is correct. The bass frequencies are dense and controlled, with no quantization noise artifacts audible in the sub-bass region. The whispered vocal textures — inherently low-level signals close to the noise floor — retain their texture precisely because the 24-bit format's noise floor sits far below the quietest vocal detail.

Sufjan Stevens — "Death With Dignity" (2015)
0:00–1:30 (solo acoustic guitar into full arrangement) · Produced by Sufjan Stevens

Carrie & Lowell was recorded to 24-bit digital by engineer Casey Foubert, and the acoustic guitar opening on this track presents one of the clearest illustrations of 24-bit dynamic range preservation in contemporary folk recording. The guitar begins almost at a whisper — the gentle fingerpicking creates level variations of 50+ dB within the performance — and the digital format tracks every nuance without the noise floor rising audibly during quiet passages. Compare directly with a 16-bit reference dither of the same passage to hear quantization noise intrude on the quietest string vibrations.

08 Types & Variants

16-Bit Integer
Sony CDP-101 (1982) · Tascam DA-88 · Original Pro Tools systems

The original CD standard, offering approximately 96 dB of dynamic range. Adequate for consumer delivery formats and legacy DAT recording, but insufficient for professional recording sessions where headroom and low-level resolution are critical. Still required for CD-format delivery and remains the delivery standard for most streaming platforms, applied after mastering via dithered conversion from 24-bit.

24-Bit Integer
Apogee AD-16X · RME Fireface UFX III · Universal Audio Apollo x8p · Prism Sound ADA-8XR

The universal professional recording standard, offering approximately 144 dB of dynamic range — more than the physical limit of any microphone or preamp. All serious tracking sessions should be conducted at 24-bit. Modern high-quality converters typically achieve 118–122 dB of actual measured dynamic range, leaving the remaining theoretical headroom as mathematical safety margin.

32-Bit Float
Sound Devices 702 (field recorder) · Zoom F6 · Tascam Portacapture X8

Used in DAW internal processing engines universally and increasingly in portable field recorders designed for broadcast and film production. In field recorders, 32-bit float capture eliminates the need for gain adjustment before recording — even severely overloaded or extremely quiet signals can be recovered in post because the floating-point format encodes the true signal level rather than clipping it. As a delivery or archival format, it is increasingly practical for mixing stems where maximum processing flexibility is required.

64-Bit Double-Precision Float
Logic Pro (internal engine) · Reaper (internal engine) · Pyramix DAW

Used internally by Logic Pro and Reaper for summing and processing, 64-bit double-precision floating-point provides theoretical resolution so far beyond audible thresholds that its benefits are entirely computational — reducing accumulation of rounding errors across thousands of floating-point operations in complex sessions. No delivery format uses 64-bit float; it exists purely as an internal processing architecture to maximize the integrity of the final 24-bit or 16-bit output.

32-Bit Integer (Legacy)
Some Mitsubishi multi-track digital recorders · Experimental broadcast systems (1990s)

Rarely encountered in practice, 32-bit integer would theoretically offer approximately 192 dB of dynamic range — exceeding any physical acoustic measurement possible with current transducer technology. It is distinct from 32-bit float (which uses an exponent/mantissa structure) and was never widely standardized for professional audio. Mentioned here for completeness and to clarify the frequently confused distinction between integer and floating-point formats at the same nominal word length.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    Recording at 16-bit for «CD compatibility» — this is backwards
    A persistent myth holds that you should track at 16-bit if your final delivery is a CD, to avoid format conversion. This is precisely wrong. Always track and mix at 24-bit; convert to 16-bit with dithering only at the mastering stage. Recording at 16-bit discards dynamic information at the source that cannot be restored. The conversion step is trivially simple; the lost low-level resolution is permanent.
  • ✕
    Dithering multiple times — stacking noise where you don't need it
    Applying dither at an intermediate bounce, then again at the final export, stacks two layers of dither noise — raising the effective noise floor by approximately 3 dB and potentially introducing coloration from overlapping noise-shaping curves. Dither must be applied exactly once, as the very last process before the final bit-depth reduction. If you're mastering from a 24-bit mix to 16-bit CD, the dither goes on after every other process is complete.
  • ✕
    Confusing bit depth with sample rate — they are independent variables
    Bit depth governs amplitude resolution and dynamic range; sample rate governs temporal resolution and frequency response. Setting a session to 48 kHz does nothing for dynamic range, and setting it to 24-bit does nothing for the frequency bandwidth of the recording. Both parameters must be set correctly for their respective purposes. The common mistake is assuming that «higher numbers are better» as a unified concept, leading to sessions set to, say, 32-bit float / 44.1 kHz or 96 kHz / 16-bit — only one axis of which is correct.
  • ✕
    Exporting stems at 16-bit for mixing — delivering crippled files
    When sending stems to a mixing engineer, exporting at 16-bit instead of 24-bit discards the low-level dynamic detail in the original production. The mixer will likely apply significant gain changes, compression, and processing to the stems; operating on 16-bit material means any quiet passages in the original have already been quantized to a relatively coarse grid. Always export stems at 24-bit WAV; the modest file size increase is irrelevant against the quality cost of 16-bit stems.
  • ✕
    Applying dither before limiting or clipping at the mastering stage
    Dither must be the absolute last process in a mastering chain — placed after every gain, dynamic, EQ, and limiting stage. Inserting dither before a limiter means the dither noise itself passes through limiting and gets shaped in an uncontrolled way, and then the limiter's output still requires proper dithering. This doubles the noise contribution without the benefit of proper quantization error randomization. Every major mastering DAW (WaveLab, SADIE, Pro Tools HD) places dither on the output chain after all other processing for this reason.
  • ✕
    Assuming 32-bit float session files don't clip in 24-bit sessions
    When a DAW exports 32-bit float WAV files (common for stems in Ableton, Logic, and FL Studio), those files can contain values above 0 dBFS — data that a floating-point format can represent faithfully but that will clip immediately upon import into a session processing that pathway at integer resolution. Engineers importing 32-bit float stems must check peak levels before routing them through gain-limited paths. This is not a file corruption issue; it is an architectural difference between float and integer formats that must be understood explicitly.

10 Producers Also Look Up

Sample RateThe number of audio snapshots taken per second, determining frequency response and Nyquist bandwidth — the time-axis complement to bit depth's amplitude resolution. Gain StagingThe practice of managing signal levels at each stage of a recording and mixing chain to optimize signal-to-noise ratio and prevent clipping. HeadroomThe available amplitude margin between a signal's peak level and the clipping point, directly tied to bit depth's dynamic range provision. Noise GateA dynamics processor that attenuates signals below a threshold, often used to suppress noise floor artifacts that become more apparent in lower bit-depth recordings. LUFSThe loudness measurement standard used for streaming normalization, downstream of the recording and mastering decisions that bit depth informs. If audio was recorded at 16-bit and the performance has clipping or audible quantization noise on quiet passages, those artifacts are permanently embedded in the file. The only legitimate response is to re-record the source at 24-bit with correct gain staging. This is why establishing correct gain and bit depth at the tracking stage is non-negotiable.
Noise shaping is a technique applied during dithering that redistributes the quantization error (noise) spectrally — typically pushing energy away from the most sensitive frequency range of human hearing (around 2–5 kHz) into less-sensitive high-frequency regions above 10–15 kHz. Standard triangular or rectangular dither adds noise evenly across the spectrum; noise-shaped dither (POW-r Type 3, MBIT+, UV22HR) uses a feedback filter to concentrate that noise where it is least perceptible. The result is a perceptually lower noise floor than flat dither at the same quantization level, which is particularly valuable for 16-bit delivery formats.
In most practical sessions, the audible difference between 32-bit float and 64-bit double-precision internal processing is unmeasurable on standard monitoring systems. The benefit of 64-bit is computational: it reduces the accumulation of floating-point rounding errors across sessions with very large plugin counts, deep processing chains, or extensive parallel routing. For a session with 200+ tracks, hundreds of plugin instances, and complex bus routing, 64-bit processing reduces the theoretical error floor by approximately 48 dB versus 32-bit float — from inaudible to essentially nonexistent. For a typical production session with 20–40 tracks, 32-bit float is more than sufficient.
