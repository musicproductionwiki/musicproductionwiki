Sampling

/ˈsæmplɪŋ/

Quick Answer Definition How It Works Parameters Quick Reference History How To Use It In The Wild Types Mistakes Related Further Reading FAQ

Sampling is the practice of recording a fragment of existing audio and repurposing it as a musical element in a new composition. It underlies entire genres—hip-hop, jungle, house—and remains one of music production's most powerful creative tools.

01 Definition

Every record ever pressed is a library waiting to be cracked open — sampling is the key, the scalpel, and the wrecking ball all at once.

Sampling, in music production, is the act of capturing a discrete segment of audio — whether from a vinyl record, a live instrument, a field recording, a film dialogue reel, or any other sound source — digitising that segment, and then deploying it as a playable, repeatable musical element within a new composition or arrangement. The captured segment is called a sample. It can range from a single transient hit (a snare crack lasting 50 ms) to a multi-bar loop, an entire chord progression, or an isolated vocal phrase lifted from a decades-old soul record. The defining characteristic is that the source audio is real-world sound reproduced faithfully, as opposed to audio synthesised mathematically from oscillators.

Conceptually, sampling sits at the intersection of synthesis and composition. A sampler — the hardware unit or software engine that manages samples — functions like a synthesiser in that it maps audio across a keyboard and applies pitch, envelope, and modulation controls to shape the sound. But unlike a synthesiser, the sampler's raw material is already acoustically rich: it contains the room, the mic, the performer's breath, the analogue tape hiss, and all the micro-imperfections that make recorded sound feel alive. This is precisely why producers reach for samples rather than synthesised equivalents: the texture is irreproducibly human.

The term carries a second, more technical meaning in digital audio theory: the process by which continuous analogue waveforms are converted into discrete digital data points. During analogue-to-digital conversion (ADC), the audio signal is measured — or 'sampled' — at regular intervals defined by the sample rate (typically 44,100 times per second for CD-quality audio). Each measurement is quantised to a bit-depth value (16-bit, 24-bit). While this meaning is foundational to understanding audio fidelity and is covered in depth in the sample-rate and bit-depth entries, the present article concerns sampling as a compositional and production technique: the creative repurposing of recorded audio.

Legally and commercially, sampling is one of the most consequential decisions a producer makes. Using a recognisable excerpt from a commercially released recording without authorisation infringes two separate copyrights simultaneously: the composition copyright (owned by the publisher or songwriter) and the master copyright (owned by the record label or recording artist). Clearance for both is required before release. Failure to clear samples has resulted in landmark lawsuits — Bridgeport Music v. Dimension Films (2004) established that even a two-second guitar chord requires clearance under US law — and has shaped how an entire generation of producers approach the technique. Royalty-free sample libraries and original interpolation have emerged as alternatives, but cleared samples from classic records remain the aspirational gold standard.

Despite its legal complexity, sampling has democratised music production more than any other single technique. A teenager with a used MPC2000 and a crate of thrift-store records could — and did — build careers by reimagining the past as the future. That creative leverage is why sampling remains central to hip-hop, house, jungle, footwork, lo-fi, and scores of subgenres that followed. Understanding how samplers work, what parameters matter, and how to deploy samples intelligently is not optional for a serious producer: it is foundational.

02 How It Works

At the hardware level, a sampler's signal chain begins with an audio input stage — a line-level, phono, or microphone input — feeding an analogue-to-digital converter. The ADC samples the incoming voltage at the set sample rate and quantises each measurement to the chosen bit depth, producing a stream of binary data stored in RAM or on-board flash. On vintage hardware like the E-mu SP-1200 (1987), RAM was brutally limited: 2.5 seconds at 26.04 kHz, forcing producers to chop aggressively and work within severe constraints that, paradoxically, defined a sonic aesthetic. Modern software samplers, by contrast, operate on host RAM measured in gigabytes, imposing no practical ceiling.

Once audio is loaded into a sampler's memory, it is mapped across a pitch grid — usually MIDI note numbers 0–127. Playing middle C (MIDI 60) triggers the sample at its original pitch; playing D above middle C transposes it up two semitones by resampling the data at a different playback rate. Early hardware achieved pitch-shifting by literally speeding up or slowing down playback (altering both pitch and duration), which introduced audible artefacts at extreme intervals — the characteristic 'chipmunk' or 'woozy' timbres of SP-1200 and Akai S900 pitched-down bass. Modern samplers use time-stretching algorithms (granular, phase-vocoder, or formant-preserving) to decouple pitch from tempo, enabling clean transposition across ±24 semitones or more without duration change.

The sampler's playback engine exposes several foundational parameters that shape how the audio behaves. The start and end points define which portion of the loaded audio plays. Loop points instruct the sampler to repeat a defined region seamlessly while a MIDI note is held — critical for sustaining pad or bass samples. An amplitude envelope (ADSR) controls how the sample's volume evolves over time from note-on to note-off. Root note tells the sampler which MIDI pitch represents the original, unshifted recording. Velocity layers allow different recordings of the same instrument at different dynamics to be triggered depending on how hard a key is struck, vastly increasing realism. Filter cutoff and resonance parameters borrowed from subtractive synthesis allow tonal shaping — darkening a bright loop, adding bite to a pad — without leaving the sampler environment.

In DAW-based workflows, sampling also encompasses the practice of 'chopping': importing a full audio loop or recording, slicing it into individual hit regions (often following transient-detection algorithms), and triggering those slices non-sequentially from a drum pad or MIDI keyboard. Ableton Live's Simpler and Drum Rack, FL Studio's SliceX, and Native Instruments' Maschine Sampler all automate this process. The result is that a six-second drum break from a 1969 soul record can become 24 individually playable hits, each re-sequenced into an entirely new rhythmic pattern — the essence of how J Dilla, Pete Rock, and DJ Premier built their drum programming vocabulary.

At its core, sampling is a playback fidelity system constrained by three variables: the quality of the source audio, the quality of the ADC/DAC conversion chain, and the intelligence of the producer's editorial choices — which fragment to take, at what start point, with what processing. Every parameter decision propagates directly into the final sound. A one-millisecond error in loop point placement creates an audible click. A root note set two semitones wrong makes every pitched use of the sample harmonically incorrect. Mastery of sampling is therefore as much about precision and ear training as it is about creative taste.

03 The Parameters

Every sampling — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these and you can work with any implementation.

SAMPLE RATE
Number of audio snapshots captured per second

Expressed in Hz or kHz, the sample rate determines the highest reproducible frequency (Nyquist = sample rate ÷ 2). At 44,100 Hz the ceiling is 22,050 Hz — beyond human hearing. Vintage hardware running at 26 kHz or lower introduced a gritty, bandwidth-limited character that producers deliberately seek; the SP-1200's 26.04 kHz ceiling rolls off content above ~13 kHz, lending its characteristic 'dusty' top end.

BIT DEPTH
Quantisation resolution of each audio sample

Each sample is stored as a binary number; bit depth sets how many values are available. 16-bit yields 65,536 amplitude levels (~96 dB dynamic range); 24-bit yields ~16.7 million levels (~144 dB). The Akai MPC60 used 12-bit conversion, producing a noise floor around -72 dB and a crunchy saturation when signals were driven hot — a sound producers actively recreate using 'bit-crusher' processing on otherwise clean 24-bit sessions.

START / END POINTS
Define the active playback region within the loaded audio

Precise start-point placement determines whether a sample begins on the exact transient attack (essential for drum hits) or fractionally before it (adding a breath or room pre-hit). A start point set too late truncates the attack transient, making drum hits sound weak. In loop-based work, mismatched end points cause pitch discontinuities or clicks at the loop boundary; crossfade loop modes smooth this by blending the tail back into the head over a user-defined sample count.

ROOT NOTE
The MIDI pitch at which the sample plays at its original speed

Setting root note incorrectly by even one semitone means every melodic or harmonic use of that sample will be in the wrong key — a mistake that costs time in large template sessions with many mapped patches. Best practice is to tune the source sample against a reference sine wave before mapping, then confirm root note after mapping by playing the sample alongside a MIDI instrument tuned to A=440.

LOOP POINTS
Define the region that repeats while a note is held

Loop start and end must be placed at zero-crossings — points where the waveform crosses the amplitude axis — to avoid clicks. For harmonic material (pads, strings, choir), loop points landing mid-cycle create phase cancellation artefacts. Crossfade looping (blending 5–50 ms of the loop tail into the loop head) is the standard fix for complex tonal material. Rhythm loops require loop length to divide evenly into the tempo-relative sample count: at 120 BPM, a one-bar loop at 44,100 Hz is exactly 22,050 samples.

ENVELOPE (ADSR)
Volume contour applied to the sample on note-on and note-off

Attack (0–500 ms) softens hard sample transients or creates a fade-in swell on sustained patches. Decay shapes the drop from peak to sustain level; for drums, near-zero decay with zero sustain gives a punchy truncated hit. Release (0–5,000 ms) determines how quickly the sample fades after a note-off MIDI message. On classic MPC workflows, a short release of 30–60 ms on chopped drum hits prevents neighbouring hits from bleeding into each other when pad buttons are released.

PITCH / TRANSPOSE
Shifts the playback pitch in semitones or cents

Semitone transposition (+/-) shifts the root note in whole-step increments; fine-tune (cents) shifts in hundredths of a semitone for precise intonation matching. Without time-stretching engaged, pitch transposition also alters playback duration — a sample pitched down 12 semitones plays back at half speed, doubling its length. Modern samplers offer formant-preserving pitch-shift modes (Ableton's 'Complex Pro', Kontakt's 'Tone' mode) that maintain natural timbral character at ±6–12 semitones before artefacts become prominent.

FILTER CUTOFF
Low-pass or multi-mode filter that shapes the sample's spectral content

A low-pass filter sweeping from 200 Hz to 18 kHz dramatically alters a bright sample's character, enabling everything from muffled lo-fi textures to wide-open clarity without resampling the source. Resonance (Q) set above 50% creates a frequency peak at the cutoff that accentuates harmonic content and can produce a self-oscillating feedback tone at extreme settings. Velocity-to-cutoff modulation — where harder key strikes open the filter further — is a standard technique for giving sampled keys and pads an organic, touch-sensitive quality.

05 History & Origin

The conceptual roots of sampling predate digital audio by decades. Pierre Schaeffer's musique concrète experiments at Radiodiffusion Française beginning in 1948 established the foundational practice: recording real-world sounds onto magnetic tape, then physically cutting, looping, reversing, and speed-altering those recordings to create new compositions. Schaeffer called his individual recorded objects 'sound objects' (objets sonores). His 1948 work Études de bruits — assembled from recordings of spinning tops, train whistles, and boats — is widely recognised as the first canonical work of sample-based composition. Contemporaries Pierre Henry and Karlheinz Stockhausen extended the practice throughout the 1950s, normalising the idea that recorded sound was as legitimate a compositional material as notation.

The electromechanical Mellotron (1963, Birmingham) and its predecessor the Chamberlin (1948, Harry Chamberlin) were the first mass-manufactured instruments to use recorded audio as playable patches. The Mellotron loaded strips of magnetic tape for each of its 35 keys; pressing a key advanced the tape across a playback head at a fixed speed, producing strings, flutes, choirs, and brass. The Beatles' use of the Mellotron flute setting on 'Strawberry Fields Forever' (1967, produced by George Martin and Geoff Emerick) introduced millions of listeners to sample-based texture, though the mechanism was invisible to them. The instrument's 8-second playback limit per note and tape-tracking instability gave it a characteristic wavering imprecision that became part of its appeal.

Digital sampling was commercially introduced by the Fairlight CMI (Computer Musical Instrument), developed in Sydney, Australia by Peter Vogel and Kim Ryrie in 1979. Kate Bush, Peter Gabriel, and Stevie Wonder were among its earliest high-profile adopters. The E-mu Emulator (1981) brought digital sampling within reach of working musicians at $10,000, followed by the LinnDrum (1982) and the Ensoniq Mirage (1985) at $1,695 — a democratising price point. The instrument that defined hip-hop sampling culture was the Akai MPC60, designed by Roger Linn and released in 1988, which combined a 12-bit stereo sampler, 16-velocity-sensitive pads, and an integrated MIDI step sequencer. Its successor, the MPC3000 (1994), extended RAM to 32 MB and offered 16-bit/44.1 kHz capture, and became the production centrepiece for artists including The Notorious B.I.G., Jay-Z, and Timbaland. The SP-1200 (E-mu, 1987) ran parallel, its 26 kHz sample rate and 12-bit character making it the preferred tool of producers like Pete Rock, DJ Premier, and Large Professor who valued its distinctive grit.

The legal landscape of sampling shifted decisively in 1991 when rapper Biz Markie lost Grand Upright Music, Ltd. v. Warner Bros. Records Inc. after sampling Gilbert O'Sullivan's 'Alone Again (Naturally)' without clearance. Judge Kevin Duffy's ruling — famously opening with a Biblical reference to 'Thou shalt not steal' — established that sampling without permission constituted copyright infringement, forcing labels and producers to formalise clearance processes. By the mid-1990s, clearance costs for prominent samples had inflated to tens or hundreds of thousands of dollars, prompting producers like DJ Shadow (whose 1996 album Endtroducing… was constructed entirely from crate-dug samples) to work independently or underground, and pushing mainstream hip-hop producers toward original interpolation and royalty-free source material. The rise of software samplers — Native Instruments Kontakt (2002), Ableton Live's Simpler and Sampler (2001–2004) — eventually moved the workflow from hardware to laptop, reducing barriers further while the legal framework remained as complex as ever.

06 How Producers Use It

Drum programming and breaks. The most historically significant sampling application is the drum break: an isolated section of a recording in which the drummer plays unaccompanied. The Amen break (from The Winstons' 'Amen, Brother', 1969), the Funky Drummer break (James Brown, 1970, drummed by Clyde Stubblefield), and the Think break (Lyn Collins, 1972) have been sampled thousands of times across hip-hop, jungle, drum and bass, and breakbeat. In practice, a producer loads the break into an MPC or DAW sampler, sets start and end points to isolate the cleanest phrase, chops individual hits onto pads, then re-sequences them at a new tempo — layering, reversing, and pitching hits to construct an entirely new rhythm that retains the organic dynamics of the original performance.

Melodic and harmonic sampling. Producers lift chord progressions, bass lines, and melodic hooks from soul, jazz, funk, and classical recordings, filtering and processing them to fit a new harmonic context. The iconic practice of 'flipping' a sample — taking a slow, lush orchestral phrase and repitching it up to sit over a hard kick-hat pattern — defines the sonic signature of producers like Kanye West, RZA, and Madlib. Key technique: when the source tempo and the production tempo differ, the pitch of the loop shifts proportionally unless time-stretching is applied. A loop sampled at 70 BPM played at 90 BPM will be pitched up by a semitone and a half without stretching. Producers either use pitch-correct time-stretching or deliberately exploit this pitch drift as a creative effect.

Vocal and texture sampling. Isolated vocal phrases — ad-libs, spoken word, choir hits, breath sounds — are deployed as rhythmic or harmonic elements. A classic technique is the 'vocal chop': slicing a sustained vocal vowel into sub-100 ms fragments and re-sequencing them as rapid-fire rhythmic events, a foundation of future bass, EDM, and trap production (popularised by producers including Skrillex, Flosstradamus, and Murda Beatz). Texture sampling involves capturing non-musical room ambience, vinyl surface noise, tape hiss, or environmental sounds and layering them beneath a mix to add warmth and depth — a technique central to lo-fi hip-hop aesthetics pioneered by producers like Nujabes and j^p^n.

Resampling and bounce-to-sample. Resampling is the practice of routing a section of your own in-progress production back through an audio input or internal routing and re-recording it as a new sample. This 'commits' processing, frees CPU, and — critically — introduces the character of the audio interface's converters a second time. Many hardware-centric producers resample aggressively to 'print' the texture of their outboard compression, EQ, or saturation. In Ableton Live, Capture Audio From Master Output creates a bounce; in the MPC workflow, resampling to a new pad is a foundational part of the creative loop that defines that instrument's feel.

07 In the Wild

Abstract knowledge becomes practical when you can hear it in music you know. These tracks demonstrate sampling used intentionally, at specific moments, for specific purposes.

Public Enemy — "Fight the Power" (1989)
0:00 onward · Produced by The Bomb Squad (Hank Shocklee, Eric Sadler, Chuck D, Keith Shocklee)

The Bomb Squad layered over 200 individual samples across the track, including James Brown's 'Funky Drummer' break (pitched and filtered), a Sly & the Family Stone horn stab, and multiple spoken-word fragments. The result is a dense, confrontational collage where no single sample dominates — each element is treated with heavy processing (filtering, time-compression, pitch-shift) to the point of near-unrecognisability. Listen at 0:14 for the rhythmic stuttering of the JB break, and at 0:32 for the repeated horn hit that anchors the chorus — both demonstrate advanced start-point manipulation and velocity variation.

Kanye West — "Soul Sister" / "Through the Wire" (2003)
0:00–0:12 · Produced by Kanye West

Kanye pitched up Chaka Khan's 1978 vocal from 'Through the Fire' by approximately a major third, placing it in a higher, more ethereal register over sparse drums. This is a textbook demonstration of repitch-based time-stretching: because Kanye used an earlier MPC workflow without sophisticated time-stretch, the pitched vocal also runs fractionally faster, contributing to the chipmunk-soul aesthetic he formalised. Note the vinyl crackle underneath — a resampled surface noise layer added for warmth. The loop crossfade is seamless across the bar line at 0:04.

DJ Shadow — "Midnight in a Perfect World" (1996)
Full track · Produced by DJ Shadow (Josh Davis)

Constructed entirely from crate-dug vinyl samples on an Akai MPC3000, this track demonstrates meticulous start/end point editing — the main piano loop at 0:00 has zero pre-hit room noise, indicating a frame-accurate start point. The underlying atmospheric texture is a layered stack of noise samples and reversed room tones that never repeat with the same timing, suggesting sample-offset variation between layers. At 1:34, the break is transposed down approximately four semitones without time-stretching, illustrating deliberate pitch-drift exploitation to create timbral weight.

Kendrick Lamar — "King Kunta" (2015)
0:00–0:08 · Produced by Sounwave, LoveDragon, and Rahki

The opening bass figure interpolates the funk bass character of James Brown-era recordings without a direct sample, but the drums in the verse use a chopped, heavily compressed break filtered aggressively below 6 kHz. The snare hit around 0:07 has an identifiably sampled texture — listen for the room ambience tail that persists briefly after the hit, characteristic of a one-shot sample with a 60 ms release rather than a synthesised drum. The filtering and saturation applied to the drums were achieved partly through deliberate resampling through analogue gear.

Burial — "Archangel" (2007)
0:25–0:50 · Produced by Burial (William Bevan)

Burial's signature method involves chopping vocal phrases into very short (20–80 ms) fragments, resequencing them with deliberate gap-and-stutter rhythms, and adding extensive reverb and vinyl-noise sampling. At 0:25, the pitched vocal fragment that forms the melodic hook is a classic 'slice-and-repitch' move — the original vocal is unrecognisable in timbre but its phoneme sequence creates a melodic contour. The vinyl surface-noise layer that runs throughout was sampled from actual vinyl on a Technics 1200 and looped with loop points placed at zero crossings to avoid clicks.

08 Types & Variants

One-Shot Sampling
Akai MPC60 · E-mu SP-1200

A one-shot sample plays from start point to end point exactly once per trigger and does not loop. It is the definitive mode for drum hits, percussion, stabs, and any percussive element where sustain is unwanted. Release time on the amplitude envelope determines how quickly the tail fades after the sample's natural end; short releases (20–50 ms) are essential when sequencing rapid hits that would otherwise bleed into each other.

Loop Sampling
Akai S1000 · Fairlight CMI

Loop sampling repeats a defined region of the sample continuously while a MIDI note is held, enabling indefinitely sustaining pads, strings, choirs, and textures without requiring a recording as long as the performance itself. Seamless looping demands careful crossfade setup and zero-crossing loop point placement; badly placed loop points produce audible clicks or amplitude steps at the loop boundary. Sustain loops (repeating the middle portion) and release loops (repeating the tail as the note releases) offer further timbral control.

Sliced / Chopped Sampling
Akai MPC3000 · Maschine

A longer audio file — typically a drum loop, a musical phrase, or a vocal performance — is divided into individual slices using transient detection or manually placed edit points. Each slice is mapped to a separate MIDI note or pad, allowing the producer to trigger slices in any order, skip slices, apply different processing to individual hits, and construct entirely new rhythmic or melodic sequences. This mode is the backbone of hip-hop beat-making, jungle production, and footwork music.

Multi-Sample / Velocity Layering
E-mu Emulator III · Native Instruments Kontakt

A single instrument is recorded at multiple pitches (typically every minor third or major second across the full range) and at multiple dynamic levels (piano, mezzo-forte, forte), with each recording mapped to its corresponding MIDI zone. Velocity determines which dynamic layer triggers. This approach produces the most realistic replications of acoustic instruments — piano, orchestral strings, ethnic instruments — and is the standard method for professional sample library design. Libraries like Spitfire Audio's BBCSO and EastWest Hollywood Strings use thousands of samples per instrument.

Granular Sampling
Roland VP-9000 · Tassman

Granular sampling decomposes a source recording into tiny grains (typically 10–200 ms) and reassembles them according to controllable parameters including grain size, density, scatter, and playback position. It enables radical time-stretching without pitch alteration, infinite freeze effects, and timbral morphing between two sound sources. Ableton Live's Granulator II (Max for Live), Native Instruments' Pharlight, and Arturia's Pigments granular engine are widely used software implementations; the aesthetic ranges from ultra-smooth cinematic pads to glitching, fragmented textures.

Resampling
Akai MPC series · Any DAW with internal routing

Resampling routes a portion of the producer's own in-progress session — often including effects, processing, and multiple layers — back through an audio input or internal record bus, capturing the result as a new sample. The purpose is to commit processing (freeing CPU), to imprint the colour of analogue outboard or converter character onto the audio a second time, or to capture a spontaneous performance or knob-twist that would be impossible to reproduce. In MPC culture, resampling is considered an essential compositional step rather than a technical workaround.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    Setting start point after the transient attack
    If the start point is positioned even a few milliseconds past the onset of a drum hit or note attack, the initial transient — the loudest, most defined moment — is cut off. The result is a sample that sounds soft, undefined, or 'late' compared to other elements. Fix: zoom to sample level, locate the first point where the waveform rises sharply above the noise floor, and set start precisely there. Many DAWs offer snap-to-zero-crossing, but for percussive material, snapping to the nearest transient peak is more accurate.
  • ✕
    Incorrect root note causing harmonic misalignment
    Loading a melodic sample and leaving root note at the default (often C3 or A3) when the sample is actually pitched to a different note means every use of that sample will be harmonically incorrect. A bass loop in F♯ with root note set to C plays in C when you press C on the keyboard — and in the wrong key for every other note. Fix: use a tuner or spectral analysis (e.g. iZotope RX's pitch analysis) to confirm the sample's fundamental pitch, then set root note to match before sequencing.
  • ✕
    Loop point clicks from non-zero-crossing placement
    Placing a loop end point where the waveform is not at or near zero amplitude creates a sudden discontinuity each time the loop cycles — heard as a sharp click or pop that repeats rhythmically and cannot be removed downstream. This mistake is especially common when loop points are set by eye rather than by ear. Fix: enable zero-crossing snap in your sampler's loop point editor, or apply a short crossfade (10–30 ms) between the loop tail and loop head to smooth the transition — most modern samplers offer this as a one-click function.
  • ✕
    Over-time-stretching without checking artefacts
    Applying extreme time-stretch ratios (moving a 90 BPM loop to 140 BPM, for example) using the wrong algorithm produces metallic, smeared, or 'flamming' artefacts that sit incompatibly in a mix. The Rhythmic or Beats algorithm should be used for drum loops (preserving transient positions), while Polyphonic or Complex Pro handles harmonic material better. Fix: test time-stretch ratios auditioning at actual tempo, check the 4 kHz–12 kHz range for metallic shimmer, and consider manually chopping transient-heavy material rather than relying on elastic algorithms at extreme ratios.
  • ✕
    Neglecting clearance for commercially released samples
    Releasing a track — even independently, on platforms like SoundCloud or Bandcamp — containing an uncleared sample from a commercially released recording exposes the producer and any associated label to copyright infringement claims covering both composition and master rights. The financial penalties can far exceed any revenue the release generates. Fix: either obtain clearance through the publisher and label before release, use royalty-free sample libraries (Splice, Loopmasters licensed content), or interpolate the musical idea as an original re-recording rather than using the original master.
  • ✕
    Ignoring tempo-pitch interaction when not time-stretching
    When a sampled loop is played at a different BPM than its original tempo without time-stretching active, its pitch shifts — higher at faster tempos, lower at slower ones. Producers sometimes import a loop into a session, sequence it to a different BPM, and cannot understand why it sounds out of tune. Fix: either engage time-stretching (warp in Ableton, Flex in Logic, Elastic Audio in Pro Tools) to maintain pitch at the new tempo, or calculate the expected pitch shift and compensate with transpose — approximately one semitone per 6% tempo change.

EnvelopeThe ADSR envelope shapes how a sampled sound's volume evolves from trigger to release, directly controlling articulation and perceived attack. FilterSamplers integrate filters borrowed from subtractive synthesis to reshape the spectral character of captured audio without requiring source re-recording. Sample RateThe number of digital snapshots taken per second during ADC conversion, determining the highest reproducible frequency and overall audio fidelity. 12 Frequently Asked Questions

Sampling is the practice of capturing a segment of existing audio — from a record, live instrument, film, or any other source — and repurposing it as a musical element in a new production. The captured audio can be a drum hit, a chord loop, a vocal phrase, or an ambient texture. It is replayed via a hardware or software sampler, mapped across a keyboard or pad controller, and shaped with envelope, pitch, and filter controls. Sampling underpins entire genres including hip-hop, jungle, and house music.
Yes, if you are sampling from a commercially released recording, you must obtain clearance for two separate copyrights: the composition (owned by the publisher or songwriter) and the master recording (owned by the label or artist). Releasing without clearance — even on free platforms — constitutes copyright infringement and can result in takedowns, injunctions, and financial damages. Alternatives include using royalty-free or licensed sample libraries (Splice, Loopmasters), recording original source material, or creating an interpolation (re-recording the musical idea without copying the original master).
A synthesiser generates audio from scratch using mathematical signal generation — oscillators, noise generators, and mathematical waveforms. A sampler uses recorded real-world audio as its raw material, storing that audio in memory and playing it back at different pitches, with applied processing. The distinction blurs in hybrid instruments (like Omnisphere or Serum's wavetable mode), but the core difference is whether the fundamental sound source is generated or recorded. Samplers tend to produce more acoustically realistic sounds; synthesisers offer more timbral flexibility from first principles.
For most production work, 44,100 Hz (44.1 kHz) at 24-bit is the standard. If your project session runs at 48 kHz (common for audio that will be paired with video), match that rate throughout to avoid sample-rate conversion artefacts. Use 24-bit rather than 16-bit during capture and in-session work; the additional headroom reduces quantisation noise, especially during processing. If you are deliberately pursuing a lo-fi aesthetic, emulate lower rates and bit depths using a bitcrusher or decimator plugin rather than actually capturing at inferior settings — this preserves the flexibility to bypass the effect.
When a loop plays at a tempo different from its recorded tempo without time-stretching, playback speed changes — which also changes pitch. Enable your DAW's time-stretching: Warp in Ableton Live, Flex Time in Logic Pro, or Elastic Audio in Pro Tools. Choose the appropriate algorithm (Rhythmic or Beats for drum loops; Complex, Polyphonic, or Monophonic for harmonic material). If artefacts are unacceptable at the required ratio, consider chopping the loop into individual hits and re-sequencing — this avoids time-stretching entirely and often produces a more rhythmically alive result.
Resampling routes your own in-progress production — including all applied effects and processing — back through a record input or internal bus, capturing the result as a new audio file that is then treated as a fresh sample. Producers resample to commit effects (freeing CPU resources), to imprint the character of analogue outboard processing onto audio, to capture spontaneous real-time knob performances, or simply to render a complex layered sound into a single manageable element. In MPC-based workflows, resampling bounce-to-pad is a rhythmic and creative habit, not just a technical workaround.
Traditional sampling plays a source recording from a defined start point to an end point at a set playback speed. Granular sampling decomposes the source into tiny grains (10–200 ms) and reconstructs audio from those grains using controllable parameters — grain size, density, scatter, position in the source file, and inter-grain pitch variation. This enables extreme, artefact-free time-stretching, freeze effects (holding a single moment of a recording indefinitely), textural morphing, and cloud-like diffuse timbres impossible with conventional playback. Ableton's Granulator II (Max for Live) and Native Instruments' Pharlight are widely used implementations.
Without time-stretching, pitch-shifting a sample by speeding up or slowing down playback introduces duration changes and at extreme intervals produces audible timbral degradation — the classic 'chipmunk' (pitched up) or 'woozy' (pitched down) effect. With modern pitch-shifting algorithms (formant-preserving modes in Kontakt, Simpler's Complex Pro, Logic's Flex Pitch), clean transposition of ±6 semitones is achievable for most material; beyond ±12 semitones, artefacts — metallic shimmer, flanging, or loss of transient definition — become prominent for most sources. Vocal content degrades faster than sustained harmonic material; percussive one-shots tolerate wider transposition before pitch-shifting sounds unnatural.
