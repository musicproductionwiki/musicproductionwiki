Additive Synthesis

/əˈdɪtɪv ˈsɪnθəsɪs/

Quick Answer Definition How It Works Parameters Quick Reference History How To Use It In The Wild Types Mistakes Calculator Related Further Reading FAQ

Additive Synthesis is a sound synthesis method that builds complex timbres by combining multiple sine waves — called partials — each with its own frequency, amplitude, and phase. It is the most direct electronic implementation of Fourier's theorem.

01 Definition

Most synthesis methods take a complex waveform and sculpt it down. Additive synthesis does the opposite — it builds sound the way the universe does, one pure frequency at a time, giving you control that feels less like engineering and more like composition.

Additive synthesis is a method of generating sound by summing multiple sinusoidal oscillators — referred to as partials or harmonics — each assigned an independent frequency, amplitude envelope, and phase value. Because every periodic sound can be mathematically decomposed into a sum of sine waves (a consequence of the Fourier theorem first formalized in 1822), additive synthesis is, in principle, capable of reproducing any stable or evolving timbre. No other synthesis paradigm offers this level of direct, component-by-component control over a sound's spectral content.

In practical terms, an additive synthesizer presents a bank of oscillators — anywhere from 16 to several hundred — each tuned to a specific frequency ratio relative to a fundamental pitch. The ratios that fall on integer multiples of the fundamental (1×, 2×, 3×, 4× …) are called harmonic partials and produce tonal, pitched sounds. Partials tuned to non-integer ratios are inharmonic and produce the clangy, metallic, or bell-like qualities you hear in instruments such as mallet percussion, cymbals, and plucked metal strings. The ability to independently address each partial's amplitude over time — typically via per-partial envelope generators — is what distinguishes additive synthesis from simply sending a complex waveform through static filters.

Compared to subtractive synthesis, which begins with a harmonically rich oscillator (sawtooth, square, pulse) and removes content through filters, additive synthesis adds nothing it did not deliberately put there. This makes spectral design explicit and auditable: if you hear an unpleasant high-frequency shimmer, you know exactly which partial caused it and can attenuate or reshape its envelope independently. This precision is invaluable for recreating acoustic instruments, designing evolving pad textures, and crafting sounds that must evolve in harmonically specific ways — such as formant-shifted vocals or bell tones with physically accurate decay curves per partial.

The principal trade-off is computational cost and interface complexity. Generating 64 or more independently enveloped sine waves in real time was prohibitively expensive before the 1990s, which is why additive synthesis remained largely confined to academic institutions and expensive commercial hardware for its first two decades. Modern CPUs handle hundreds of partials without strain, but the interface problem persists: editing dozens of individual partial envelopes is time-consuming without specialized graphical tools. Contemporary additive synthesizers — both hardware and software — typically provide spectral displays, grouping tools, and morphing features to make this manageable. Understanding the underlying model, however, remains essential to using those tools purposefully.

02 How It Works

At its core, additive synthesis is the real-time computational execution of Fourier synthesis. Each partial is generated by a numerically controlled oscillator (NCO) or wavetable lookup that produces a pure sine wave at a specified frequency. These individual sine outputs are multiplied by a time-varying gain value — controlled by an envelope generator specific to that partial — and then summed together. The summed signal passes to the audio output without any further filtering in a pure additive architecture. The mathematical expression is: y(t) = Σ Aₙ(t) · sin(2π·fₙ·t + φₙ), where Aₙ is the time-varying amplitude of the nth partial, fₙ is its frequency, and φₙ is its phase offset.

Partial frequencies can be set in three main ways. Fixed-ratio mode locks each partial to an integer or non-integer multiple of the fundamental, so when you play a different pitch the entire harmonic structure transposes coherently — useful for tonal instruments. Fixed-frequency mode keeps each partial at an absolute Hz value regardless of MIDI note, which is useful for inharmonic textures like bells or gongs where the spectral relationships should not transpose. Hybrid mode allows some partials to track pitch while others remain fixed, enabling complex instrument modeling where, for example, a body resonance stays constant while the string harmonics transpose. Most hardware and software additive synthesizers offer at least fixed-ratio and fixed-frequency modes; sophisticated instruments like the Kawai K5000 offered full per-partial control of both.

The temporal evolution of each partial is governed by its envelope — minimally an attack and decay, but often a full multi-stage ADSR or arbitrary breakpoint envelope. This is where additive synthesis diverges most significantly from all other methods: a bell sound, for instance, requires high partials to decay faster than low partials, which is a physical property of struck metal that a single shared envelope cannot capture but per-partial envelopes can model exactly. Analysis-resynthesis pipelines — where a recording is Fourier-analyzed to extract per-partial amplitude tracks, then used to drive an additive synthesizer — exploit this directly, enabling faithful acoustic instrument cloning and time-stretching without pitch artifacts. Tools such as SPEAR (Sinusoidal Partial Editing and Resynthesis) and the analysis engine inside Camel Audio Alchemy made this workflow accessible to producers.

Phase relationships between partials are audible primarily at the attack transient and in extreme cases can cause waveform asymmetry or DC offset. In most musical contexts, listeners are not sensitive to steady-state inter-partial phase, which is why many additive systems randomize or ignore phase. However, for physical modeling accuracy — particularly percussive transients — phase initialization matters and some advanced synthesizers allow per-partial phase setting. Noise partials (band-limited noise generators assigned to specific frequency bands) extend pure additive synthesis toward hybrid additive-noise models, essential for reproducing the breathiness of flutes, the bow noise of strings, or the cymbal wash component of hi-hats alongside their tonal shimmer.

The practical result of all these mechanisms is a synthesis method with effectively infinite timbral resolution but a steep editing curve. Producers who invest in understanding the harmonic series — knowing that odd harmonics dominate clarinet-like timbres, that inharmonic partials concentrated in the 3–5× range create bell tones, and that amplitude relationships following a 1/n rolloff produce sawtooth-like brightness — can design sounds with a precision that no subtractive or FM approach can match at a component level.

03 The Parameters

Every additive synthesis — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these and you can work with any implementation.

PARTIAL COUNT
Number of simultaneously active sine-wave oscillators

Determines timbral resolution and computational load. 16 partials covers the audible harmonic series for most tonal sounds; 64+ partials are needed for dense, acoustically realistic instruments or complex inharmonic textures. In CPU-limited contexts, reducing partial count to 32 is a practical starting point that preserves perceptual richness while freeing headroom.

PARTIAL FREQUENCY RATIO
Frequency relationship of each partial to the fundamental

Integer ratios (1, 2, 3, 4…) produce harmonic, tonal sounds like strings and brass. Non-integer ratios such as 1.41, 2.76, or 4.03 produce inharmonic, metallic timbres characteristic of bells, gongs, and struck metal. Shifting a single mid-range partial by 3–5% from its integer ratio introduces subtle roughness that mimics the acoustic detuning of real instruments — a powerful realism tool.

PARTIAL AMPLITUDE
Level of each individual sine-wave partial at steady state

The amplitude distribution across partials defines the fundamental character of a sound. A 1/n rolloff (each harmonic at 1/n of the fundamental's amplitude) approximates a sawtooth wave and produces bright, buzzy tones. Emphasizing only odd harmonics (1, 3, 5…) at declining levels recreates a square-wave-like hollow quality associated with clarinets and flutes. Boosting higher partials (above the 8th) adds presence and air that feels distinct from EQ-applied brightness.

PER-PARTIAL ENVELOPE
Attack, decay, sustain, and release times unique to each partial

The most powerful and most CPU-intensive parameter in additive synthesis. High partials of a piano decay significantly faster than the fundamental — modeling this realistically requires independent decay times per partial, which a single shared envelope cannot approximate. Setting partials 1–4 to long sustain while partials 8–16 decay in 200–400ms produces convincing acoustic piano-like evolution without sampling.

PARTIAL PHASE
Initial phase offset of each sine-wave partial at note onset

Phase relationships are largely inaudible in steady state but critically affect transient character. Aligning all partials to 0° produces a sharp, peaky attack; randomizing phases creates a softer onset with a more diffuse initial transient. Some additive instruments use phase randomization as a default to avoid the percussive click that cosine-aligned starts produce at low fundamental frequencies.

MORPHING / INTERPOLATION
Crossfading between two or more spectral frames or preset states

Advanced additive synthesizers store multiple amplitude-frequency 'snapshots' called spectral frames and interpolate between them over time or via modulation. This enables smooth timbral evolution — for example, morphing from a flute timbre to an oboe timbre over 4 bars — that is physically impossible in subtractive or FM synthesis without sample crossfading. Assigning an LFO or macro to morph depth at slow rates (0.05–0.2 Hz) produces lush, organic movement in pads.

NOISE FLOOR / NOISE PARTIALS
Band-limited noise components mixed with the sinusoidal partials

Pure additive synthesis produces overly clean tones. Mixing narrow bands of noise — centered on each harmonic, with independent level and bandwidth controls — adds the breath, bow noise, and room ambience that acoustic instruments exhibit. In most hybrid additive synthesizers (Alchemy, Razor), noise partials are separate generators with their own frequency, bandwidth, and envelope, mixed into the main partial sum.

05 History & Origin

The theoretical foundation of additive synthesis is Jean-Baptiste Joseph Fourier's 1822 work Théorie analytique de la chaleur, which proved that any periodic function — including any sustained musical tone — can be represented as a sum of sinusoids. Hermann von Helmholtz validated this principle acoustically in his 1863 treatise On the Sensations of Tone, building mechanical resonators that selectively amplified individual harmonics of live instruments and demonstrating that the ratio of harmonic amplitudes determines timbre. These studies established the perceptual and mathematical framework that additive synthesis would later implement electronically.

The first electronic implementation came with the Hammond Organ, patented in 1934 by Laurens Hammond and John M. Hanert. While not a synthesizer in the modern sense, the Hammond's tonewheel system generated discrete sine-wave partials via electromagnetic induction from rotating steel wheels, and its drawbar interface allowed players to mix nine harmonically spaced partials in real time — a direct analog of additive synthesis under producer control. The RCA Mark II Sound Synthesizer, installed at the Columbia-Princeton Electronic Music Center in 1957 and used by composers including Milton Babbitt and Vladimir Ussachevsky, represented the first true programmatic additive synthesizer: it could address individual frequency bands and amplitudes via punched paper tape to generate composite tones unavailable from any acoustic instrument.

The digital era of additive synthesis opened in the 1970s at Bell Labs and MIT. James Moorer's 1977 paper on analysis-resynthesis using digital Fourier analysis demonstrated that acoustic instruments could be captured, decomposed into partials, and rebuilt from sine waves with startling fidelity. This research directly influenced the Fairlight CMI (1979) and, more decisively, the Synclavier II (1980), which offered additive timbre frames that could be sequenced to produce evolving spectra. The most commercially significant additive hardware arrived with the Technos Acxel (1987) and especially the Kawai K5 (1987) and its successor the K5000 (1996). The K5000 provided 128 partials per patch with independently controlled amplitude and pitch envelopes per partial across six additive oscillator stacks — an architecture that remains a benchmark for hardware additive synthesis and which produced the recognizable glass-bell pad textures heard throughout late-1990s electronic and ambient music.

Software brought additive synthesis to the producer mainstream in the 2000s. Native Instruments' Reaktor (first released 1996, mature by 2000) allowed users to build custom additive engines from component modules. Camel Audio's Alchemy (2007), later acquired by Apple and integrated into Logic Pro as the Alchemy synth in 2015, presented an additive engine with up to 1024 partials, a visual partial editor, and analysis-resynthesis import — making professional-grade additive sound design accessible inside a major DAW for the first time at no additional cost. NI's Razor (2011, designed by Errorsmith) took an opposite approach, treating the entire additive model as a sculptural surface and applying spectral effects — harmonic distortion, spectral blur, and frequency shifting — directly in the partial domain, spawning a generation of producers who used its distinctive formant-shifted textures in electronic and club music throughout the 2010s.

06 How Producers Use It

Pads and atmospheres are additive synthesis's most commercially prevalent application. The ability to assign slow, asynchronous envelopes to upper partials — letting harmonics 6 through 16 swell in over 3–8 seconds while the fundamental establishes immediately — produces the breathing, living quality that distinguishes great cinematic pads from static waveforms run through reverb. Producers working in ambient, film scoring, and modern pop frequently use morphing to crossfade between two spectral frames: one dense and bright, one sparse and dark, driven by an LFO or a velocity curve so the pad evolves differently on every hold. This cannot be replicated convincingly with subtractive synthesis alone.

Bell, mallet, and metallic timbres are the paradigmatic additive synthesis use case. A convincing music-box tone requires inharmonic partial tuning (partial 2 at roughly 2.76× the fundamental, partial 3 at approximately 5.40×) combined with rapid high-partial decay (50–120 ms for partials 4–8) and slow fundamental decay (2–4 seconds). Attempting this in FM synthesis requires careful operator ratio selection and level envelopes, while in additive synthesis it is a straightforward graphical exercise. Producers scoring to picture regularly build custom bell libraries this way, tuning each partial to a specific Hz value in fixed-frequency mode so the timbral identity of the bell remains constant regardless of pitch.

Vocal formant design exploits additive synthesis's ability to shape formant peaks — resonant frequency bands that the vocal tract amplifies. By boosting groups of 3–5 adjacent partials near 700 Hz (first formant), 1200 Hz (second formant), and 2500 Hz (third formant) and applying subtle amplitude modulation to those groups, producers can create convincing synthetic vowel sounds and choir textures. Alchemy's additive engine with formant shifting is particularly effective here; boosting partials 4–6 while frequency-shifting the group downward by 15–20% produces recognizable 'ooh' to 'aah' transitions over note duration.

Bass sounds benefit from additive synthesis when producers need sub-bass precision. By setting partial 1 at the fundamental (40–80 Hz for a bass note) with maximum amplitude and a long sustain, then controlling exactly how much second harmonic (80–160 Hz) is present, producers can tune the sub/mid balance of a bass tone with surgical accuracy — a task that is approximate at best through filter resonance in subtractive synthesis. Adding a narrow band of noise to partial 1 introduces the pick or pluck transient without requiring a separate layer, keeping the patch coherent and phase-consistent.

Sakamoto used the Roland MC-8 microcomposer to sequence the Moog Modular's sine-wave oscillators in stacked configurations approximating additive synthesis. The opening sequence features a bassline whose harmonic content changes independently of its pitch — a property only achievable by controlling individual oscillator amplitudes rather than shared filters. Listen at 0:12 for the moment the upper partial layer drops out abruptly, leaving only the sub-fundamental, demonstrating per-partial amplitude control in a compositional context.

Jean-Michel Jarre — "Oxygène Part IV" (1976)
0:00–1:30 · Produced by Jean-Michel Jarre

The lead melody tone on 'Oxygène Part IV' was generated using the Eminent 310 organ's tonewheels combined with the EMS VCS 3, creating an additive-style harmonic stack with prominent 2nd and 4th partial emphasis. The tone has a clarity and inter-harmonic precision impossible to achieve with the filter-based synthesis dominant in 1976. The slow amplitude envelope on the upper harmonics — audible as the tone 'blooms' over the first 200ms of each note — became one of the defining additive pad textures in electronic music history.

Aphex Twin — "Ageispolis" (1992)
0:00–2:00 · Produced by Richard D. James

The lead melodic texture on 'Ageispolis' exhibits the characteristic inharmonic partial spacing and per-partial decay differential of additive synthesis, consistent with the Kawai K1 and K4 instruments Richard D. James used during this period. High partials decay within 300–500ms while the fundamental sustains through the full note, producing the shimmering-yet-centered quality that distinguishes the record. At 1:14, the held chord reveals partial beating — a subtle amplitude modulation between adjacent partials — that no subtractive or sample-based instrument would produce with this character.

Daft Punk — "Voyager" (2001)
0:20–1:00 · Produced by Daft Punk

The lush, choir-like pad that anchors 'Voyager' throughout the Discovery album exhibits additive synthesis vocal formant modeling — groups of partials near 1.2 kHz and 2.5 kHz swell at different rates than the fundamental, creating an 'aah' vowel quality without recording a vocal. The Yamaha AN1x and Access Virus C used in the Discovery sessions both offer spectral partial control in their virtual analog engines sufficient to replicate this behavior. The pad enters fully at 0:47; listening on headphones reveals the independent motion of the upper harmonic cluster relative to the bass tone.

08 Types & Variants

Classic Harmonic Additive
Kawai K5000 · Hammond B-3 (tonewheel approximation)

Uses strictly integer-multiple partial ratios tuned to the harmonic series, producing tonal, pitch-stable sounds that transpose coherently across the keyboard. The defining architecture for organ emulation, choir pads, strings, and any pitched instrument where harmonic integrity is paramount. The Kawai K5000's 128-partial bank and per-partial envelope control define the ceiling of this category.

Inharmonic / Spectral Additive
Technos Acxel · Synclavier II

Allows arbitrary, non-integer partial frequency ratios, enabling the bell tones, gong textures, metallic impacts, and pitched noise beds that acoustic percussion instruments produce. The Synclavier II's additive timbre frames with freely settable partial frequencies set the standard; the Technos Acxel used a graphical tablet to draw spectral shapes in real time. Essential for sound design, film scoring, and any context where inharmonicity contributes to the instrument's character.

Analysis-Resynthesis Additive
Fairlight CMI Series IIx · New England Digital Synclavier II (resynthesis mode)

Combines Fourier analysis of a recorded sound — extracting per-partial amplitude tracks over time — with an additive engine that replays those tracks. The resulting resynthesized sound can be time-stretched, pitch-shifted, morphed with other analyzed sounds, and selectively edited at the partial level. This architecture underlies Alchemy's sample import feature, SPEAR, and many spectral plug-ins. It is the most acoustically accurate cloning tool available to producers outside of convolution sampling.

Hybrid Additive-Subtractive
NI Razor · Camel Audio Alchemy

Generates a full additive partial bank and then applies spectral processing — band-limited filtering, harmonic distortion, frequency shifting, or spectral blurring — directly in the frequency domain rather than post-summing in the time domain. This means filtering affects individual partials rather than the summed waveform, producing artifacts and timbral behaviors impossible in traditional subtractive synthesis. Razor's 'Spectral Filter' is the canonical example, producing dense, harmonically rich textures from a modest partial count.

Wavetable-Additive Hybrid
PPG Wave 2.3 · Waldorf Wave

Stores pre-computed additive spectra as wavetables and interpolates between them using a wavetable position parameter rather than computing partial sums in real time. This dramatically reduces CPU cost while preserving much of the timbral evolution capability of additive synthesis. The Waldorf Wave extended the PPG concept with up to 32-voice polyphony and manual wavetable editing, making it a studio staple for evolving pad textures in the 1990s. Modern wavetable synthesizers like Serum and Massive X incorporate additive-inspired spectral editing in their wavetable generation tools.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    Running too many partials without CPU budgeting
    128+ active partials per voice, multiplied across polyphony, can consume 30–60% of a single CPU core in some software implementations. The sound degradation is subtle — occasional dropout on transients — but the cause is hard to diagnose without CPU metering. Start at 32 partials, A/B the sound at 64 and 128, and only increase if you can perceive a meaningful difference. For most musical contexts, 48 partials captures more than 95% of audible timbre.
  • ✕
    Leaving all partials at the same amplitude
    The default partial bank in many additive synthesizers initializes all partials to equal amplitude, which produces a jagged, electronica-specific timbre far removed from any acoustic instrument. Equal-amplitude partials produce a spectrum whose brightness is tonally harsh and contextually narrow. Apply a 1/n amplitude rolloff as a starting point — set partial N to 1/N of the fundamental's amplitude — and adjust from there for your target timbre.
  • ✕
    Using a single shared envelope for all partials
    Sharing one ADSR across the entire partial bank negates the core advantage of additive synthesis. A single envelope produces timbral evolution that is perceptually equivalent to passing a simple waveform through a VCA, which any synthesizer can do. Per-partial envelopes — particularly independent decay times — are what make additive synthesis sound acoustically alive. Even rough grouping (partials 1–4 share one envelope, 5–10 another, 11+ a third) dramatically improves realism.
  • ✕
    Ignoring inharmonic partial ratios for 'realistic' acoustic sounds
    Real acoustic instruments — even 'harmonic' ones like pianos and guitars — have slightly inharmonic upper partials due to string stiffness (the Railsback curve in pianos). Setting all partials to perfect integer ratios produces a mathematically correct but perceptually clinical tone. Introducing ±1–3% ratio deviation on partials 6 and above creates the slight roughness that makes electronic tones read as acoustically credible. The Kawai K5000's 'detune per partial' function was designed specifically for this purpose.
  • ✕
    Phase-aligning all partials to 0° and wondering why the attack clicks
    When all partial cosine generators start at maximum amplitude simultaneously, the summed signal instantaneously jumps from zero to a large value, producing an audible click or thump at note onset — especially prominent at low fundamental frequencies (below 80 Hz). Randomizing per-partial start phase to values between 0° and 360° distributes the initial energy across the wave cycle and eliminates the click without requiring a volume fade-in that would otherwise soften a desired sharp attack.
  • ✕
    Forgetting to add noise partials for breathiness and texture
    Pure additive synthesis from sinusoids alone sounds unnervingly clean — an artifact of its mathematical purity that acoustic instruments never exhibit. Flutes have breath noise, bowed strings have rosin hiss, and even pianos have hammer mechanism transients. Adding noise partials at 5–20% of total mix level, centered on the lower harmonics and with their own short decay, provides the contact-layer transient that signals 'real instrument' to a listener. Neglecting this step is the most reliable way to make an additive patch sound synthesized rather than organic.

EnvelopeThe time-varying amplitude contour applied to each partial in additive synthesis; independent per-partial envelopes are the technique's defining feature. 12 Frequently Asked Questions

Subtractive synthesis starts with a harmonically rich waveform (sawtooth, square, noise) and uses filters to remove harmonic content, shaping the tone by subtraction. Additive synthesis starts with silence and builds a tone by adding individual sine waves at specified frequencies and amplitudes. Subtractive synthesis is faster and more intuitive for classic analog sounds; additive synthesis is more precise for acoustic modeling and unusual timbres but requires more programming effort. Most modern synthesizers combine both approaches.
In theory, Fourier's theorem guarantees that any periodic sound can be represented as a sum of sinusoids, so yes. In practice, real sounds include noise, transient irregularities, and very fast spectral changes that require a large number of partials and very fine envelope resolution to capture. Additive synthesis can produce highly convincing acoustic instrument approximations and exact resynthesis when combined with analysis tools, but a pure additive system with a fixed partial count will always be an approximation of sounds with dense, noisy, or chaotic components.
Logic Pro's Alchemy (free with Logic) is the most feature-complete DAW-bundled option, offering up to 1024 partials with per-partial editing and resynthesis import. Native Instruments' Razor (VST/AU/AAX) provides a spectral-domain approach with harmonic filters and effects applied before the partial sum. Image Line's Harmor (bundled with FL Studio Producer Edition) offers additive-resynthesis with image-to-spectrum import. Free options include SPEAR (standalone, cross-platform) for resynthesis work, and various Reaktor user libraries.
FM synthesis achieves complex spectra through frequency modulation between operator oscillators, which generates sidebands according to Bessel function mathematics — the resulting harmonic content is determined indirectly by operator ratios, indices, and envelope interactions and is difficult to predict intuitively. Additive synthesis controls each harmonic directly. At matching settings both can produce similar steady-state tones, but their dynamic behaviors differ: FM's sidebands scale non-linearly with modulation depth, producing rich but sometimes unpredictable spectral changes, while additive synthesis evolves exactly according to its per-partial envelopes.
It can be, depending on partial count and polyphony. A 64-partial, 16-voice patch requires computing and summing 1,024 enveloped sine waves per audio buffer, which is manageable on modern CPUs but non-trivial. Software like Alchemy and Razor are optimized with SIMD (single instruction, multiple data) CPU instructions to process multiple partials in parallel, making 64-voice polyphony with 128+ partials per voice practical on 2020s-era hardware. Reducing polyphony or partial count is the first remedy for CPU overload.
Analysis-resynthesis workflow: record a clean sustained note from the instrument, ideally at multiple velocities and multiple pitches. Import the audio into the additive synthesizer's analysis engine (in Alchemy, use the A source import; in Harmor, use the image/audio import). The engine runs a Short-Time Fourier Transform (STFT) to extract per-partial amplitude tracks over the note's duration and maps them to the synthesizer's partial bank. You can then edit, thin, or modify the resulting partial data. The quality of the resynthesis depends on the recording quality, the analysis window size, and how many partials the engine tracks.
A tubular bell approximation uses these inharmonic partial ratios relative to the fundamental: 1.00, 2.76, 5.40, 8.93, 13.34, 18.64, 20.65 — derived from physical modeling of hollow cylindrical shells. Assign each partial a rapid decay (80–200ms), with higher partials decaying faster. The fundamental and second partial carry most sustain. Add 5–8% noise mix across all partials for the metallic strike transient. For a softer music-box tone, use ratios closer to integer values (1, 2, 3, 4) with slightly more detuning on partials 3–5 (±4%).
When an additive engine analyzes audio into per-partial amplitude tracks, those tracks are indexed by time, not sample position. Stretching the sound means replaying the amplitude tracks at a slower rate — partial frequencies remain the same (preserving pitch) while the envelopes evolve more slowly. Because the sine-wave generators run continuously at their assigned frequencies, there are no windowing artifacts, granular dropouts, or phase smearing that afflict time-domain stretching algorithms. The result is phase-coherent time-stretching with theoretically unlimited stretch ratios, limited in practice by partial count and the accuracy of the initial analysis.
