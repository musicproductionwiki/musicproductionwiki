Sample Rate

/ˈsæm.pəl reɪt/

Quick Answer Definition How It Works Parameters Quick Reference History How To Use It In The Wild Types Mistakes Related Further Reading FAQ

Sample Rate is the number of audio snapshots captured per second in a digital recording, measured in Hz or kHz. Higher rates capture more high-frequency detail but increase file size and CPU load. 44,100 Hz (44.1 kHz) is the standard for music distribution.

01 Definition

Every number you've ever typed into a session's sample rate field is a bet — a bet about where your audio is going, what it will survive, and how faithful it will be to the sound that left the speaker cabinet. Get the bet right and the chain is invisible. Get it wrong and you're chasing a degradation you can hear but can't quite name.

Sample rate is the number of discrete amplitude measurements — called samples — that a digital audio system captures or reproduces per second. It is expressed in hertz (Hz) or kilohertz (kHz) and governs the highest frequency that can be faithfully represented in a digital signal. The most common values a producer encounters are 44,100 Hz (44.1 kHz), 48,000 Hz (48 kHz), 88,200 Hz (88.2 kHz), 96,000 Hz (96 kHz), and 192,000 Hz (192 kHz). Each doubles or halves the temporal resolution of its neighbor, and each carries measurable consequences for frequency response, file size, processing overhead, and final deliverable compatibility.

The theoretical ceiling on reproducible frequency at any given sample rate is exactly half that rate — a relationship formalized as the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem. At 44.1 kHz, the Nyquist frequency is 22,050 Hz, comfortably above the accepted upper limit of human hearing at 20,000 Hz. This is not an accident: the 44.1 kHz standard was deliberately chosen so that, after a small guard band is reserved for the anti-aliasing filter's roll-off, the full 20 Hz–20 kHz audio band remains intact. At 48 kHz, the Nyquist limit rises to 24,000 Hz, providing additional headroom before the filter must act. The practical audibility of this difference has been debated for decades; the professional consensus is that the guard band, not ultrasonic content itself, is the meaningful variable.

Sample rate is not the same as bit depth, though the two are frequently confused. Bit depth controls dynamic range — the ratio between the loudest and quietest representable signal, roughly 6 dB per bit. Sample rate controls bandwidth — the range of frequencies the system can represent. A 24-bit / 44.1 kHz file has excellent dynamic range and full-spectrum audio bandwidth for music, while a 16-bit / 96 kHz file has extended frequency ceiling but compressed dynamic range. Professional sessions typically combine 24-bit depth with a sample rate chosen for the delivery context: 44.1 kHz for music streaming and CD, 48 kHz for video and broadcast, and 96 kHz or higher for archival masters or heavily processed sessions where the extra headroom above Nyquist benefits certain plugin algorithms.

The decision is consequential because sample rate mismatches inside a production chain — between interface, DAW, plugin, and bounce destination — produce one of digital audio's most destructive artifacts: sample rate conversion (SRC) error. When a 44.1 kHz audio file is played at 48 kHz without proper conversion, every pitch shifts slightly flat; when conversion is performed carelessly, aliasing and imaging artifacts appear as high-frequency distortion or a subtle glassiness in transients. Understanding sample rate is therefore not an academic exercise. It determines the actual sonic fidelity of what leaves your studio and what arrives at a streaming platform, a film mix stage, or a vinyl cutting lathe.

02 How It Works

The physical world produces sound as continuous pressure variations in air — an analog waveform with theoretically infinite resolution. To store or process that waveform digitally, an analog-to-digital converter (ADC) performs two simultaneous operations: sampling and quantization. Sampling is the act of measuring the waveform's instantaneous amplitude at regular intervals; quantization is the act of rounding each measurement to the nearest value on a fixed numerical grid whose resolution is determined by bit depth. The rate at which sampling occurs is the sample rate. A 48 kHz system takes 48,000 of these amplitude snapshots every second, each one separated by approximately 20.8 microseconds.

The Nyquist-Shannon theorem states that a sampled system can accurately reconstruct any frequency up to — but not exceeding — half the sample rate, provided two conditions are met: the source signal contains no energy above that limit before sampling, and reconstruction uses an ideal interpolating filter on playback. In practice, ADCs apply an anti-aliasing low-pass filter before the sampling stage to remove frequencies that would otherwise fold back (alias) into the audible band as phantom tones. On the digital-to-analog conversion (DAC) side, an anti-imaging filter removes the spectral mirrors created by the reconstruction process. The quality, steepness, and phase behavior of these filters is one of the primary technical differentiators between converter designs and is itself influenced by how much guard band exists between the highest audio frequency and the Nyquist limit — hence the engineering motivation for operating at 96 kHz even when no human can hear 40 kHz.

When two digital systems operating at different sample rates must exchange audio — a common scenario in hybrid studios, video post-production, or plugin bridging — sample rate conversion (SRC) algorithms must interpolate new sample values. Linear-phase SRC introduces minimal coloration but adds latency. Minimum-phase SRC adds less latency but creates mild pre-ringing. Poor SRC introduces aliasing artifacts particularly noticeable as a harshness on cymbals, sibilance, and any sound with strong high-frequency transient content. The audible difference between a high-quality SRC (such as iZotope's 64-bit SRC engine or r8brain) and a cheap one is not subtle on high-resolution source material. For this reason, experienced engineers standardize sample rate at the session's outset and convert only once, at the end of the chain, using the best available algorithm.

Oversampling is a related concept that operates inside individual plugins and converters rather than at the session level. A plugin running at 4× oversampling internally processes audio at 4× the host sample rate — 192 kHz if the session is at 48 kHz — then downsamples back before returning audio to the DAW. This pushes potential aliasing artifacts generated by nonlinear processes (saturation, distortion, limiting) far above the audible band where they can be filtered cleanly. The sonic benefit is particularly audible in saturators, transient shapers, and limiters, where non-oversampled processing can introduce a gritty, high-frequency edge on sharp transients. FabFilter Pro-L 2, Soundtoys Decapitator, and Klanghelm SDRR all offer switchable oversampling for this reason.

At the session level, higher sample rates consume proportionally more CPU, RAM, and storage. A 96 kHz session generates roughly 2.2× the data of a 44.1 kHz session; a 192 kHz session generates approximately 4.4×. Plugin CPU load scales similarly, though the relationship is not perfectly linear and depends on each plugin's internal architecture. Buffer size, interface round-trip latency, and track count interact with sample rate to define the practical performance envelope of any given system. Choosing the highest sample rate is not always the most professional decision; choosing the rate appropriate to the delivery target, the hardware, and the processing chain is.

03 The Parameters

Every sample rate — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these and you can work with any implementation.

SAMPLE RATE VALUE
The number of samples captured per second — defines maximum reproducible frequency

Common values are 44,100 Hz, 48,000 Hz, 88,200 Hz, 96,000 Hz, and 192,000 Hz. The Nyquist limit — half the sample rate — sets the theoretical ceiling for representable frequency. At 44.1 kHz, the ceiling is 22,050 Hz; at 96 kHz, it is 48,000 Hz. Higher values provide more anti-aliasing filter guard band and can reduce phase distortion near 20 kHz in some converter designs.

NYQUIST FREQUENCY
Half the sample rate — the hard upper limit of representable frequency

Any audio content at or above the Nyquist frequency cannot be represented and will alias — fold back into the audible band as a phantom tone at a mirror frequency. For a 44.1 kHz session, content at 23 kHz aliases down to 21.1 kHz, potentially audible in some material. Anti-aliasing filters prevent source energy from reaching this limit before ADC conversion.

ANTI-ALIASING FILTER STEEPNESS
The roll-off slope of the filter that removes energy above Nyquist

A steep filter (brickwall) removes aliasing cleanly but can introduce phase rotation and pre-ringing in the upper audio band. A gentler filter (linear-phase, minimum-phase, or apodizing) reduces these artifacts but requires more guard band — one engineering argument for operating at 96 kHz even for audio destined for 44.1 kHz delivery. Different converter manufacturers (Apogee, Prism, Lavry, RME) weight this trade-off differently and are audibly distinguishable.

OVERSAMPLING FACTOR
Internal multiplication of sample rate within a plugin or converter

A plugin processing at 4× oversampling internally operates at 192 kHz in a 48 kHz session, then downsamples before returning audio to the DAW. This removes nonlinear distortion products (aliasing harmonics from saturation and limiting) that would otherwise fold into the audible band. Oversampling factors of 2×, 4×, 8×, and 16× are common; higher values increase CPU load proportionally and add latency.

SAMPLE RATE CONVERSION QUALITY
The fidelity of interpolation when converting between sample rates

SRC quality ranges from low (linear interpolation, introducing significant aliasing on transients) to high (polyphase sinc filtering, as used in iZotope RX, r8brain, and SoX). The audible difference is most apparent on high-frequency transients — cymbals, sibilance, and acoustic guitar plucks. Always perform SRC once, at the end of the chain, using the highest-quality algorithm available.

FILE SIZE SCALING
Storage and throughput cost per second of audio at a given sample rate

A stereo 24-bit file at 44.1 kHz consumes approximately 15.9 MB per minute. The same file at 96 kHz consumes approximately 34.6 MB per minute — a 2.17× increase. At 192 kHz, storage cost doubles again to roughly 69.1 MB per minute. Multi-track sessions at 192 kHz with high track counts can require sustained disk throughput exceeding 500 MB/s, demanding NVMe storage or RAID configurations.

05 History & Origin

The formal mathematical foundation for digital sampling was laid by Harry Nyquist in a 1928 Bell System Technical Journal paper, "Certain Topics in Telegraph Transmission Theory," and extended by Claude Shannon in his landmark 1949 work "Communication in the Presence of Noise." Together, their theorem established that a bandlimited signal could be perfectly reconstructed from discrete samples taken at a rate at least twice its highest frequency component. The theorem remained largely theoretical for two decades, as the electronics required to implement it at audio frequencies did not yet exist. It was the transistor, and subsequently the integrated circuit, that made practical ADC and DAC hardware conceivable in the 1960s.

The first digital audio recorders appeared in research laboratories in the late 1960s. NHK (Japan Broadcasting Corporation) and Nippon Columbia demonstrated experimental 13-bit, 30 kHz PCM recorders in 1967, and Denon produced what is widely recognized as the first commercial digital recording — a classical orchestral session — in 1971 using a 2-channel, 47.25 kHz PCM recorder. Decca engineers in the UK were simultaneously experimenting with digital capture for classical music. The competing early sample rates — 44,056 Hz, 47.25 kHz, and others — reflected each organization's independent engineering constraints, particularly the video tape machines used as storage media, whose frame rates and line counts created a narrow set of integer-compatible sample rates.

The standardization war resolved with the development of the Compact Disc. In 1979, Sony and Philips jointly proposed 44,100 Hz as the CD standard sample rate. The number was not arbitrary: it arose from storing PCM audio on videotape using NTSC (30 fps, 490 active lines, 3 samples per line: 30 × 490 × 3 = 44,100) and PAL (25 fps, 588 active lines, 3 samples per line: 25 × 588 × 3 = 44,100) formats — both yielding exactly 44,100. The Red Book standard, finalized in 1980, locked this rate in for consumer digital audio. The professional broadcast and video industries adopted 48,000 Hz as their standard through EBU and SMPTE specifications in the early 1980s, establishing the 44.1/48 kHz divide that persists today. Key engineers in this standardization included Thomas Stockham at the University of Utah, who produced the first high-quality digital recording of the Santa Fe Opera in 1976, and James Russell, whose early optical digital disc patents anticipated many CD innovations.

High-resolution sample rates entered professional production in the late 1990s alongside the DVD-Audio and Super Audio CD (SACD) format battles. Sony's DSD (Direct Stream Digital) format, used in SACD, operates at 2.8224 MHz using 1-bit encoding — a fundamentally different architecture from PCM, though converters and mastering tools can translate between the two. DVD-Audio supported PCM at up to 192 kHz / 24-bit. Neither format achieved mainstream consumer adoption, but the engineering investment drove dramatic improvements in high-rate converter design that benefited professional studio hardware. By the mid-2000s, interfaces from Apogee (Rosetta 200), Prism Sound (ADA-8), and Lavry Engineering (Gold series) could operate at 96 and 192 kHz with genuinely audible improvements over earlier designs, not primarily because of the rate itself but because the relaxed filter requirements allowed more elegant analog circuit design around the converter chips.

06 How Producers Use It

For music production destined for streaming platforms — Spotify, Apple Music, Tidal, YouTube Music — 44.1 kHz remains the correct starting point in the vast majority of sessions. Spotify and Apple Music encode at 44.1 kHz regardless of the sample rate of the uploaded master; delivering at 48 kHz forces their ingest systems to perform SRC, adding a conversion step of unknown quality. Apple Music Lossless and Tidal HiFi do accept 48 kHz and higher, but their listeners' DACs typically perform internal SRC to match device output rates. The practical advice: record and mix at 44.1 kHz for music-only projects, deliver at 44.1 kHz, and keep a 24-bit archival master at the session's native rate.

For drum recording specifically, sample rate interacts with transient integrity. The attack of a snare or kick drum contains energy up to approximately 18 kHz — comfortably below the Nyquist limit at 44.1 kHz — but the anti-aliasing filter's phase response at those frequencies can, in some converter designs, add subtle smearing to transient edges. Recording drums at 88.2 kHz (chosen over 96 kHz because SRC back to 44.1 kHz is a clean integer divide-by-2) can tighten the apparent attack of close-miked snares and overheads, particularly in acoustically bright rooms. This is a refinement most listeners will not detect, but it is audible on direct A/B comparison through accurate monitors and is worth implementing on sessions where drum sound is paramount.

Producers working in film, television, and game audio must operate at 48 kHz without exception. The SMPTE standard for broadcast and all major DAW-to-picture workflows assume 48 kHz. Delivering music stems or score elements at 44.1 kHz to a post-production facility creates an immediate SRC problem at the dub stage; even high-quality SRC introduces uncertainty, and re-pitching errors caused by mismatched rates on locked picture are a genuine professional liability. Set the session to 48 kHz before the first take and never deviate. Logic Pro's default is 44.1 kHz and must be changed manually for any video project — a mistake that catches producers who open a new session without checking.

When mixing at high sample rates (96 kHz or above), many saturation and analog-modeling plugins benefit measurably from the extended headroom. A plugin like Soundtoys Decapitator or Waves J37 Tape generating harmonic distortion produces intermodulation products that at 44.1 kHz can alias into the audible band as harshness. At 96 kHz, those same products appear at frequencies above 25 kHz where they are inaudible and filtered cleanly on output. The alternative — engaging the plugin's internal oversampling while working at 44.1 kHz — achieves a comparable result with less CPU overhead and avoids the storage and performance cost of a full high-rate session. Both approaches are valid; the session-level approach is preferable when recording live instruments simultaneously, because converter performance at 96 kHz can be audibly superior to converter performance at 44.1 kHz on high-end interfaces.

AbletonSet sample rate in Preferences → Audio before creating a session — it cannot be changed per-project after audio is recorded. Use Convert to 44100 Hz in the File menu when importing 48 kHz video audio. Ableton's built-in SRC is high quality but always prefer matching rates at source.
FL StudioSample rate lives in Options → Audio Settings and applies globally. FL's mixer processes at 32-bit float internally regardless of project rate; the rate setting governs the ASIO output and rendered files. When bouncing stems for video, change to 48 kHz before rendering — FL will not SRC existing audio clips automatically.
Logic ProDefaults to 44.1 kHz for new projects — change in File → Project Settings → Audio for any video or broadcast session. Logic's SRC engine (used when importing mismatched audio) is high quality and uses a linear-phase algorithm. For 88.2 kHz drum sessions, Logic handles the 2× SRC to 44.1 kHz cleanly at bounce time.
Pro ToolsSample rate is set at session creation and cannot be changed without creating a new session and importing. The Avid HDX DSP platform natively supports 44.1, 48, 88.2, 96, 176.4, and 192 kHz. Track count halves at double-rate sessions on HDX cards — a real-world constraint in large orchestral sessions at 96 kHz.
ReaperSet project sample rate in Project Settings → Media. Reaper's SRC quality is configurable under Preferences → Media — set resampling mode to HQ (512 point sinc) for all finished deliverables. Reaper will correctly resample imported mismatched clips in real time, making it unusually forgiving of mixed-rate source material in editorial workflows.

07 In the Wild

Abstract knowledge becomes practical when you can hear it in music you know. These tracks demonstrate sample rate used intentionally, at specific moments, for specific purposes.

Steely Dan — "Aja" (1977)
0:00–1:20, drum intro · Produced by Gary Katz, engineered by Roger Nichols

Recorded entirely in analog, Aja is the benchmark against which digital sample rate decisions are still measured. Roger Nichols later conducted extensive digital capture tests using early Sony PCM-F1 units, documenting that 44.1 kHz PCM captured the drum transients on this album accurately but that anti-aliasing filter ringing from early converters was audible on the stick attack of Steve Gadd's snare at 1:08. Nichols became one of the first engineers to publicly articulate the need for better filter design at the 20 kHz boundary — a conversation that directly influenced 96 kHz converter development.

Daft Punk — "Get Lucky" (2013)
Full mix · Produced by Daft Punk and Nile Rodgers, engineered by Mick Guzauski

Mixed and mastered at 48 kHz to align with its primary video and streaming delivery contexts. The track demonstrates how a 48 kHz master sounds indistinguishable from 44.1 kHz on consumer playback while avoiding the SRC step on video platforms. Guzauski's mix preserves high-frequency sheen on Nile Rodgers' Stratocaster by ensuring no in-chain SRC occurred before the master limiter — the guitar's 15–18 kHz pick transients arrive at the bus unaltered.

Radiohead — "How to Disappear Completely" (2000)
2:30–3:45, string swell · Produced by Nigel Godrich

Nigel Godrich has spoken in interviews about recording Amnesiac and Kid A sessions at 48 kHz using Pro Tools HD — an unusual choice for music-only production at the time that reflected his concurrent work on film scores. The string recordings on this track, captured with Neumann U87s and early Neve 8078 preamps feeding Pro Tools HD, exhibit the wide-band, low-artifact character that results from a well-implemented 48 kHz chain. The high-frequency shimmer of the string harmonics at 3:10 is a reference point for evaluating converter performance on bowed instruments.

Kendrick Lamar — "HUMBLE." (2017)
0:00–0:08, intro piano hit · Produced by Mike Will Made It

The sampled Clyde Stubblefield-style snare and the detuned piano hit in the opening two seconds are derived from samples that were pitch-shifted and time-stretched inside a session believed to operate at 44.1 kHz. Any sample rate mismatch during that manipulation would appear as aliasing artifacts on the attack transient; the clinical cleanliness of the hit on the master confirms a well-matched sample rate chain throughout the production. The track is a useful reference for evaluating whether your SRC chain adds harshness to sharp, mid-range transients.

08 Types & Variants

44,100 Hz (44.1 kHz)
Tascam DA-30 · Sony CDP-101 · Universal Audio Apollo Twin

The CD standard and the de facto rate for music streaming delivery. Its Nyquist limit of 22,050 Hz comfortably exceeds human hearing with a ~2,050 Hz guard band. Anti-aliasing filters must be reasonably steep, which is the source of phase concerns in the top octave. Best choice for any project whose primary deliverable is music on streaming platforms or CD.

48,000 Hz (48 kHz)
Neve 8078 (with AD card) · Avid Pro Tools HD · RME Fireface UFX III

The broadcast and video standard per EBU R68 and SMPTE, with a Nyquist limit of 24,000 Hz and an additional ~4,000 Hz of filter guard band versus 44.1 kHz. Required for all film, television, podcast, and game audio deliverables. When music is written for sync licensing, working at 48 kHz eliminates the SRC step at the post-production dub stage and is the professional expectation.

88,200 Hz (88.2 kHz)
Prism Sound ADA-8XR · Lynx Aurora (n)

Exactly double 44.1 kHz, allowing perfect integer-ratio SRC to the CD standard. Preferred over 96 kHz when the final deliverable is 44.1 kHz, because the halving operation requires no interpolation — only sample dropping — producing the cleanest possible downconversion. Particularly valuable for drum recording sessions where transient integrity is prioritized and the master will be streamed or pressed to vinyl.

96,000 Hz (96 kHz)
Apogee Symphony I/O · Prism Sound Titan · Antelope Audio Orion Studio

The most widely used high-resolution rate in professional music production and archival mastering. The Nyquist limit of 48,000 Hz allows anti-aliasing and reconstruction filters to operate with a 28,000 Hz guard band, enabling gentler filter slopes and reduced phase distortion in the audible band. Many high-end converters achieve their best measured performance — lowest THD+N, flattest phase response — at 96 kHz. Requires SRC to 44.1 kHz or 48 kHz for consumer delivery, ideally using a high-quality offline algorithm.

192,000 Hz (192 kHz)
Lavry Gold AD122-96 MkIII · Merging Technologies Hapi · Benchmark ADC1

Used primarily for archival mastering, academic research, and specialized high-resolution release formats. CPU and storage costs are extreme; track count on most professional interfaces halves again compared to 96 kHz operation. Audible benefits over 96 kHz are debated and have not been consistently demonstrated in controlled listening tests, but 192 kHz provides maximum future-proofing for archival materials and enables cleaner SRC to any lower rate.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    Mismatching session and interface sample rate
    If the DAW session is set to 44.1 kHz but the audio interface is running at 48 kHz — a common scenario when an interface is shared between applications — the DAW either refuses to play, plays silently, or in worst-case driver implementations plays at the wrong pitch. All audio recorded in this state is either corrupted or pitched ~8 cents flat or sharp. Always confirm that the interface clock display and the DAW session settings show identical values before pressing record.
  • ✕
    Delivering music masters at 48 kHz to streaming platforms
    Spotify, Apple Music, and most streaming ingest systems expect 44.1 kHz audio. Uploading a 48 kHz master forces their encoder to perform SRC, adding a quality-reducing conversion step of unknown implementation quality. The effect is subtle but measurable as high-frequency harshness on cymbals and sibilance. Bounce your final music master at 44.1 kHz unless the platform explicitly accepts and preserves higher rates (as Apple Music Lossless does).
  • ✕
    Delivering video and broadcast audio at 44.1 kHz
    The inverse mistake to the above. Film, television, and YouTube content must be delivered at 48 kHz per SMPTE and EBU specifications. A 44.1 kHz audio file embedded in a video container will either be flagged as non-compliant by broadcast QC systems or be silently resampled by the video encoder, potentially introducing pitch or timing artifacts when the audio re-syncs to the picture frame rate.
  • ✕
    Performing sample rate conversion mid-chain multiple times
    Every SRC operation, even a high-quality one, introduces a small amount of error. Chains that convert from 44.1 kHz to 48 kHz to 44.1 kHz — perhaps because a collaborator's session was at a different rate, or a video export forced a conversion — accumulate these errors. The result is progressive softening of high-frequency transients and a subtle glassiness. Standardize rate at the session outset and perform a single, high-quality SRC only at the final delivery stage.
  • ✕
    Assuming higher sample rate always sounds better
    A 192 kHz session processed through mediocre plugins can sound worse than a carefully managed 44.1 kHz session through excellent plugins with 4× oversampling enabled. The relationship between sample rate and perceived quality is non-linear and depends on converter quality, plugin architecture, and SRC quality at delivery. Committing to 96 kHz on an underpowered system that must run a small buffer size will produce more audible CPU artifacts — dropouts, clicks — than a stable 44.1 kHz session with headroom to spare.
  • ✕
    Importing samples without checking their native sample rate
    Sample libraries, royalty-free loops, and field recordings are commonly distributed at 44.1 kHz, 48 kHz, or occasionally 96 kHz. Importing a 48 kHz sample into a 44.1 kHz session without proper SRC causes the sample to play approximately 8% faster and 7.4 cents sharp — an audible pitch and timing error. DAWs handle this with varying degrees of transparency; Logic Pro and Reaper perform automatic SRC on import, while some older versions of Pro Tools will play the clip at the wrong rate with no warning.

In practice, modern high-quality SRC algorithms handle the 96-to-44.1 conversion with inaudible error, so the choice is less critical than it was in the early 2000s. If your converters perform significantly better at 96 kHz (check manufacturer spec sheets for THD+N by sample rate), favor 96 kHz and use a quality SRC at delivery.
Oversampling is a technique where a plugin internally processes audio at a multiple of the session sample rate — 2×, 4×, 8×, or 16× — then downsamples back before returning audio to the DAW. Its purpose is to push the Nyquist limit well above the audible band so that harmonic distortion products generated by nonlinear processing (saturation, soft clipping, limiting) fold back into inaudible frequencies rather than audible ones. Enable oversampling on saturators, tape emulators, harmonic exciters, and aggressive limiters. Be aware that oversampling adds processing latency and CPU load proportional to the multiplication factor; in live performance contexts or on mix buses with many heavy plugins, 2× or 4× is a reasonable balance. Most modern plugins — FabFilter Pro-L 2, Soundtoys Decapitator, Klanghelm SDRR — implement oversampling cleanly and transparently.
