ADSR

/ˌeɪ.diː.ɛs.ˈɑːr/

Quick Answer Definition How It Works Parameters Quick Reference History How To Use It In The Wild Types Mistakes Related Further Reading FAQ

ADSR is a four-stage envelope generator — Attack, Decay, Sustain, Release — that controls how a synthesized or sampled sound evolves in amplitude, filter cutoff, or pitch over the duration of a note.

01 Definition

Every sound you have ever loved — the slow-blooming pad, the snappy snare, the bass note that punches then settles — was shaped by four numbers. Learn those four numbers deeply enough and you stop chasing presets and start sculpting intention.

ADSR stands for Attack, Decay, Sustain, and Release — the four segments of a envelope generator, a circuit or algorithm that produces a time-varying control signal used to modulate other parameters inside a synthesizer or sampler. The term describes both the shape of that control signal and the hardware or software module that generates it. In virtually every synthesizer architecture built after 1965, at least one ADSR envelope is hard-wired to the VCA (Voltage-Controlled Amplifier), meaning it directly governs the overall loudness contour of each note. Secondary envelopes commonly modulate the VCF (filter cutoff), pitch, oscillator pulse width, or effect depth.

The envelope metaphor is apt: just as a physical envelope contains and shapes its contents, an ADSR envelope contains and shapes the energy of a sound across time. When a MIDI note-on message is received — or a key is pressed on an analogue keyboard — the envelope generator begins its cycle. Attack defines how quickly the amplitude (or whichever parameter is being modulated) rises from zero to its peak value. Decay defines how quickly it then falls to the Sustain level. Sustain is not a time parameter but a level — the amplitude at which the signal holds as long as the key remains depressed. Release is the time the signal takes to fall from the sustain level back to zero after the key is lifted (note-off).

The distinction between Sustain as a level and Attack/Decay/Release as times trips up many producers. If you set a very short attack, a moderate decay, and sustain at 100%, the decay phase is effectively inaudible — the signal rises to peak and stays there. Conversely, if sustain is at 0%, the sound will die away completely during the decay phase regardless of how long the key is held, producing a plucked or percussive character. This interplay between the four parameters is where expressive sound design lives.

Envelopes modulate far more than amplitude. Routing an ADSR to a low-pass filter's cutoff frequency is one of the most fundamental synthesis techniques in existence — it produces the characteristic "wah" or "vowel" movement heard in classic analogue leads and basses. The envelope amount (often labelled Env Amt, Env Depth, or simply a bipolar knob on the filter section) scales how dramatically the envelope shifts the cutoff. At high positive amounts with a fast attack and medium decay, you get the snappy filter sweep characteristic of a TB-303 acid line. At slow attack with high sustain, the filter gradually opens as long as the key is held, producing the feel of a sound slowly waking up.

Modern DAW-based instruments and hardware synths expand the classic four-stage model in numerous ways — ADBDSR (adding a second decay/break point), DAHDSR (adding a Delay and Hold stage), multi-segment "free" envelopes with arbitrary breakpoints, and looping envelopes that behave like LFOs at fast rates. Despite these extensions, the ADSR model remains the lingua franca of synthesis: every major synth platform, every synthesizer tutorial, and every professional sound designer defaults to ADSR as the common vocabulary for describing envelope shapes.

02 How It Works

At the circuit level, a classic analogue ADSR envelope generator produces a control voltage (CV) that follows a specific trajectory when triggered. In early implementations such as those found on the Minimoog and ARP 2600, the Attack, Decay, and Release stages were implemented with RC (resistor-capacitor) circuits — the time constants determined by variable resistors (the knobs) charging or discharging a capacitor. Because capacitors charge and discharge exponentially rather than linearly, analogue ADSR curves are inherently curved rather than perfectly straight. This curvature is perceptually important: a linear attack sounds slower and less punchy than an exponential one at the same time setting, while an exponential decay sounds more natural because it mirrors the physical acoustics of nearly every real instrument.

In digital synthesizers and DAW-based instruments, the envelope is computed as a sequence of line segments or curves between breakpoints. The designer chooses the shape — linear, exponential, logarithmic, or custom — and this choice has a profound timbral impact. Soft synths like Serum and Vital allow per-stage curve adjustment via a draggable tension handle, letting the producer dial in exactly the character they want. A convex (fast-initial, slow-finishing) attack mimics the analogue capacitor charge and gives a sharp transient; a concave attack gives a more mechanical, electronic feel. Most professional soft synths default to a slightly curved (quasi-exponential) shape to emulate the warmth of vintage hardware.

The relationship between the envelope and the note duration is also critical. In a held-note scenario, the envelope spends its attack and decay phases, then parks at the sustain level until note-off, at which point release begins. In a short staccato note — where note-off arrives before the attack has even finished — the envelope is interrupted and immediately begins its release from whatever level it has reached. This is why very short MIDI note lengths on a slow-attack pad can produce nearly silent notes: the release fires before the attack completes. Lengthening the MIDI note or reducing the attack time resolves the issue. Some synthesizers implement a retriggering or legato mode that changes this behaviour for overlapping notes.

Velocity sensitivity adds a crucial dynamic dimension to ADSR behaviour. On most synthesizers, incoming MIDI velocity scales the envelope's peak level and optionally its attack time — harder velocity produces a louder peak and/or a faster attack, replicating the behaviour of an acoustic instrument. On a piano, a hard strike produces a loud, fast-building tone; a soft touch produces a quiet, slower-feeling onset. Producers working with realistic instruments should always check the velocity-to-envelope routing in their sampler or synth, as a flat velocity response is one of the primary reasons programmed parts sound robotic and unconvincing.

The envelope generator's output is typically summed or multiplied with other modulation sources before reaching its destination. In a modular or semi-modular context, the envelope CV can be patched to any parameter — oscillator pitch, wavefolder depth, reverb mix — turning it into a powerful general-purpose shaping tool far beyond simple amplitude control. This modularity is central to the power of the ADSR concept: it is not merely a volume knob over time, but a universal control signal that can impose any time-varying shape onto any aspect of a sound.

03 The Parameters

Every adsr — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these and you can work with any implementation.

ATTACK
Time from note-on to peak amplitude

Attack is measured in milliseconds or seconds and defines how rapidly the envelope rises from zero to its maximum value after a key is pressed or a note-on message is received. Short attacks (0–10 ms) produce punchy, percussive transients; long attacks (500 ms–several seconds) create slow swells. A 0 ms attack means the signal is at full amplitude almost instantaneously, which can produce audible clicks if not handled carefully by the synth's anti-click circuitry.

DECAY
Time from peak to the sustain level

Decay is the time the envelope takes to fall from its peak value down to the sustain level after the attack phase completes. Typical musical ranges are 10 ms for tight percussion to 2 seconds for a lush pad. The interaction with sustain is critical: a long decay into a high sustain is almost inaudible, while a long decay into a sustain of 0% creates a slow fade-out resembling a natural instrument's ring-down.

SUSTAIN
Amplitude level held while key is depressed

Unlike the other three parameters, Sustain is a level (0–100% of peak, or 0 dB to negative infinity) rather than a time. The envelope holds at this value indefinitely as long as the note is active. Setting Sustain to 0 produces an entirely transient sound — the envelope always decays to silence regardless of held duration. At 100%, the decay stage is bypassed in effect, and the sound holds at its peak for as long as the key is held.

RELEASE
Time from note-off to silence

Release defines how long the envelope takes to fall from the current sustain level to zero after the key is lifted. Short releases (5–30 ms) produce abrupt, staccato endings; long releases (1–10 seconds) create the characteristic reverb-like tail of a concert piano or a lush string section. If a note-off arrives during the attack or decay phase, release begins from the current envelope level — not from the sustain level — which affects the perceived fade length.

ENVELOPE AMOUNT
Depth of modulation applied to the destination

Often labelled Env Amt, Env Depth, or shown as a bipolar knob on a filter section, this parameter scales how much the envelope shifts the target parameter. At maximum positive depth on a filter envelope, the cutoff sweeps from its base value up to its maximum; at negative depth, the cutoff starts open and closes as the envelope progresses. Envelope amount is not strictly part of the ADSR definition itself, but in practice it is always the fifth knob producers reach for when sculpting an envelope-modulated filter or pitch.

CURVE / SHAPE
Exponential versus linear trajectory of each stage

Many modern synths expose a curve parameter per stage, typically ranging from fully concave (logarithmic) through linear to fully convex (exponential). Analogue circuits are naturally exponential due to RC charging behaviour, and this shape is widely considered more musical because it matches the acoustic behaviour of most real instruments. Linear envelopes can sound mechanical and are sometimes preferred for electronic, industrial, or percussive contexts where a harder attack character is desired.

05 History & Origin

The conceptual roots of the ADSR envelope trace to the mid-1960s, when electronic instrument designers began formalising the observation that almost every acoustic sound follows a predictable amplitude trajectory: it rises, partially falls, holds while energy is sustained, then decays to silence. Robert Moog and his collaborators at Moog Music were instrumental in codifying this into a four-stage model. The Moog 900-series modular systems of 1965–1967 included a dedicated Envelope Generator module (the 911) offering Attack, Decay, Sustain, and Release controls. Critically, this was implemented as a voltage-control signal, meaning it could be patched to any CV-accepting input — amplitude, filter cutoff, or oscillator pitch — establishing the paradigm of the ADSR as a universal modulation source.

ARP Instruments refined and popularised the ADSR architecture with the ARP 2600 (1971) and ARP Odyssey (1972). The Odyssey in particular brought ADSR envelopes to a wider audience with its semi-modular design and competitive price point relative to the Moog. Simultaneously, Don Buchla was developing alternative envelope concepts on the West Coast — his Buchla 200 series featured more complex, function-generator-style envelopes with independent looping and cycling capabilities — but the ADSR model, with its intuitive mapping to real instrument behaviour, won the battle for mainstream adoption. By the mid-1970s, every major polyphonic synthesizer from Oberheim, Sequential Circuits, and Roland used ADSR generators as standard architecture.

The introduction of digital synthesizers in the late 1970s and early 1980s expanded and complicated the envelope model. Yamaha's DX7 (1983), designed by John Chowning and refined by Yamaha engineers including Ichiro Kakehashi's influence on broader synthesis thinking, used an eight-stage envelope with independent rate and level parameters for each stage — far more complex than a simple ADSR but serving the demands of FM synthesis. Roland's D-50 (1987) combined analogue-style ADSR envelopes with digital PCM samples in its hybrid architecture, and Korg's M1 (1988) used ADSR envelopes on its wave sequences, bringing the model firmly into the sample-playback era. Throughout this period, the four-parameter ADSR remained the reference vocabulary even when implementations exceeded it.

In the software synthesis era, Native Instruments Massive (2006) and Xfer Records Serum (2014) both refined the ADSR model with per-stage curve handles and modulation-matrix routing, bringing fine-grained envelope control to a new generation of producers. Serum's visual envelope editor — showing the curve in real time as audio plays — became an industry benchmark for envelope visualisation. The widespread adoption of Eurorack modular systems from the 2010s onward renewed interest in analogue envelope circuits, with manufacturers like Mutable Instruments, Make Noise, and Intellijel producing envelope modules that honour the classic ADSR topology while extending it with looping, cycling, and voltage-controlled time parameters. The ADSR envelope thus has an unbroken lineage from Robert Moog's 1965 circuit designs to the most advanced soft synths and modular systems of today.

06 How Producers Use It

Drums and percussion. For kick drums, a near-zero attack (0.1–1 ms) and very short decay (30–80 ms) with sustain at zero creates the tight, punchy character essential to trap, house, and drum and bass. Extending the decay to 150–250 ms on a sub-heavy kick allows the low-frequency energy to bloom naturally. For snares, a fast attack preserves the crack of the transient while decay and sustain shape how long the body rings. Many producers automate or modulate the decay length of hi-hats to create dynamism — shorter decays on closed hats, gradually increasing decay on open hats — all dialled on the sampler's amplitude envelope.

Bass and keys. On a sub bass or reese bass, the amplitude envelope largely mirrors what compression would do but with finer control: a fast attack (1–5 ms) and medium decay (100–200 ms) with moderate sustain produces a punchy bass note with a defined transient and a stable body. Longer attack times (20–50 ms) on bass synths create a "swelling" quality that softens the fundamental and lets the mix breathe. For electric piano sounds, a medium attack (5–15 ms), moderate decay, and relatively low sustain replicates the physical behaviour of a tine dying away after it is struck. A secondary filter envelope with moderate positive depth adds the characteristic "plonk" vowel movement of a Rhodes or Wurlitzer.

Leads and pads. Lead synths typically use very fast attacks to cut through dense arrangements, while pad sounds almost universally employ slow attacks (500 ms–3 s) to create their signature bloom. The release time on a pad is equally important: short releases sound abrupt and unnatural, while long releases (2–8 seconds) allow notes to overlap and blur into a wash. When layering pad sounds, staggering the attack times across layers (e.g., 800 ms on one layer, 2 s on another) creates a complex, evolving swell that feels organic rather than synthesized. Filter envelope with slow attack and moderate depth adds harmonic evolution to an otherwise static timbre.

Samples and samplers. In samplers like Native Instruments Kontakt, the Built-In Instrument and Ableton Sampler, the amplitude ADSR works on the playback of an audio file rather than a synthesized waveform. Short attack with moderate release can clean up loop points, while a long attack can convert a sharp one-shot sample into a pad-like texture. Release is particularly important for piano and orchestral samples: too short a release causes unnatural cutoffs; too long causes muddy build-up in fast passages. Many sample library designers bake envelope behaviour into the instrument itself via scripting, but understanding the underlying ADSR allows producers to override these defaults meaningfully.

AbletonIn Ableton's Analog instrument, each oscillator and the global amp section has its own ADSR. Use the Amp envelope's Attack and Release for overall shape, then assign the second envelope to Filter Freq with positive Env amount for a filter sweep. In Sampler, the Amp envelope's Release is critical — set too short, samples click on note-off; use 10–20 ms minimum even for percussive material.
FL StudioFL's 3xOsc and Harmor both expose per-voice ADSR envelopes in the instrument envelope section (ENV/LFO panel). In Harmor, the envelope can be drawn as a free curve using the envelope editor tab — a major upgrade over a standard four-knob layout. For samples in the FPC or DirectWave, the amplitude ADSR controls are in the voice section; set Release to at least 30 ms to avoid click artifacts when notes release quickly.
Logic ProES2 and Retro Synth both offer ADSR envelopes for amplitude and filter. In ES2, Envelope 3 is hardwired to the VCA while Envelopes 1 and 2 are free-routable via the router matrix — a powerful setup for complex modulation. The Alchemy synth extends this with multi-segment AHDSR envelopes per-source, with per-stage curve control. For realistic playing, always enable Velocity to Env Attack routing in ES2's Vector section.
Pro ToolsPro Tools' bundled Xpand!2 features a straightforward ADSR per-part, accessible from the ENV tab. Eleven Rack and AIR instrument plugins like Loom II expose ADSR controls with deeper modulation routing. For serious synthesis work within Pro Tools, most engineers use third-party VSTs — Serum or Massive X — since the native synth offerings are limited compared to other DAWs. Ensure Plugin Delay Compensation is active when using high-polyphony instruments with long release tails.
ReaperReaper ships with ReaSynth, a simple oscillator with ADSR controls useful for prototyping and sound design basics. The real power in Reaper comes from VST3 hosting with no instrument track overhead: load Serum, Vital, or Surge XT directly on an instrument track and automate ADSR parameters via the Param control surfaces or MIDI CC mapping. Reaper's parameter modulation system can also link envelope time values to a BPM-synced source, effectively automating ADSR evolution over a song section.

07 In the Wild

Abstract knowledge becomes practical when you can hear it in music you know. These tracks demonstrate adsr used intentionally, at specific moments, for specific purposes.

Daft Punk — "Get Lucky" (2013)
0:00–0:45 · Produced by Daft Punk & Nile Rodgers

The iconic funk guitar sits atop a synth bass whose amplitude envelope is a masterclass in subtlety: a very fast attack (sub-2 ms), a moderate decay settling to a high sustain, and a release just long enough to avoid click but short enough to feel tight and rhythmic. The synthesizer strings heard from the intro use the classic slow-attack pad treatment — roughly 1.5 seconds of attack — so they swell in beneath the guitar without competing with its transient. Listen specifically at the four-bar intro for how the contrast between the guitar's transient attack and the pad's slow bloom creates a dynamic push-pull that keeps the low-energy section engaging.

Aphex Twin — "Xtal" (1992)
0:00–1:30 · Produced by Richard D. James

The Roland Juno-106 pad layers in the opening minute demonstrate extreme release times — estimated 4–6 seconds — which allows successive notes to overlap into a shimmering, reverberant wash without any external reverb being needed for the sense of space. James also employs a Juno filter envelope with a medium attack and moderate depth, causing each held chord to slowly brighten in timbre over its duration. This dual envelope treatment (slow amp attack + slow filter attack) is the defining sonic fingerprint of early ambient techno pad design and remains a reference point for sound designers today.

Dr. Dre ft. Snoop Dogg — "Nuthin' But a G Thang" (1992)
0:00–0:08 · Produced by Dr. Dre

The opening synth bass — a classic G-funk lead played on what is widely attributed to a Roland Juno or similar polysynth — uses a near-instantaneous attack with a short decay and medium sustain that gives the bass its staccato, punchy quality even though the notes are relatively long. The filter envelope is set with a fast attack and moderate decay at high depth, producing the characteristic "nyah" vowel movement that became the signature of West Coast G-funk production. Isolate the low end in a spectrum analyser to see how the filter envelope causes the fundamental to pulse with each note, adding rhythmic interest beyond just the amplitude shape.

Flume — "Never Be Like You" (2016)
0:55–1:20 · Produced by Flume

The metallic, pitched percussion hits throughout the chorus demonstrate creative use of a short attack and medium release envelope on a sample with significant high-frequency content — likely processed through a gate with fast attack and slow release to extend the tail while keeping the front transient sharp. The synth elements show deliberate decay shaping: medium decay (roughly 300 ms) into a 0% sustain produces the bouncy, plucked quality that Flume favours in his lead melodic lines. This sustain-at-zero technique is an often-underused trick for giving synths a more organic, physically-modelled feel.

08 Types & Variants

Analogue RC Envelope
Moog 911 · ARP 2600 · Roland SH-101

Implemented via resistor-capacitor circuits, analogue envelopes produce inherently exponential curves due to the physics of capacitor charging. The Attack rises with a convex (fast-then-slow) curve; Decay and Release fall with a concave (fast-initial-drop) shape. This asymmetry mirrors acoustic instrument behaviour closely and is considered by many engineers to sound warmer and more natural than linear digital equivalents.

Linear Digital Envelope
Yamaha DX7 · Roland D-50 · Korg M1

Early digital synthesizers computed envelope stages as straight lines between breakpoints, primarily for computational simplicity. Linear envelopes can sound mechanical and overly precise but suit digital timbres and FM synthesis well. The DX7 extended the concept to an eight-stage Rate/Level envelope, giving far more programmable complexity at the cost of intuitive operation — a trade-off that defined the programming challenge of that instrument.

Curved Digital Envelope (Adjustable)
Xfer Serum · Native Instruments Massive X · Moog Subsequent 37

Modern synthesizers expose a per-stage curve control, typically a draggable handle or a numeric tension value. This allows producers to select the exact curvature — from fully linear to deeply exponential — for each individual stage of the ADSR. In Serum, right-clicking any envelope node reveals a contextual menu for curve presets; this single feature makes it possible to replicate any classic analogue envelope character as well as create entirely novel shapes impossible in hardware.

DAHDSR (Extended Envelope)
Mutable Instruments Stages · Make Noise Maths · Arturia MatrixBrute

DAHDSR adds a Delay stage (time before attack begins after note-on) and a Hold stage (time the envelope remains at peak before decay begins). Delay is critical for creating staggered layer entries in unison synth stacks; Hold is invaluable for emulating bowed instruments and brass, where the note blooms to full amplitude and holds there briefly before the resonance begins to decay. These stages are common in modular systems and are increasingly appearing in software instruments.

Looping / Cycling Envelope
Buchla 200 Looping Function Generator · Intellijel Quadrax · NI Massive (LFO-envelope hybrid)

A looping envelope automatically retriggeres itself at the end of the Release stage, cycling continuously and behaving like an LFO with a complex shape. At slow cycle rates this produces tremolo or filter-wobble effects; at audio rates it generates timbral complexity akin to amplitude modulation. This concept, pioneered by Don Buchla in the late 1960s, represents a fundamental departure from the keyboard-triggered ADSR model and is central to generative and modular synthesis techniques.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    Setting attack too slow on leads and bass, losing the transient
    A lead or bass with 50+ ms of attack will sound like it is being cross-faded in rather than played — the transient that defines the note's identity and rhythmic placement is removed entirely. This makes the part feel sluggish and difficult to lock to a groove. Fix it by reducing attack to under 10 ms for most melodic content, then use a filter envelope with a slower attack to add movement without sacrificing the amplitude transient.
  • ✕
    Ignoring release time and causing click artifacts or muddy tails
    Setting release to zero causes an abrupt amplitude step to silence on note-off, which creates an audible click — especially on bass frequencies. At the other extreme, a release left at its default long value on every instrument causes notes to blur into each other, muddying the arrangement. Treat every synth and sampler's release time as a deliberate compositional choice, not an afterthought. A minimum 10–15 ms release eliminates clicks; tune beyond that for musical effect.
  • ✕
    Confusing Sustain as a time parameter rather than a level
    This is the most common beginner misunderstanding: Sustain does not define how long the sound sustains — it defines the level at which it sustains. If sustain is at 0%, the sound will always decay to silence during the decay stage, even if the key is held for minutes. If you want a long-held note to remain at full volume, set sustain to 100%. Confusing this leads to patches that seem broken — notes that always fade even with seemingly generous envelope times.
  • ✕
    Using identical ADSR settings across all layers of a unison patch
    When stacking multiple oscillator voices or layered synths, identical envelope settings cause all layers to move in perfect synchrony, which can sound thin or phasey despite the layering. Introducing subtle differences — a few milliseconds of attack offset, a slightly different release per layer — creates the slight incoherence that makes unison sounds feel large and three-dimensional, similar to how real orchestral sections feel fuller than a single player even at the same dynamic level.
  • ✕
    Over-driving the filter envelope amount without adjusting base cutoff
    Cranking filter envelope amount to maximum while leaving the base cutoff at a neutral mid-point results in an envelope that sweeps through most of the filter's range — including frequency areas that create harshness or mud. The correct approach is to set the base cutoff where you want the sound during sustain, then set envelope amount to determine how far above or below that point the attack transient sweeps. This gives conscious control over both the body of the sound and its transient colour.
  • ✕
    Not accounting for ADSR interaction with MIDI note length in a DAW
    Short MIDI notes — common when quantising or programming percussion-style sequences — can fire note-off messages before the attack stage finishes, causing the release to trigger from a low amplitude and producing nearly inaudible notes. This is especially problematic with slow-attack pad patches in piano-roll sequences with tight note lengths. The fix is either to lengthen the MIDI notes, reduce the attack time, or enable the synth's legato or sustain-pedal mode to prevent premature release triggering.

12 Frequently Asked Questions

ADSR stands for Attack, Decay, Sustain, and Release — the four stages of an envelope generator. Attack is the time to rise from zero to peak; Decay is the time to fall from peak to the sustain level; Sustain is the level held while a note is active; and Release is the time to fall to silence after the note ends. Together these four parameters define the complete amplitude (or modulation) contour of a synthesized note.
If your Attack time is longer than the duration of your MIDI note, the note-off message fires before the Attack phase completes, immediately triggering Release from a very low amplitude — resulting in an almost inaudible note. Fix this by either reducing the Attack time so it completes within the note length, or extending the MIDI note in the piano roll so the attack has time to reach its peak before note-off.
Sustain is a level — specifically, the amplitude level at which the envelope holds as long as the note is active. It is the only parameter in ADSR that does not represent a time duration. If Sustain is set to 0%, the sound will always fade to silence during the Decay phase, even if the key is held indefinitely. If Sustain is at 100%, the sound holds at its peak amplitude for as long as the note lasts.
The amplitude envelope is typically hardwired to the VCA and controls the overall loudness contour of the note. The filter envelope (often a second, freely routable ADSR) is assigned to the filter's cutoff frequency, controlling how the harmonic brightness evolves over time. They usually have separate knobs and can have entirely different settings — a fast attack on the amplitude envelope but a slow attack on the filter envelope, for example, gives a sound that jumps to full volume instantly while its timbre brightens gradually.
Set the amplitude envelope with a fast attack (0–5 ms), short decay (50–200 ms), and Sustain at 0%. This causes the sound to reach peak amplitude immediately and then fade to silence during the decay phase, regardless of how long the key is held — exactly like a plucked string whose energy dissipates quickly after the initial excitation. Adjust decay length to control how quickly the 'pluck' fades: shorter for a tight pizzicato, longer for a full guitar-style ring.
Yes — ADSR envelopes are general-purpose modulation sources that can be routed to virtually any synth parameter. Common secondary destinations include filter cutoff frequency (producing the characteristic vowel sweep), oscillator pitch (for subtle or dramatic pitch transients), effect depth, oscillator pulse width, and wavefolder amount. In modular synthesizers, envelope CV outputs can be patched to any parameter accepting a control voltage input, making the ADSR one of the most versatile tools in a modular rig.
A linear envelope moves at a constant rate between its start and end values — equal amplitude change per unit time. An exponential envelope moves quickly at first and then slows down (for attack) or drops rapidly initially then slows (for decay/release). Analogue circuits produce exponential curves naturally, and most engineers consider them more musical because they mirror the acoustic behaviour of real instruments. Linear envelopes can sound mechanical and are often used deliberately for electronic or robotic timbres.
On most synthesizers and samplers, MIDI velocity scales the envelope's peak level and optionally its attack time. At full velocity, the envelope reaches its maximum peak amplitude with the programmed attack time; at low velocity, the peak is scaled downward and (with velocity-to-attack routing active) the attack may slow slightly, mimicking the softer onset of a gently struck acoustic instrument. Professional sound designers layer multiple velocity responses — not just amplitude but also filter cutoff, envelope depth, and even sample layer switching — to create instruments that respond expressively across the full dynamic range of a performance.
