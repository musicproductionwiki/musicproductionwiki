LFO

/ˌɛl.ɛf.oʊ/

Quick Answer Definition How It Works Parameters Quick Reference History How To Use It In The Wild Types Mistakes Calculator Related Further Reading FAQ

LFO is a low-frequency oscillator that generates a repeating signal — typically below 20 Hz — used to modulate synthesizer parameters such as pitch, filter cutoff, and amplitude over time, creating effects like vibrato, tremolo, and auto-wah.

01 Definition

Every wobble, shimmer, and pulse that makes a synth feel alive instead of static — that motion is almost certainly an LFO doing quiet, relentless work underneath the surface.

An LFO — low-frequency oscillator — is an oscillator whose output frequency sits below the threshold of human hearing, conventionally between 0.001 Hz and roughly 20 Hz, though many modern implementations allow rates well into audio range. Unlike a VCO (voltage-controlled oscillator) or DCO (digitally controlled oscillator), which generate pitched audio signals, the LFO's sole job is modulation: its output voltage or digital value is routed to a destination parameter — filter cutoff, oscillator pitch, amplifier level, pan position, wavetable position, or almost anything else — and continuously pushes that parameter up and down in a repeating pattern. The audible result is movement, and movement is what separates a lifeless static tone from something that breathes.

The LFO is one of the three core modulation sources in classical subtractive synthesis, alongside the envelope and velocity. Where envelopes produce one-shot contour shapes that respond to note events, the LFO is free-running or tempo-synced and keeps cycling for as long as a note is held — or, in many architectures, indefinitely regardless of note state. This distinction is fundamental: envelopes sculpt the attack, sustain, and release of a sound over its lifetime, while an LFO imposes an ongoing periodic variation that can be slower than a whole note or faster than a sixteenth-note tremolo depending on the tempo and rate setting.

The practical vocabulary producers use for LFO effects is almost entirely borrowed from acoustic music and vintage recording. Vibrato — periodic pitch variation — is a guitarist's or vocalist's most expressive tool, and an LFO routed to oscillator pitch with a sine wave at around 5–6 Hz replicates it precisely. Tremolo — periodic amplitude variation — is an LFO routed to the VCA or amplitude parameter. Auto-wah and filter sweeps are LFOs routed to filter cutoff, while flanger and chorus effects at their core rely on an LFO modulating a short delay time. Understanding that these familiar sonic landmarks are all LFO modulation in disguise is the conceptual unlock that makes the whole domain snap into focus.

Modern DAW-based LFOs and soft-synth implementations have dramatically expanded what was possible in hardware. Many now offer more than a dozen waveform shapes including user-drawable custom shapes, sample-and-hold stepped random output, probability controls, per-cycle variation, and polyphonic modes where each voice in a chord receives an independently phased or independently randomised LFO — a feature sometimes called LFO spread or voice detune. Some LFOs can themselves be modulated by other LFOs (called FM modulation of the LFO or LFO-to-LFO routing), creating non-repeating, organic-feeling motion that animates a pad or lead for minutes without obvious cycling. The LFO has evolved from a single wiggling sine wave in a Minimoog to a fully programmable modulation engine in instruments like Serum, Vital, and Massive X.

02 How It Works

At its most fundamental level an LFO is an oscillator — a circuit or algorithm that generates a periodic waveform — running at a frequency the ear cannot register as pitch. In analog hardware such as the Oberheim OB-Xa or Roland Juno-106, the LFO is a dedicated op-amp relaxation oscillator or triangle-core circuit whose output voltage is summed into the control voltage path of the target module: a positive voltage swing raises the destination parameter, a negative swing lowers it, and the amplitude of that swing (depth) determines how wide the modulation window is. The rate knob adjusts the oscillator's frequency, and the waveform selector routes different output shapes — smooth sine, sawtooth ramp, square pulse, triangle, sample-and-hold random — to the destination.

In the digital domain, LFOs are computed as wavetable lookups or formula-based oscillators running in the synth engine's control-rate thread, which typically processes at a lower sample rate than the audio thread (commonly 1/32 or 1/64 of the audio sample rate, so at 44.1 kHz audio you might see control-rate updates every 1–2 ms). This is fast enough for sub-20 Hz modulation with no perceptible stepping, but once you push a software LFO into audio-rate territory the step size becomes audible unless the synth interpolates the LFO output between control-rate ticks. High-quality instruments like Serum compute LFOs at full audio rate when the rate is set above roughly 200 Hz, seamlessly bridging LFO and FM synthesis territory. The destination receives the LFO value as an additive or multiplicative offset — additive for pitch (semitones added to base pitch) and often multiplicative for amplitude (scaling from 0 to 1).

Tempo sync is the feature that turned the LFO from a studio curiosity into a central production tool. When sync is engaged, the LFO rate is expressed as a musical division — whole note, half, quarter, eighth, eighth-triplet, sixteenth, thirty-second — and the synth locks its LFO cycle to the DAW's clock. A tremolo at 1/8 note creates a 120 BPM pulse every 250 ms; a filter sweep at 4 bars creates a slow 8-second open-close cycle at the same tempo. Phase offset, which sets where in the cycle the LFO begins when a note is triggered, is a related parameter that becomes critical for ensemble playing: two synths with LFOs at the same rate but 180° phase offset will produce complementary motion rather than unison wobble, widening the stereo image.

The modulation matrix is the routing architecture that connects LFO outputs to destinations. In a fixed-architecture synth like a Juno-106 the routing is hard-wired: the single LFO can go to pitch, filter, or VCA via dedicated panel switches. In matrix-architecture instruments like the Sequential Prophet-5 Rev 4 or any serious soft-synth, any modulation source can be connected to almost any modulatable parameter with an adjustable amount and optionally an inverted polarity. Producers should think of the modulation matrix as the patch bay of a software instrument: the LFO is one of several sources (others include envelopes, velocity, mod wheel, aftertouch, macros) and the destination parameter is whatever you want to animate. Combining multiple LFOs with different rates and waveforms on different destinations — say, LFO 1 on filter cutoff at 1/4 note and LFO 2 on pan at 1/3 note — produces polyrhythmic motion that never exactly repeats on a human listening timescale.

The depth parameter is arguably as important as the rate. Depth controls the amplitude of the LFO signal before it reaches the destination, determining how far the parameter travels on each cycle. Set too high, modulation becomes obviously mechanical; set too subtly, it may be felt rather than heard, adding warmth and life without drawing attention to itself. The best practice is to automate or macro-map depth so it can be increased gradually during a performance or mixdown, letting the modulation grow as an arrangement develops rather than sitting at a fixed intensity for an entire track.

03 The Parameters

Every lfo — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these and you can work with any implementation.

RATE
Speed of the LFO cycle

Rate sets how fast the LFO completes one full cycle, expressed either in Hz (0.01–20+ Hz in free mode) or as a musical subdivision when tempo-synced (1/32 through 4 bars and beyond). At 0.1 Hz the LFO takes 10 seconds per cycle — ideal for slow pad swells — while 6 Hz produces classic vibrato speed. Tempo-synced rates at 1/16 or 1/8 note lock modulation tightly to the groove and are essential for modern electronic production.

DEPTH
Intensity of the modulation amount

Depth (sometimes labeled Amount or Intensity) scales the LFO's output signal before it reaches the destination, controlling how far the target parameter deviates from its resting value. At 0% there is no modulation; at 100% the parameter swings through its full mapped range. For filter cutoff modulation, a depth of 20–40% often yields musical results without sounding mechanical, while vibrato typically requires only 5–15 semitones of pitch depth for realism.

WAVEFORM
Shape of the repeating modulation curve

The waveform determines the character of the modulation motion. A sine wave produces smooth, curved oscillation ideal for vibrato and tremolo. A triangle wave is similar but with linear rather than curved transitions. A square wave snaps between two discrete values — used for hard octave jumps or rhythmic gating. A descending sawtooth ramps smoothly down and resets abruptly, creating a characteristic 'downer' sweep. Sample-and-hold (S&H) outputs a new random stepped value at each cycle, generating unpredictable arpeggiated-style modulation popular in analog techno and industrial music.

SYNC
Locks LFO rate to DAW tempo

When sync is enabled, the LFO rate is quantised to musical divisions of the project BPM. A 1/4 note LFO at 120 BPM completes one cycle every 500 ms, and this relationship holds at any tempo because the LFO tracks tempo changes in real time. Sync modes may include straight, dotted (1.5×), and triplet (2/3×) variants of each subdivision. Unsynced (free) mode allows rates that drift through bar lines, creating phasing effects reminiscent of Steve Reich minimalism.

PHASE
Start position within the LFO cycle

Phase (0–360°) sets where in its waveform the LFO begins when a note is triggered or when the LFO resets. At 0° a sine wave starts at its midpoint and rises; at 90° it starts at its positive peak and falls. Phase is critical for ensemble patches — offsetting LFO phase between voices by 30–120° creates a chorus-like spreading effect without adding CPU-heavy chorus processing. A phase of 180° inverts the direction relative to another LFO at 0°, producing complementary stereo motion.

RETRIGGER / RESET
Whether the LFO resets on each new note

In retrigger mode the LFO phase resets to the set phase value on every new MIDI note-on event, making the modulation perfectly consistent regardless of when a note is played. In free-running mode the LFO ignores note events and continues cycling continuously, which can create useful variability between repeated notes but makes live performance less predictable. A third mode, one-shot, runs the LFO a single cycle then stops — functionally equivalent to an envelope shape, useful for filter plucks or attack transient modulation.

05 History & Origin

The conceptual lineage of the LFO begins with the tremolo and vibrato circuits found in electric organs and early guitar amplifiers of the 1940s and 1950s. Fender's 1955 Tremolux amplifier used a tube-based oscillator running at approximately 1–8 Hz to modulate its output stage, producing the amplitude pulsing that defined surf, country, and early rock and roll rhythm guitar. The Hammond organ's vibrato chorus scanner — a physical rotating drum that varied the pitch of tonewheel outputs — served an analogous function. Neither was called an LFO; the term emerged from the modular synthesis world of the 1960s.

Robert Moog's collaboration with composer Herbert Deutsch in 1964 and the subsequent commercial Moog modular system (1965) established the LFO as a discrete, patchable module. In the Moog system, a low-frequency oscillator was a standard VCO running below audio rate, patched via a 1/4-inch cable to the CV input of a filter or another VCO. Don Buchla's San Francisco-developed Series 100 system (1963) similarly offered slow oscillator sources, though Buchla's aesthetic — favouring random voltage sources and touch-plate controllers — meant that periodic LFO waveforms were less central to his philosophy than to Moog's. The Minimoog of 1970 integrated a dedicated LFO (Moog called it the Modulation Oscillator) with a three-position waveform selector and a single speed knob, making vibrato and tremolo available to keyboardists without patch cables. It was this integration that introduced the LFO to working musicians.

Through the 1970s every major polysynth design incorporated at least one dedicated LFO. The ARP 2600 (1971) exposed its LFO as a patchable module; the Oberheim Four Voice (1975) gave each voice independent LFOs; the Sequential Circuits Prophet-5 (1978) offered a single shared LFO with five waveforms routable to pitch, filter, and pulse width, synced across all five voices. The Roland Juno-106 (1984) implemented the LFO in the DCO-106 custom chip, making it one of the first fully digitally controlled LFOs in a mass-market instrument. The Juno's LFO — a single triangle/square/saw/random source routable to DCO pitch, DCO pulse width, and VCF cutoff — defined the chorus-pad aesthetic of 1980s pop and became the archetypal LFO sound for an entire generation of producers.

Digital synthesis from the late 1980s onward expanded LFO capabilities dramatically. Yamaha's DX7 (1983) had implemented six operators capable of running at very low rates as de facto LFOs in FM patches, and the successor SY77 (1989) added a dedicated LFO section with delay and fade-in controls. The Korg M1 (1988) and Wavestation (1990) offered multi-LFO routing. The software synthesis era — beginning in earnest with Native Instruments' Generator (later Reaktor) in 1996 and Propellerhead's ReBirth in 1997 — removed hardware constraints entirely. By 2006, instruments like Absynth 4 and Massive offered up to four LFOs each with custom-drawn waveforms, audio-rate capability, and LFO-to-LFO modulation. Xfer Records' Serum (2014), designed by Steve Duda, set a contemporary benchmark with its drag-to-draw LFO editor, per-voice random phase, and BPM-synced rate display that rendered cycle length in bars and milliseconds simultaneously — features that have since been adopted by competitors across the market.

06 How Producers Use It

On synthesizer leads and basses, the most common LFO application is filter cutoff modulation for wobble bass — a technique that defines dubstep, drum and bass, and many forms of EDM. A square or compound waveform LFO synced to a 1/8 or 1/16 note division at moderate depth (40–70%) on the filter cutoff of a heavily detuned or heavily driven oscillator produces the characteristic 'wub' texture. Varying the LFO depth and rate via automation or mod wheel during a drop creates the crescendo and breakdown dynamics that define the genre. For subtler lead work — a hypnotic analog lead in house or techno — a slow sine LFO at free-running 0.3–0.8 Hz on filter cutoff with 20–30% depth adds warmth and drift without obvious movement.

On pads and atmospheric textures, LFO modulation is applied across multiple destinations simultaneously to create complex, evolving motion. A common recipe for a living pad uses LFO 1 on filter cutoff at a slow 0.1 Hz sine wave, LFO 2 on oscillator pitch (very small depth, ±2–5 cents) at a slightly different rate to create beating between voices, and LFO 3 on pan position at 0.2 Hz to create slow stereo movement. None of these modulations may be individually audible as periodic oscillation — the combined effect is simply that the pad sounds like it breathes and shifts in space rather than sitting statically. This is the difference between a lifeless preset and a production-ready sound.

For rhythmic effects on drums, samplers, and melodic percussion, a square-wave LFO synced to musical subdivisions can act as a rhythmic gate or auto-slicer. Routing a 1/8-note square LFO to the amplitude of a synth chord stab creates a rhythmic chop effect without manual volume automation. S&H (sample-and-hold) LFOs on filter cutoff of a drum bus — typically at 1/8 or 1/16 note resolution — produce a stepped resonant filter effect that can drive percussion forward in a mix with unpredictable, organic variation. This technique is audible in much Detroit techno and minimal techno, where filter movement on the drum bus is as compositionally significant as the kick pattern itself.

Producers increasingly use DAW-internal MIDI LFOs and automation LFOs — not attached to any particular synth — to modulate effect parameters such as reverb send level, delay feedback, or distortion drive. Ableton's LFO device in Max for Live, for instance, can be routed to any automatable parameter in the session, including the parameters of other effect devices. This separates the LFO from synthesis entirely and turns it into a general-purpose DAW modulation utility. A slow sine LFO on a reverb room size, for example, creates a subtle pitching of the reverb tail that simulates an acoustic space with non-uniform reflections — a technique used by producers like Jon Hopkins and Four Tet to give electronic music an organic, physical quality.

07 In the Wild

Abstract knowledge becomes practical when you can hear it in music you know. These tracks demonstrate lfo used intentionally, at specific moments, for specific purposes.

Skrillex — "Scary Monsters and Nice Sprites" (2010)
0:42 drop · Produced by Skrillex (Sonny Moore)

The signature 'monster' wobble bass that defined commercial dubstep is a heavily distorted synth bass — widely believed to be FM8 or Massive — with a compound-waveform LFO synced to 1/8 note on a resonant low-pass filter. As the drop hits, the LFO depth automation increases sharply, widening the filter sweep range. The effect is compounded by a second LFO on pitch modulation adding a slight detune that gives the wobble its vocal-like formant quality. Listen from 0:42 through 1:10 to hear the LFO rate shift between 1/8 and 1/16 note divisions for dynamic variation.

Daft Punk — "Get Lucky" (2013)
Throughout · Produced by Daft Punk & Pharrell Williams

The lead synthesizer melody (played on what sounds like a vintage Roland SH-101 or Minimoog-style patch) features a consistent gentle vibrato — a slow sine LFO at approximately 5 Hz routed to oscillator pitch with shallow depth (around ±15 cents). This is a textbook vibrato application: the LFO is set to retrigger mode so each new note begins at the center pitch and the vibrato rises smoothly in, mimicking the natural vibrato onset of a skilled guitarist or vocalist. Compare the held notes against the short staccato hits where the vibrato is barely audible.

Burial — "Archangel" (2007)
0:00–5:15 throughout · Produced by Burial (William Bevan)

Burial's signature ethereal vocal textures are timestretched and pitch-shifted samples with slow, irregular LFO modulation on pitch and formant that creates the characteristic 'underwater' wavering. The LFO here appears to be a very slow, slightly randomized sine running at around 0.05–0.1 Hz with small depth — the pitch variation is subtle enough that many listeners do not consciously register it as LFO modulation, perceiving it instead as organic vocal breath. The absence of tempo-sync gives the motion a floating, non-metronomic quality consistent with Burial's deliberately ambiguous rhythm aesthetic.

Flying Lotus — "Zodiac Shit" (2010)
0:00–1:45 · Produced by Flying Lotus (Steven Ellison)

The evolving, shape-shifting pad textures throughout this track demonstrate multi-LFO modulation on a single sound — filter cutoff, oscillator spread, and reverb send all moving at mutually prime LFO rates (approximately 0.13 Hz, 0.29 Hz, and 0.07 Hz) creating a texture that never settles into a pattern. This is a classic approach in psychedelic electronic music: use three or more LFOs whose periods share no simple ratio so the combined modulation pattern's theoretical repeat time exceeds the duration of the piece.

08 Types & Variants

Sine / Triangle LFO
Moog Minimoog Model D · Roland Juno-106

The smoothest, most musical LFO shape, the sine moves through its cycle with continuous acceleration and deceleration rather than linear motion. It is the correct choice for vibrato, tremolo, and subtle filter warmth where the modulation should feel organic and where abrupt transitions would be out of place. The triangle variant is similar but with linear slopes rather than curves, producing a slightly more mechanical feel.

Square / Pulse LFO
Korg MS-20 · Sequential Prophet-5

The square LFO snaps instantly between two values — maximum positive and maximum negative — with zero transition time. Used on amplitude it creates a hard on/off gate; on pitch it produces trill and octave-jump effects; on filter cutoff it gives rhythmic, stuttering movement. The duty cycle (pulse width) may be adjustable, allowing asymmetric timing — for example a 25% duty cycle pulse spends one quarter of its time at the high value and three quarters at the low.

Sawtooth / Ramp LFO
ARP Odyssey · Oberheim OB-Xa

The descending sawtooth ramps steadily downward then resets abruptly to the top — or, in ascending ramp mode, climbs steadily upward and resets to the bottom. On filter cutoff it creates a characteristic sweep-and-reset that drives techno, industrial, and certain EDM textures. The reset transient can be softened by slew limiting. Ascending ramp on pitch creates a rising arpeggio-like effect used in experimental and noise music.

Sample-and-Hold (S&H) LFO
Buchla 265 Source of Uncertainty · Korg MS-20

S&H outputs a new random stepped voltage value on each clock pulse, creating unpredictable staircase-style modulation that was central to classic sci-fi movie sound design and Wendy Carlos's Switched-On Bach era synthesis. Applied to filter cutoff at moderate rates it produces a funky, unpredictable sweep. At fast rates approaching audio frequency it generates a sample-and-hold noise texture. Most modern soft-synths offer both stepped random (true S&H) and smoothed random (Lorenz or random walk) variants.

Custom / Drawn LFO
Serum (Xfer Records, software) · Vital (Matt Tytel, software)

Digital instruments since the mid-2000s have allowed producers to draw arbitrary LFO waveforms in a graphical editor, interpolating between user-placed control points. This unlocks asymmetric, non-periodic, rhythmically complex shapes — for example a waveform that rises slowly, pauses, drops abruptly, and holds low for two-thirds of its cycle. The Serum LFO editor introduced this paradigm to the mainstream and it has become a signature capability of modern wavetable synthesis workflows.

Audio-Rate LFO
Moog DFAM · Buchla 259e Complex Waveform Generator

When an LFO's rate is increased into the audible range (above approximately 20 Hz) it crosses into FM synthesis territory — the LFO becomes a modulator oscillator and its destination (a pitched oscillator) becomes the carrier. This is the operational principle behind FM synthesis as developed by John Chowning at Stanford in 1967. Many modern soft-synths allow seamless sweep from sub-Hz LFO rates to 20 kHz audio-rate modulation, unifying LFO modulation and FM synthesis within a single architecture.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    LFO rate unsynced when the track needs rhythmic lock
    Free-running LFOs drift relative to bar lines and will phase against the groove of a tempo-synced track. On a 128 BPM house track, a 2.1 Hz free LFO completes a cycle every 476 ms — close to 1/4 note at 500 ms but drifting further out of phase every bar. Enable tempo sync and use musical subdivisions (1/8, 1/4, 1/2) whenever rhythmic precision matters. Reserve free mode for deliberate phasing effects or atmospheric textures where loose timing is the aesthetic goal.
  • ✕
    Excessive depth creating uncontrolled pitch deviation
    Setting LFO depth too high on oscillator pitch modulation causes obvious, unmusical pitch wavering that sounds broken rather than expressive. Vibrato typically requires no more than ±25 cents (a quarter semitone) to sound authentic; beyond that it begins to sound like malfunction. Even in experimental contexts, large pitch LFO depth should be intentional and controlled. When in doubt, start depth at 5% and increase in small increments while monitoring the effect in context with the full mix.
  • ✕
    Same LFO rate and phase on all voices in a poly patch
    On a polyphonic synthesizer in free-running mode, if all voices share a single LFO at identical phase, every note of a chord undergoes the exact same modulation simultaneously — the result is a synchronised, mechanical pumping that sounds nothing like organic unison. Activate per-voice random phase or LFO spread (typically labelled Voice Spread, Poly LFO, or Uni Detune) so each voice enters the LFO cycle at a different starting point. The result is a rich, chorusing motion far closer to the sound of multiple acoustic instruments playing together.
  • ✕
    Ignoring the LFO's retrigger mode for consistent performance
    When retrigger is off, the LFO position depends on where it was in its cycle when the note arrived — meaning the same note played twice may have opposite filter movement depending on timing. For lead lines and bass where the producer expects consistent timbre on every note hit, retrigger mode is essential. Reserve free-running mode for sustained pads or textures where variability across notes is desirable.
  • ✕
    Assigning LFO to too many destinations at full depth
    Stacking multiple full-depth LFOs on a single sound — pitch, filter, amplitude, and pan all moving at maximum — creates chaotic, uncontrolled modulation where no single element is clearly audible. The sound becomes a mess rather than an animated texture. Effective multi-LFO patches use different depths for each destination, with the primary modulation at the highest depth and secondary modulations at fractions of that amount. Think of it as a mix: each modulation destination gets its own level to balance the overall motion.
  • ✕
    Using a filter LFO without considering resonance interaction
    A low-pass filter LFO at high depth with resonance above 50% will ring loudly as the cutoff sweeps through the resonant frequency peak — an effect that can easily clip the output or introduce harsh artifacts. If the wobble sounds unexpectedly harsh or distorted, reduce resonance first before lowering LFO depth. Conversely, using controlled resonance-LFO interaction is the core technique for classic analog bass sounds: the resonant peak 'tracks' through the sweep, emphasising different harmonics on each pass.

12 Frequently Asked Questions

LFO stands for Low-Frequency Oscillator. It generates a repeating signal below the range of human hearing — typically between 0.001 Hz and 20 Hz — and uses that signal to drive cyclical changes in a synthesizer parameter such as filter cutoff, pitch, or volume. The result is movement: vibrato when routed to pitch, tremolo when routed to amplitude, and auto-wah or wobble when routed to a filter. The LFO itself makes no sound; it is a control signal that animates other parts of a synth.
An envelope (ADSR) is a one-shot modulation shape that runs once from attack through release each time a note is triggered, then stops. An LFO is a continuously repeating modulation source that keeps cycling independently of note events. In practice: if you want the filter to open once as a note begins and then close as it releases, use an envelope. If you want the filter to keep sweeping up and down rhythmically for the duration a note is held, use an LFO.
On most software synthesizers, find a button or toggle labelled 'Sync', 'Tempo Sync', or 'BPM' in the LFO section. When enabled, the rate display changes from Hz to musical subdivisions (1/4, 1/8, 1/16, etc.). Select the subdivision you want — 1/8 note is a common starting point for rhythmic wobble, 1/4 or 1/2 for slower sweeps. In hardware synths, MIDI clock sync is required; ensure your DAW is transmitting MIDI clock and that the synthesizer's clock input is enabled. Some hardware synths require a specific menu setting to switch from internal to external clock.
Sample-and-hold (S&H) is an LFO waveform mode that outputs a new random stepped value each time the LFO completes a cycle, rather than moving smoothly between values. The name refers to the analog circuit that samples an input voltage and holds it until the next clock pulse. Applied to filter cutoff it produces an unpredictable, staircase-like sweep; applied to pitch it can generate random arpeggios. S&H was a defining element of early analog electronic music and classic sci-fi sound design.
Rate in Hz describes the literal speed of the LFO in cycles per second, regardless of musical context — 2 Hz means two complete cycles per second at any tempo. Rate in note divisions (1/4, 1/8, etc.) describes the cycle duration relative to the current tempo, so a 1/4 note LFO at 120 BPM completes one cycle every 500 ms, but at 90 BPM it takes 667 ms. Use Hz for atmospheric, non-rhythmic modulation (vibrato, pad motion). Use note divisions whenever the modulation needs to lock to the groove of the track.
Yes — when an LFO is swept above approximately 20 Hz it enters audio-frequency range and its modulation effect on pitch becomes FM synthesis (frequency modulation), generating new harmonics called sidebands. The sonic character changes from smooth vibrato to metallic, bell-like, or harsh timbres depending on the ratio between the LFO rate and the carrier oscillator's frequency. Some synthesizers like Serum and Vital handle this seamlessly by switching to audio-rate computation above a threshold rate, while others may produce stepping artifacts. This audio-rate LFO territory is genuinely a form of FM synthesis.
In most DAWs you can modulate effect parameters with an external LFO device. In Ableton Live, the Max for Live LFO can map to any device or track parameter via MIDI mapping or direct assignment. In Reaper, right-click any FX parameter and choose 'Parameter Modulation' to attach a built-in LFO. Third-party plugins like Xfer LFO Tool can be inserted in a chain and sidechain-routed to a following effect's wet/dry mix or a filter cutoff. Common drum bus applications include slow filter sweeps, rhythmically synced tremolo, and random reverb size modulation for organic-feeling electronic percussion.
Phase offset sets the starting point within the LFO waveform when a note is triggered or when the LFO is reset. A 0° sine wave rises from center; a 90° sine starts at its positive peak. In performance, carefully chosen phase can ensure the LFO begins its sweep in the direction you want — for example, a filter opening from the bottom (0° ascending) versus closing from the top (180° descending) on note attack. In polyphonic or unison patches, staggering the phase across voices by 30–120° creates a wide, chorus-like movement as each voice modulates out of step with the others — a technique critical for producing professional-sounding wide pads and super saws.
