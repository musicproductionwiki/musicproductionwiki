Convolution Reverb

/ˌkɒn.vəˈluː.ʃən rɪˈvɜːb/

01 Definition

The room isn't a setting you dial in — it's a place you've actually been. Convolution reverb is the only tool in your session that starts with a microphone, not a parameter.

Convolution reverb is a signal-processing method that replicates the acoustic behavior of a real physical space by performing a mathematical operation — convolution — between a dry input signal and an impulse response (IR). An impulse response is a recording that captures every reflection, absorption characteristic, and resonant frequency of a specific environment when that environment is excited by a broadband transient, such as a starter pistol, a sine sweep, or a balloon pop. The resulting file is not audio in the conventional sense; it is a precise time-domain portrait of how that room transforms sound energy over time.

The distinction between convolution reverb and algorithmic reverb is foundational. Algorithmic reverb — the kind found in hardware units like the Lexicon 480L or the EMT 250 — builds reverb tails using networks of delays, allpass filters, and feedback loops governed by parameters like diffusion, density, and room size. These systems model the perceptual character of reverberation without literally measuring any real space. Convolution reverb does the opposite: it captures a specific real space, hardware unit, or physical acoustic chain, and uses that capture as a computational blueprint. The trade-off is flexibility versus authenticity. An algorithmic reverb can be tuned to whatever the mix demands; a convolution reverb is, at its purest, a playback of reality.

The term convolution refers to a specific mathematical integral that expresses how the shape of one function is modified by another. In audio terms, convolving a dry vocal recording with the impulse response of the Hagia Sophia means that every single sample of that vocal is used as the origin point for a scaled, time-shifted copy of the entire IR. Summed together, these copies produce the complete reverberated signal. The process is computationally expensive — convolving a 44,100-sample-per-second signal with a three-second IR means processing roughly 132,300 multiplications per output sample — which is why real-time convolution reverb required dedicated hardware DSP until the mid-2000s, when consumer CPUs became fast enough to handle it in software via fast Fourier transform (FFT) optimization.

Modern convolution reverb plugins — Waves IR-1, UAD Lexicon 480L (which uses convolution to recreate vintage hardware), Altiverb by Audio Ease, Liquidsonics Reverberate 3, and Logic Pro's Space Designer — all implement this FFT-based approach, breaking the convolution into frequency-domain multiplication blocks to reduce computational load. Most also add a hybrid layer of editable parameters — pre-delay, envelope shaping, EQ, and stretch — that allow producers to modify the IR without re-capturing the space, extending the creative utility of what would otherwise be a static snapshot.

For producers, the practical implication is significant: convolution reverb provides access to acoustic environments that would otherwise require expensive location recording or studio bookings. A film score can place a string section in the resonant chamber of the Concertgebouw in Amsterdam. A hip-hop producer can summon the exact plate reverb tail of a specific vintage EMT 140 that lives in a studio in Nashville. A podcaster can de-room a voice and re-room it in a flattering, professionally treated space. The physical world becomes a library, and convolution reverb is the playback mechanism.

02 How It Works

The process begins with impulse response capture. To record an IR, an engineer introduces a known, broadband signal — most commonly a sine sweep from 20 Hz to 20 kHz, or a sharp impulsive transient like a starter pistol — into the target acoustic space. Microphones capture the space's response to that excitation. When a sine sweep is used, a mathematical inverse filter is applied post-capture to extract the pure impulse response from the recording, eliminating nonlinearities and the frequency-dependent behavior of the loudspeaker used for playback. The result is a WAV or AIFF file of typically 0.5 to 10 seconds in length that encodes the complete acoustic impulse of that space: every early reflection from every surface, every modal resonance in the low end, and the full diffuse reverberant tail as energy decays to the noise floor.

At the plugin level, the dry input signal is processed using a technique called partitioned convolution, implemented in the frequency domain via the FFT. Instead of performing a single enormous convolution (which would introduce unacceptable latency), the IR is divided into time-domain partitions — typically 512 to 4096 samples per block. Each block of the input signal is transformed into the frequency domain, multiplied element-wise by the corresponding IR partition's frequency-domain representation, then inverse-transformed back to the time domain. These partitioned outputs are summed using an overlap-add or overlap-save method to reconstruct the complete convolved signal. This architecture allows convolution reverb to run at latencies as low as 64 samples on modern hardware while processing IRs several seconds in length.

The frequency-domain multiplication step is where the acoustic information actually transfers to the dry signal. Because multiplication in the frequency domain is equivalent to convolution in the time domain (a consequence of the convolution theorem), the spectral signature of the room — which frequencies it amplifies, which it absorbs, how quickly different frequency bands decay — is imprinted onto every moment of the input signal. Low-frequency room modes that cause the sub frequencies of a grand hall to ring for four seconds while the high-frequency diffusion decays in one second are automatically encoded. This is why a high-quality convolution reverb sounds fundamentally different from even the best algorithmic reverbs: the frequency-dependent decay is not modeled or approximated, it is measured.

Most production-grade convolution reverb plugins augment the raw mathematical process with several real-time modifications. Pre-delay — inserting silence between the dry signal and the start of IR playback — allows producers to separate the direct sound from reverberant energy, preserving transient clarity while adding perceived size. Envelope shaping tools allow the IR's amplitude to be re-contoured: pulling the attack portion down reduces early reflection density, while truncating or applying gain-riding to the tail controls total reverb length without altering the captured decay character. Stretch or time-scaling functions use pitch-preserving algorithms to lengthen or shorten the IR. EQ applied to the IR itself shapes the tonal character of the reverb — rolling off below 200 Hz prevents muddy low-end buildup, while shelving the high end softens a room that captures too much air. These parameters turn a static measurement into a flexible production tool.

It is worth understanding the inherent limitation of the convolution model: it assumes the system being captured is linear and time-invariant. Real acoustic spaces violate this assumption at the margins — a concert hall sounds different when empty versus full of audience members, and a physical spring reverb unit introduces nonlinear saturation that no linear IR can capture. Tape-based plate reverb units and analog outboard gear have harmonic distortion signatures that convolution cannot reproduce. This is why hybrid reverb designs — such as those in Exponential Audio NIMBUS or Liquidsonics Seventh Heaven — combine convolution accuracy with algorithmic nonlinear modeling layers, covering the gap between mathematical precision and mechanical character.

03 The Parameters

Every convolution reverb — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these and you can work with any implementation.

PRE-DELAY
Time between dry signal and start of reverb

Pre-delay inserts silence (in milliseconds) between the dry transient and the onset of IR playback. Values of 10–30 ms preserve lead vocal intelligibility in dense mixes by allowing the direct sound to land before reflections arrive. Values above 60 ms create a noticeable rhythmic gap that can be tuned to tempo: at 120 BPM, 62.5 ms aligns to a 32nd note.

IR LENGTH / TAIL TRIM
How much of the impulse response is used

Trimming the IR tail reduces CPU load and controls how long the reverb sustains after the source signal ends. A cathedral IR may be 8 seconds long; trimming to 2.5 seconds gives orchestral size without blurring rhythmic content. Many plugins display RT60 — the time for the tail to decay 60 dB — which is the standard acoustic metric for room size.

DRY / WET MIX
Ratio of processed to unprocessed signal

When convolution reverb is used on an aux return, this is typically set to 100% wet — the send level controls the blend. On an insert, 15–30% wet suits most vocal applications; 40–60% wet works for atmospheric pads or sound design. Never run 100% wet on an insert for lead elements; you lose the direct signal's phase integrity.

IR EQ / FILTER
Tonal shaping applied to the impulse response itself

EQ applied directly to the IR shapes the reverb's spectral character before it is convolved with the source. A high-pass at 120–200 Hz prevents sub-frequency buildup from large rooms that adds mud in busy mixes. A high-frequency shelf roll-off above 8–10 kHz softens bright IR captures that can add unwanted shimmer to dense arrangements.

STRETCH / TIME-SCALE
Pitch-preserving length adjustment of the IR

Stretch algorithms — typically phase vocoder or WSOLA-based — allow the IR to be lengthened (up to 200% in most plugins) or compressed (down to 30%) without altering its pitch or tonal character. Stretching a small room IR creates a hybrid space that has the early reflection pattern of a tight room with the tail of a larger hall. Subtle use (80–120%) goes undetected; extreme values (200%+) create obvious artifacts as a deliberate effect.

REVERSE
Time-reversed playback of the impulse response

Playing the IR in reverse causes the reverb tail to swell into the transient rather than decay away from it, creating the classic reverse reverb effect. This technique — pioneered on recordings by The Beatles and Jimi Hendrix via tape manipulation — can now be achieved instantly in any convolution reverb plugin. Most effective on snare drums, vocals, and guitar hits at 80–120% wet on a dedicated aux.

STEREO WIDTH
Mid-side balance of the wet reverb signal

Narrowing the reverb to mono-compatible center energy can clean up low-frequency buildup in the reverb tail. Widening increases the sense of immersion. On bus and master processing, keeping reverb sends wide (80–100% stereo) while ensuring the dry signal is mono-compatible prevents phase cancellation at lower listening levels.

05 History & Origin

The mathematical foundation for convolution reverb stretches to 1933, when American mathematician Norbert Wiener formalized convolution theory in his work on generalized harmonic analysis. The acoustic application came significantly later: in 1966, acoustician Manfred Schroeder at Bell Laboratories published his seminal work on digital simulation of reverberation and room acoustics, establishing that a room's impulse response fully characterizes its acoustic behavior for any input signal. Schroeder also developed the Maximum Length Sequence (MLS) method for measuring impulse responses, which became a standard capture technique through the 1980s and 1990s.

The first practical implementation of convolution reverb in a commercial product arrived in 1978, with the Sony DRE-2000, a purpose-built digital reverb unit that used preloaded impulse responses of real acoustic spaces — including recordings made in Japanese concert halls — convolved via dedicated DSP hardware. The unit cost approximately $30,000 and required mainframe-level computing resources by the standards of the era. Engineers at major studios used it primarily for orchestral and film scoring work. Around the same time, researchers at the Stanford Center for Computer Research in Music and Acoustics (CCRMA), including Julius O. Smith III, were developing the theoretical underpinnings of FFT-based convolution that would later make real-time software implementation feasible.

The breakthrough that brought convolution reverb into the production mainstream arrived in 1999, when Audio Ease released Altiverb for Mac OS — the first software plugin to offer real-time convolution reverb using commercially available CPU processing rather than dedicated DSP hardware. Lead developer Arjen van der Schoot's work leveraged the then-novel partitioned convolution approach to reduce latency to workable levels on PowerPC G3 and G4 processors. Altiverb launched with an IR library that included spaces such as the Concertgebouw in Amsterdam and the Sydney Opera House, captured using balloon pops and sine sweeps. The product was an immediate success in post-production and film scoring communities, winning a Technical Achievement Award from the Cinema Audio Society in 2003.

The 2000s saw an explosion of convolution reverb development. Waves released the IR-1 in 2002, bringing the technology to a broader recording audience with a factory IR library that included emulations of classic hardware units such as the EMT 140 plate and various Lexicon 480L programs. Universal Audio introduced UAD-powered convolution reverb options that combined measured hardware IRs with the additional DSP headroom of their PCIe accelerator cards. Logic Pro 7 (2004) shipped with Space Designer as a bundled plugin — a milestone that made professional-grade convolution reverb standard equipment for millions of producers without any additional expenditure. By 2010, convolution reverb had migrated from a specialist tool to a session-day expectation, with IR libraries measured in venues across six continents freely available through communities such as the Open AIR project at the University of York.

06 How Producers Use It

On drums, convolution reverb functions most powerfully as a room or ambience sender. Loading an IR captured in a specific studio live room — Electric Lady, Abbey Road Studio Two, or Blackbird Studio A — allows a close-miked kit to inhabit a defined acoustic space without requiring the session to have been tracked there. Most engineers route the room send at −12 to −18 dB relative to the close mics, high-passing the reverb return at 200 Hz to prevent the kick and toms from accumulating low-frequency mud. A tight room IR (RT60 under 1 second) with 5–10 ms pre-delay preserves snare transients while adding body. For parallel drum processing, blending a heavily roomed convolution return at −6 dB under the dry mix can replace the need for parallel compression to add weight.

On lead vocals, convolution reverb excels at placing the singer in a believable acoustic environment — particularly useful when tracking happens in a dry project studio. Chamber IRs (spaces with RT60 of 2–3 seconds, high diffusion, and even decay across frequencies) are the classic choice for lead vocals in pop and R&B: they add space without the artificial shimmer of many algorithmic programs. Pre-delay of 20–35 ms, set to tempo if possible, is essential to maintain intelligibility. For background vocals, a different, shorter IR — a small room or vocal booth capture — keeps harmonies tighter in the stereo field while preserving the lead's sense of being the front-most element in the depth field.

In film scoring and cinematic production, convolution reverb is often used as the primary spatial glue across an entire orchestral template. The same large hall IR — applied consistently across strings, brass, woodwinds, and percussion returns — creates the illusion that every virtual instrument section was tracked simultaneously in the same space. Engineers at Hans Zimmer's Remote Control Productions have documented using Altiverb's Royal Albert Hall and Teldex Studio Berlin IRs as the spatial foundation for blockbuster scores. The key discipline is consistency: once a hall IR is chosen for an orchestral template, all wet returns should share it, with instrument-specific EQ shaping on the IR to simulate mic placement differences between sections.

In electronic music and hip-hop, convolution reverb often serves more unusual roles. Hardware gear IRs — spring reverb tanks, physical plates, tape echo units — add analog warmth and nonlinear character to digital sources. A 808 sub bass run through a short spring IR at 8–12% wet introduces subtle tail coloration without audible reverb; the effect reads as the instrument sounding more physical and less synthetic. Producers including Metro Boomin and Boi-1da have spoken in interviews about using convolution reverb to make hi-hats and snares feel like they exist in real spaces rather than the sterile null-room of DAW channels. Convolution reverb on a send after a transient shaper allows the tail to be present without competing with the attack of the source.

AbletonLive's stock Convolution Reverb (introduced in Live 11.2) supports stereo and true stereo IR loading. Use the Damping parameter to high-shelf the IR at 8 kHz, and map the Dry/Wet to a Macro for quick send-vs-insert adjustment. The Reverb device is algorithmic — use Convolution Reverb specifically when authenticity to a real space matters.
FL StudioFruity Convolver is FL Studio's native convolution reverb. Load custom IRs via the IR file field. A key workflow tip: route Fruity Convolver on a Mixer insert at 100% wet, then use the Mixer's send level to control depth from each instrument channel. FL's Fruity Convolver supports stereo IR files natively; mono IRs are automatically spread.
Logic ProSpace Designer is Logic's bundled convolution reverb and one of the most feature-complete stock implementations available. Its Synthesized IR mode generates an algorithmic envelope when no IR is loaded — useful for hybrid textures. The Envelope section allows independent attack, decay, and size shaping of any loaded IR without destructive editing. Enable Preserve Length to prevent pitch artifacts when stretching.
Pro ToolsPro Tools bundles AIR Reverb, which is algorithmic, not convolution-based. For genuine convolution reverb in Pro Tools, third-party plugins are standard: Altiverb 8 and Waves IR-1 are session-day staples in professional post-production facilities. On AAX DSP systems (HDX), UAD plugins require an external satellite; native AAX plugins like Waves IR-1 run on the host CPU. Use AudioSuite offline rendering for stems where real-time CPU is a concern.
ReaperReaVerb is Reaper's native convolution engine and is unusually flexible for a stock plugin. It operates as a signal chain of modules — you can combine an IR file with programmatic additions like low-pass filtering, time-reversal, or a generated impulse within a single instance. This makes ReaVerb excellent for experimental use: load a real room IR, reverse it, add a low-pass module, and blend with a noise generator for textural design. CPU use is comparable to commercial alternatives.

08 Types & Variants

Real Acoustic Space IRs
Neumann KM184 capture rigs · DPA 4006A omni measurement mics

IRs measured in physical locations — concert halls, cathedrals, stairwells, parking garages, forests — using sine sweeps or starter pistols. These are the core application of convolution reverb and provide the most realistic, complex reverb behavior available to any production tool. Character varies radically between spaces: a cathedral in Erfurt has RT60 above 8 seconds with heavy low-frequency resonance, while a tiled bathroom has fast, metallic reflections under 0.5 seconds. Commercial IR libraries from Altiverb, Waves, and the Open AIR project cover hundreds of documented spaces.

Hardware Unit IRs
EMT 140 Steel Plate · Lexicon 224 / 480L · AMS RMX16 · Eventide SP2016

IRs captured from specific instances of classic hardware reverb units — measured by feeding a test signal into the hardware's input and recording the output with calibrated microphones. These captures reproduce the tonal character and decay shape of legendary units without requiring ownership of the hardware itself. Because convolution cannot capture nonlinear saturation or wow-and-flutter artifacts from electromechanical units like the EMT 140, hardware unit IRs tend to sound cleaner than the actual hardware at high drive levels; plugins like Liquidsonics Seventh Heaven address this with additional analog modeling layers.

Instrument Body IRs
Guitar body resonators · Piano soundboard captures · Snare drum shell measurements

IRs measured inside or from acoustic instruments — the resonant body of a hollow-body guitar, the soundboard of a Steinway grand, or the interior of a snare drum shell. When convolved with a signal, these IRs transfer the physical resonance characteristics of the instrument to the input source. Convolving a synthesizer pad with a guitar body IR, for example, imparts a subtle woody, acoustic quality to the pad's tail. These specialized IRs are used in sound design and cinematic production to create organic-feeling hybrid instruments.

Outboard & Effect IRs
Accutronics spring tanks · WEM Copicat tape echo · Binson Echorec

IRs captured from spring reverb tanks, tape echo units, speaker cabinet impulse responses, and other analog signal-chain devices. Spring tank IRs preserve the iconic 'boing' characteristic of the physical spring's resonant modes, something algorithmic spring models struggle to reproduce convincingly. Speaker cabinet IRs (used primarily for guitar amp simulation) are technically a convolution application as well — the same mathematical process. These captures tend to be shorter (under 2 seconds) and are more commonly used at heavier wet levels to fully characterize the color of the hardware being emulated.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    Using convolution reverb on an insert with 50% wet — the mud trap
    Placing convolution reverb directly on an instrument channel at 50% wet means the reverb tail — which contains all the low-frequency content of the room — is blended into the channel at equal level with the dry source. In a dense mix, this creates a layer of low-mid mud that is difficult to EQ out without damaging the dry sound. The fix is to use convolution reverb exclusively on aux returns at 100% wet, controlling blend via send level, and applying a high-pass filter on the reverb return at 100–200 Hz.
  • ✕
    No pre-delay on lead vocal convolution reverb
    Applying a convolution reverb with zero pre-delay to a lead vocal causes the reverb onset to coincide exactly with each consonant and transient in the performance, pushing the vocal into the reverb wash and reducing intelligibility. Even 15 ms of pre-delay creates a perceptual separation between the direct sound and the space that makes the vocal feel present and positioned in front of the reverb. At tempo-synced sessions, calculate pre-delay in milliseconds as 60,000 / BPM / subdivision value.
  • ✕
    Ignoring the IR's low-frequency character in dense arrangements
    Large hall IRs often have substantial energy below 100 Hz — the room's natural bass resonance — which becomes a problem when multiple instruments share the same convolution reverb aux. Bass guitar, kick drum, and low piano notes all accumulate in this low-frequency reverb tail, reducing punch and definition. Always apply a high-pass filter to the convolution reverb return — typically 100–200 Hz for rhythm instruments, 80–120 Hz for melodic elements — before this IR low-end buildup compounds across multiple sends.
  • ✕
    Loading a stereo IR into a mono chain and losing the room character
    Many convolution reverb plugins will accept a stereo IR file loaded into a mono channel, but they process only the left channel of the IR, discarding the right channel's timing differences that create the sense of spatial width. In Altiverb and Space Designer, verify that the plugin is inserted on a stereo track or return when using stereo IRs. True stereo IRs — captured with two sets of source speakers and two sets of mics — require a four-channel IR file and a true stereo plugin instance to be reproduced accurately.
  • ✕
    Extreme IR stretching without checking for artifacting
    Stretching an IR to 200% or more to create a super-long reverb tail introduces phase vocoder artifacts: metallic smearing, pitch instability in the decay, and a characteristic 'watery' flutter on transient-rich material. This happens because the stretch algorithm must create new spectral frames from existing data, and errors accumulate over long stretch factors. If you need a very long reverb tail, start with a longer IR rather than stretching a short one beyond 130%. If artifacting is acceptable as an effect, commit to it deliberately and use it consistently.
  • ✕
    Treating all convolution reverbs as interchangeable regardless of IR source
    A plate IR, a room IR, a cathedral IR, and a hardware spring IR are fundamentally different acoustic objects and cannot be substituted for each other based on RT60 alone. A plate has dense, highly diffuse early reflections with a smooth tail; a cathedral has sparse early reflections and a long, colored decay shaped by stone resonance. Using a cathedral IR on a snare drum in a hip-hop mix — even with the tail trimmed — will still sound wrong because the early reflection pattern has the wrong density and timing for the genre context. Choose IR type based on character, not length.

12 Frequently Asked Questions

Convolution reverb uses a recorded impulse response — a measurement of a real space or hardware unit — to recreate that specific acoustic environment mathematically. Algorithmic reverb builds a reverb tail from scratch using delay networks, filters, and feedback parameters designed to model the perceptual qualities of reverberation without capturing any real space. Convolution reverb is more accurate to its source but less tweakable; algorithmic reverb is more flexible but less faithful to any specific real environment. Most professional productions use both: convolution for realistic space placement and algorithmic for creative, controllable ambience design.
An impulse response is a short audio file — typically a WAV — that encodes the complete acoustic behavior of a space, hardware unit, or other system when excited by a broadband transient signal. IRs are acquired through measurement (pointing a loudspeaker into a room, playing a sine sweep, and recording the result with calibrated mics) or downloaded from commercial and free libraries. Free sources include the Open AIR project from the University of York, which hosts hundreds of real-space measurements, and community IR repositories such as VoxengoImpulses.com. Commercial IR libraries from Audio Ease, Waves, and Liquidsonics include meticulously measured studio rooms and vintage hardware units.
In 2025, CPU load from software convolution reverb is manageable on any modern production computer, provided the session is structured correctly. The most efficient approach is to route multiple instrument channels to a single convolution reverb aux return at 100% wet, rather than inserting a separate convolution reverb instance on each channel. A single Altiverb or Space Designer instance on an aux return typically uses 3–8% of a single CPU core on an Apple M-series or AMD Ryzen 7 processor. Running six or more simultaneous instances can cause performance issues at low buffer sizes; using offline rendering or increasing the buffer size to 512 samples during mix passes resolves this.

The minimum requirements are a loudspeaker (or even a clapper board), two microphones, a recording interface, and free software like Room EQ Wizard (REW) or Altiverb's companion IR capture app. The sine sweep method is preferred for accuracy: REW generates the sweep, you play it through a speaker into the target space, record the result, and REW deconvolves the recording into a clean IR. Balloon pops and starter pistols are simpler but less spectrally flat, which can introduce tonal coloration into the resulting IR. Home IR captures of interesting spaces — tiled bathrooms, parking structures, vehicle interiors — can produce unique reverb textures not available in commercial libraries.
In dialogue post-production, convolution reverb is used for ADR matching — recording replacement dialogue in a studio, then applying an IR measured in the original filming location to make the ADR sound as though it was captured on set. The process requires measuring an IR in the actual location (or using a location-matched IR database) and carefully aligning early reflection timing to the on-set acoustics. In music for picture, convolution reverb is used as described in the orchestral template approach: a single hall IR applied to all music elements creates cohesive spatial depth. For sound design, convolution reverb with unusual IRs — underground tunnels, water tanks, industrial equipment — adds location-specific acoustic identity to sound effects.
The most common cause is low-frequency buildup from the IR, particularly when large room or hall IRs are applied to bass-heavy material without filtering. Apply a high-pass filter to the convolution reverb return channel — starting at 120 Hz and adjusting by ear — to remove the low-frequency portion of the room tail. A second cause is excessive wet level: if the reverb is louder than −12 dB relative to the dry signal on most instrument channels, it will dominate the mix. A third cause is IR length: long tails (RT60 above 3 seconds) sustain between notes and beats, creating a smeared, indistinct texture in rhythmic music. Trim the IR tail or choose a shorter IR for uptempo material.
Without partitioning, a three-second IR at 44.1 kHz contains 132,300 samples. To process a single output sample, the convolution algorithm would need all 132,300 IR samples and all preceding input samples — computationally, this means a buffer size of 132,300 samples, introducing over 3 seconds of latency. Partitioned convolution splits the IR into small blocks (typically 512–2048 samples). The earliest IR partitions are processed in a small buffer (low latency), while later partitions are processed in progressively larger buffers on a deferred schedule. The output contributions from all partitions are summed via overlap-add. The result is that only the first partition determines latency, typically 10–46 ms, while computational efficiency is maintained across the full IR length.
Beyond standard room placement, convolution reverb enables several advanced production techniques. Reverse reverb — playing the IR backward before applying it — creates a swell-into-transient effect used on snare drums and vocal hits. IR morphing (supported in plugins like Reverberate 3) crossfades between two IRs over time, creating evolving spatial motion within a single long note. Convolving a signal with an IR derived from another instrument body — a cello resonance, a drumhead measurement — transfers that instrument's formant character to any source. Frequency-dependent reverb, achieved by parallel-routing multiple convolution reverb instances each processed by band-specific EQ before recombination, replicates how real rooms decay at different rates across the frequency spectrum — a technique used in Atmos mixing workflows to add three-dimensional depth to immersive audio beds.
