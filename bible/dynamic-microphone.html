Dynamic Microphone

01 Definition

Every snare crack that ever made a room hold its breath, every overdriven guitar that ever cut through a wall of distortion — odds are a dynamic microphone was responsible. Understanding why that is changes how you record forever.

A dynamic microphone is an electroacoustic transducer that converts acoustic energy into electrical energy through the principle of electromagnetic induction. The defining characteristic shared by all dynamic microphones is the absence of any active electronic components in the signal path at the capsule level — no polarisation voltage, no field-effect transistors, no battery or phantom power required to make the capsule function. Instead, the physical motion of air molecules excites a mechanical system that generates voltage entirely passively, placing the dynamic microphone in a fundamentally different technological class from condenser and piezoelectric designs. Within the broad family of dynamic microphones, two distinct transducer architectures exist: moving-coil (the dominant form in professional recording) and ribbon, each with its own sonic character, practical strengths, and engineering trade-offs that producers must understand to exploit them effectively.

Moving-coil dynamics accomplish transduction by bonding a lightweight coil of wire — the voice coil — directly to a diaphragm, typically formed from polyethylene terephthalate (Mylar), polycarbonate, or occasionally aluminium. When sound pressure waves cause the diaphragm to move axially, the voice coil travels through the gap of a strong permanent magnet, and Faraday's law dictates that this relative motion between conductor and magnetic field induces a voltage across the coil's terminals. That voltage, though small (typically in the range of –55 dBV to –45 dBV at one Pascal), is the raw audio signal. The mechanical robustness of this system is extraordinary: the same properties that make it self-generating make it resistant to shock, humidity, and radio-frequency interference. A Shure SM58 dropped from waist height onto a concrete floor will typically survive and continue performing to spec — a feat no condenser microphone could replicate.

Ribbon microphones represent the second major dynamic family. Here the transducing element is an extremely thin corrugated metallic ribbon — historically aluminium foil at 1–2 microns thick — suspended under tension in a strong magnetic field. Unlike the moving-coil design, the ribbon itself is simultaneously the diaphragm and the conductor. The ribbon's very low mass gives it exceptional high-frequency transient response and a naturally figure-eight (bidirectional) polar pattern, because sound can move the ribbon from either face while pressure from the sides produces no net displacement. Ribbons have historically been fragile — a strong breath blast or reversed phantom power could destroy the element — though modern designs from manufacturers like Royer and AEA have substantially improved durability without sacrificing the format's signature smooth, vintage tonality.

From a frequency-response perspective, dynamic microphones exhibit characteristics that are deeply consequential in production contexts. Most moving-coil dynamics have a frequency response that rolls off more steeply above 12–16 kHz than a large-diaphragm condenser, and they display a pronounced proximity effect — a bass boost of up to 16 dB that increases as the source moves closer to the capsule. Far from being a flaw, proximity effect is a creative tool: it is why a vocalist working an SM7B at two inches sounds dramatically fuller and more intimate than at six inches, and why broadcasters and podcast hosts exploit the same property deliberately. Ribbons share the proximity effect and typically offer a smoother, more gradual high-frequency roll-off than moving-coil designs, contributing to their reputation for flattering 'vintage air' on strings, brass, and room ambience.

In the signal chain hierarchy, the dynamic microphone's low output level and low source impedance (typically 150–300 Ω) make preamplifier selection more consequential than with condensers. A clean, high-gain preamp — the Neve 1073 and its descendants, the API 512c, the Universal Audio 610 — can bring a dynamic's signal up to operating level without introducing noise that would degrade the recording. The relationship between a specific dynamic microphone and a specific preamp is not incidental: it is a primary sonic variable, and experienced engineers choose preamp and dynamic pairings with the same intentionality they apply to microphone placement itself.

02 How It Works

At the core of every moving-coil dynamic microphone is a carefully engineered mechanical resonant system. The diaphragm, voice coil, and the compliance (stiffness) of the diaphragm surround form a second-order mass-spring-damper system with a resonant frequency — typically between 50 Hz and 200 Hz depending on design goals. Below resonance, the system is stiffness-controlled, meaning output falls with decreasing frequency. At resonance, a peak in output occurs (often smoothed by acoustic damping). Above resonance, the system is mass-controlled, and output remains relatively flat until the voice coil inductance and mechanical limitations cause high-frequency roll-off. Engineers tune this system through diaphragm mass, surround compliance, and the geometry of the magnetic gap to target specific frequency-response curves appropriate for the microphone's intended application.

The magnetic circuit is the source of the motive force and is engineered for maximum flux density in the gap through which the voice coil moves. Most moving-coil dynamics use neodymium or ceramic (ferrite) permanent magnets. Neodymium offers significantly higher energy density per unit mass, enabling smaller, lighter capsule assemblies without sacrificing sensitivity. The voice coil itself is wound from copper or aluminium wire — aluminium is lighter (improving high-frequency response) but harder to bond reliably. The coil is wound around a former that is bonded to the diaphragm with precision adhesives, and the entire assembly must maintain concentricity within the magnetic gap of fractions of a millimetre across the full range of displacement. This manufacturing precision is why there is a meaningful quality difference between a $30 dynamic microphone and a $300 one even when the operating principle is identical.

Ribbon transduction follows the same electromagnetic law but with a radically different mechanical implementation. The corrugated aluminium ribbon — corrugations added to increase compliance and allow displacement without tearing — has a mass so low (often less than 0.5 mg) that it can respond to air motion with minimal inertia. This near-massless response is the physical basis for the ribbon's celebrated transient accuracy: a ribbon capsule can track the fine structure of a cymbal shimmer or violin bow articulation with a resolution that moving-coil designs rarely match at high frequencies. The trade-off is fragility and low sensitivity: ribbon output is often 10–15 dB lower than a comparable condenser, demanding quiet, high-gain preamps. Active ribbons — those containing an internal step-up transformer and JFET buffer — address this by providing 12–15 dB of internal gain, requiring phantom power for the active stage (not the ribbon itself, which phantom power must never reach directly in passive designs).

The output transformer, present in virtually all professional dynamic microphones, performs multiple functions simultaneously: it steps up voltage (increasing sensitivity), transforms impedance for optimal source-load matching to the preamp, provides galvanic isolation that rejects common-mode noise and hum, and adds subtle harmonic coloration that many engineers consider part of the microphone's character. The transformer's core material, winding ratio, and primary inductance all influence the microphone's measured and perceived tonality, particularly in the low-frequency region where transformer saturation can introduce euphonic second-order harmonics. This is why the Shure SM7B, the Electro-Voice RE20, and the Sennheiser MD 421 — all moving-coil dynamics — each have recognisably distinct sonic signatures despite sharing the same fundamental operating principle.

Understanding the interaction between polar pattern and transducer physics illuminates several practical decisions. Most moving-coil dynamics present a cardioid polar pattern, achieved through acoustic path-length differences between the front and rear of the diaphragm using carefully designed porting and acoustic labyrinths. Ribbon microphones are inherently bidirectional because the ribbon is driven by the pressure gradient (velocity component) of the sound field rather than by pressure alone — a fundamental consequence of the ribbon's bilateral exposure. Supercardioid and hypercardioid moving-coil designs achieve tighter front rejection by reducing rear-port aperture and adjusting acoustic resistance, trading off some rear rejection for narrower front pickup and two small rear lobes. Every polar pattern decision is ultimately a decision about room rejection, off-axis coloration, and proximity effect severity — all of which carry direct production consequences.

03 The Parameters

Every dynamic microphone — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these and you can work with any implementation.

SENSITIVITY
Output voltage relative to input SPL

Expressed in dBV/Pa, typical moving-coil dynamics measure –55 dBV to –47 dBV. Lower sensitivity than condensers (which often reach –32 dBV) means the preamp must supply more clean gain — typically 55–70 dB — before signal reaches operating level. Preamp noise floor becomes the binding constraint: at 60 dB of gain, a preamp with an EIN of –130 dBu introduces approximately –70 dBu of noise, which must remain well below the microphone's self-noise floor.

FREQUENCY RESPONSE
SPL output across the audible spectrum

Specified in Hz, typically with a ±3 dB or ±10 dB window. The SM7B is rated 50 Hz–20 kHz, the EV RE20 45 Hz–18 kHz, but the shape of the curve matters more than the endpoints. Dynamic microphones characteristically exhibit a presence peak between 2 kHz and 10 kHz (3–6 dB above flat), which adds intelligibility and punch on transient-heavy sources. The LF roll-off below 100 Hz and HF roll-off above 15 kHz are primary distinguishing characteristics between models.

POLAR PATTERN
Directional sensitivity across angles and frequencies

Cardioid is the most common dynamic pattern, offering approximately 6 dB rejection at 90° and 25 dB at 180°. Supercardioid tightens front pickup to roughly 115° acceptance angle, improving off-axis rejection at the cost of two small rear lobes at 126°. Bidirectional (figure-eight) is native to ribbon designs. Pattern behaviour is frequency-dependent: cardioid dynamics typically widen at low frequencies and narrow at high frequencies, meaning the stated pattern is a simplification of a complex three-dimensional behaviour.

MAXIMUM SPL
Highest acoustic pressure before distortion

Moving-coil dynamics handle 130–160 dB SPL before clipping, limited by voice coil excursion rather than capsule electronics. The Shure Beta 52A is rated to 174 dB SPL peak — effectively unlimited for any real-world source. This headroom is the primary reason dynamics dominate close-miking of kick drums, snare, and screaming guitar amplifiers, where a condenser would be mechanically or electronically overloaded before the engineer could adjust the gain.

PROXIMITY EFFECT
Bass boost as source-to-capsule distance decreases

A physical property of directional (pressure-gradient) microphones, proximity effect increases low-frequency output by up to 16 dB as distance drops from 30 cm to 2 cm. This is exploited deliberately in broadcast (the 'radio voice' quality of the RE20) and in music production where a vocalist working the mic creates dynamic tonal variation tied to emotional intensity. The EV RE20 incorporates a variable-D design that significantly reduces proximity effect compared to conventional cardioid geometries.

OUTPUT IMPEDANCE
Source impedance presented to the preamp input

Most professional dynamic microphones present 150–300 Ω at their XLR output. Low-impedance balanced output enables long cable runs (up to 100 m) with minimal high-frequency loss and strong common-mode noise rejection. Preamp input impedance should typically be at least 5–10× the microphone's output impedance — for a 150 Ω microphone, 1,500 Ω or higher is preferred to avoid transformer loading effects that can alter the microphone's low-frequency response and tonal character.

05 History & Origin

The electromagnetic microphone principle was established by Ernst Werner von Siemens in 1877, but the practical moving-coil microphone did not emerge until the late 1920s. Engineers Edward Wente and Albert Thuras at Bell Laboratories filed the foundational moving-coil microphone patent in 1931, describing a design remarkably similar in principle to the microphones used in studios today. Almost simultaneously, Georg Neumann in Berlin and the teams at RCA in New York were developing competing implementations. RCA's ribbon microphones — the 44 and 77 series, introduced through the 1930s and 1940s — became the dominant broadcast and recording microphones of the pre-tape era, their figure-eight patterns and silky high-frequency response defining the sonic character of big band recordings, radio broadcasts, and early Hollywood film scoring sessions. The voices of Bing Crosby, Frank Sinatra, and Ella Fitzgerald were shaped in large part by these ribbon designs.

The moving-coil dynamic emerged as the dominant format for live and studio work in the 1960s and 1970s, driven by the needs of popular music recording and live reinforcement. Shure Brothers, founded in Chicago in 1925, introduced the Unidyne series in 1939 — the first single-element cardioid microphone — a design lineage that evolved directly into the SM57 and SM58, introduced in 1965 and 1966 respectively. These two microphones, more than any others, established the sonic vocabulary of rock and popular music recording. The SM57 became the universal standard for snare drum and guitar amplifier close-miking, appearing on records by the Beatles, Led Zeppelin, and virtually every rock act that followed. Electro-Voice's RE20, introduced in 1968, pioneered the Variable-D technology that minimised proximity effect and made it the preferred broadcast and kick-drum microphone of engineers at studios from Capitol Records to Abbey Road.

The Sennheiser MD 421, introduced in 1960, represented a German engineering perspective on the dynamic microphone problem: a five-position bass roll-off switch, exceptional low-frequency extension, and a cardioid pattern engineered for minimum coloration made it the standard choice for toms, horns, and woodwinds. Engineers like Glyn Johns, Tom Dowd, and Eddie Kramer became inseparable from specific dynamic microphone choices: Kramer's Hendrix recordings at Electric Lady Studios used SM57s on amplifiers in combinations that are now considered canonical. The Shure SM7 — later revised to the SM7B in 2001 — found early fame at Westlake Audio where Michael Jackson's lead vocals for Thriller were reportedly recorded through it in 1982, establishing the microphone's reputation for a warmth and midrange density that differentiated it from condenser alternatives.

Ribbon microphone technology experienced a renaissance in the late 1990s and 2000s after decades of relative dormancy. Royer Labs, founded by David Royer and Rick Warrener in Burbank, California in 1998, introduced the R-121, which addressed the fragility criticisms of vintage ribbon designs while preserving and refining the format's tonality. The R-121's figure-eight pattern, smooth off-axis response, and ability to handle high SPL when positioned away from the source made it immediately popular for guitar amplifier recording, where its natural high-frequency attenuation complemented the already-bright character of many boutique amplifiers. Active ribbon designs from Royer (the R-122) and AEA (the R84A) extended the format's usability by providing internal gain, bringing ribbon sensitivity closer to condenser levels and enabling use with preamps that lack the extreme gain necessary for passive ribbons.

06 How Producers Use It

Drums: The dynamic microphone is the foundation of any drum recording setup. The kick drum, producing SPL levels that would mechanically destroy most condenser capsules, demands a dynamic: the AKG D112 and Shure Beta 52A are near-universal choices for front-of-kick placement, while the EV RE20 inside the kick shell — popularised by engineers at Motown and later codified in the work of Al Schmitt and Chuck Ainlay — provides a rounder, more controlled low-end character. Snare drum top-miking with an SM57 placed at 1–2 inches from the head, angled 30–45° toward the centre, is so standard it is effectively the default starting position for every recording engineer. Tom miking with MD 421s (positioned over the rim at roughly 45°) has been the industry standard since the 1970s. Overhead dynamics — often ribbons at Recorderman or Glyn Johns positions — provide a cohesive room sound with natural high-frequency compression that condenser overheads can lack on live, energetic performances.

Electric Guitar and Bass: The SM57 on a guitar amplifier is arguably the single most-recorded microphone position in the history of popular music. Its presence peak between 5–8 kHz adds articulation and cut, while its natural high-frequency roll-off above 15 kHz removes harshness from bright amplifiers without requiring corrective EQ. Standard placement is on-axis to the cone at the dust-cap edge — slightly off the geometric centre — at 0–2 inches from the grille cloth. Blending a second microphone (a ribbon R-121 four to eight inches back, or a large-diaphragm condenser at room distance) with the close SM57 is the technique used on the majority of commercially released guitar recordings from the 1990s onward. Bass amplifier recording follows similar logic: a combination of DI signal and a close-miked dynamic (Beta 52A, RE20, or Sennheiser MD 421) blended in the mix gives low-end weight and room character simultaneously.

Vocals and Broadcast: Condenser microphones dominate studio vocal recording, but dynamic microphones serve critical vocal applications that condensers handle poorly. The SM7B, RE20, and Heil PR 40 are standard tools for rap and hip-hop vocal recording, where artists frequently work the microphone at very close distances with high dynamic intensity — conditions where a condenser capsule would be overwhelmed or would capture too much room noise and pop artifact. The SM7B's internal pop filter and flat plate guard assembly allows mouth-at-mic distances that would require heavy de-essing and pop filtering on a condenser. For broadcast podcast and voice-over, the RE20's proximity effect minimisation makes it forgiving of position changes during a take — an important practical consideration in non-studio recording environments.

Live Recording and Field Recording: In hybrid live-to-tape session recording, dynamic microphones provide the SPL tolerance and isolation necessary to work in untreated or semi-treated spaces. The rejection characteristics of a tight supercardioid dynamic allow close-miking of individual instruments in an ensemble recording situation where bleed management is essential. Producers tracking rhythm sections live — a workflow favoured by producers including Rick Rubin, Brendan O'Brien, and T Bone Burnett — rely on dynamic microphones precisely because their limited low-noise floor and modest high-frequency extension become advantages: the reduced sensitivity to room reflections means the microphone captures the immediate sonic event with less acoustic context, enabling a dry, present, powerful sound without extensive acoustic treatment.

AbletonRecord into an Audio track with input monitoring off during tracking to avoid latency; use the stock Channel EQ to apply a high-pass filter at 80–100 Hz and a gentle +2 dB shelf at 10 kHz to restore air that a dynamic's high-frequency roll-off removes. Clip-launch recording in Session View is useful for capturing multiple vocal takes from an SM7B for quick comparison.
FL StudioSet your audio interface buffer to 256 samples or lower when tracking through a dynamic to minimise monitoring latency; use Parametric EQ 2 as the first insert on the channel, applying a 6 dB/octave high-pass at 100 Hz and boosting the presence region at 3–5 kHz by 2–3 dB to compensate for the typical dynamic roll-off in that range.
Logic ProUse Logic's built-in Channel EQ with the Analogue setting enabled for gentle saturation that complements the transformer character of dynamics like the SM7B; the Vintage Console compressor plug-in, set to a slow attack (30–40 ms) and moderate ratio (3:1), is an excellent starting point for controlling dynamic microphone vocal recordings without over-compressing transients.
Pro ToolsEnable low-latency monitoring mode (Command+Shift+L) when tracking through a dynamic microphone to keep monitoring latency under 3 ms; use the EQ3 7-Band plug-in with a high-pass at 80 Hz and the AIR Vintage Filter for smooth high-frequency shaping that doesn't exaggerate the dynamic's presence peak into harshness on close-mic vocal takes.

07 In the Wild

Abstract knowledge becomes practical when you can hear it in music you know. These tracks demonstrate dynamic microphone used intentionally, at specific moments, for specific purposes.

Nirvana — "Smells Like Teen Spirit" (1991)
0:00 onward · Produced by Butch Vig

Butch Vig recorded Dave Grohl's drums at Sound City Studios in Van Nuys with a combination of moving-coil dynamics — SM57s on snare and toms, a D112 on kick — supplemented by room condensers. The snare sound, one of the most imitated in alternative rock history, combines the SM57's characteristic presence peak (lending crack and attack) with the natural roll-off that prevented the hi-hat bleed from overwhelming the close-mic signal. The guitar amplifier channels used SM57s at the speaker cone, capturing the Marshall amplifier's full-range distortion without the brittleness a condenser would have introduced. Listen at 1:18 when the full band enters: the separation between kick, snare, and guitar is almost entirely a product of dynamic microphone proximity and SPL handling.

Michael Jackson — "Thriller" (1982)
0:00 throughout · Produced by Quincy Jones, engineered by Bruce Swedien

Bruce Swedien's vocal chain for Michael Jackson on Thriller at Westlake Audio reportedly centred on the Shure SM7 (predecessor to the SM7B) through a Neve console. Swedien was drawn to the SM7's midrange density and its natural de-essing characteristic — the microphone's high-frequency roll-off above 15 kHz reduced sibilance without requiring aggressive processing. Listen to Jackson's lead vocal at 0:48 on the title track: the vocal sits with a presence and intimacy that many engineers attribute not to post-processing but to the physical working distance and the transformer-coupled warmth of the SM7's output stage. The contrast with the harder, more exposed quality of contemporary condenser-recorded vocals is instructive.

Radiohead — "Where I End and You Begin" (2003)
Throughout · Produced by Nigel Godrich

Nigel Godrich's drum production on Hail to the Thief used a combination of close-miked dynamics and room microphones processed through outboard compression and tape saturation. The dry, punchy quality of Phil Selway's tom sound on this track is characteristic of MD 421s at close range — their extended low-frequency response and controlled presence peak capture the initial transient attack and body of the drum without the smearing that room reflections would introduce through ambient condensers. At 1:30, the rising tom fill illustrates how the MD 421's five-position bass roll-off can be tuned to remove excess low-end resonance from each specific drum size without resorting to post-EQ corrections.

Kendrick Lamar — "HUMBLE." (2017)
0:00 throughout · Produced by Mike WiLL Made-It

Kendrick Lamar's vocal recording on DAMN. reportedly used a Telefunken condenser for primary takes, but numerous tracks feature SM7B passes used for their midrange character and isolation. HUMBLE. itself demonstrates dynamic microphone vocal aesthetics: the vocal clarity without sibilance harshness, the slight compression of the upper frequencies, and the directness of the delivery — all characteristic of a close-worked dynamic — are reference-level examples of hip-hop vocal production. The extreme low-end of the beat (808s centred around 50–60 Hz) sits cleanly beneath the vocal because a dynamic's natural roll-off below 80 Hz prevents the microphone from capturing room-excited bass frequencies that would create masking.

08 Types & Variants

Moving-Coil Dynamic (Standard Cardioid)
Shure SM57 · Shure SM7B · Electro-Voice RE20 · Sennheiser MD 421 · AKG D112

The dominant dynamic microphone type in professional recording, characterised by a diaphragm-bonded voice coil operating in a permanent magnetic gap. Standard cardioid pattern, robust enough for stage and studio abuse, SPL handling above 140 dB, and sensitivity in the –55 to –47 dBV/Pa range. Each model presents a distinct frequency-response shape suited to specific source types — the MD 421's extended low end for toms, the RE20's flat response for broadcast, the SM7B's midrange warmth for close-worked vocals.

Ribbon Dynamic (Bidirectional / Figure-Eight)
Royer R-121 · AEA R84A · Coles 4038 · RCA 44-BX (vintage) · Beyerdynamic M 160

Ribbon microphones use a thin corrugated metallic ribbon as both diaphragm and conductor, suspended in a strong magnetic field. Their inherent figure-eight polar pattern, exceptional transient response, and natural high-frequency roll-off (typically gentle above 12–15 kHz rather than abrupt) produce a smooth, vintage tonality associated with brass, strings, room ambience, and guitar amplifiers. The Royer R-121 on guitar amplifiers and the Coles 4038 on orchestral brass are canonical pairings. Passive ribbons require high-gain preamps; active variants address this at the cost of requiring phantom power for the electronics.

Moving-Coil Supercardioid / Hypercardioid
Sennheiser MD 441 · Beyerdynamic M 88 · Audix D6 · Shure Beta 52A · Heil PR 40

Tighter polar pattern variants of the moving-coil design, achieving narrower front acceptance angles (typically 100–115°) and improved off-axis rejection — critical in live performance and multi-instrument studio sessions where bleed management is essential. The trade-off is two small rear lobes (at approximately 126° in true hypercardioid) that require careful positioning relative to unwanted sources. The Audix D6 in hypercardioid configuration is a standard kick drum microphone choice; the Sennheiser MD 441 is considered one of the most accurate dynamic microphones for broadband source capture including acoustic guitar and orchestral instruments.

Active Ribbon
Royer R-122 MKII · AEA R84A · Samar MF65 · Sontronics Sigma

Active ribbon microphones incorporate an internal JFET buffer and step-up transformer that provide 12–15 dB of internal gain, requiring phantom power for the electronics while protecting the ribbon element from phantom voltage through careful circuit design. The active stage raises sensitivity to condenser-comparable levels (typically –38 to –34 dBV/Pa), enabling use with preamps that lack the extreme gain required for passive ribbons. Active ribbons retain all the sonic characteristics of the ribbon format — figure-eight pattern, smooth high-frequency roll-off, excellent transient response — while dramatically expanding preamp compatibility.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    Applying phantom power to a passive ribbon microphone
    Phantom power (+48V DC) applied to a passive ribbon microphone can destroy the ribbon element if the XLR cable has unbalanced wiring, a broken pin, or if the cable is connected while phantom is already active and there is a momentary imbalance between pins 2 and 3. The DC current that flows through an asymmetric circuit can physically stretch or rupture the ribbon foil, which is often less than 2 microns thick. Always verify that your interface or console's phantom power is disabled before connecting a passive ribbon microphone, and use only fully balanced cables with intact, properly-wired connectors.
  • ✕
    Insufficient preamp gain, compensated with digital trim or plugin gain
    Dynamic microphones require 55–70 dB of clean analogue gain to reach operating level — significantly more than condensers. Producers with lower-gain interfaces often add only 40–45 dB at the preamp and then boost the level in the DAW with a plugin or clip gain. This raises the signal floor at the same time as the noise floor of the preamp, and any thermal noise or electromagnetic interference captured at inadequate gain is permanently embedded in the recording. The correct approach is to maximise clean analogue gain at the preamp stage before any digital conversion occurs.
  • ✕
    Ignoring proximity effect as a creative and tonal variable
    Most producers know proximity effect exists, but many treat it only as a problem — filtering out the low-end buildup rather than using it intentionally. A vocalist working an SM7B at 2 inches versus 8 inches produces measurably different tonal characters: the close position creates a bodied, intimate quality that can reduce the need for low-mid EQ boost, while the distant position captures a cleaner, more neutral tonality. Exploring this variable during pre-session sound checks — not compensating for it blindly — is a fundamental part of extracting the best performance from a dynamic microphone.
  • ✕
    Positioning the microphone on-axis to the tweet of a guitar cabinet
    An SM57 placed on-axis to the geometric centre of the speaker cone captures the brightest, harshest point of radiation from a guitar amplifier — the high-frequency 'tweet' region of the speaker. The result is a thin, harsh sound with exaggerated presence that requires heavy corrective EQ. The standard technique — positioning the capsule at the edge of the dust cap or slightly off-axis, between the cone centre and the rim — captures a fuller, more balanced blend of the speaker's frequency output. Moving the microphone progressively toward the edge of the cone darkens the sound; angling off-axis reduces high-frequency content further.
  • ✕
    Using the wrong dynamic type for the source SPL and distance
    A ribbon microphone placed inside a kick drum shell will be destroyed by the high-SPL air blast from the drum head on the first strike — even modern reinforced ribbons have maximum SPL limits of 130–135 dB, and inside a kick shell peak SPL can exceed 140 dB. Moving-coil dynamics are the correct tool for extreme SPL close-miking. Conversely, using a moving-coil dynamic as a room microphone at 10 feet from a drum kit often yields a muddy, poorly defined image — a ribbon or large-diaphragm condenser captures room detail and transient accuracy that a moving-coil's limited high-frequency response and lower sensitivity cannot deliver from that distance.
  • ✕
    Neglecting microphone cable and connector quality for low-output dynamic signals
    Because dynamic microphone output is low (–55 to –47 dBV), any resistance, capacitance, or interference in the cable path is amplified along with the signal when the preamp applies 60+ dB of gain. Long cable runs with cheap connectors introduce high-frequency roll-off (capacitive loading) and hum pickup (poor shielding). For dynamic microphone applications especially, use star-quad balanced cable (such as Mogami 2534 or Canare L-4E6S) for runs exceeding 5 metres, and verify connector integrity — oxidised or intermittent XLR contacts cause noise at high preamp gain settings that is easily mistaken for a microphone defect.

CompressionDynamic range control applied after capture to manage the transient peaks and level inconsistencies that dynamic microphones deliver from high-SPL sources like drums. EQFrequency shaping used to compensate for or enhance a dynamic microphone's inherent frequency response characteristics, including presence peaks and high-frequency roll-off. Noise GateA dynamics processor used to mute a dynamic microphone's signal during silence, particularly important for close-miked drum channels where bleed from adjacent instruments is constant. TransientThe initial attack portion of a sound — dynamic microphones are chosen for their ability to handle extreme transients from drums and amplifiers without distorting. HeadroomThe margin between operating level and the clipping point — dynamic microphones offer exceptional headroom before mechanical distortion, making headroom management a preamp concern, not a capsule concern.

12 Frequently Asked Questions

Standard passive moving-coil dynamic microphones do not require or use phantom power — they generate voltage through electromagnetic induction and need no external power source. Passive ribbon microphones similarly require no phantom power and can be damaged by it under certain conditions. The exception is active dynamic or active ribbon microphones, which contain internal electronics that require phantom power (+48V) to operate the active buffer stage, though in these designs the phantom voltage never reaches the transducer element itself. Always check the specification sheet for your specific microphone before enabling phantom power.
A condenser microphone uses a capacitor (two conductive plates separated by air) to transduce sound — the moving diaphragm changes the capacitance, which is converted to voltage by an active circuit requiring phantom power or battery. A dynamic microphone uses electromagnetic induction — a coil moving in a magnetic field — and requires no power at the transducer level. The practical consequences are significant: condensers are more sensitive, have flatter and more extended frequency responses, and are more delicate; dynamics handle higher SPL, require more preamp gain, and are far more durable. Neither is inherently superior — they are complementary tools.
The SM57's frequency response has a natural presence peak between 5–10 kHz that enhances the attack and intelligibility of a guitar amplifier's midrange content, while its high-frequency roll-off above 15 kHz removes the harsh, ultra-high frequencies that can make recorded guitar unpleasant. Its –57 dBV/Pa sensitivity and extreme SPL tolerance (up to 165 dB with pad) mean it never clips on any amplifier volume setting. The cardioid pattern rejects reflections and bleed, and the capsule's small size allows precise positioning on the speaker cone. These properties make it the lowest-risk, highest-return microphone choice for the application.
Absolutely. The Shure SM7B, Electro-Voice RE20, and Heil PR 40 are professional standard microphones used on major-label recordings across hip-hop, pop, rock, and broadcast. Michael Jackson's Thriller vocals were reportedly recorded on the SM7, the SM7B's predecessor. Dynamic microphones are preferred for close-worked, high-energy vocal performances because they handle proximity and SPL variations without distortion, require no pop filter beyond the integral design, and have a midrange warmth that flatters many voice types. The tradeoff is lower sensitivity and reduced high-frequency air, which can be partially compensated with high-gain preamps and gentle EQ.
Most passive moving-coil dynamics require between 55 and 70 dB of clean preamp gain to reach a nominal operating level of 0 dBu at a typical recording input. A Shure SM7B at –59 dBV/Pa sensitivity with a source at 94 dB SPL produces approximately –59 dBV at its output, requiring around 60 dB of gain to reach +4 dBu operating level. Preamps like the Neve 1073 (maximum gain approximately 80 dB), API 512c (65 dB), and Cloudlifter-assisted interfaces can achieve this cleanly. Ribbon microphones often need 70–80 dB of gain, which exceeds many budget interfaces — an inline preamp such as the Cloudlifter CL-1 provides a passive transformer-based 25 dB of gain boost without adding electronics to the signal path.
Proximity effect is a physical property of all directional (pressure-gradient) microphones whereby low-frequency sensitivity increases as source-to-microphone distance decreases. At 2 cm, a cardioid dynamic can exhibit 10–16 dB more output below 100 Hz than at 30 cm. To exploit it: work closer to the microphone for more warmth and body, especially on thin voices or under-resonant acoustic instruments. To minimise it: increase working distance (6–12 inches), apply a high-pass filter at 80–120 Hz after recording, or choose a microphone with a reduced proximity characteristic like the Electro-Voice RE20, which uses Variable-D technology to significantly attenuate the effect through careful porting geometry.
Choose a ribbon dynamic when you need the figure-eight polar pattern's rear rejection for stereo or M/S techniques, when the source's high-frequency content is already bright or harsh and you want natural attenuation rather than corrective EQ, or when transient accuracy is critical — ribbons' near-zero mass gives them exceptional impulse response that moving-coil designs can't match. Specific applications where ribbons excel include guitar amplifiers at moderate SPL (positioned 12–24 inches back), brass instruments, strings, room ambience, and acoustic guitar combined with a close condenser. Avoid passive ribbons on extreme SPL sources (inside kick drums, close to loud brass) and always check maximum SPL specifications.
A cardioid dynamic at 6 dB rejection at 90° means that a source directly to the side of the microphone — a hi-hat when the dynamic is pointed at the snare — is attenuated by 6 dB relative to the on-axis source. A supercardioid tightens this to approximately 8–10 dB at 90° but introduces two rear lobes at 126°, which must be aimed away from kick or bass amplifiers behind the kit. The frequency-dependent narrowing of the pattern means that high-frequency content from an angled source (a crash cymbal above and to the right) is rejected more aggressively than low-frequency content from the same direction. Understanding this interaction — not just the named pattern — is what separates competent microphone placement from expert placement.
