Polar Pattern

/ˈpoʊlər ˈpætərn/

Quick Answer Definition How It Works Parameters Quick Reference History How To Use It In The Wild Types Mistakes Related Further Reading FAQ

Polar pattern is the three-dimensional map of a microphone's sensitivity to sound arriving from different directions. It determines how much signal the mic captures from front, rear, and sides — directly shaping bleed, room tone, and stereo width in any recording.

01 Definition

The difference between a vocal that sits in the mix and one that fights it often has nothing to do with EQ or compression — it was decided the moment you chose which way the microphone was listening.

A polar pattern — also called a directional response pattern or pickup pattern — is a graphical and mathematical representation of a microphone's sensitivity to incoming sound as a function of arrival angle. Plotted on a circular coordinate system, it shows how strongly the microphone responds to sound at every degree around its capsule, typically measured on-axis (0°), at the sides (90° and 270°), and directly rear (180°). The result is a characteristic shape: a teardrop, a circle, a figure eight, or some hybrid in between. That shape is not fixed aesthetic information — it is an engineering specification with direct, audible consequences in your session.

Every transducer element — whether a moving-coil dynamic, a ribbon, or a condenser capsule — has a native directional response determined by its physical construction. A pressure-sensitive capsule sealed on both sides responds equally to sound from all directions, producing an omnidirectional pattern. A pressure-gradient capsule open on both sides responds to the difference in pressure between its two faces, generating maximum sensitivity at 0° and 180° and a theoretical null at 90° — the figure-eight or bidirectional pattern. All other patterns, including the ubiquitous cardioid, are derived by combining pressure and pressure-gradient elements or by shaping the capsule's acoustic environment to weight front sensitivity while attenuating rear pickup.

The practical implications are immediate and profound. A cardioid microphone aimed at a snare drum rejects sound arriving from behind — typically the hi-hat — by roughly 20 dB at 180°. An omni microphone placed in the same position captures the full acoustic environment with no rejection at any angle. A figure-eight ribbon microphone hung above a grand piano captures the full width of the strings on its front face and the natural room reflection off the back wall on its rear face, a property that stereo techniques like Blumlein pair and Mid-Side recording exploit deliberately. Choosing the wrong polar pattern is not a mistake you can fully correct downstream; it is baked into every sample of audio from the moment recording begins.

Polar patterns are also frequency-dependent — a fact that manufacturers often acknowledge only in fine print. Most cardioid microphones are genuinely cardioid at mid-frequencies (1–4 kHz), but they narrow toward super-cardioid or even hypercardioid behavior at high frequencies, and they tend toward omnidirectional pickup at very low frequencies. This is why a vocalist working extremely close to a cardioid mic may still hear room coloration on sibilants, and why low-frequency proximity effect — the bass boost that occurs as a directional mic is brought close to a sound source — is physically inseparable from pressure-gradient capsule design. Understanding polar patterns at the physics level rather than the icon level is what separates engineers who control their recordings from those who correct them.

Modern multi-pattern microphones — such as the AKG C414, Neumann U 87, and Rode NT2-A — allow the engineer to switch between cardioid, omnidirectional, figure-eight, wide cardioid, and hypercardioid patterns via a front-panel switch or, in some designs, by continuously blending two back-to-back capsules. This flexibility makes them invaluable in professional studios where a single microphone must serve different sources across a session. However, switching patterns is not merely a cosmetic adjustment; it changes the microphone's proximity effect, off-axis coloration, self-noise behavior, and maximum SPL handling simultaneously, and each change demands a reassessment of placement, preamp gain, and room treatment strategy.

02 How It Works

At the capsule level, directional response emerges from the ratio of pressure sensitivity to pressure-gradient sensitivity. A purely omnidirectional capsule is sealed — it responds only to absolute air pressure changes and is therefore equally sensitive regardless of sound direction. A purely bidirectional (figure-eight) capsule is open on both sides; it responds to the instantaneous pressure difference across its two faces, which is maximum when sound arrives on-axis (front or rear) and zero when sound arrives from 90° because the wavefront strikes both faces simultaneously. The polar equation for a figure-eight is simply cosθ, where θ is the angle of arrival. Combining these two responses in a 1:1 ratio yields the cardioid equation: sensitivity = 0.5 + 0.5 cosθ. This blending can be achieved either physically — by porting the rear of a capsule through a tuned acoustic labyrinth — or electrically, by summing the outputs of two back-to-back omnidirectional capsules with equal gain but opposite polarity on one of them.

The cardioid family extends across a spectrum depending on the ratio of pressure to gradient components. A wide cardioid (sub-cardioid) uses a higher proportion of pressure response, producing a pattern broader than the standard cardioid with a null that doesn't reach 0 dB rejection at the rear. A hypercardioid uses a lower proportion of pressure, narrowing the front lobe and pushing the null points to approximately ±110° while allowing a small rear lobe at 180° (-12 dB relative to on-axis). A supercardioid sits between hypercardioid and standard cardioid with nulls near ±126°. These distinctions matter in practice: hypercardioid microphones reject side-arriving sound more effectively than standard cardioids but are vulnerable to rear pickup — a critical consideration when placing monitors behind a vocalist or positioning drum overheads near a wall-mounted speaker.

Proximity effect — the bass-frequency buildup that occurs when a directional microphone is placed very close to a sound source — is a direct consequence of pressure-gradient operation. At close distances, the inverse-square law creates a significant pressure difference between the near and far faces of the capsule at low frequencies, where wavelengths are long relative to the capsule's front-to-back spacing. This effectively amplifies the gradient component at low frequencies, boosting bass by as much as 16 dB at 100 Hz when the mic is within a few centimeters of the source. Engineers exploit this to add weight to a kick drum or intimacy to a vocal; they compensate for it with the low-cut filter that is built into most directional microphones precisely for this reason. Omnidirectional microphones, having no pressure-gradient component, do not exhibit proximity effect.

In multi-microphone sessions, polar patterns determine acoustic isolation between sources, which directly sets the floor for bleed and crosstalk. The rejection of a cardioid microphone at its null (180°) is theoretically infinite but practically limited to 20–25 dB in real rooms due to reflections filling in from all angles. Hypercardioid mics achieve approximately 12 dB rejection at 180° but better side rejection. For drum recording, this means the choice between cardioid and hypercardioid on snare top has genuine implications for how much hi-hat bleed appears in the snare channel — and therefore how much phase interference is introduced when the full kit is summed. The interaction between multiple polar patterns in a complex acoustic space is what makes mic placement an engineering discipline rather than a rote procedure.

Polar patterns are always measured in the far field under anechoic conditions at a specific frequency — typically 1 kHz — and the resulting diagram is a two-dimensional cross-section of what is actually a three-dimensional pickup sphere. In practice, a cardioid microphone's sensitivity at 90° off-axis might be approximately -6 dB at 1 kHz, but significantly better (closer to -3 dB) at 100 Hz and considerably worse (closer to -15 dB) at 10 kHz, because high-frequency diffraction around the capsule body creates narrow, uneven lobes. This frequency-dependent narrowing is why off-axis coloration — the tonal change applied to sounds arriving from angles other than 0° — matters as much as raw rejection numbers when choosing a microphone for a source surrounded by other instruments.

03 The Parameters

Every polar pattern — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these and you can work with any implementation.

ACCEPTANCE ANGLE
The forward arc over which sensitivity remains above −3 dB

For a standard cardioid, the −3 dB acceptance angle is approximately ±60° (120° total). A hypercardioid narrows this to roughly ±65° — paradoxically close to cardioid at −3 dB but much tighter at −6 dB and beyond. In practice, every degree of off-axis movement by a vocalist introduces tonal darkening; knowing the acceptance angle lets you define the physical boundary within which a performer can move without audible timbre change.

REAR REJECTION
Attenuation of sound arriving from 180° relative to 0° sensitivity

Cardioid microphones specify 20–25 dB of rear rejection in anechoic conditions; in typical rooms with reflective surfaces, effective rejection drops to 15–18 dB. Figure-eight mics have theoretical infinite rejection at 90°/270° but only −10 to −12 dB at 180°. Rear rejection directly determines bleed floor — a 20 dB rejection on snare top means the hi-hat at 180° appears in the snare channel at roughly one-tenth its actual level, measurably affecting phase coherence in the kit mix.

NULL POINT ANGLE
The precise angle(s) at which sensitivity drops to minimum

Cardioids null at 180°; hypercardioids null at approximately ±110°; figure-eights null at ±90°. Null placement is one of the most powerful tools in multi-mic setups — deliberately orienting a microphone's null toward a bleed source (a floor monitor, adjacent amplifier, or adjacent drum) maximizes isolation. A vocalist standing in front of a monitor wedge firing directly at 180° relative to the mic benefits from 20+ dB of monitor rejection with a correctly oriented cardioid.

PROXIMITY EFFECT COEFFICIENT
The rate of low-frequency boost as source-to-capsule distance decreases

Directional microphones boost low frequencies at close working distances due to the near-field pressure gradient. At 1 cm, a typical cardioid may exhibit +10 to +16 dB at 100 Hz relative to flat far-field response. The effect is halved (approximately) for each doubling of distance. Engineers leverage this for vocal weight and kick-drum thump; they counteract it by engaging the mic's built-in 80–120 Hz low-cut switch, which is calibrated assuming a working distance of roughly 15–30 cm.

OFF-AXIS COLORATION
The tonal character of sounds captured at angles outside the primary lobe

Even when a microphone successfully attenuates an off-axis source, the frequency response of that attenuation is rarely flat. A side-arriving guitar amplifier at 90° may be down 6 dB overall but lose 10–15 dB of its high-frequency content, making the bleed sound dull and phasey compared to a direct capture. Off-axis coloration is often more damaging to a mix than the bleed level itself; ribbon microphones are generally praised for more uniform off-axis frequency response, while condenser capsules with complex diaphragm geometries can introduce strong comb-filtering in the off-axis zone.

PATTERN SWITCHING RANGE
The set of discrete or continuously variable patterns available on a multi-pattern mic

Multi-pattern large-diaphragm condensers typically offer 3–9 discrete positions: omni, wide cardioid, cardioid, supercardioid, hypercardioid, and figure-eight, with some designs offering in-between positions via continuously variable controls. Each position represents a different blend of the two back-to-back capsule elements. Switching to figure-eight on a multi-pattern mic also changes the microphone's proximity effect, self-noise floor (typically 1–3 dB higher in figure-eight due to cancellation at the edges), and maximum SPL before overload, requiring fresh gain staging.

05 History & Origin

The systematic study of microphone directionality began in the late 1920s as telephone and broadcasting engineers sought to isolate voices from room noise and mechanical interference. Western Electric's engineers, including Harvey Fletcher at Bell Labs, characterized early carbon and condenser capsule directivity in the 1928–1932 period, establishing the mathematical framework of the pressure and pressure-gradient model that still underpins capsule design today. The first commercially significant directional microphone was the RCA Model 44, introduced in 1931 — a large ribbon design with a figure-eight pattern that became the dominant broadcast and recording microphone throughout the 1930s and 1940s, heard on virtually every major radio network program and major-label recording session of the era.

The cardioid pattern was first achieved in a practical microphone in 1939, when Georg Neumann GmbH introduced the CMV 563 modular condenser system with interchangeable M7 capsule heads. By combining two back-to-back omnidirectional capsule membranes and summing their outputs with a phase-shifted component, Neumann's engineers produced a microphone with a heart-shaped unidirectional response. Western Electric simultaneously developed the 639A in 1939 — a ribbon/dynamic combination that could be switched between figure-eight, cardioid, and omnidirectional patterns by changing the acoustic loading of the ribbon element, making it the first commercially available multi-pattern microphone. The 639A was widely used by CBS and NBC for drama and variety broadcast through the 1940s.

The 1950s and 1960s saw the refinement of large-diaphragm condenser microphone design at Neumann, AKG, and Telefunken. The Neumann U 47 (1947) offered switchable cardioid and figure-eight patterns; its successor the U 48 (1957) added omnidirectional switching. AKG introduced the C12 in 1953 with nine selectable polar patterns via a remote control head. The Neumann U 87 (1967) and the Telefunken ELA M 251 (1960) codified the multi-pattern large-diaphragm condenser format that remains the studio standard today. Engineers including Rudy Van Gelder, Roy Halee, and Ken Scott routinely documented their pattern choices in session notes, recognizing that the same capsule in cardioid versus omni versus figure-eight produced recordings with entirely different acoustic signatures — not just in isolation but in how the microphone interacted with the room.

The development of the small-diaphragm condenser in the 1960s — exemplified by the Neumann KM 84 (1966) and Schoeps CMC series — brought tighter, more frequency-consistent polar patterns to instrument recording. Small-diaphragm capsules maintain a more consistent cardioid shape across a wider frequency range because the capsule diameter is smaller relative to the wavelengths being captured, reducing high-frequency diffraction anomalies. This made them the preferred choice for drum overheads, orchestral spot mics, and acoustic instrument recording where off-axis coloration would compromise ensemble blend. The Blumlein pair technique, conceived by Alan Blumlein in his 1933 UK patent, gained widespread practical adoption in the 1960s and 1970s as high-quality figure-eight ribbon and condenser mics became available, establishing stereo polar pattern interaction as a core orchestral and jazz recording methodology. By the 1980s, the polar pattern had been established not merely as a capsule specification but as a primary creative and engineering decision made before any signal reached a preamp or tape machine.

06 How Producers Use It

For vocals, most home and professional studio engineers default to cardioid — it maximizes separation from the room, provides the proximity-effect bass warmth that flatters the human voice, and minimizes bleed from headphone spill. At working distances of 15–25 cm with a pop filter, a large-diaphragm cardioid condenser like the Neumann U 87 or Rode NT1 delivers the intimate, present character that defines contemporary pop and R&B vocal production. For vocalists who move significantly while performing, engineers sometimes switch to wide cardioid or even omnidirectional to avoid tonal changes as the singer drifts off-axis — accepting more room pickup in exchange for consistent timbre. Omnidirectional vocal capture is also preferred in rooms with excellent acoustic treatment where the captured room tone adds desirable space rather than muddiness.

On drum kits, polar pattern selection is a direct function of isolation requirements. The snare top microphone — typically a Shure SM57 in cardioid — is positioned with its null angled toward the hi-hat to maximize rejection of hat bleed. Many engineers, including Steve Albini, have documented switching to hypercardioid on snare when the hi-hat is particularly loud or physically close, accepting a small rear lobe toward the kick drum in exchange for tighter side rejection. Kick drum microphones are almost universally cardioid dynamics placed inside the shell, where the null at 180° faces the resonant head and the tight acceptance angle reduces floor tom bleed. Overheads present an interesting choice: a coincident cardioid pair (XY) captures the kit with moderate stereo width and good mono compatibility; a spaced omni pair captures more room and natural low-frequency extension but with potential mono-compatibility issues from phase differences between the two capsules.

For acoustic guitars, the standard approach is a small-diaphragm cardioid aimed at the 12th fret from 20–30 cm, which balances string attack, body resonance, and room. When recording two acoustic guitars simultaneously in the same room, Blumlein pair placement — two figure-eight ribbons at 90° to each other, crossed at the same point — captures both instruments and their acoustic interaction in a naturally stereo image with no comb-filtering between the pair. This technique was a defining characteristic of early Beatles and Led Zeppelin acoustic recordings. For piano, a stereo omni or figure-eight arrangement above the open lid captures the full range of the instrument without proximity effect coloring the bass register, allowing natural overtones and room reflection to integrate freely.

In live recording and broadcast contexts, hypercardioid and supercardioid patterns are favored for handheld and stand-mounted vocal mics where floor monitor rejection is critical. The Shure Beta 58A (supercardioid) and Audix OM7 (hypercardioid) are designed specifically so that the null angles fall on the floor monitor position when the microphone is held at standard singing angle — a deliberate acoustic engineering choice that reduces feedback threshold by 3–6 dB compared to a standard cardioid in the same setup. In location recording and field production, hypercardioid shotgun microphones extend the directional principle using an interference tube (a slotted acoustic waveguide ahead of the capsule) to achieve extremely narrow acceptance angles — as tight as ±30° — at the cost of pronounced off-axis coloration and significant frequency-dependent pattern narrowing.

AbletonAbleton Live does not process polar patterns natively, but the Spectrum utility (Ctrl+Shift+S on a recording track) helps visualize captured frequency response and identify off-axis coloration or bleed — compare on-axis and off-axis test recordings in Arrangement View to audit your mic placement before committing a full session take.
FL StudioUse the Parametric EQ 2 on an audio clip recorded in each pattern to compare tonal differences — specifically the low-end shelf shift caused by proximity effect; zoom in on 80–200 Hz to see cardioid bass buildup versus flat omni response, and apply the built-in high-pass at 80–100 Hz to compensate for close-proximity cardioid recording.
Logic ProLogic's Channel EQ with the Analyzer active is useful for A/B comparisons between tracks recorded with different polar patterns; use the Low Cut filter (set around 80–120 Hz, 12 dB/oct) on any close-miked cardioid vocal or instrument track to tame proximity effect buildup before any compression is applied.
Pro ToolsIn Pro Tools, use Clip Gain and the Trim plug-in to balance bleed levels between multi-mic drum tracks recorded with different patterns; the Phase Scope (bundled with Pro Tools Ultimate) helps identify phase cancellation between a cardioid close mic and a more omni-leaning room mic pair, especially at low frequencies where pattern differences are most pronounced.
ReaperReaper's ReaEQ with spectrum analyzer enabled allows direct comparison of on-axis versus off-axis captures within the same project; use the JS: Stereo Alignment Delay plug-in to time-align a cardioid close mic with an omni room mic, compensating for the physical distance difference between the two capsule positions before summing them.

07 In the Wild

Abstract knowledge becomes practical when you can hear it in music you know. These tracks demonstrate polar pattern used intentionally, at specific moments, for specific purposes.

The Beatles — "Blackbird" (1968)
Full track · Produced by George Martin, engineered by Ken Scott

Recorded at Abbey Road Studio Two on June 11, 1968, 'Blackbird' was captured with a single Neumann U 47 in cardioid positioned close to Paul McCartney's acoustic guitar and voice simultaneously — a deliberate choice that exploited proximity effect for warmth on the guitar body while the cardioid pattern rejected room reflections from the large live space. The result is the intimately dry, centered stereo image that defines the recording. Listen to how the guitar body resonance is forward and present without muddiness — a direct consequence of close cardioid placement on a large-diaphragm capsule.

Nirvana — "In Bloom" (1991)
0:00–0:15 drum intro · Produced by Butch Vig

Recorded at Smart Studios and Sound City, the drum sound on Nevermind was defined by Dave Grohl's playing and Butch Vig's mic selection. The snare top — a Shure SM57 in cardioid — was deliberately pointed away from the hi-hat to leverage the cardioid null. The kick drum used a dynamic cardioid inside the shell. Room microphones were placed far back in cardioid to capture the large live room ambience that defines the album's sound. In the drum intro, notice the separation between snare crack and hi-hat; the tightness of that isolation is entirely a function of polar pattern placement rather than gating or heavy compression.

Norah Jones — "Come Away with Me" (2002)
Full track · Produced by Arif Mardin

Recorded at Avatar Studios, New York, the acoustic guitar and vocal tracks on this album used a Neumann U 47 and AKG C12 in cardioid positions, chosen specifically for their proximity effect warmth on Jones's lower-midrange vocal character. Engineer Jay Newland documented using cardioid patterns throughout with close placement — 20–25 cm on vocals — to achieve the intimate, slightly rounded tone that characterizes the entire record. Compare the vocal's low-midrange density to a hypothetical omni capture of the same source: the cardioid proximity effect adds approximately 4–6 dB in the 150–250 Hz range, contributing the 'warm' quality that critics described extensively on the album's release.

Radiohead — "How to Disappear Completely" (2000)
0:00–1:00 string intro · Produced by Nigel Godrich

Nigel Godrich and engineer Graeme Stewart used Decca Tree omni microphone arrays for the string orchestration on Kid A, specifically choosing omnidirectional capsules to capture the natural hall reverb at AIR Studios Lyndhurst without proximity effect coloring the cello register. Listen to the opening strings from 0:00: there is no low-frequency distance fall-off, no artificial intimacy imposed by proximity effect, and the room is an integral part of the tone rather than something attenuated. Switching any of those mics to cardioid would have compressed the depth perspective and boosted bass unnaturally — exactly what Godrich wanted to avoid.

08 Types & Variants

Omnidirectional
Neumann KM 183 · DPA 4006 · Sennheiser MKH 8020

Responds equally to sound from all directions; no null points and no proximity effect. Omnidirectional microphones typically exhibit lower self-noise and flatter low-frequency response than directional equivalents because the capsule does not need acoustic labyrinths to create directional loading. Best suited for orchestral recording, choral capture, acoustic piano, and any situation where natural room ambience and consistent timbre at varying source distances are priorities.

Cardioid
Shure SM7B · Neumann U 87 · Audio-Technica AT4040 · AKG C414 (cardioid mode)

The most common pattern in studio recording, with maximum sensitivity at 0°, −6 dB at ±90°, and approximately −20 to −25 dB null at 180°. Produces significant proximity effect when used within 30 cm of a source. The dominant choice for lead vocals, acoustic guitars, snare drums, and any source that requires isolation from a sound behind the microphone — such as floor monitors in a live-to-tape session.

Supercardioid
Shure Beta 58A · Sennheiser MD 441 · Beyerdynamic M 88

A tighter frontal pattern than standard cardioid with nulls at approximately ±126° and a small rear lobe at 180° approximately −10 dB below on-axis sensitivity. Provides better side rejection than cardioid — useful on stage and in situations with significant side-source bleed — while the rear lobe must be managed so it does not face an unwanted source. Common on live vocals and instrument mics in dense stage setups.

Hypercardioid
Beyerdynamic M 160 (narrow) · Audix OM7 · Neumann KM 184 (tight config)

Narrower front lobe than supercardioid with nulls at approximately ±110° and a larger rear lobe at 180° approximately −6 to −12 dB. Maximum side rejection of any single-element pattern; the rear lobe must be pointed deliberately away from bleed sources. Favored for snare top mics in loud drum environments, instrument spot mics in orchestral sessions, and any application where side bleed is the primary problem and rear lobe management is feasible.

Figure-Eight (Bidirectional)
Royer R-121 · AEA R44 · Coles 4038 · Neumann U 87 (figure-eight mode)

Equal sensitivity front and rear, with nulls at exactly ±90°. The native pattern of ribbon microphones due to the bidirectional pressure-gradient response of the ribbon element. Essential for Blumlein pair stereo recording, Mid-Side technique (as the S capsule), and any application requiring simultaneous capture of two sources from opposite sides of the microphone — such as two vocalists facing each other in a vocalist-guitarist recording session.

Shotgun (Line-Gradient)
Sennheiser MKH 416 · Audio-Technica AT4073a · Rode NTG3

An interference tube mounted ahead of a hypercardioid or supercardioid capsule creates a highly directional front lobe — as narrow as ±30° at 10 kHz — by allowing wavelengths arriving off-axis to cancel through the tube's slotted ports. Effective angle narrows with frequency, making long-wavelength (low-frequency) pickup nearly as wide as a standard cardioid. Primarily used in film/video production and broadcast where the microphone must remain outside the camera frame.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    Ignoring the rear lobe on hypercardioid and supercardioid mics
    Many engineers switch to hypercardioid for better side rejection without repositioning the microphone, inadvertently pointing the rear lobe directly at a floor monitor, snare drum, or adjacent amplifier. The rear lobe on a hypercardioid is approximately −6 to −12 dB — still enough to introduce significant bleed from a loud source at 180°. Always physically rotate or reposition a hypercardioid mic after switching so the rear lobe faces the least sonically active direction in the room.
  • ✕
    Treating polar pattern diagrams as frequency-independent
    A cardioid pattern printed on a microphone's specification sheet is measured at 1 kHz in anechoic conditions. At 10 kHz, most large-diaphragm condensers behave more like hypercardioids; at 100 Hz, they approach omnidirectional. This means a vocalist who drifts 45° off-axis loses significantly more high-frequency content than the pattern diagram suggests, and a side-arriving guitar cab may appear almost uncanceled at low frequencies. Reference the microphone's published polar plots at multiple frequencies — 250 Hz, 1 kHz, and 8 kHz at minimum — before trusting the single-frequency diagram.
  • ✕
    Applying heavy low-cut EQ to fix proximity effect instead of adjusting distance
    Proximity effect from close-miked cardioid vocals is most cleanly handled by physically moving the microphone 5–10 cm farther from the source, not by cutting 6–10 dB at 80–120 Hz with a high-pass filter. The EQ approach removes fundamental energy from the signal and can thin a vocal irreparably; moving the mic simply captures less proximity effect to begin with while preserving the natural tonal balance. Use the microphone's built-in low-cut switch (if available) as a first step — it is calibrated for typical working distances and removes less fundamental energy than a steep filter applied post-capture.
  • ✕
    Using omni mics in acoustically untreated rooms
    Omnidirectional microphones capture the room equally from all directions, which is an asset in a beautiful tracking room and a disaster in an untreated bedroom studio. An omni positioned close to a vocalist in a room with parallel walls and no absorption will capture early reflections at essentially full level, introducing comb-filtering and muddiness that cannot be separated from the direct signal in post-production. In untreated or semi-treated spaces, cardioid or hypercardioid patterns are always preferable because their directional attenuation reduces the proportion of room sound in the capture even without ideal acoustic treatment.
  • ✕
    Misorienting the microphone relative to its stated polar axis
    End-address microphones (such as condensers with the capsule at the top of the body) are addressed at 0° on the vertical axis; side-address microphones (such as the Neumann U 87) are addressed at 0° on the horizontal axis, perpendicular to the body. A common mistake is aiming a side-address mic at the source by pointing the top of the body toward it rather than the side of the capsule window. This captures the source at 90° off-axis — the most attenuated and tonally colored point of the polar pattern — resulting in a thin, dull, and poorly isolated recording.
  • ✕
    Selecting cardioid by default for every stereo pair
    ORTF and XY stereo pairs using cardioid microphones are the standard default, but spaced omni pairs (often called AB spacing at 40–100 cm) produce significantly more natural low-frequency stereo imaging and no proximity effect for orchestral, ensemble, and room recording applications. The reflexive choice of cardioid on overheads or room mics simply because it is the default pattern results in a narrower, more processed-sounding stereo image than omni placement in the same positions. In well-treated or naturally reverberant spaces, always consider whether omni placement would better serve the material before committing to cardioid.

12 Frequently Asked Questions

Cardioid is by far the most common polar pattern in home studio recording. It rejects sound from the rear (approximately 20 dB attenuation at 180°), which reduces room noise, HVAC hum, and monitor bleed without requiring the room to be fully treated. Most studio microphones — from budget large-diaphragm condensers to the Shure SM7B — ship in cardioid as their only or default pattern, and it is the appropriate starting point for vocals, acoustic guitar, and most single-source recording tasks.
You cannot change the polar pattern of a single microphone recording after the fact — the directional response is encoded in the physics of the capsule and the acoustic conditions at the time of capture. However, if you recorded with a true multi-capsule microphone (such as the Sennheiser MKH 800 Twin or certain ambisonic arrays) that captures multiple capsules to separate tracks simultaneously, you can derive different polar patterns by summing the capsule signals with different ratios in post-production. This is also the principle behind Ambisonics and first-order B-format processing, where cardioid, figure-eight, and omnidirectional patterns can all be decoded from the same four-channel recording.
Cardioid microphones attenuate their rear rejection by approximately 20–25 dB in anechoic conditions, but in a typical room with reflective surfaces, sound arrives at the capsule from every direction via reflections — not just directly from the source and the rear. The effective isolation of a cardioid in a reverberant room may be as little as 10–12 dB over an omnidirectional mic in the same position. The solution is closer microphone placement (which increases the direct-to-reverberant ratio), acoustic treatment to reduce early reflections, or switching to hypercardioid to improve side rejection specifically.
Both patterns are cardioid family variants that offer tighter frontal lobes and better side rejection than standard cardioid. Supercardioid has null points at approximately ±126° and a small rear lobe at 180° measuring about −10 dB. Hypercardioid has null points at approximately ±110° — slightly narrower acceptance angle — and a larger rear lobe at 180° measuring about −6 dB. In practical terms, hypercardioid offers marginally better side rejection but is more sensitive to sources directly behind the mic than supercardioid. Both require the engineer to manage the rear lobe deliberately.
Yes — dramatically. Directional microphones (cardioid, hypercardioid, figure-eight) all exhibit proximity effect: a boost in low-frequency response as the source-to-capsule distance decreases, which can add 10–16 dB at 100 Hz at very close working distances (under 5 cm). Omnidirectional microphones do not exhibit proximity effect. This means a cardioid microphone at 5 cm from a voice sounds significantly warmer and bassier than the same microphone at 30 cm, or an omni mic at any distance. Use the microphone's built-in low-cut filter or physical distance to control this effect.
A figure-eight microphone positioned between both hosts — with each person addressing one face of the bidirectional pattern — is an elegant solution that captures both voices with equal sensitivity while the 90°/270° nulls reject room sound from the sides. Alternatively, two cardioid microphones pointing away from each other (back-to-back) with each person addressing the front face of their respective microphone provides good isolation with minimal bleed from the other speaker appearing through each mic's rear null. Avoid two cardioids facing the same direction with both speakers to the sides — this places both voices in the worst-rejection zone of each pattern.
Polar patterns determine how much bleed each drum microphone captures from adjacent drums, and bleed arriving at different microphones from the same source introduces phase relationships determined by the arrival-time difference between capsule positions. A hi-hat signal captured at its loudest by the overhead mics but also present (attenuated 20 dB) in the snare cardioid mic will arrive at slightly different times at each capsule, creating comb-filtering when the two tracks are summed. Tighter patterns (hypercardioid on snare) reduce bleed amplitude, reducing comb-filtering severity. Engineers like Glyn Johns and Al Schmitt documented pattern selection and distance parity as primary tools for achieving phase-coherent drum recordings without heavy time-alignment correction in post-production.
In standard M/S technique, the Mid (M) capsule is typically cardioid pointed directly at the source, and the Side (S) capsule is a figure-eight pattern oriented with its two lobes pointing left and right at 90° to the Mid capsule's axis. During decoding, the S signal is duplicated, with one copy inverted in polarity; the left channel is M+S and the right channel is M−S. The decoded stereo width is adjustable by changing the level of the S signal relative to M before decoding — narrowing to mono when S=0. The figure-eight pattern of the S capsule is non-negotiable for correct M/S geometry; substituting a cardioid produces inaccurate stereo imaging and compromises mono compatibility.
