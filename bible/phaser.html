Phaser

/ˈfeɪzər/

Quick Answer Definition How It Works Parameters Quick Reference History How To Use It In The Wild Types Mistakes Related Further Reading FAQ

Phaser is a modulation effect that splits audio through a chain of all-pass filters, introducing frequency-dependent phase shifts that create sweeping notches in the frequency spectrum. When the phase-shifted signal is blended with the dry signal, those notches produce the distinctive swooshing, swirling sound associated with classic psychedelic and funk recordings.

01 Definition

The phaser doesn't add anything — it rearranges what's already there. That's its genius: pure phase physics turned into one of the most alive-sounding effects in recorded music.

A phaser — formally known as a phase shifter — is a modulation effect that processes audio by routing it through one or more all-pass filter stages, each of which delays specific frequencies by varying amounts without attenuating them. When this phase-shifted copy is summed back with the original dry signal, the resulting phase relationships cause constructive and destructive interference at discrete frequency points, carving a series of notches and peaks into the frequency spectrum. A low-frequency oscillator (LFO) sweeps the center frequencies of those notches continuously up and down, producing the characteristically liquid, spiraling modulation that has defined countless records since the late 1960s.

Unlike a flanger — its close sonic relative — a phaser's notches are not harmonically related. In a flanger, the notches are evenly spaced across the spectrum, because the effect is produced by delaying the entire signal by a few milliseconds. In a phaser, each all-pass stage introduces its own phase shift curve, and the notches cluster and spread in ways that are musically irregular, giving the phaser a softer, more organic quality. The number of all-pass stages — referred to as the stage count or pole count — is the single most important architectural variable in phaser design: two stages produce one notch, four stages produce two notches, and so on up to the sixteen-stage designs found in studio-grade units. More stages mean denser, richer modulation with more complex harmonic interaction.

Phasers sit in the broader modulation family alongside chorus, flanger, and tremolo, but they occupy a unique tonal niche. Where chorus thickens by simulating pitch drift and flanging delivers a metallic jet-sweep, the phaser's all-pass architecture keeps the effect purely in the phase domain. No pitch change, no delay-line comb filtering — just the elegant mathematics of phase rotation. This makes the phaser extremely compatible with melodic and harmonic content; it enhances movement without disrupting tuning or timbral identity, which is why it became the go-to modulation tool for guitars, electric pianos, and synthesizers across rock, funk, soul, jazz fusion, and electronic music.

In a production context, the phaser serves two distinct roles. Used subtly — slow LFO rate, moderate depth, low feedback — it imparts a gentle, breathing animation that makes a static part feel alive in the mix without drawing attention to itself. Used aggressively — fast rate, maximum depth, high resonance feedback — it becomes a dominant texture and rhythmic element in its own right, the kind of effect that defines a record's sonic identity. Understanding how to move between these two operational modes, and how the underlying all-pass filter count shapes the character of both, is the core competency every producer needs when reaching for a phaser.

02 How It Works

At the core of every phaser is the all-pass filter. Unlike a low-pass or high-pass filter, an all-pass filter passes all frequencies at equal amplitude but delays them by different amounts depending on frequency. The amount of delay — expressed as phase shift in degrees — transitions smoothly from 0° at low frequencies to 180° at the filter's center frequency, continuing toward 360° at high frequencies. A single all-pass stage therefore does not alter the sound you hear in isolation; the effect only emerges when this phase-rotated version is summed with the original unprocessed signal. At frequencies where the two copies are 180° out of phase, they cancel, carving a notch. At frequencies where they are in phase, they reinforce, creating a peak. One all-pass stage produces one notch-peak pair; cascading N stages doubles the notch count to N/2.

The sweeping motion comes from the LFO, which modulates the cutoff frequency of all the all-pass stages simultaneously. As the LFO rises, the notch frequencies sweep upward through the spectrum; as it falls, they descend. The LFO rate controls how fast this sweep occurs — measured in Hz or as a tempo-synced note value — while the depth parameter controls how wide the notch frequencies travel during each cycle. A feedback loop, sometimes labeled resonance or color, routes a portion of the output back to the input. This narrows and emphasizes the notches, making them deeper and more pronounced and giving the phaser a singing, vocal quality at high settings. Excessive feedback can tip the circuit into self-oscillation, producing a pitched tone that some synthesizer-oriented producers exploit deliberately.

The LFO waveform further shapes the character of the sweep. A sine wave LFO produces a smooth, symmetrical rise and fall, yielding the canonical organic phaser motion. A triangle wave is similar but has a slightly more mechanical quality at the turnaround points. A square wave snaps the notch frequencies between two fixed positions, creating a stuttering, rhythmic phasing that works well for funk and electronic applications. Some phasers offer random or sample-and-hold LFO shapes, which scatter the notches unpredictably and are useful for generating experimental textures. Modern digital phasers extend these options with envelope followers and MIDI-controlled modulation sources, allowing the phaser sweep to track dynamics, pitch, or incoming MIDI automation.

The stereo configuration of a phaser adds a spatial dimension. In a mono phaser, all notches sweep in unison across both channels. In a stereo phaser, two independent phase-shift paths are run with their LFOs offset — typically by 90° or 180° — so notches arrive at the left and right speakers at slightly different times. This creates the wide, three-dimensional swirling motion associated with classic stereo recordings and is largely responsible for the phaser's reputation as a spatial effect despite having no delay-based timing offset. When inserted on a stereo channel or bus, a phaser can meaningfully increase perceived width without requiring any panning changes, making it a useful, if unconventional, stereo-enhancement tool in mixing.

In summary: a phaser creates its sound entirely through phase cancellation between an all-pass-filtered signal and its dry counterpart, swept dynamically by an LFO. The filter stage count determines notch density, the LFO shapes the motion, and feedback controls notch depth. Everything else in phaser design — analog versus digital topology, transistor versus OTA circuitry — is a variation on these fundamentals.

03 The Parameters

Every phaser — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these and you can work with any implementation.

RATE
LFO sweep speed

Controls how quickly the notch frequencies sweep up and down the spectrum, measured in Hz or as a tempo-synced note value. Slow rates (0.1–0.5 Hz) produce gentle, breathing modulation suitable for pads and guitars; faster rates (2–8 Hz) generate vibrato-like shimmering on leads and funk rhythms. Tempo-syncing to 1/4 or 1/8 notes locks the sweep to the groove and is almost always preferable in rhythmic contexts.

DEPTH
LFO modulation amount

Determines how wide the notch frequencies travel during each sweep cycle — a higher depth means the notches cover a broader range of the spectrum. At low depth (10–30%) the effect is subtle, adding gentle animation without obvious coloration. At maximum depth the notches sweep dramatically from sub-bass through high-mids, producing the exaggerated whoosh associated with 1970s rock and psychedelic recordings.

FEEDBACK
Notch resonance and depth

Routes a portion of the output back to the input, which narrows and emphasizes the notches, making the effect more pronounced and vocal in character. Values between 30–60% add richness and complexity; above 75% the phaser becomes aggressive and can approach self-oscillation on sustained tones. Negative feedback inverts the resonance character, creating peaks where there would normally be notches and subtly brightening the output.

STAGES
Number of all-pass filter stages

Determines how many notch-peak pairs appear in the frequency response — each pair of stages adds one notch. A 4-stage phaser produces two notches and sounds lighter and more transparent, typical of 1970s guitar pedal sounds. An 8-stage phaser produces four notches for a thicker, more complex character, while 12 and 16 stages approach the lush density associated with studio-grade units like the Mu-Tron Bi-Phase or AMS hardware phasers.

MIX / WET-DRY
Blend between processed and dry signal

Controls the ratio of phase-shifted signal to original dry signal at the output. 50% is the canonical starting point, as full cancellation occurs when the wet and dry signals are perfectly out of phase at equal amplitude. Reducing the mix below 50% softens the notches and makes the effect more transparent, useful for subtle modulation on vocals or bass. Some productions use 100% wet for a filtered, lo-fi sound where the notches become the dominant spectral feature.

LFO WAVEFORM
Shape of the modulation sweep

Selects the shape of the LFO's output, which directly determines the motion and feel of the notch sweep. Sine and triangle waves produce smooth, organic rises and falls suited to melodic content; square waves create an abrupt, switching effect useful for rhythmic applications in funk and EDM. Some phasers include random, sample-and-hold, or envelope-follower modes, allowing dynamic or pitch-responsive modulation that goes well beyond standard LFO behavior.

CENTER FREQUENCY
Midpoint of the sweep range

Sets the frequency around which the LFO sweep is centered, effectively biasing the notches toward the low, mid, or high frequency range. Positioning the center in the 400–800 Hz range keeps the effect focused on midrange warmth and works well for guitars and keys. Lowering the center to 100–200 Hz engages bass content for a heavier, woofer-engaging sweep; raising it above 2 kHz produces a trebly, air-affecting shimmer.

05 History & Origin

The phase shifter emerged from the same mid-1960s engineering culture that produced the wah pedal and the fuzz box. The earliest production phase-shifting circuits appeared in Hammond organs and electronic pianos as vibrato and tremolo effects, but the standalone phaser as a guitar effect crystallized around 1967–1969. Univox and Maestro — the Chicago-based subsidiary of Gibson — were among the first to market foot-pedal phase shifters in the United States, with the Maestro PS-1 Phase Shifter (1971) standing as one of the first widely distributed units. Built around a series of RC all-pass filter stages driven by a transistor-based LFO, the PS-1 established the four-stage topology that would define the classic phaser character for decades.

The effect achieved its defining cultural moment through two near-simultaneous hardware breakthroughs in the early 1970s. MXR introduced the Phase 90 in 1974 — a compact, four-stage, single-knob pedal designed by Keith Barr that became the most influential guitar phaser of all time. Its orange enclosure was seen on the pedalboards of Eddie Van Halen, David Gilmour, and Brian May, and the unit's warm, transistor-colored character remains the reference standard for four-stage phasing. In the same period, Electro-Harmonix released the Small Stone (1974), offering a similar four-stage topology with the addition of a color switch that engaged feedback, deepening the resonance. Both units retailed for under forty dollars and put studio-quality phasing in reach of every working musician.

For studio applications, the benchmark units were considerably more elaborate. The Mu-Tron Bi-Phase (1974), designed by Mike Beigel, offered two independent twelve-stage phaser channels that could be linked and cross-modulated, producing the dense, layered phasing heard on Stevie Wonder's recordings with Malcolm Cecil and Robert Margouleff at Tonto's Expanding Head Band studio. The Eventide Instant Phaser (1971) and its successor the Eventide Omnipressor represented the professional rack-mount tier, used extensively at studios including Electric Lady and Record Plant throughout the 1970s. The AMS (Advanced Music Systems) hardware phasers of the late 1970s and early 1980s brought high-stage digital processing to major-label recording sessions, with engineers like Alan Parsons and Tony Visconti exploiting their precision sweep control for orchestral string and keyboard treatments.

By the mid-1980s, digital multi-effects processors — the Roland Dimension D, the Lexicon PCM series, and Yamaha's SPX90 — integrated phaser algorithms alongside reverb, chorus, and delay, shifting the effect from dedicated hardware to programmable DSP. This democratization accelerated through the 1990s with software plug-ins: Steinberg's early VST format, released in 1996, included basic phaser algorithms, and by 2000 developers including Native Instruments, Waves, and Antares had published dedicated phase-shifter plug-ins that modeled specific classic hardware circuits. Today, plug-ins such as U-He Twangström, Arturia's Mini V phaser section, and Eventide's TriceraChorus/Phaser module exist alongside the continued production of boutique analog hardware phasers by companies including EarthQuaker Devices, Chase Bliss Audio, and MXR itself, which continues to manufacture the Phase 90 with minimal circuit changes from Barr's 1974 design.

06 How Producers Use It

On electric guitar, the phaser is most commonly used as an insert effect, either in the signal chain before the amplifier (for amp-driven coloration) or in an effects loop for a cleaner, post-preamp application. The classic approach for rhythm guitar in funk and R&B is a four-stage phaser — set to a moderate rate of around 1 Hz with the mix at 50% — sitting just before a slightly overdriven amp. This is the signature sound of Isaac Hayes's band on the Shaft soundtrack, Nile Rodgers's rhythm guitar on countless Chic recordings, and the rhythm parts throughout Prince's 1999 and Purple Rain. For lead guitar, slower rates (0.3–0.5 Hz) and higher feedback values produce a singing, sustained quality that fills out single-note lines in the mix without the pitch wobble of vibrato.

Electric piano — specifically the Fender Rhodes and Wurlitzer — is perhaps the instrument most intrinsically associated with the phaser. The Rhodes's natural bell-like decay and its tendency to sit in a dense midrange makes it prone to disappearing in a busy mix; a phaser with a slow sine LFO at around 0.4 Hz and a four-to-eight stage depth animates the sustain phase of each note, creating the gently wavering quality heard on Herbie Hancock's Headhunters, Weather Report's Heavy Weather, and virtually every jazz-fusion record from 1973–1985. In contemporary R&B and neo-soul production, the same approach is applied to sampled Rhodes patches in software instruments; the phaser is placed on the instrument's dedicated channel with automation riding the rate parameter to increase during choruses.

In electronic and synth-based production, the phaser functions as a timbral animator for sustained pads, basslines, and arpeggiated sequences. Applied to a monophonic synth bassline with a tempo-synced LFO at a 1/4-note rate, the phaser creates a rhythmic pumping or filtering motion without the amplitude changes of a tremolo, keeping the low end consistent in level while varying its spectral character. On a pad sound, a slow twelve-stage phaser introduces gradual harmonic evolution that can substitute for the longer modulation envelopes of a hardware synth, making a simple static patch sound like it is slowly breathing. Producers working in ambient, drone, and experimental electronic genres often use multiple phaser instances in series — sometimes three or four — with intentionally detuned LFO rates to create slowly evolving, beating interference patterns.

On drums and percussion, the phaser is less common but highly effective in specific applications. A phaser applied to a room microphone or drum bus — with the mix low (15–25%) and the rate set to a slow eighth-note sync — adds an organic shimmer to the decay of snare and cymbal hits without the metallic coloration of a flanger. Some producers use a phaser on a parallel drum bus alongside parallel compression to give a compressed room sound additional textural complexity. In hip-hop and lo-fi production, a phaser applied to a drum break sampled in the midrange frequencies can give the sample a distinctly vintage quality, mimicking the tape-path variations and circuit coloration of original 1970s recordings while adding new movement to a familiar loop.

AbletonAbleton's stock Phaser-Flanger (formerly Phaser) offers up to 8 stages with independent LFO control per stereo side — engage the Stereo switch and set the LFO Phase offset to 90° for an instant wide sweep. Use the Env section to modulate depth from an envelope follower on dynamic sources like guitars.
FL StudioParametric Phase is FL's native option but lacks stage depth; instead, reach for Fruity Peak Controller routed to a third-party phaser's rate parameter for tempo-synced modulation. For built-in options, the Stereo Enhancer with phase inversion can approximate a single-stage effect in a pinch.
Logic ProLogic's stock Modulation > Phaser provides 4 or 8 stages with feedback and envelope follower input — a capable starting point. For more control, use the Spreader in stereo mode to offset the phase sweep between channels, and try automating the Warm button on the Logic Phaser for a subtle tonal shift mid-arrangement.
Pro ToolsPro Tools ships with the AIR Phaser in the AIR effects bundle — a clean, low-CPU four-stage design good for insert use on guitars and keys. For studio-grade phasing, insert Soundtoys PhaseMistress or MXR Phase 90 emulations from the Waves Classic Collection; both respond to tempo sync via Pro Tools' plugin host clock.
ReaperReaper's built-in JS Phaser (found in the JS plugin folder) is a functional multi-stage design that responds well to REAPER's parameter modulation system — right-click any knob and assign an LFO from the parameter modulation menu for a second-order modulation layer that creates slow, complex sweeps ideal for ambient production.

07 In the Wild

Abstract knowledge becomes practical when you can hear it in music you know. These tracks demonstrate phaser used intentionally, at specific moments, for specific purposes.

Shuggie Otis — "Strawberry Letter 23" (1971)
0:00 intro through entire track · Produced by Shuggie Otis and Al Bell

The electric piano part that opens this track is one of the most studied phaser applications in recorded music. Otis ran his keyboard through a four-stage phaser with a slow LFO and moderate feedback, producing a liquid, wavering quality on each note's sustain. Listen specifically for how the phaser notches interact with the Rhodes's natural harmonic overtones around 500–800 Hz — the combination creates a tone that sounds almost vocal. The Brothers Johnson covered this song in 1977 and made the phased Rhodes even more central to the arrangement.

Van Halen — "Eruption" (1978)
0:00–1:42 (full track) · Produced by Ted Templeman

Eddie Van Halen's use of the MXR Phase 90 throughout Van Halen's debut album is a masterclass in four-stage phaser on electric guitar. On 'Eruption,' the Phase 90 is audible in the sustained notes and harmonics of the tapping sections — listen for the sweeping notch moving through the 1–3 kHz range as notes decay. Van Halen ran the Phase 90 directly into the front end of his Marshall Super Lead, which means the phaser interacts with the amp's preamp saturation, creating a more complex, colored result than a clean-signal phaser would produce.

Herbie Hancock — "Chameleon" (1973)
0:30 synthesizer entry · Produced by Herbie Hancock

The ARP Odyssey bassline that defines 'Chameleon' was processed through a phaser set to a slow, deep sweep that gives the synth bass its characteristic wah-like undulation. This is one of the earliest high-profile examples of phasing applied to a synthesizer bass voice in a funk context. Compare the synthesizer sound in the intro to when the full band enters — the phaser's contribution to the bass line's perceived movement against a rhythmically static groove is unmistakable and became a template for funk and fusion productions throughout the following decade.

Tame Impala — "Let It Happen" (2015)
0:00–2:10 intro and verse · Produced by Kevin Parker

Kevin Parker's production on Currents is the defining contemporary document of phaser use in psychedelic pop. The drum machine pattern in the intro is processed with a slowly sweeping phaser that creates a sense of rotation and spatial movement before the full arrangement enters. Parker uses phasing across multiple tracks simultaneously — guitars, synths, and drums — with LFO rates intentionally offset from each other, creating an evolving, complex interference pattern that gives the record its distinctive hallucinatory quality. The stereo phasing on the overhead-style drum sound is particularly worth isolating in headphones.

Stevie Wonder — "Higher Ground" (1973)
0:00–0:25 intro · Produced by Stevie Wonder

The Hohner Clavinet through a phaser is the rhythmic engine of this track. Wonder used a Mu-Tron phaser (in some accounts a modified unit worked on by Malcolm Cecil) set to a fast rate synchronized with the sixteenth-note groove. The phaser's notches sweep through the Clavinet's nasal midrange during each strummed chord, producing a rhythmic filtering that reinforces the percussive attack of the instrument. This treatment proved enormously influential in funk and became the template for the phased Clavinet sound used throughout the genre.

08 Types & Variants

4-Stage Analog Phaser
MXR Phase 90 · Electro-Harmonix Small Stone

The simplest and most common topology, producing two notches with a relatively transparent, open character. The MXR Phase 90's OTA-based (operational transconductance amplifier) circuit gives a warm, slightly compressed coloration that sits naturally in dense mixes. Best for guitar rhythm parts, Rhodes, and any application where subtlety is required.

8-Stage Analog Phaser
Electro-Harmonix Bad Stone · DOD Phasor 490

Doubling the stage count to eight produces four notches for a noticeably denser, more complex sweep that covers more of the frequency spectrum simultaneously. The effect is richer and more immediately identifiable as a phaser, making it well-suited to lead instruments, synthesizers, and any context where the phasing is intended to be a prominent sonic element rather than a background texture.

12 / 16-Stage Studio Phaser
Mu-Tron Bi-Phase · Eventide Instant Phaser

High-stage-count units designed for studio use offer dense, harmonically complex sweeping with feedback paths that allow deep resonance without instability. The Mu-Tron Bi-Phase's dual twelve-stage configuration — which can be linked for twenty-four effective stages — is the standard reference for lush, studio-grade phasing on ensemble instruments and full mixes. These units are rare and expensive as hardware but are well-modeled in software by plugins such as Arturia's Mu-Tron emulation.

Stereo / Quadrature Phaser
Phase Linear 4000 · Boss PH-3 in stereo mode

Stereo phasers run two independent phase-shift paths with their LFOs offset — typically by 90° (quadrature) or 180° — creating a rotating, three-dimensional sweep across the stereo field. This topology is the basis for the spatial width enhancement use of phasers and is the configuration recommended for any application where the phaser is inserted on a stereo bus or used to widen a stereo instrument channel.

Digital / Software Phaser
Waves MetaFlanger · U-He Twangström · Soundtoys PhaseMistress

Digital phasers offer precise parameter control, tempo sync, LFO waveform selection, and preset recall unavailable in hardware designs. The best software implementations — particularly Soundtoys PhaseMistress and the Arturia Phase series — model specific analog circuit behaviors including transistor saturation and OTA compression characteristics, delivering sonic results that are indistinguishable from hardware to most listeners.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    Setting the rate too fast for the tempo
    One of the most common phaser errors is dialing in a rate by ear in a solo context that then fights the groove when the full mix is playing. A phaser sweeping at 1.3 Hz over a 120 BPM track is not synchronized to any tempo subdivision, creating a restless, unmoored quality that makes rhythm parts sound uncertain. Always sync the LFO to the project tempo in rhythmic contexts, or deliberately audition the rate with the full mix playing before committing.
  • ✕
    Overusing feedback on sustained mix elements
    High feedback settings deepen and narrow the notches dramatically, which sounds impressive on an isolated guitar or synth but creates unpredictable phase cancellation in a dense mix — particularly in the 200–600 Hz range where fundamental frequencies of bass, guitars, and keys overlap. If a phased instrument sounds suddenly thin or hollow when other tracks are playing, reduce the feedback by 20–30% before making any other adjustments.
  • ✕
    Applying phaser to the full mix at high depth
    A phaser on the master bus at 50% mix and full depth can hollow out the low-mids catastrophically, creating the impression of a scoop that translates poorly on consumer speakers. If using a phaser on a mix bus for stereo enhancement, keep the mix below 20% and the depth below 30%, and A/B constantly against the unprocessed signal. The effect should be audible as widening or animation, not as tonal change.
  • ✕
    Ignoring mono compatibility
    A stereo phaser introduces phase relationships between the left and right channels that can cause frequency-specific cancellation when the stereo signal is summed to mono. Before printing a mix with a stereo phaser on a prominent element, check mono compatibility by summing your monitor output and listening for any sudden level changes or tonal shifts. A mono-safe phaser configuration uses a single shared LFO for both channels rather than an offset quadrature configuration.
  • ✕
    Using the same phaser settings across all instruments in a dense arrangement
    When multiple instruments in an arrangement are all phased with the same rate and depth, their sweeps synchronize and the notches align across the frequency spectrum simultaneously, creating a dramatic, periodic dip in the overall mix level at each sweep peak. Stagger rates across instruments — for example, guitar at 0.4 Hz, keys at 0.6 Hz, and synth pad at 0.25 Hz — so the notches are always distributed rather than simultaneous.
  • ✕
    Confusing phaser with flanger and applying it where a flanger is needed
    Phaser and flanger are related but distinct effects with different spectral behaviors: a flanger's harmonically spaced notches create a metallic, jet-like comb filter sound, while a phaser's irregularly spaced notches produce a smoother, more organic sweep. Applying a phaser where a flanger's metallic character is required — for example, on an industrial drum bus or a synth designed to sound mechanical — will produce a result that is pleasant but lacks the intended aggressive edge. Know which effect you are reaching for before inserting.

10 Producers Also Look Up

ChorusChorus thickens sound by layering slightly pitch-modulated copies of the dry signal, creating ensemble width where the phaser creates notch-based animation. DelayDelay repeats the input signal at set time intervals and is the foundational building block of the flanger effect, the phaser's closest spectral relative. LFOThe low-frequency oscillator is the modulation source that drives the phaser's notch sweep, and its rate, depth, and waveform shape are the primary variables a producer controls. FilterThe all-pass filter is the specific filter type used inside every phaser; understanding filter topology explains why a phaser sounds different from an EQ cut. Stereo WidthA quadrature stereo phaser with LFOs offset by 90° is an effective and underused stereo-width tool that achieves widening through phase rather than timing or level differences. EQEQ and phaser both shape the frequency spectrum, but EQ does so with static amplitude changes while the phaser's spectral notches are dynamic and time-varying.

Use a quadrature stereo phaser with the LFO offset set to 90° between channels, and keep the overall mix below 20%. At this level, the alternating notch positions between left and right create a perception of rotation and width without introducing the severe phase cancellation that occurs at 50% mix and maximum depth. Always check mono compatibility after inserting a stereo phaser: sum to mono in your monitoring chain and confirm that there is no more than 1–2 dB of level change and no meaningful tonal shift. If the mono sum sounds hollow or filtered, reduce mix or switch to a mono phaser topology.
These two parameters control different aspects of the sweep. Center frequency sets the midpoint of the frequency range the LFO sweeps around — it biases the notches toward the low end, midrange, or high frequencies. Depth controls how wide the LFO's excursion is around that center point — a higher depth means the notches travel further from the center with each sweep cycle. Think of center frequency as choosing where in the spectrum the effect lives, and depth as determining how much of the spectrum it covers on each pass. Adjusting center frequency is particularly useful when sharing a mix with other instruments, as it allows you to park the phaser's activity in a frequency range where it won't conflict with critical harmonic content from other sources.
