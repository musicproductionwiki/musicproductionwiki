Noise Gate

/nɔɪz ɡeɪt/

Quick Answer Definition How It Works Parameters Quick Reference History How To Use It In The Wild Types Mistakes Related Further Reading FAQ

Noise Gate is a dynamic processor that silences or attenuates a signal when its level falls below a set threshold, removing unwanted noise, bleed, and room ambience between musical phrases.

01 Definition

Every great recording has silence that earns its keep — a noise gate is the tool that decides what gets to breathe and what disappears.

A noise gate is a dynamic signal processor that monitors the amplitude of an incoming audio signal and selectively allows or blocks that signal from passing through to the output, based on whether the signal crosses a user-defined amplitude threshold. When signal level exceeds the threshold, the gate opens and audio passes through at full level. When the signal drops below the threshold, the gate closes — attenuating or completely silencing the output. The result is a controlled, intentional silence wherever the original source material would otherwise produce low-level noise, hum, bleed from adjacent instruments, room rumble, or breath sounds.

Unlike a compressor, which continuously modulates gain across a wide dynamic range, a noise gate operates in a binary-adjacent fashion: it is primarily concerned with the distinction between signal and silence. The processor does not change the character of the audio when the gate is open; it simply decides whether to let audio through at all. This makes the noise gate one of the most transparent processors in a signal chain when used correctly — the listener hears only the intended performance, not the room between the notes. In live sound engineering, gates are essential infrastructure; in the studio, they are equally fundamental to a clean, professional mix.

Noise gates are integral to nearly every genre of recorded music. In rock and metal production, they tame the ferocious hum of high-gain guitar amplifiers. In hip-hop and electronic music, they sculpt the transient character of drum samples and synthesizers, creating rhythmic chopping effects that are as compositional as they are corrective. In broadcast and podcast production, gates strip out HVAC noise and keyboard clatter between sentences. In orchestral recording, they prevent the ambient noise floor of a concert hall from accumulating across dozens of open microphone channels. The noise gate is simultaneously a surgical corrective tool and a creative instrument with enormous expressive potential.

The mechanical metaphor of a gate — a physical barrier that either stands open or closed — captures the processor's fundamental behavior, but modern implementations introduce nuance far beyond a simple on/off switch. Parameters including attack time, hold time, release time, and range allow a skilled producer to craft gate behavior that is completely transparent and natural-sounding or dramatically rhythmic and artificial. The hysteresis concept, implemented as separate open and close thresholds in some designs, prevents the gate from chattering — rapidly opening and closing — on signals that hover near the threshold. Sidechain inputs allow the gate to respond to a different signal than the one being gated, enabling the famous technique of triggering a gated reverb from a snare hit while processing the room microphone, or ducking an instrument whenever a vocalist begins a phrase.

Understanding the noise gate deeply means understanding the relationship between time, amplitude, and musical intent. A gate set with an instantaneous attack and release applied to a sustained violin note will produce an unnatural, clipped artifact; the same parameters on a sharp snare transient will sound perfectly natural and punchy. The tool rewards the producer who listens analytically to how sounds begin, sustain, and decay — and who appreciates that controlled silence is as much a part of a mix as any instrument in it.

02 How It Works

At its core, a noise gate contains three functional blocks: a level detector, a comparator, and a gain control element. The level detector continuously measures the RMS or peak amplitude of the incoming signal (or the sidechain signal, if one is assigned). The comparator checks whether that measured level is above or below the threshold setting. Based on the comparator's output, the gain control element — historically a voltage-controlled amplifier (VCA), an optical element, or a field-effect transistor (FET); today most commonly a digital gain multiplication stage — attenuates or passes the signal. The speed at which the gain control element responds to the comparator's command is governed by the attack and release parameters, which are time constants that shape the envelope of the gate's opening and closing behavior.

The state machine model of a gate has four phases. Attack begins the moment the signal crosses above the threshold: the gate opens, ramping from the closed attenuation level (defined by the Range parameter) to 0 dB of attenuation over the attack time. Hold begins once the signal falls back below the threshold: the gate remains fully open for the hold duration, ignoring the comparator, which prevents the gate from flickering closed on brief dips in the source signal such as drum ghosting or a vocalist's short pause. Release follows the hold phase: the gate closes gradually, fading from fully open back to the attenuation floor over the release time. If the signal rises above threshold again during release, the gate re-enters the attack phase, which typically produces a smooth retrigger. Closed is the steady state in which the signal is attenuated by the Range amount — usually between −30 dB and −∞ dB — until the signal rises above threshold again.

Hysteresis (also called the threshold window or open/close threshold separation) is a stability mechanism that assigns a slightly higher threshold for opening the gate than for keeping it open. For example, the gate might open at −30 dBFS but not close again until the signal drops to −36 dBFS. This 6 dB window prevents chatter — the audible stuttering that occurs when a signal fluctuates right at the threshold boundary. Some hardware gates implement this as a distinct hysteresis knob; others build a fixed window into the circuit. Sidechain filtering allows the detection circuit to focus on a particular frequency band — high-pass filtering the sidechain before threshold comparison is a classic technique for ensuring that low-frequency room rumble below a kick drum does not falsely hold the gate open when the kick is silent. In FabFilter Pro-G and similar modern plugins, the sidechain can be a completely separate audio input, enabling cross-gating between instruments.

In the digital domain, look-ahead processing allows the gate's algorithm to analyze a short buffer of incoming audio (typically 0–20 ms) before applying gain changes. This enables the gate to begin opening fractionally before the transient actually arrives in the output stream, eliminating any truncation of fast attack transients that would otherwise be clipped by a zero-latency attack stage. Lookahead introduces processing latency equal to its duration — DAWs compensate for this automatically via plugin delay compensation (PDC), but live sound engineers must account for it explicitly when look-ahead gates are inserted into live monitor or PA paths.

The practical result of all these mechanisms is a processor that, when well-calibrated to the source material, is functionally invisible during performance — the audience hears only clean signal — but when deliberately pushed into aggressive settings, becomes a percussive, rhythmic effect tool capable of creating entirely new sonic textures from sustained pads, room microphones, and synthesizers.

03 The Parameters

Every noise gate — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these and you can work with any implementation.

THRESHOLD
The amplitude level that triggers gate open/close

Threshold is expressed in dBFS and defines the boundary between gate-open and gate-closed states. Signals exceeding the threshold cause the gate to open; signals below it cause the gate to close. Typical settings range from −60 dBFS for noise-floor cleanup to −10 dBFS for aggressive rhythmic gating of loud sustained sources. Setting threshold too high chops off the tail of notes; too low lets noise through continuously.

ATTACK
Speed at which the gate opens when threshold is crossed

Attack time, measured in milliseconds (typically 0.1–100 ms), controls how quickly the gate transitions from fully closed to fully open after the signal exceeds the threshold. Fast attack times (0.1–1 ms) preserve the full transient of a snare or kick drum. Slow attack times (10–50 ms) let the transient's initial click through silently before the gate opens — a creative effect sometimes used on room mics. For most corrective gating, attack should be set as fast as possible without introducing zipper distortion.

HOLD
Minimum time gate stays open after signal drops below threshold

Hold time (typically 0–500 ms) keeps the gate fully open for a fixed duration after the signal falls below the threshold, regardless of the comparator's output. This is essential for sources with short pauses mid-phrase — a snare with ghost notes, a guitarist's staccato chord — where a zero-hold setting would cause the gate to close and reopen in rapid succession, creating audible chatter. Setting hold to approximately the shortest inter-note gap of the performance eliminates chatter without artificially lengthening the tail.

RELEASE
Speed at which the gate closes after hold expires

Release time (typically 10 ms–3 s) controls how gradually the gate fades from fully open to the closed attenuation level after the hold phase completes. Short release times (10–50 ms) create a tight, truncated cut-off that can sound unnatural on reverb tails or sustained notes but excellent on tight, percussive drum tracks. Long release times (200 ms–1 s) allow natural-sounding decays that taper gradually into silence, preserving the room character of a snare hit while still removing the noise floor between hits.

RANGE
Amount of attenuation applied when gate is closed

Range (also called Depth or Floor) sets how much the signal is attenuated in the closed state, expressed in negative dB — from 0 dB (no gating) to −∞ dB (complete silence). A range of −∞ dB is total muting; a range of −10 to −20 dB is transparent noise reduction that preserves the natural ambience of the room. For bleed control on drum overheads, −20 to −30 dB is often sufficient to remove hi-hat bleed without creating an obvious chopping effect. Infinite range on a snare bottom microphone with aggressive settings is standard practice.

SIDECHAIN / KEY INPUT
External signal used to trigger gate open/close

The sidechain (or key input) allows the gate's detector circuit to respond to a different signal than the audio being processed. A common studio application is using a clean DI snare signal to trigger gating on a heavily bled snare room microphone. Another is triggering a gated reverb: the dry snare signal drives the gate's key input while the gate processes a long reverb return — the reverb opens only when the snare fires, producing the iconic '80s gated reverb sound. Sidechain high-pass filtering (typically 80–200 Hz) prevents kick drum low-frequency energy from prematurely triggering a snare gate.

HYSTERESIS
Threshold window between gate open and close points

Hysteresis sets a different threshold for closing the gate than for opening it — the close threshold is lower than the open threshold by the hysteresis amount. A hysteresis setting of 6 dB means the gate opens at the main threshold but does not close until the signal drops 6 dB below it. This creates stability on signals that fluctuate near the threshold boundary and eliminates the rapid open/close chatter known as 'gate flutter.' Many plugin gates implement a fixed hysteresis internally; hardware classics like the dbx 903 and SSL G-bus gate expose it as a front-panel control.

05 History & Origin

The conceptual origins of the noise gate lie in early noise reduction research of the 1950s and 1960s. Bell Laboratories engineers studying telephony identified that channel noise was most perceptible during pauses in speech, and that dynamically muting the channel during silence produced a subjectively cleaner signal. This insight — that noise reduction need not be continuous, only applied when signal is absent — was the seed of gate design. The first commercially targeted audio gate hardware emerged in the late 1960s. Allison Research (later Kepex) produced one of the first standalone studio gates, the Kepex I (Keyable Program EXpander), in 1971, designed by engineer Dennis Bohn. The Kepex was a key-input controlled expander with independently assignable side-chain inputs, and it became a staple of major recording studios within two years of release, appearing in facilities including Record Plant New York and Sunset Sound in Hollywood.

The early 1970s saw rapid proliferation of gate designs as recording budgets expanded and multi-track studio sessions became the norm. dbx introduced the 900-series module system in 1974, including the dbx 903 noise gate — a widely copied design whose distinct two-threshold hysteresis control was influential on later products. Drawmer, founded in the UK in 1977, produced the DS201, which became the definitive professional hardware gate of the late 1970s and 1980s. Its four-stage time constant model (attack, hold, release, and a separate 'fast' attack switch) established the parametric template that most gates follow today. The DS201's audio transformer-coupled input path gave it a particular sonic warmth that engineers on sessions with David Bowie, Peter Gabriel, and Bryan Ferry used as much for its coloration as for its gating function.

The most culturally transformative application of the noise gate arrived in 1979–1981, when producers Hugh Padgham and Steve Lillywhite, working with engineer Bill Price at Townhouse Studios in London, discovered the dramatic sound of gated reverb on drums. The accidental discovery is well-documented: Padgham, using the studio's SSL 4000 console with its built-in noise gate on the talkback channel, heard drummer Phil Collins playing through a large live room. The console's gate closed aggressively on the decaying reverb tail, producing an explosive, truncated ambience. Collins and producer Peter Gabriel incorporated this technique explicitly on the 1980 track 'Intruder' from Gabriel's third solo album, and Padgham applied it again on Collins' own 'In the Air Tonight' (1981), which made the gated snare reverb one of the most recognizable sonic signatures of the decade. This single application of the noise gate transformed the aesthetic of popular music production for the next fifteen years.

Digital noise gates emerged in the early 1990s as digital audio workstations matured. Digidesign's early TDM plug-ins included a basic gate, and Waves introduced the C1 Gate/Expander in 1992 as part of the Native Power Pack — among the first commercially successful software gates with a graphical threshold display. Through the 2000s, FabFilter's Pro-G (released 2012) elevated the software gate category significantly with its interactive visual threshold display, look-ahead processing, and sidechain spectrum analyzer, setting a new benchmark for transparency and control. iZotope's RX noise reduction suite (first released 2006, with major gate-adjacent spectral repair tools from version 3 onward) extended the gate concept into the spectral domain, enabling frequency-selective gating impossible with traditional broadband amplitude detection. Today, hardware gates from companies including Rupert Neve Designs, SSL, and API remain in active studio use alongside software implementations, with engineers frequently choosing hardware for its circuit coloration and tactile control even when digital alternatives are technically superior on paper.

06 How Producers Use It

Drums and Percussion: The most widespread studio application of noise gates is on individual drum microphones, particularly snare and kick drum channels. A close-mic'd snare in a live room will pick up significant hi-hat bleed, overhead wash, and room ambience whenever the kit is being played. A gate set to open at −25 to −30 dBFS with a fast attack (0.3 ms), moderate hold (40 ms), and medium release (80–120 ms) will pass the snare transient and its natural ring while silencing the bleed during hi-hat patterns and cymbals. High-pass filtering the sidechain to 100–150 Hz prevents the low-frequency thump of the kick drum from accidentally triggering the snare gate. Drum replacement and drum sample layering workflows also commonly use gates: a gate with a short attack is inserted on the live drum track, and its sidechain output is used to trigger a MIDI note that fires a sample layer, with the gate ensuring the sample fires only when the live drum is struck.

Electric Guitar: High-gain electric guitar amplifiers — particularly those using multiple cascaded gain stages — produce significant 60 Hz hum and broadband hiss even when the guitarist is not playing. A gate placed after the preamp stage (either in the amp's effects loop or on the recording channel) with a threshold set just above the noise floor (−40 to −50 dBFS), a fast attack, and a release of 200–400 ms preserves the natural pick attack and sustain while eliminating inter-note noise. Guitar players must tune this carefully: too fast a release truncates the natural feedback and sustain that defines the instrument's character. Many guitar-specific pedal gates (Boss NS-2, ISP Decimator) place the detection element before the gain stage and the gain control element after it — a topology that responds to the dry signal level rather than the amplified, noisy signal, giving more accurate and musical triggering behavior.

Vocals and Voice: Vocal gating requires a much lighter touch than drum gating. The goal is usually to remove breath sounds, room noise, and low-level handling noise between lines, not to aggressively truncate the vocal. A slow attack (10–20 ms), long hold (200 ms), and long release (400–600 ms) with a modest range (−15 to −20 dB rather than −∞) allows the gate to fade out between phrases naturally rather than snapping shut. Sidechain filtering is rarely needed on vocals. Many engineers prefer to perform manual volume automation on vocal tracks rather than using a gate, which gives more control and avoids the risk of an over-aggressive gate cutting off a quiet phrase-ending word. Gates are more commonly applied to dialogue in broadcast contexts, where they must manage room noise reliably across hours of content without manual intervention.

Creative and Rhythmic Applications: Beyond corrective use, noise gates are powerful compositional tools. Gated reverb on snare — pioneered by Hugh Padgham — involves sending the snare to a long reverb return and placing a gate on that return, synchronized to the track's tempo. The gate closes before the reverb tail decays naturally, creating the explosive, truncated ambience associated with 1980s production. Rhythmic gating of synthesizer pads (sometimes called a tremolo-gate or stutter effect) uses an LFO-triggered or tempo-synced gate to slice a sustained pad into rhythmic pulses. In this application, attack and release times set to match the BPM create anything from smooth tremolo to hard-gated chops. Artists from Madonna's production team in the early 1980s to modern EDM producers use rhythmic gating as a primary textural and rhythmic device rather than a corrective one.

AbletonUse the stock Gate device (found in Audio Effects → Dynamics). Enable the sidechain section to assign a separate trigger channel — essential for drum bleed work. Activate the Flip button to invert the gate logic for ducking applications. The visual gain reduction meter updates in real time; use it to verify the gate is not false-triggering on bleed.
FL StudioThe Fruity Peak Controller combined with a Volume Automation clip can approximate gating on internal mixer channels, but for proper amplitude-following gating, use the bundled Parametric EQ 2 in sidechain mode or reach for the third-party option. Gross Beat (bundled) is the go-to for creative rhythmic gating with volume templates synced to project tempo — far more flexible than a traditional gate for beat-chopping effects.
Logic ProLogic's Noise Gate plugin (Dynamics category) includes a Key Listen button that routes the sidechain signal to the output so you can audition exactly what the detector is hearing before committing to threshold settings. The Hysteresis parameter (labeled as a separate control) is exposed in Logic's implementation — set it to 3–6 dB on sources that chatter. For classic gated reverb, use the Space Designer with Logic's Gate in series on the reverb aux return.
Pro ToolsAvid's stock Expander/Gate (from the Dynamics III plugin) is reliable and low-latency, making it the standard choice for tracking sessions where PDC management matters. Assign a sidechain key input by using a bus send from the trigger source and selecting that bus in the key input drop-down. For mix sessions, FabFilter Pro-G or Waves C1 Gate are preferred by most professional Pro Tools engineers for their superior visual feedback and look-ahead options.
ReaperReaGate (bundled) is a full-featured gate with sidechain HPF, hold, range, and look-ahead parameters, and it is extremely low on CPU. Enable the 'Show EQ' section to apply bandpass filtering to the sidechain detection signal — especially useful for drum bleed suppression. Reaper's flexible routing means you can sidechain any track to any other track's gate without needing to create auxiliary sends; use the track's FX chain routing options to assign the key input directly.

07 In the Wild

Abstract knowledge becomes practical when you can hear it in music you know. These tracks demonstrate noise gate used intentionally, at specific moments, for specific purposes.

Phil Collins — "In the Air Tonight" (1981)
0:00–3:40, especially 3:40 snare entrance · Produced by Hugh Padgham and Phil Collins

The definitive gated reverb recording. The famous snare hit at 3:40 demonstrates a gate applied to a long reverb return — the reverb tail is abruptly truncated rather than allowed to decay naturally. Producer Hugh Padgham achieved this using the SSL 4000 console's built-in noise gate on the talkback compressor, with a fast release (approximately 50–80 ms) creating the explosive, chopped ambience. Listen in the left speaker for the room tail being cut off mid-decay — a technically 'wrong' artifact that became one of the most imitated sounds in pop production history.

Nine Inch Nails — "Wish" (1992)
0:00–0:08, drum intro · Produced by Trent Reznor

Reznor used hard noise gating on the drum kit's close microphones to achieve the hyper-compressed, violence-adjacent drum sound that defined industrial rock production. The snare on 'Wish' has virtually no room — gate range is set to −∞ dB with an extremely fast release, producing a dry, punishing transient that immediately cuts to silence. The contrast between the snare's sharp attack and its complete absence of tail is the defining textural quality of the track's intro. Listen for the gated kick drum as well: the sub-frequency energy punches in and disappears within 30–40 ms.

Kendrick Lamar — "HUMBLE." (2017)
0:00–0:04, kick and snare intro · Produced by Mike WiLL Made-It

Mike WiLL Made-It's production uses extreme low-frequency gating on the 808 kick to create the tight, punchy sub pulse that defines the track's rhythmic foundation. The 808 bass is gated to approximately its first 200 ms of sustain, preventing the low-frequency energy from accumulating in the mix and muddying the space between beats. This creates maximum contrast between the 808's impact and the silence that follows — a technique used extensively in trap production. The snare also exhibits classic sample-layer gating: a clean transient with an abrupt, non-reverberant tail.

Peter Gabriel — "Intruder" (1980)
Full track · Produced by Steve Lillywhite, engineered by Hugh Padgham

The first documented intentional use of gated reverb as a creative effect. Padgham and Lillywhite processed drummer Jerry Marotta's kit through the Townhouse SSL console's talk-back gate to produce the cavernous, artificially truncated drum sound that opens the track. The gated ambience is audible on every snare and tom hit — a roomy, almost violent burst of reverb that snaps shut like a door closing. Gabriel and Lillywhite reportedly saw the accidental discovery on this session as a defining sonic identity rather than a problem to correct.

08 Types & Variants

VCA Hardware Gate
Drawmer DS201 · dbx 903 · SSL G-Bus Gate (console channel)

Voltage-controlled amplifier gates use a VCA as the gain control element. They are characterized by extremely fast, accurate response times and a clean, uncolored audio path. The Drawmer DS201 is the canonical studio reference: its separate frequency filter section on the sidechain input, combined with four fully variable time constants, makes it the most flexible and widely used hardware gate in professional studio history. VCA gates in console channel strips (SSL 4000/9000, Neve 8078) are valued for their integration into the mixing workflow and minimal additional signal path coloration.

Optical Gate
Empirical Labs EL8 Distressor (gate mode) · LA-2A-style opto expander circuits

Optical gates use a light-dependent resistor (LDR) and LED pair as the gain control element. The LDR's inherent attack and release curves — shaped by the physics of the opto element rather than a time constant circuit — produce a soft, program-dependent response that is musically natural on sustained instruments. Pure optical gating is less common than optical compression, but some hybrid designs use optical elements in the gate's gain path to create a softer, more transparent close that avoids the abruptness of VCA gating on complex signals like sustained guitar chords.

Software / Digital Gate
FabFilter Pro-G · Waves C1 Gate · iZotope RX Dialogue Isolation

Software gates offer features impossible in analog hardware: look-ahead processing, visual threshold display, sidechain spectrum analysis, and zero-channel-count constraints. FabFilter Pro-G is the current professional benchmark, with a dual-threshold interface, six gate knee shapes, MIDI learn, and an interactive transfer curve display. iZotope RX's gate-adjacent tools operate in the spectral domain, applying amplitude thresholding independently per frequency band — enabling surgically selective noise removal that leaves natural reverb tails intact while eliminating broadband noise.

Transient Gate (Envelope Shaper)
Drawmer DL241 · SPL Transient Designer · Eventide Transient Processor

Transient gates combine amplitude gating with transient shaping, allowing producers to independently control the attack and sustain portions of a percussive sound. Rather than simply opening and closing, a transient gate can boost the attack of a snare hit while gating its room tail — adding punch while cleaning bleed. The SPL Transient Designer (1998) was foundational to this category, though it operates on a different topology (attack/release deviation rather than threshold comparison). These devices are especially popular on drum room and overhead microphones.

Rhythmic / Tempo-Synced Gate
Boss NS-2 (with sequencer) · Strymon Mobius (gate tremolo) · Moog MF-102 ring-mod gate hybrid

Tempo-synced gates treat the gate as a rhythmic modulation effect rather than a noise-reduction tool. The gate opens and closes at a rate set by the project BPM or an internal clock — typically in note values from whole notes to 1/32 subdivisions. This creates the gated pad texture ubiquitous in EDM, trance, and progressive house production. Modern DAW implementations (Ableton's Gate with MIDI trigger, or third-party LFO-gate plugins such as LFOTool by Xfer Records) are far more common than hardware for this application, offering visual step-sequencer interfaces for programming gate patterns.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    Setting the threshold too high — chopping off note tails
    When the threshold is set above the natural decay of the instrument, the gate closes before the note has finished ringing, producing an unnaturally abrupt cut-off. On drums, this sounds like the drum being hit with a muffler; on vocals, it clips the end of words and phrases. Diagnosis is simple: solo the gated channel, extend the release, and lower the threshold by 3–6 dB increments while listening to the tail of each note. The gate should close only after the natural reverb of the instrument has decayed below audibility.
  • ✕
    Ignoring hold time — creating gate chatter on complex material
    Gate chatter is the rapid open/close flutter that occurs when a signal oscillates near the threshold — common on hi-hat bleed into a snare mic during fast hi-hat patterns, or on guitar with tremolo picking. Without sufficient hold time, the gate interprets each individual bleed spike as a new gate trigger and closes between them, producing a stuttering artifact. Increasing hold time to span the longest gap between intended note events (e.g., 50–80 ms for 16th-note drum patterns at 120 BPM) eliminates chatter without keeping the gate open unnecessarily long.
  • ✕
    Not filtering the sidechain — false triggers from low-frequency bleed
    Applying a gate to a snare microphone without high-pass filtering the sidechain input means the low-frequency energy of a kick drum hit can exceed the threshold and hold the snare gate open during kick hits. This allows kick bleed into the snare channel to pass through unattenuated. The fix is to apply a high-pass filter (100–150 Hz) to the gate's sidechain input, ensuring that only frequencies associated with the snare body trigger gate opening. Most gates expose this as a 'key filter' or 'sidechain HPF' control; enable it routinely on drum tracking sessions.
  • ✕
    Using a noise gate when volume automation would be more appropriate
    Noise gates make amplitude decisions based on rules, not musical judgment. On vocal tracks with variable performance intensity — a singer who crescendos from near-silence to full voice within a phrase — a gate may close on quiet passages that should be audible, or fail to close on loud passages where the noise floor is still present. Manual volume automation (drawing level curves in the DAW) provides more precise, case-by-case control over which segments are silenced. Experienced mix engineers treat the gate as a supplement to automation, not a replacement for it.
  • ✕
    Applying a gate to a bus or mix group — masking mix problems
    Placing a noise gate on a drum bus or full mix bus is almost always a mistake. The threshold will inevitably trigger on the loudest elements of the mix (kicks, snares, full-band peaks), causing subtle amplitude modulation on the quieter elements sharing the bus, and the gate cannot intelligently discriminate between noise and intentional low-level content in a complex mix. If noise is present on a mix bus, it should be traced to its source and addressed with channel-level gating. Bus gating is a last resort and always introduces artifacts at the bus-open/bus-close transitions.
  • ✕
    Treating release as a substitute for natural room decay
    A long release time creates a fade-out rather than a natural room tail — the difference is perceptible and usually unpleasant on organic acoustic sources. A natural reverb decay has a specific frequency shape (high frequencies decay faster than low) and a particular envelope that a gate's linear gain ramp cannot replicate. For acoustic drums, preserving the natural room tail by setting a relatively modest range (−20 to −30 dB) rather than −∞ dB produces a far more natural sound than attempting to simulate room decay with a slow release. Reserve −∞ dB range for sources where full silence between events is genuinely correct — high-gain guitar, dialog, electronic drums.

A noise gate is used to silence unwanted audio — such as amplifier hum, room noise, microphone bleed, and breath sounds — between musical phrases. When the audio signal drops below the threshold, the gate closes and mutes or attenuates the output. This produces a cleaner recording with no noise floor audible between notes. It can also be used creatively to create rhythmically chopped sounds and the famous gated reverb effect on drums.
A compressor reduces the dynamic range of a signal by turning down loud parts relative to quiet ones, continuously modulating gain above a threshold. A noise gate operates below the threshold: it silences the signal when it is too quiet, rather than controlling loud material. In practice, compressors and gates are often used together — the compressor evens out performance dynamics while the gate removes noise between phrases. Some processors (called expanders or compressor/gates) combine both functions.
Start by finding the level of the noise you want to remove. Play the track and watch the gate's gain reduction meter while gradually raising the threshold from its lowest setting until the gate just begins to close on the silent sections. Then fine-tune so the gate opens cleanly on every intended note and closes fully between them. A well-set threshold should be 3–6 dB above the noise floor but well below the quietest intended signal, creating a clear separation between music and noise.
Hold sets a minimum duration for which the gate stays fully open after the signal drops below the threshold. It prevents the gate from closing and reopening rapidly during short pauses in the performance — such as gaps between ghost notes on a snare or brief silences between words in a vocal line. Without sufficient hold time, the gate 'chatters,' producing an audible flutter. Set hold to approximately the length of the longest expected gap between notes that should remain audible within a phrase.
Gated reverb is the effect of a long reverb tail being abruptly cut off mid-decay by a noise gate, producing an explosive, truncated ambience. To create it: send a drum channel (typically snare) to a reverb with a long decay (2–4 seconds). Insert a noise gate on the reverb return channel. Use the dry drum signal as the sidechain trigger input for the gate. Set the gate's release to 50–150 ms and threshold so it opens on each drum hit and closes before the reverb tail fully decays. The result is the iconic 1980s drum sound pioneered by Hugh Padgham.
In most signal chains, place the noise gate before the compressor. This way, the compressor acts only on the intended musical signal, and the gate's threshold is evaluated against the uncompressed input level — making threshold setting more predictable. If the compressor is placed before the gate, it amplifies the noise floor (as compression raises quieter signals), making threshold setting harder and potentially requiring a higher threshold that risks clipping the performance. Exceptions exist for creative applications, such as using a compressor to trigger a sidechain gate on a parallel bus.
Both processors attenuate signals below a threshold, but they do so differently. A noise gate applies a fixed, binary-style attenuation (the Range setting) below the threshold — it closes to a defined floor. An expander applies a ratio-based gain reduction: for every dB the signal drops below the threshold, it drops an additional amount determined by the ratio. An expander ratio of 2:1 means a signal 6 dB below threshold is attenuated by 12 dB; a gate's range might attenuate by −∞ dB regardless of how far below threshold the signal falls. Expanders tend to sound more natural and transparent on complex material.
Yes — rhythmic gating is one of the most musically powerful applications of the noise gate. Route a sustained synthesizer pad to a gate whose open/close behavior is controlled by a tempo-synced LFO, MIDI trigger, or step sequencer. Setting attack and release to match the BPM creates tremolo-style pulses from a continuous pad. Setting attack near-instant and release very short creates hard, choppy stutter effects. In Ableton Live, the Gate device can be triggered by MIDI side-chain input for fully programmable gate patterns. Xfer Records' LFOTool, though technically an LFO processor, is purpose-designed for this application in DAW contexts.
