Chorus

/ˈkɔːr.əs/

Quick Answer Definition How It Works Parameters Quick Reference History How To Use It In The Wild Types Mistakes Related Further Reading FAQ

Chorus is a time-based modulation effect that thickens and widens a sound by blending the dry signal with one or more slightly delayed, pitch-modulated copies. The subtle pitch variation mimics the natural detuning heard when multiple performers play the same part.

01 Definition

Every great chorus patch is a small act of physics-based deception — you are convincing the ear that one guitar, one synth, one voice is actually many, and that lie sounds magnificent when it's done right.

Chorus is a time-based modulation effect that creates a thicker, wider, and more animated version of any audio signal by mixing the original dry signal with one or more copies of itself. Each copy is subjected to a continuously varying short delay — typically between 15 ms and 35 ms — whose length is modulated by a low-frequency oscillator (LFO). Because the delay time is constantly changing, the pitch of the wet copy fluctuates in a slow, periodic arc around the pitch of the dry signal. The resulting blend of slightly-detuned signals is what the human auditory system interprets as multiple performers playing or singing in unison, each with their own natural micro-timing and intonation imperfections.

The psychoacoustic mechanism at work is the same one that makes a string section sound larger than a single violin or a choir sound fuller than one singer. When two pitches are very close but not identical, they produce amplitude beating at a rate equal to the difference in their frequencies. At slow rates — below about 20 Hz — this beating is perceived not as a separate tone but as a gentle tremolo and a spaciousness that draws the ear in. Chorus exploits this phenomenon in a controllable, repeatable way, giving producers access to an ensemble-thickening effect without requiring a second performance or additional recordings.

Chorus occupies a distinct position in the time-based effects family. It is closely related to flanging and phasing, which also rely on LFO-modulated delay, but the delay times are different enough to produce fundamentally different characters. Flanging uses delays of roughly 1–15 ms, creating the jet-plane comb-filtering sweep associated with classic rock drums and guitars. Phasing uses all-pass filters rather than delay lines to shift phase relationships, producing a more subtle, hollow shimmer. Chorus sits in the middle ground — long enough to avoid pronounced comb filtering but short enough that the copies remain tightly locked in rhythmic feel, preserving intelligibility and articulation.

In modern production, chorus fulfills both corrective and creative roles. On the corrective side, producers apply subtle chorus to thin-sounding monophonic synth leads, narrow room recordings of acoustic instruments, or vocals that lack the fullness of a double-tracked performance. On the creative side, chorus is a defining texture in entire genre palettes: the wide, liquid guitars of 1980s new wave and dream pop; the shimmering pad layers of electronic and ambient music; the lush, slow-evolving synth bass of R&B and neo-soul. Understanding chorus means understanding not just its mechanics, but the specific parameter combinations that generate each of these identifiable sounds.

02 How It Works

At its core, a chorus unit contains four functional blocks: an input buffer, a variable delay line, an LFO, and a wet/dry mixer. The input signal is simultaneously fed into the dry path and into the variable delay line. The LFO — a periodic oscillator running far below the audible range, typically between 0.1 Hz and 5 Hz — continuously modulates the read-head position of the delay line. When the LFO sweeps the delay time longer, the playback pitch drops slightly below the source; when it sweeps shorter, the pitch rises. The delay line's output is then mixed back with the unprocessed dry signal at a ratio determined by the mix or depth control. The result is that constant, gentle undulation of pitch and timing that defines the chorus character.

The key spectral consequence of this architecture is comb filtering. Any time a signal is added to a delayed version of itself, peaks and nulls appear in the frequency response at intervals determined by the delay time. At chorus-range delays of 15–35 ms, these comb notches are spaced closely enough together — roughly every 28–66 Hz across the spectrum — that they affect nearly every harmonic of a musical tone. Because the LFO continuously sweeps the delay time, these notches are also continuously moving, creating that animated, sweeping quality rather than a static coloration. The slower the LFO rate, the more gradual and musical the sweep; the faster the rate, the more pronounced and artificial the modulation becomes, eventually approaching vibrato territory above about 4–5 Hz.

Multi-voice chorus units — found in hardware devices like the Roland Dimension D, the Juno-106's built-in BBD chorus, and modern plugins that emulate them — run two, four, or more parallel delay lines simultaneously, each with its own LFO phase offset. When voices are mixed together with LFOs at 90° or 120° phase relationships, the comb filtering patterns of each voice partially cancel each other's notches, producing a smoother, more even frequency response than a single-voice chorus. This is why multi-voice chorus sounds more like a real ensemble and less like a processed effect: the spectral irregularities average out over time and across voices, much as they do when multiple live players perform together with natural timing and tuning variations.

Bucket-brigade device (BBD) chips — analog charge-coupled delay lines — were the hardware implementation in virtually all classic chorus units of the 1970s and 1980s. BBDs introduce their own sonic character: a gentle high-frequency roll-off caused by the finite bandwidth of the charge-transfer process, a faint noise floor that adds warmth, and a subtle nonlinearity that creates mild saturation on transients. Digital chorus algorithms reproduce the mathematical behavior of LFO-modulated delay precisely but can sound cleaner and thinner without additional processing. Many modern plugins — including Arturia's Chorus DIMENSION-D and Valhalla Chorus — model the specific BBD chip behavior to recapture the analog warmth that producers associate with classic chorus sounds.

Understanding the signal-flow architecture of chorus allows producers to troubleshoot and sculpt the effect with intention. Low mix values (10–30%) with a slow LFO rate produce subtle ensemble width without audible pitch modulation. High mix values (50–100%) with faster rates exaggerate the effect toward vibrato or an obviously processed character. Stereo chorus implementations pan multiple voices across the stereo field — often left and right at opposite LFO phases — generating the wide, immersive spread associated with 1980s pop and synth-wave productions.

03 The Parameters

Every chorus — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these and you can work with any implementation.

RATE
LFO speed — how fast the pitch modulation cycles

Controls the frequency of the LFO that sweeps the delay time, measured in Hz. Values between 0.2–0.8 Hz produce slow, ensemble-like undulation; 1–3 Hz generates an obvious, more lush effect; above 4 Hz approaches vibrato. On vocals, 0.3–0.6 Hz is the sweet spot for transparent doubling without audible wobble.

DEPTH
LFO modulation amount — how wide the pitch swing is

Determines how far the LFO sweeps the delay time, directly controlling the magnitude of the pitch deviation. Small depth values (10–30%) keep pitch variation imperceptible, emphasizing width over movement. Large values (70–100%) create pronounced pitch wobble and a more synthetic character. For subtle ensemble effects, keep depth below 40%.

DELAY TIME
Base delay length — the center of the modulation sweep

Sets the resting delay time around which the LFO modulates, typically ranging from 10 ms to 50 ms. Shorter settings (10–20 ms) produce tighter doubling with some comb-filter coloration. Longer settings (25–40 ms) yield a more spacious, slightly pre-echo effect. Values below 10 ms push the character toward flanging.

MIX / WET
Dry/wet blend — proportion of effected to original signal

Controls the balance between the unprocessed dry signal and the delayed, modulated wet copies. For transparent thickening, 25–40% wet is typical. Full wet (100%) — used creatively on insert or as a parallel return — exposes the full sweep and is useful for dramatic effect, but removes the phase anchor of the dry signal and can sound thin in the low end.

VOICES / STAGES
Number of parallel delay lines

Determines how many independently modulated delay lines are summed together. Single-voice chorus sounds clear but can exhibit periodic comb filtering. Two-voice units (like the Roland Dimension D's Mode 1 and 2) with opposing LFO phases produce a fuller, smoother sweep. Four-voice configurations deliver the richest ensemble texture and widest stereo spread, as used by the Juno-106 in its broadest chorus mode.

STEREO SPREAD / PHASE
LFO phase offset between left and right channels

In stereo chorus implementations, the LFO driving the left channel is offset in phase from the right channel — typically by 90° or 180°. A 90° offset creates a continuously rotating stereo image; 180° produces maximum width with a ping-pong feel. Mono-compatible checks are essential: a full 180° offset can cause significant cancellation when summed to mono.

FEEDBACK
Proportion of wet output fed back to delay input

Adding a portion of the delay line's output back to its input increases resonance and creates a more metallic, filtered coloration. Low feedback (5–15%) subtly enriches harmonic content. Higher values (above 30%) push chorus toward a flanging or comb-filtered character and can become self-oscillating at extreme settings on some hardware and software models.

05 History & Origin

The conceptual ancestor of the chorus effect is the natural phenomenon engineers first tried to reproduce in the 1940s and 1950s: the sound of multiple musicians performing simultaneously. Early tape-based attempts involved running a signal through two synchronized tape machines and slightly varying the speed of one using manual pressure on the capstan — a technique known as flanging. By the late 1960s, Abbey Road engineers were using double-tracking with ADT (Automatic Double Tracking), a device designed at the BBC by engineer Ken Townsend in 1966 at John Lennon's request. ADT used a second tape head with a variable speed control to generate an automatically detuned copy of a vocal, and the result is audible across countless Beatles recordings from Revolver onward. This was not strictly chorus — the delay times and modulation methods differed — but ADT established the foundational psychoacoustic logic that chorus would later systematize.

The first purpose-built chorus units appeared in the mid-1970s, enabled by the commercial availability of bucket-brigade device (BBD) chips from companies including Reticon, Panasonic, and later Matsushita. Roland's Roland CE-1 Chorus Ensemble, released in 1976, is widely considered the first dedicated chorus pedal and the direct ancestor of all subsequent BBD-based chorus designs. The CE-1 used two MN3002 BBD chips to create a lush, wide stereo chorus that became foundational to Roland's subsequent synthesizer architecture. When Roland released the Juno-106 polysynth in 1984, its onboard two-mode BBD chorus — built on MN3102/MN3207 chips — became one of the most replicated sounds in all of synthesis history. Mode I provided a subtle, transparent shimmer at roughly 0.5 Hz; Mode II doubled the voice count and introduced a deeper, slightly faster sweep that defined the sound of countless 1980s pop and dance productions.

Perhaps the most influential chorus hardware unit in studio history was the Roland Dimension D (SDD-320), released in 1979. Rather than offering continuously variable controls, the Dimension D provided four pushbutton mode presets combining different LFO rates, depths, and voice counts. Its output was exceptionally smooth — the four-voice architecture with carefully offset LFO phases minimized comb filtering artifacts — and it was embraced by producers and engineers including Roger Nichols, who used it extensively on Steely Dan recordings, and Bob Clearmountain, who applied it to countless rock and pop mixes throughout the 1980s. Chorus became a defining texture of that era's sound: the glassy, wide electric pianos of Toto and Supertramp, the shimmering clean guitar tones of The Police and U2, the expansive pad layers of Depeche Mode and New Order.

Through the 1990s and into the digital era, chorus migrated from hardware units into the software realm. Early digital chorus implementations in devices like the Lexicon PCM70 and Eventide H3000 added algorithmic precision and extended parameter control at the cost of some of the organic warmth that BBD noise and bandwidth limitations had contributed. Plugin developers in the early 2000s began modeling the specific nonlinear behavior of BBD chips, and by the 2010s emulation fidelity had reached a point where tools like Arturia's Chorus DIMENSION-D, Softube's Chorus, and Valhalla Chorus offered near-indistinguishable recreations of the classic hardware timbres. Today, chorus remains one of the most widely used effects in music production, deeply embedded in the sonic identity of dream pop, shoegaze, vaporwave, nu-disco, synthwave, and modern R&B.

06 How Producers Use It

Electric guitar and bass: Chorus is woven into the DNA of clean electric guitar in pop and rock production. Producers typically place chorus on a dedicated insert or as a parallel send at 30–45% wet, using a two-voice stereo setting to push a mono DI or close-miked amp recording into a wider plane. For bass guitar, chorus must be used carefully: below 150–200 Hz, the pitch modulation creates low-end instability and can cause kick-bass pumping issues in the mix. A high-pass filter on the chorus return at around 150–200 Hz, or a parallel chorus path blended only into the mid-range and upper harmonics, preserves the low-end solidity while adding shimmer to the upper register. Iconic examples include the clean guitar tones on The Police's "Every Breath You Take," recorded by Hugh Padgham with a Roland CE-1 in the signal chain.

Synthesizers and pads: Chorus is the primary tool for transforming a monophonic or thin-sounding synth patch into a lush, wide sonic object. On Roland Juno-style leads and pads, the onboard chorus is often engaged at the preset level before the signal even leaves the synthesizer. In the box, producers building from oscillators typically reach for a slow-rate (0.2–0.5 Hz), moderate-depth (25–40%) chorus on pad layers before the reverb insert, allowing the modulation to animate the sustain portion of pads without muddying the attack. Stacking two chorus instances on the same synth track — one with a very slow rate and minimal depth for width, a second with a slightly faster rate for movement — produces the layered ensemble character that characterizes modern synthwave and cinematic productions.

Vocals: Chorus on lead vocals is a stylistic choice that divides engineers sharply. When applied at low wet levels (20–30%) with a slow rate and minimal depth, chorus functions similarly to ADT, thickening a single take without the cost of booking another session. This approach is common in lo-fi, dream pop, and shoegaze production, where Cocteau Twins engineer Ray Shulman used extreme chorus and reverb on Elizabeth Fraser's voice to create a signature ethereal texture throughout the 1980s. In contemporary pop and R&B, lead vocal chorus is typically reserved for special sections — a pre-chorus build or an atmospheric breakdown — with automation pulling the effect in and out. On backing vocals, moderate chorus at 35–50% wet can glue a stack of doubles into a cohesive ensemble without requiring the precision tuning work that natural doubles demand.

Mix bus and parallel applications: Applying chorus on the mix bus is a technique associated with certain 1980s and early 1990s mixing styles, where engineers printed mixes through hardware chorus units at very low wet levels (10–20%) to add subtle movement and width to a finished mix. In modern practice this is a controversial approach — the pitch modulation can destabilize tuning-sensitive elements across the entire mix — and is more safely approximated by applying very slow, low-depth chorus to specific stereo group busses (synths, guitars, backing vocals) rather than to the 2-bus. A safer alternative for bus use is a multi-voice chorus at maximum voices with minimal depth, functioning essentially as a stereo widener with gentle movement rather than a pronounced modulation effect.

AbletonChorus-Ensemble (Live 11+) replaces the older Chorus device and offers Clean, Analog, and Vintage circuit modes. Use the Vintage mode for BBD-style warmth; engage the Stereo switch and set LFO Phase to 90° for the widest image. The Ensemble mode (four voices) is ideal for pads and synths. Use on a return track with a high-pass filter before it to protect low-end elements.
FL StudioChorus is included in the stock Parametric EQ 2 or as a standalone Chorus plugin in the native effects menu. More capable results come from the Fruity Flangus plugin set to longer delay times and low LFO rates, or from the third-party Cableguys Shaperbox Chorus, which many FL producers prefer for its modulation flexibility. For classic Juno tones, the free TAL-Chorus-LX VST integrates cleanly into FL's mixer.
Logic ProLogic's stock Chorus plugin is straightforward and clean, with Intensity (depth), Rate, and Mix controls. For more character, Ensemble — also bundled with Logic — combines multiple chorus voices with a warmer analog flavor and is better suited for lush pad and string textures. The Modulation Delay plugin in Logic can also be configured as a custom chorus by setting delay time to 15–30 ms and enabling LFO modulation.
Pro ToolsPro Tools ships with the Avid Chorus AAX plugin as part of the standard bundle, offering basic rate, depth, and mix controls with mono-to-stereo processing. For professional sessions, most engineers reach for third-party options: Waves MondoMod, Soundtoys MicroShift (for ADT-style doubling), or iZotope Nectar's modulation module on vocals. When using AAX Chorus on a mono channel, always instantiate it as a mono-to-stereo insert to recover stereo width.
ReaperReaper bundles the ReaChorus JS plugin, which is functional and low-latency but basic. Most Reaper users install free VSTs such as TAL-Chorus-LX (a Juno-60 BBD emulation, free) or mda Chorus for transparent doubling. Because Reaper supports JS scripting, advanced users can build custom multi-voice chorus chains using the JS effects framework with the ReaDelay JS as the base delay line modulated by the JS LFO source.

07 In the Wild

Abstract knowledge becomes practical when you can hear it in music you know. These tracks demonstrate chorus used intentionally, at specific moments, for specific purposes.

The Police — "Every Breath You Take" (1983)
0:00 intro guitar / Throughout · Produced by Hugh Padgham

Andy Summers's iconic clean Telecaster tone through an Roland CE-1 Chorus Ensemble is one of the most recognized chorus applications in pop history. Listen for the slow, smooth LFO sweep (approximately 0.4 Hz) that gives the guitar a slightly liquid quality without obvious pitch wobble. The effect is at roughly 40% wet, preserving the pick attack of the dry signal while adding ensemble width. The mono-compatible design of the CE-1 means the chorus survives well on AM radio — a key engineering consideration of the era.

Cocteau Twins — "Lorelei" (1984)
0:00–1:20 opening / Throughout · Produced by Robin Guthrie

Robin Guthrie applied deep chorus and reverb processing to Elizabeth Fraser's voice as a compositional texture rather than a corrective tool. The chorus is used at very high wet levels on the doubled vocal stack, creating a shimmering, pitch-uncertain quality that contributes to the band's famously ethereal and linguistically abstract sound. Rate is relatively fast at approximately 1–1.5 Hz, producing audible pitch modulation that blurs the boundary between the voice and the surrounding reverb wash.

Daft Punk — "Too Long" (2001)
1:05 synth lead enters / 3:30 onward · Produced by Daft Punk

The central analog-style synth lead on this Discovery deep cut exhibits classic Roland Juno-style four-voice BBD chorus. The slow LFO rate (approximately 0.3 Hz) and deep stereo spread create a wide, almost hypnotic movement in the sound. Pay attention to how the chorus is automated to increase in depth as the track progresses — the modulation becomes more pronounced in the extended outro, functioning as an arrangement tool that builds intensity without adding new elements. A textbook example of chorus as compositional dynamic.

Toto — "Africa" (1982)
0:17 electric piano entrance · Produced by David Paich and Steve Lukather

David Paich's electric piano sound is a direct product of the Roland Dimension D in Mode 2, applied to a Rhodes or a Yamaha CP70 electric grand. The four-voice chorus at this mode setting produces an extremely smooth, wide shimmer with no pronounced LFO sweep artifacts — a hallmark of the Dimension D's architecture. Compare carefully to a dry electric piano recording: the chorus adds approximately 2–3 dB of perceived width and a continuous, gentle spectral animation that makes the piano sit forward in the mix without level increases.

Mazzy Star — "Fade Into You" (1993)
0:00 guitar intro / 1:10 vocal entrance · Produced by David Roback

The clean guitar tone throughout this recording uses chorus at a low wet level (estimated 25–35%) with a very slow rate (approximately 0.2 Hz), producing a barely perceptible shimmer that contributes to the song's warm, dreamy intimacy without telegraphing the effect. Hope Sandoval's vocal has a subtle chorus applied that increases during the chorus sections (the song's chorus, not the effect), demonstrating the classic automation technique of reserving additional modulation depth for emotional peaks. The sum of these restrained applications creates a unified textural palette.

08 Types & Variants

BBD Analog Chorus
Roland CE-1 Chorus Ensemble · Roland Dimension D (SDD-320) · Boss CE-2 · Roland Juno-106 onboard

The original circuit topology: bucket-brigade device delay lines modulated by analog LFOs, with characteristic high-frequency roll-off, a gentle noise floor, and mild saturation on transients. BBD chorus has an organic warmth that digital implementations have spent decades trying to replicate. The Roland Dimension D's four-voice multi-phase architecture remains the gold standard for smooth, studio-quality analog chorus, and its emulations — particularly Arturia's Chorus DIMENSION-D — are among the most-used chorus plugins in professional sessions.

Digital / Algorithmic Chorus
Lexicon PCM70 · Eventide H3000 · TC Electronic Chorus+Pitch

Digital implementations use DSP-based delay lines and precisely computed LFO mathematics, offering greater parameter control and zero noise floor compared to BBD designs. The clarity can be an advantage on high-resolution digital mixes but may sound sterile on warmer analog-recorded material. The Eventide H3000's chorus algorithms added pitch-shifting components alongside the standard delay modulation, creating a richer ensemble effect that became popular on drums and orchestral sounds in late-1980s sessions.

Multi-Voice Ensemble Chorus
Roland Dimension D · Solina String Ensemble · Korg Lambda

Uses four or more independently modulated delay lines, each with staggered LFO phases, to simulate a large ensemble of players. The multiple voices average out comb-filter artifacts and produce a much smoother, more even frequency response than single- or two-voice designs. This is the type most associated with string ensemble and pad sounds in 1970s and 1980s electric keyboards, and remains the preferred setting for lush synth pads and layered background vocals.

Vibrato Mode (Wet-Only Chorus)
Roland CE-1 Vibrato mode · Univox Uni-Vibe

When the dry signal is removed and only the LFO-modulated wet copy is output, chorus becomes vibrato — continuous pitch modulation without the harmonic beating produced by mixing dry and wet together. The Uni-Vibe, designed by Fumio Miida and popularized by Jimi Hendrix on songs including "Machine Gun," used four photocell-based all-pass stages to produce a unique vibrato/chorus hybrid with a pulsing, almost respiratory quality. Used on guitar, keys, and occasionally vocals for expressive pitch animation.

ADT / Doubling Chorus
EMI ADT machine · AMS DMX 15-80 · Cooper Time Cube

A specialized chorus configuration using very short, minimally modulated delay times (8–20 ms) with low LFO depth to simulate manual double-tracking rather than a full ensemble. The effect thickens the signal without obvious pitch wobble, making it the most transparent chorus variant for lead vocals and solo instruments. The Soundtoys MicroShift and Waves ADT plugins are modern software implementations of this approach, used extensively in contemporary pop vocal production.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    Applying chorus below 150 Hz on bass instruments — low-end pitch instability
    Chorus applied full-range to bass guitar or bass synths modulates the sub and low-bass frequencies, creating pitch beating that destabilizes the harmonic foundation of the mix. This manifests as a wavy, unfocused low end that makes bass and kick drum feel disconnected. Fix it by high-passing the chorus return at 150–200 Hz, or using a parallel chorus that only blends into the upper harmonics of the bass signal, keeping the fundamental clean and phase-coherent.
  • ✕
    Using 100% wet on a lead element — loss of phase anchor
    Setting chorus to fully wet removes the unmodulated dry signal entirely, which eliminates the phase anchor that makes the pitch-beating effect work and often makes the element sound thin or distant rather than wide. The ensemble illusion depends on the contrast between the fixed dry signal and the moving wet copy. Reduce mix to 30–50% for most applications; reserve 100% wet for sound-design contexts or when chorus is deliberately used as a vibrato-style effect.
  • ✕
    Ignoring mono compatibility — phase cancellation on summing
    Stereo chorus running at 90° or 180° LFO phase offset between channels can produce significant cancellation when the stereo output is summed to mono, as occurs in club sound systems, phone speakers, and many streaming playback scenarios. Check every chorused element in mono before printing. Reduce stereo spread if cancellation is severe, or use a chorus type with built-in mono compatibility like the Dimension D emulation modes that are specifically designed to remain coherent in mono.
  • ✕
    Overusing LFO rate — turning ensemble thickening into obvious modulation
    Setting LFO rate above 2 Hz on sustained sources causes the pitch wobble to become perceptible as an expressive ornament rather than a transparent doubling effect. On vocals, this reads as unintentional vibrato. On pads, it creates a seasick, unstable quality. Unless deliberate vibrato is the intent, keep rate below 1 Hz for transparent width and below 0.5 Hz for completely imperceptible modulation. A/B frequently against the unprocessed signal to detect when modulation has crossed from enhancement into artifact.
  • ✕
    Stacking chorus on chorus without differentiation — muddying the texture
    Adding multiple chorus instances at similar rate and depth settings on the same element creates complex, clashing LFO interactions that produce an unfocused, washy result rather than a clearer ensemble effect. If layering chorus instances, differentiate them significantly: one at 0.2 Hz and one at 0.8 Hz, or one with minimal depth for width and one at higher depth on a narrow-band parallel path. This stagger produces a richer, more natural-sounding texture than identical settings stacked.
  • ✕
    Applying chorus before distortion or saturation — amplifying the artifacts
    Placing chorus upstream of distortion or saturation in a signal chain feeds the pitch-modulated, comb-filtered signal into the nonlinear processor, which then amplifies and harmonically multiplies the artifacts of the chorus. The result is an unstable, intermodulation-heavy distortion character that is usually undesirable. Chorus almost always belongs after saturation in the signal chain unless the specific interaction sound is intentional — as in some experimental shoegaze guitar tones where the effect is sought deliberately.

Use a multiband or parallel approach: send only the high-passed bass signal (HP filter at 150–200 Hz) to the chorus, then blend the chorused return with the unprocessed full-range signal. This adds shimmer and movement to the upper harmonics — which create the tonal character of the bass — without applying pitch modulation to the sub frequencies that provide weight and punch.
The Juno-106's chorus used MN3207 BBD chips at two modes: Mode I (single voice, approximately 0.5 Hz rate, moderate depth) and Mode II (dual voice, slightly faster and deeper). To emulate it, use TAL-Chorus-LX (free) or Arturia's Juno-60 V / Chorus DIMENSION-D (paid). Key settings: sine LFO at 0.4–0.6 Hz, 20–35% depth, delay centered around 20–25 ms, stereo spread at 90°. The BBD high-frequency roll-off is essential to the character — if your plugin has a circuit mode or BBD mode, engage it.
Technically yes, but with strong caveats. Very subtle chorus on the bus — 10–15% wet, 0.1–0.2 Hz rate, minimum depth — adds a gentle movement and spread that can glue a mix and enhance perceived width. However, it introduces pitch modulation across every element simultaneously, which can destabilize tuning-critical elements like sustained synth chords and piano. A safer alternative is applying gentle chorus to specific group busses (synths, guitars) rather than the 2-bus, or using a purpose-built stereo widener that uses the same principles with better mono-compatibility engineering.
