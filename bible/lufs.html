LUFS

/luːfs/

Quick Answer Definition How It Works Parameters Quick Reference History How To Use It In The Wild Types Mistakes Calculator Related Further Reading FAQ

LUFS (Loudness Units Full Scale) is a standardized unit for measuring perceived loudness in audio, accounting for how humans hear different frequencies. Streaming platforms use LUFS targets to normalize playback volume across all content.

01 Definition

Every mix you've ever over-limited, every master you've crushed chasing volume that streaming platforms immediately turned down anyway — LUFS is the number that was silently judging you the whole time.

LUFS stands for Loudness Units Full Scale, a unit of measurement that quantifies the perceived loudness of an audio signal as experienced by human listeners rather than as raw electrical amplitude. Unlike peak meters, which respond to instantaneous signal peaks that the ear often cannot perceive as loudness differences, LUFS meters apply psychoacoustic weighting — specifically the ITU-R BS.1770 K-weighting filter — to model how the human auditory system responds to different frequencies over time. The result is a single number, expressed in negative values relative to digital full scale (0 dBFS), that tracks how loud your audio actually sounds rather than how loud it momentarily is.

The distinction between LUFS and peak-based measurements is not academic. Two recordings can share identical peak levels — say, −1 dBFS true peak — while differing by 6 LU or more in integrated loudness, depending on their dynamic range, spectral content, and temporal density. A sparse folk guitar track and a heavily compressed EDM drop can both read −1 dBFS on a peak meter, yet the EDM track will sound dramatically louder on every playback system. LUFS captures that perceptual gap. Because it accounts for duration (integrated LUFS averages over an entire program), frequency weighting (bass-heavy content is partially corrected), and gating (silence and near-silence periods are excluded from the average), it provides a measurement that correlates reliably with listener fatigue, platform normalization behavior, and competitive loudness in any genre.

The term originates in the EBU R128 broadcast standard published in 2010 and the simultaneously developed ITU-R BS.1770 algorithm. Before this standardization, the industry operated in a fragmented landscape of VU meters, PPM meters, and RMS measurements, none of which agreed with one another or with listener perception. Broadcast engineers were the first to suffer the consequences: television commercials were routinely 10–12 dB louder than program content, triggering audience complaints worldwide. EBU R128 imposed a target of −23 LUFS for broadcast, and the algorithm it codified — ITU-R BS.1770 — became the technical backbone of every streaming platform's normalization system that followed. Spotify, Apple Music, YouTube, Tidal, and Amazon Music all use variants of this algorithm to measure your master before playback and apply gain accordingly.

For producers and mastering engineers, LUFS is the governing metric of the modern delivery workflow. Understanding it means understanding three related but distinct measurements: Integrated LUFS (the average over a full program, gated to exclude silence), Short-Term LUFS (a 3-second sliding window useful for monitoring section-by-section loudness), and Momentary LUFS (a 400ms window that tracks transient loudness behavior). True Peak, technically separate but always reported alongside LUFS in compliant meters, measures the inter-sample peak reconstruction value that digital-to-analog converters will produce — a value that can exceed 0 dBFS even when the digital sample values do not, causing clipping on playback hardware. Mastering to LUFS targets without monitoring true peak is an incomplete workflow; both values must be managed simultaneously.

02 How It Works

The ITU-R BS.1770 algorithm that underlies all LUFS measurement operates in three sequential stages. First, a K-weighting filter is applied to the audio signal. K-weighting is a two-stage filter: a high-shelf pre-filter boosting frequencies above approximately 1.6 kHz by +4 dB (modeling the acoustic effect of the human head on sound reaching the ear) followed by a high-pass filter rolling off below 60 Hz at −3 dB per octave (reducing the perceptual weight of sub-bass energy, which listeners do not perceive as loudness as strongly as midrange energy). This filtered signal more closely approximates how the auditory system weights different frequency regions when judging loudness.

Second, the mean square value of the K-weighted signal is calculated — effectively an energy average — across each audio channel. For multichannel content, channels are summed with specific weighting coefficients: left, right, center, and back channels each carry a coefficient of 1.0, while LFE (subwoofer) channels carry a coefficient of 0 and are excluded from the loudness calculation entirely. The per-channel mean square values are then summed and converted to a logarithmic (decibel) scale, yielding the loudness in Loudness Units relative to Full Scale. Third, for Integrated LUFS specifically, a two-stage gating process is applied. An absolute gate at −70 LUFS excludes digital silence and near-silence from the average. A relative gate then excludes any 400ms block that falls more than 10 LU below the ungated average — this prevents quiet passages like intro silence or fade-outs from artificially dragging down the integrated loudness reading for a program that is loud in its active sections.

Short-Term LUFS uses a 3-second rectangular integration window without gating, updated every 100ms. This makes it ideal for real-time monitoring of verses, choruses, and drops during a mix session — you can see immediately if your chorus is 3 LU louder than your verse, which is a useful dynamic shaping tool independent of mastering targets. Momentary LUFS uses a 400ms window, also updated every 100ms, and is the fastest-responding measurement in a compliant meter. It tracks the perceptual loudness of individual hits, drum fills, and vocal lines in near real time, providing a bridge between the transient-sensitivity of peak meters and the program-sensitivity of integrated measurements.

True Peak measurement, while not technically a LUFS value, is inseparable from compliant loudness metering. Digital audio is stored as discrete samples, but playback hardware reconstructs a continuous waveform through digital-to-analog conversion. During this reconstruction, the interpolated waveform can reach amplitudes higher than any stored sample value — a phenomenon called inter-sample peaking. ITU-R BS.1775 and EBU R128 require true peak meters to oversample the signal by at least 4× before measuring peaks, ensuring these reconstructed excursions are captured. A master that reads −1.0 dBFS on a standard peak meter may have true peaks of +0.3 dBTP or higher, which will clip on playback. Streaming platforms typically specify a true peak ceiling of −1.0 dBTP or −2.0 dBTP to provide reconstruction headroom.

Together, these mechanisms make LUFS the most technically complete loudness measurement system available in standard production practice. The algorithm correlates well — though not perfectly — with equal-loudness contours (ISO 226), and ongoing revisions to the standard (BS.1770-4, current as of this writing) have refined the gating and multichannel weighting to improve accuracy across diverse program material. For music producers, the practical takeaway is that LUFS rewards dynamic range: a mix with natural peaks and valleys reads its integrated LUFS primarily from its loud sections, meaning dynamic mixes can achieve competitive streaming loudness without the loudness penalty that forces static, over-compressed material to be turned down uniformly.

03 The Parameters

Every lufs — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these and you can work with any implementation.

INTEGRATED LUFS
Full-program loudness average with gating

The primary delivery metric: the mean loudness of an entire track, measured from start to finish with a two-stage gate that excludes silence (absolute gate: −70 LUFS) and quiet passages more than 10 LU below the ungated average. Streaming platforms measure your master's Integrated LUFS on ingest and apply a gain offset to match their normalization target — Spotify targets −14 LUFS, Apple Music −16 LUFS, YouTube −14 LUFS. A master reading −8 LUFS integrated will be turned down by 6 dB on Spotify; it will not be turned up if it's already quiet.

SHORT-TERM LUFS
3-second sliding loudness window

Measures the loudness of any 3-second window, updated every 100ms, without gating. Short-Term LUFS is the most practical monitoring view during mixing and mastering sessions, revealing dynamic contrast between song sections — a well-mastered commercial track typically shows 3–8 LU of difference between verse and chorus Short-Term readings. If your chorus Short-Term reads only 1–2 LU louder than your verse, your arrangement lacks contrast regardless of Integrated target compliance.

MOMENTARY LUFS
400ms instantaneous loudness window

The fastest LUFS measurement, using a 400ms rectangular window updated every 100ms. Momentary LUFS tracks individual drum hits, vocal phrases, and transient events with enough speed to be diagnostically useful without the peak-chasing noise of a true instantaneous meter. A kick drum hit on a well-mastered record may momentarily read 6–10 LU above the Short-Term reading; if that momentary excursion is absent, your low end may be over-compressed or the transient has been removed by limiting.

LOUDNESS RANGE (LRA)
Statistical spread of loudness across the program

LRA, measured in LU, describes the difference between the 10th and 95th percentile Short-Term loudness values across the program — effectively the statistical dynamic range. An uncompressed orchestral recording may have an LRA of 20+ LU; a heavily limited modern pop master typically falls in the 4–8 LU range; a maximized EDM track may compress LRA to 2–4 LU. EBU R128 recommends broadcast material not exceed an LRA that results in listener discomfort, but for music delivery the target varies by genre. Low LRA correlates with listener fatigue on extended listening.

TRUE PEAK (dBTP)
Inter-sample peak ceiling for playback-safe delivery

True Peak measures the peak amplitude of the reconstructed analog waveform after oversampled interpolation (minimum 4× oversample per ITU-R BS.1775), capturing inter-sample peaks that standard peak meters miss. A signal reading −0.3 dBFS on a standard peak meter can easily produce true peaks of +0.2 dBTP or higher, causing clipping on DAC hardware and codec distortion during MP3/AAC encoding. Streaming platforms specify maximum true peak ceilings: Spotify and Apple Music recommend −1.0 dBTP; YouTube and Tidal recommend −1.0 dBTP; broadcast EBU R128 specifies −1.0 dBTP. Target −1.0 dBTP minimum, −2.0 dBTP for material with complex transient content.

K-WEIGHTING
Psychoacoustic frequency weighting applied before measurement

The filtering stage applied to audio before loudness calculation: a high-shelf filter (+4 dB above ~1.6 kHz, modeling the ear–head acoustic transfer function) combined with a high-pass filter (−3 dB/octave below 60 Hz, reducing the perceptual weight of deep bass). K-weighting means bass-heavy mixes with heavy sub content will not measure as loud as their raw energy suggests — a track with 12 dB of 40Hz content relative to its mids may measure 2–3 LUFS quieter than a purely midrange-weighted track at the same RMS level. This is by design: the human ear is genuinely less sensitive to low bass in loudness perception.

05 History & Origin

The practical problem that LUFS was designed to solve had plagued broadcasters since the 1950s: audio content produced in different studios at different times varied wildly in perceived loudness, and listeners were compelled to constantly adjust their volume controls. VU meters, standardized jointly by the American Standards Association and the EBU in the late 1930s and 1940s, attempted to bridge peak measurement and perceived loudness by using a 300ms ballistic integration time — fast enough to catch transients but slow enough to suggest program energy. But VU meters were a compromise that satisfied neither use case, and by the arrival of digital audio in the 1980s, the loudness problem had intensified. Digital audio's clean headroom enabled aggressive peak-based maximization that was technically legal (below 0 dBFS) yet perceptually obnoxious, and the Loudness War — competitive over-limiting in commercial music mastering — became the defining pathology of the CD era.

The intellectual foundation for LUFS came from psychoacoustics research spanning decades. Harvey Fletcher and Wilden Munson's 1933 equal-loudness contour measurements, refined by Robinson and Dadson in 1956 and codified as ISO 226 in 2003, established the scientific basis for frequency-weighted loudness measurement. Bob Katz's K-System, proposed in his 2000 AES paper and developed in his landmark book Mastering Audio (2002, Focal Press), brought these ideas into mastering practice by proposing a reference-level framework tied to monitor calibration and RMS measurement. Katz's K-System used three reference levels (K-20, K-14, K-12) corresponding to different dynamic range requirements, and while it never achieved regulatory adoption, it shifted the professional mastering conversation toward loudness measurement and away from pure peak chasing.

The formal standard emerged from the ITU-R Study Group 6 process in the mid-2000s. Researchers including Gilbert Soulodre and Michel Lavoie of the Communications Research Centre Canada published critical work on perceptual loudness models in the early 2000s, and Florian Camerer of the Austrian Broadcasting Corporation (ORF) was instrumental in translating the research into a practical broadcast implementation. ITU-R BS.1770 was published in 2006, specifying K-weighting, mean-square energy summation, multichannel coefficients, and the gating algorithm. The European Broadcasting Union simultaneously developed EBU R128, ratified in 2010, which adopted BS.1770's algorithm, specified −23 LUFS as the European broadcast loudness target, introduced the Loudness Range metric, and mandated true peak measurement. The TC Electronic LM2 hardware loudness meter, released in 2010, was among the first professional hardware implementations of the complete EBU R128 specification and became the reference standard for broadcast quality control rooms.

The transition from broadcast to music streaming began when Spotify introduced loudness normalization in 2013, initially targeting approximately −11 LUFS before revising to −14 LUFS in 2017 following listener research. Apple's Sound Check feature, which predates streaming normalization in its iTunes-era form, was retuned to −16 LUFS for Apple Music. YouTube's Content ID normalization system, implemented progressively from 2013, settled at −14 LUFS. These decisions — made by engineering teams at technology companies, not by the music industry — effectively transferred control of competitive loudness from mastering engineers to algorithm designers, ending the Loudness War for streaming delivery in a way that fifteen years of industry debate had failed to accomplish. The mastering profession responded by developing the multi-target delivery workflow that is now standard practice: separate masters rendered at different LUFS targets for streaming, broadcast, and download contexts, all deriving from a single dynamic mix.

06 How Producers Use It

In a mixing session, LUFS meters serve a fundamentally different purpose than in mastering. During mixing, Integrated LUFS is largely irrelevant — you're making relative decisions about level balance, not absolute delivery decisions. What matters in mixing is Short-Term LUFS as a cross-section comparison tool: load a reference track into your session, set its gain so it reads the same Short-Term LUFS as your mix in the loudest section, and then A/B them. This reference comparison approach, using LUFS as the common loudness currency, is far more reliable than simple fader-level matching because it accounts for spectral and dynamic differences between your mix and the reference. Most professional mix engineers target a mix bus peak level of −6 dBFS or lower before the master limiter, preserving headroom for the mastering stage to apply limiting without the signal arriving pre-clipped.

In mastering, Integrated LUFS becomes the primary delivery specification. The workflow is: apply EQ, multiband processing, and stereo enhancement on the mastering chain, then bring the limiter's threshold down until the Integrated LUFS reads at or just below the target (−14 LUFS for Spotify/YouTube, −16 LUFS for Apple Music, −16 to −18 LUFS for Tidal's high-quality tier). Simultaneously, watch the True Peak reading and ensure it does not exceed −1.0 dBTP. The critical mistake to avoid is over-limiting to hit the target: if your limiter is reducing gain by more than 3–4 dB on average to reach −14 LUFS, the dynamics are being compromised. A better approach is to increase the pre-limiter gain until the signal naturally arrives closer to the target, requiring less limiting gain reduction. This is the core gain staging principle of loudness-normalized mastering.

For stem and stem-master workflows, Short-Term LUFS comparisons between stems provide an objective way to balance stem levels before committing to a final mix-down. A vocal stem that reads 3 LUFS louder than the instrumental stem in the Short-Term meter during the chorus is objectively dominant — whether that's the right artistic choice is a separate question, but the measurement removes the ambiguity of subjective level perception in acoustically untreated spaces. This application of LUFS as a mixing diagnostic tool rather than a delivery compliance tool is underused by producers working below the professional mastering tier.

In content creation and post-production contexts — scoring for games, producing sync-ready music, delivering for broadcast — the target shifts dramatically. EBU R128 broadcast spec of −23 LUFS is 9 LU quieter than Spotify's normalization target, meaning music mixed and mastered for streaming will sound far too loud in a broadcast mix. Producers working across delivery formats must either maintain separate masters for each format or use a LUFS-aware mastering chain that can quickly re-render at different targets without re-doing the creative work. Most professional mastering engineers maintain at least three render configurations: streaming (−14 LUFS, −1 dBTP), broadcast (−23 LUFS, −1 dBTP), and CD/download (−12 to −10 LUFS, −0.3 dBFS peak, for physical and download contexts where normalization is not guaranteed).

07 In the Wild

Abstract knowledge becomes practical when you can hear it in music you know. These tracks demonstrate lufs used intentionally, at specific moments, for specific purposes.

Billie Eilish — "bad guy" (2019)
0:00–3:14 full track · Produced by Finneas O'Connell

One of the most-cited examples in loudness normalization discourse, 'bad guy' was mastered to approximately −14 LUFS integrated, sitting precisely at Spotify's normalization target and receiving zero gain offset on playback. Finneas's production relies on dramatic dynamic contrast: the verses sit around −20 to −22 LUFS Short-Term, while the drop sections peak near −10 LUFS Short-Term, creating a perceived 10–12 LU dynamic swing that sounds enormous through normalization because the quiet sections read as genuinely quiet, not just relatively quiet. The mix demonstrates that hitting the streaming target does not require sacrifice of dynamic range — it requires designing dynamics into the arrangement itself rather than relying on post-production limiting to manufacture loudness.

Kendrick Lamar — "HUMBLE." (2017)
0:00–2:57 full track · Produced by Mike WiLL Made-It

"HUMBLE." measures approximately −8 to −9 LUFS integrated, well above Spotify's −14 LUFS normalization target, meaning it is turned down by 5–6 dB on streaming playback relative to a compliant master. Despite this, the track retains significant perceived impact because the limiting is applied with surgical precision: the 808 sub has been shaped and limited in a way that preserves its punch through the gain reduction. Listen at 0:03 when the kick enters — on a LUFS meter, the Momentary reading spikes 8–10 LU above the Short-Term reading for that single hit, revealing that the transients are preserved even in a heavily limited master. A lesson in understanding that competitive loudness and normalization compliance are two separate engineering goals.

Daft Punk — "Get Lucky" (2013)
0:00–6:09 · Produced by Daft Punk and Nile Rodgers

Released before major streaming platforms adopted loudness normalization targets, 'Get Lucky' was mastered by Darcy Proper at a moderate −12 to −13 LUFS integrated — exceptionally dynamic by 2013 commercial standards, at a time when many pop masters were pushed to −7 or −8 LUFS. Monitor the Short-Term LUFS through the instrumental sections: the interplay between the live drums, Nile Rodgers's rhythm guitar, and the synth bass creates natural Short-Term LUFS fluctuations of 3–5 LU within individual bars, which is the sonic signature of program material that has not been over-limited. The LRA of the track is estimated at 9–11 LU — notably wide for a commercial release of its era — and its translation to streaming normalization environments was seamless, requiring almost no gain offset and losing none of its dynamic character.

Taylor Swift — "Anti-Hero" (2022)
0:00–3:21 full track · Produced by Jack Antonoff

Mastered by Randy Merrill to approximately −9 LUFS integrated, 'Anti-Hero' is a modern example of a commercially successful track that relies on streaming normalization gain reduction rather than dynamic range to compete on playlists. On Apple Music (−16 LUFS target), the track receives approximately 7 dB of gain reduction on playback. On Spotify (−14 LUFS target), it receives approximately 5 dB of reduction. Despite the heavy normalization offset, the production retains its clarity because the mix's frequency balance and stereo image are designed to translate through the level reduction. Note the chorus Short-Term LUFS: it peaks near −7 LUFS Short-Term on the loudest iteration, reflecting the maximized-limiter approach to the final master that was characteristic of the genre in 2022.

08 Types & Variants

Integrated LUFS Metering
TC Electronic LM2 · Distopik MasterCheck

The primary delivery measurement, averaging loudness across the entire program with gating. Used to confirm compliance with streaming and broadcast delivery specifications at the end of a mastering session. Integrated LUFS is the number printed on a delivery spec sheet and the value streaming platforms measure on ingest — it is the final authority on whether a master will be normalized up, down, or not at all on a given platform.

Short-Term LUFS Metering
Nugen VisLM · iZotope Insight 2

A 3-second loudness window used for real-time section monitoring during mixing and mastering. Short-Term LUFS is the producer's most useful in-session LUFS view: it reveals the loudness of verses, choruses, and drops in isolation, enabling dynamic shaping decisions that the slower Integrated measurement obscures. A Short-Term meter running during a mix session is as important as a peak meter — arguably more so for capturing perceived dynamic contrast.

Momentary LUFS Metering
Waves WLM Plus · Youlean Loudness Meter 2

The 400ms window measurement that bridges LUFS and peak metering in transient responsiveness. Momentary LUFS is useful for diagnosing over-compression: if your Momentary LUFS barely deviates from your Short-Term reading during drum hits and vocal onsets, your transients have been suppressed by limiting or heavy compression. Healthy transients should produce 6–10 LU momentary excursions above the Short-Term baseline in well-produced music.

True Peak Metering
TC Electronic LM6 Mk2 · Sonnox Oxford Limiter v2

Technically distinct from LUFS measurement but inseparable in professional delivery workflows, true peak meters oversample the audio signal to reconstruct the inter-sample peaks that occur during digital-to-analog conversion and codec processing. Delivery without true peak monitoring risks clipping on playback hardware and codec distortion — MP3 and AAC encoding are particularly prone to inter-sample peak amplification, making −1.0 to −2.0 dBTP true peak ceilings a non-negotiable specification for streaming delivery.

Loudness Range (LRA) Analysis
Nugen VisLM · Orban Loudness Meter

LRA quantifies the statistical dynamic range of a program in Loudness Units, calculated from the spread between the 10th and 95th percentile Short-Term values. LRA is the mastering engineer's primary tool for assessing whether a program's dynamics are appropriate for its context: broadcast specs suggest LRA values that avoid listener fatigue, while competitive EDM mastering often accepts LRA values of 2–4 LU as genre-appropriate. LRA values below 3 LU typically indicate over-limiting regardless of genre.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    Chasing peak LUFS rather than Integrated LUFS for delivery
    Newer producers often confuse Short-Term or Momentary LUFS readings with the Integrated value that streaming platforms measure. A track whose chorus peaks at −10 LUFS Short-Term may integrate to −14 LUFS easily across the full program, but a producer watching the wrong meter readout and limiting harder to push Short-Term peaks lower is destroying transient information unnecessarily. Always switch to Integrated view for final delivery compliance checks, and understand that quieter sections actively help you hit louder Integrated targets by pulling the weighted average toward your threshold.
  • ✕
    Over-limiting a mix to hit a LUFS target, then getting turned down anyway
    The most common misunderstanding of streaming normalization: producers apply heavy limiting to push their master to −8 or −9 LUFS integrated, believing louder masters will sound louder on streaming platforms. Spotify will apply −5 to −6 dB of gain reduction on playback, and the surviving audio is a limited, distorted, dynamically crushed version of their mix — quieter and worse-sounding than a master targeted at −14 LUFS with 3–4 dB less limiting. The fix is to understand that on normalized platforms, the loudness race is over: target the platform spec and spend your limiting budget on transparency rather than volume.
  • ✕
    Ignoring true peak when mastering for streaming
    A master delivered at −14 LUFS integrated but without true peak control can have inter-sample peaks of +0.5 dBTP or higher, which cause clipping artifacts on DAC output and are amplified further by lossy codec encoding (AAC, MP3). Both Spotify and Apple Music transcode uploaded audio to lossy formats for delivery, and this transcoding process can add 0.5–2 dB to inter-sample peak values. A master with true peaks at 0.0 dBFS will almost certainly clip after codec encoding. The solution is simple: set your limiter's true peak ceiling to −1.0 dBTP or lower and confirm with a compliant true peak meter before delivery.
  • ✕
    Using LUFS targets designed for one platform when delivering to another
    A master optimized for Spotify (−14 LUFS, −1 dBTP) will be turned down by 2 LU on Apple Music (−16 LUFS target) and left untouched or turned down further on YouTube. While this is not catastrophic — the track simply plays at a consistent level — it means the master is not optimized for any platform when a single master is used for all. Professional workflow: render separate masters for streaming (−14 LUFS), Apple Music (−16 LUFS), broadcast (−23 LUFS), and download/CD (−10 to −12 LUFS). The creative work is identical; only the final limiter threshold and output ceiling change between renders.
  • ✕
    Measuring LUFS without K-weighting applied — trusting the wrong meter
    Not all meters labeled 'loudness meters' implement ITU-R BS.1770 K-weighting. Some legacy RMS meters, VU meters, and certain DAW stock meters display average power without frequency weighting, producing readings that will be significantly different from what streaming platforms measure on ingest. A bass-heavy mix measured on a non-K-weighted meter may appear to comply with a −14 LUFS target while actually integrating 2–3 LUFS hotter when measured correctly. Always verify that your metering plugin explicitly states ITU-R BS.1770 compliance (version 3 or 4) before trusting its output for delivery decisions.
  • ✕
    Treating LUFS as an artistic constraint rather than a delivery specification
    Some producers restrict their creative decisions during mixing — holding back a bass hit here, softening a drum transient there — because they're worried about LUFS compliance at the mixing stage. LUFS targets apply at the mastering output stage, not during mixing. Mix for the best-sounding result at whatever level feels right; adjust pre-limiter gain and limiting threshold during mastering to hit the delivery target. A mix bus reading −10 LUFS Short-Term at its loudest is not 'too loud to master' — it is simply a gain offset away from any delivery target, and the mastering engineer (or mastering stage) will handle that translation.

12 Frequently Asked Questions

LUFS stands for Loudness Units Full Scale. The number represents the perceived loudness of audio measured on a scale relative to digital full scale (0 dBFS), where all values are negative. A reading of −14 LUFS means the perceived loudness of the audio is 14 Loudness Units below the maximum possible digital level. The lower (more negative) the number, the quieter the audio; the higher (less negative), the louder. Streaming platforms use this number to normalize all music to a consistent playback level.
The most widely applicable streaming target is −14 LUFS integrated, which matches Spotify's normalization reference level and is close to YouTube's −14 LUFS target. Apple Music normalizes to −16 LUFS, and Tidal uses −14 LUFS. In practice, mastering to −14 LUFS integrated with a true peak ceiling of −1.0 dBTP produces a master that performs well across all major platforms — it will be turned down slightly on Apple Music but will not suffer gain reduction on Spotify or YouTube. For maximum cross-platform performance, some engineers render separate masters at −14 and −16 LUFS.

Both are energy-averaging measurements (as opposed to peak measurements), but LUFS applies K-weighting (a psychoacoustic filter that boosts high frequencies and reduces low frequencies to match how humans perceive loudness), whereas standard RMS measures unweighted signal power. LUFS also applies a gating algorithm to exclude silence from the average. On program material with typical spectral balance, LUFS and RMS readings may be within a few decibels of each other, but on bass-heavy material the K-weighting filter will make the LUFS reading significantly lower than the RMS reading. For streaming delivery, only LUFS is a compliant measurement — RMS values cannot be reliably converted to LUFS without knowing the full spectral content of the audio.

Spotify applies gain reduction only — it turns down tracks louder than −14 LUFS but does not apply gain boost to tracks quieter than the target. YouTube similarly applies downward normalization only. Apple Music's Sound Check feature does apply upward gain correction to tracks quieter than −16 LUFS, though this gain boost can introduce its own artifacts. In practice, mastering significantly quieter than the target (e.g., −20 LUFS) is rarely desirable even on platforms that boost: the extra gain applied post-hoc cannot restore dynamics destroyed earlier in the chain, and ambient noise becomes more audible on quiet passages.
Loudness Range (LRA) is a statistical measure of the dynamic variation within a program, expressed in Loudness Units. It represents the range between the 10th and 95th percentile Short-Term LUFS values across the program — essentially, how much the loudness fluctuates over time, excluding extreme outliers. LRA matters because it captures whether a master has dynamic contrast: a heavily limited pop master may show LRA of 3–4 LU, while an orchestral recording may show 20+ LU. Low LRA correlates with listener fatigue on extended listening and indicates over-compression. For most commercial music, target an LRA of at least 6 LU to maintain perceived dynamic interest through streaming normalization.
True Peak measures the peak amplitude of the reconstructed continuous waveform after digital-to-analog conversion, using oversampled interpolation to detect inter-sample peaks that fall between stored digital sample values. It is separate from LUFS because LUFS is an integrated energy measurement (loudness over time) while true peak is an instantaneous ceiling measurement — they address different failure modes. LUFS compliance ensures your track plays at the right relative volume; true peak compliance ensures it does not clip on DAC output or during codec encoding. Both are required for professional delivery: target −14 LUFS integrated and −1.0 dBTP true peak for streaming.

Because platforms normalize to a fixed LUFS target, a master with 8 LU of dynamic range and a −14 LUFS integrated reading will play back at exactly the same volume as a crushed master with 3 LU of dynamic range and a −14 LUFS reading. The dynamic master will sound better: more open, more punchy, with audible transients and natural low-end response. The key is designing loudness into the arrangement (via dense production choices, spectral balance, and mid-section energy) rather than manufacturing it with post-production limiting. Reserve your limiter for true peak control and minor gain trimming, not as the primary loudness tool.
K-weighting applies a high-pass filter that attenuates frequencies below 60 Hz at −3 dB per octave, reducing the measured loudness contribution of sub-bass content. This means bass-heavy genres with significant sub-60Hz energy will measure lower LUFS than their raw energy suggests — a track with 12 dB of 40Hz sub content relative to its mids may integrate 2–3 LUFS quieter than its RMS level implies. For producers, this has a practical consequence: you can push sub-bass harder without driving your LUFS reading over the delivery target, but you also cannot rely on sub-bass to 'fill up' your LUFS budget. The K-weighting filter reflects the genuine perceptual reality — listeners are less sensitive to deep bass for loudness judgment than to midrange content — so the measurement is accurate to the listening experience even when it surprises producers accustomed to RMS-based loudness assessment.
