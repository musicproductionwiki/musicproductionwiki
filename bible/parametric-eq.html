Parametric EQ

/ˌpærəˈmɛtrɪk ˌiːˈkjuː/

Quick Answer Definition How It Works Parameters Quick Reference History How To Use It In The Wild Types Mistakes Calculator Related Further Reading FAQ

Parametric EQ is a type of equalizer that gives the user independent control over frequency, gain, and bandwidth (Q) for each filter band. Unlike graphic EQs with fixed bands, parametric EQs let you target any frequency across the audible spectrum with variable precision.

01 Definition

Every mix decision is ultimately a frequency decision — and the parametric EQ is the instrument you use to make them count.

Parametric EQ is a class of equalizer in which each filter band exposes three independently adjustable parameters: center frequency, gain (boost or cut in decibels), and bandwidth — the last of these commonly expressed as Q, the ratio of center frequency to bandwidth. Because all three parameters are continuously variable rather than fixed at preset positions, parametric EQs allow a producer or engineer to address virtually any spectral problem or enhancement with a single unit. This stands in contrast to graphic equalizers, which provide fixed center frequencies and fixed bandwidths, or shelving tone controls, which affect everything above or below a fixed point. The parametric form is standard on professional mixing consoles, dedicated hardware outboard units, and every major DAW's built-in channel strip.

The term "parametric" was coined specifically to describe this multi-dimensional control architecture. A fully parametric band controls all three dimensions simultaneously. A semi-parametric (or quasi-parametric) band makes the center frequency sweepable but keeps the Q fixed — a common compromise on hardware consoles where panel space was at a premium. Understanding the distinction matters in practice: a semi-parametric mid band on a vintage console behaves differently from a fully parametric software band even when both are set to nominally the same frequency and gain.

In practical mixing, parametric EQ is the primary tool for tasks including subtractive carving (removing resonances, mud, and masking frequencies), additive enhancement (adding air, presence, or weight), high-pass and low-pass filtering (shelving or rolling off frequency extremes), and mid-side processing (treating the center and sides of a stereo signal independently). Its versatility makes it the last EQ most engineers would surrender from their signal chain. A well-executed parametric EQ move can make the difference between a mix element that sits in the pocket and one that fights with everything around it.

Modern implementations add capabilities beyond the original hardware paradigm: linear-phase modes that eliminate the phase rotation inherent in minimum-phase filter designs, dynamic EQ modes where gain responds to input level like a frequency-specific compressor, mid-side matrix processing, and spectrum analyzer overlays that display real-time frequency content behind the EQ curve. Plugins like FabFilter Pro-Q 3, Waves SSL G-Equalizer, and iZotope Neutron have pushed the definition of what a parametric EQ can do, yet every one of them is built on the same three-parameter foundation that George Massenburg formalized in 1972.

02 How It Works

At its core, a parametric EQ band is a second-order IIR (infinite impulse response) filter — a mathematical transfer function that shapes the amplitude of a signal as a function of frequency. In the analog domain, this is implemented using op-amp circuits with variable resistors or inductors tuned by the frequency and Q controls. In the digital domain, the analog prototype is transformed into a discrete-time filter using techniques such as the bilinear transform, which maps the analog frequency axis onto the digital frequency axis while preserving filter shape. The result is a biquad filter described by five coefficients (b0, b1, b2, a1, a2) that are recalculated in real time whenever the user moves a control.

The frequency parameter selects the center frequency of the bell (peak/notch) or the corner frequency of a shelf or high/low-pass filter. The gain parameter determines how many decibels of boost or cut are applied at that center frequency. The Q parameter governs how wide or narrow the affected region is: a low Q (0.3–0.7) creates a broad, musical curve affecting two or more octaves, while a high Q (8–30) creates a surgical notch that might span only a few hertz. These three interact: boosting with a high Q at 3 kHz adds presence to a vocal; cutting with a high Q at 120 Hz removes a room resonance without touching the kick body at 80 Hz or the low-mid warmth at 200 Hz.

Filter types within a parametric EQ unit include the bell (or peaking) filter, the low shelf, the high shelf, the low-pass filter, the high-pass filter, the band-pass filter, and the notch filter. High-pass and low-pass filters are additionally characterized by their slope, measured in dB per octave or as a filter order (12 dB/oct = second-order, 24 dB/oct = fourth-order, 48 dB/oct = eighth-order). Steeper slopes are useful for removing sub-rumble on vocals or high-frequency hash on bass, but very steep minimum-phase filters introduce significant phase rotation near their cutoff frequency, which can affect transient integrity and stereo imaging. Linear-phase variants avoid this at the cost of pre-ringing and additional latency.

Minimum-phase EQ designs — the overwhelming majority of hardware units and most software emulations — introduce phase shift as a byproduct of amplitude change. This is not inherently a problem; human hearing is relatively insensitive to phase differences in a single mono signal. Problems emerge when a minimum-phase EQ is applied differently to two signals that are later summed, or when heavy processing accumulates enough phase shift to noticeably smear transients. Linear-phase EQ trades phase accuracy for latency and pre-ringing, making it appropriate for mastering and bus work but less ideal for individual tracks where low latency matters more than phase transparency.

Understanding these technical foundations is not academic pedantry — it directly determines which tool is right for a given task. A narrow surgical notch to kill a room mode on a guitar cabinet recording? Minimum-phase is fine, fast, and transparent. A broad high shelf lift on a mastering bus intended to match the tonality of a reference track? Linear-phase prevents the shelf from interacting with other processing in unpredictable ways. Knowing the filter math behind the knobs makes you a more intentional engineer.

03 The Parameters

Every parametric eq — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these and you can work with any implementation.

FREQUENCY
Center or corner frequency — where the filter acts

Expressed in Hz or kHz, the frequency parameter determines the point of maximum gain change for bell filters or the −3 dB point for high/low-pass and shelf filters. Sweeping this control in real time while audio plays — a technique called frequency sweeping — is the fastest way to identify problem resonances. Typical ranges span 20 Hz to 20 kHz, covering the full audible spectrum.

GAIN
Boost or cut amount in decibels

Gain sets how many dB of level change occur at the center frequency. Positive values boost, negative values cut. Most bands allow ±15 to ±24 dB of range; in practice, cuts beyond −12 dB and boosts beyond +8 dB are rarely musical. Subtractive EQ (cutting problem frequencies rather than boosting desired ones) generally produces more transparent results and preserves headroom.

Q (BANDWIDTH)
The width of the affected frequency region

Q is calculated as center frequency divided by bandwidth in Hz. A Q of 0.7 affects roughly two octaves and sounds smooth and musical; a Q of 10 affects only a fraction of an octave and is useful for surgical resonance removal. Many engineers use the rule of thumb 'cut narrow, boost wide' — high Q for notching problems, low Q for adding character — because narrow boosts tend to sound unnatural to the ear.

FILTER TYPE
The shape of the EQ curve applied

Common types include bell/peaking (symmetrical boost or cut centered on the frequency), low shelf (affects all frequencies below the set point), high shelf (affects all frequencies above), high-pass (rolls off below the cutoff), low-pass (rolls off above the cutoff), notch (extremely narrow deep cut), and band-pass (passes only a narrow band, attenuating everything else). Each serves a different corrective or creative purpose in the signal chain.

SLOPE (FILTER ORDER)
Steepness of high-pass and low-pass filter roll-offs

Expressed in dB per octave or as filter order (6, 12, 18, 24, 36, 48 dB/oct), slope determines how aggressively a high-pass or low-pass filter attenuates beyond its cutoff. A 6 dB/oct slope is gentle and phase-transparent but may not adequately remove unwanted low-end rumble; a 48 dB/oct slope removes content rapidly but introduces significant phase rotation in minimum-phase implementations. Match slope to task: 12–18 dB/oct for general HPF work on instruments, steeper for subwoofer crossovers or noise removal.

BAND ENABLE / BYPASS
Per-band on/off switching for A/B comparison

Toggling individual bands in and out at equal loudness is the most reliable technique for evaluating whether an EQ move is actually improving the sound or merely making it louder (a cognitive bias that makes louder automatically sound better). Professional EQ plugins like FabFilter Pro-Q 3 include per-band bypass specifically for this workflow. Always compare with gain-compensated bypass before committing to a move.

05 History & Origin

The story of the parametric EQ begins with George Massenburg, an engineer and technologist who presented a paper titled "Parametric Equalization" at the 42nd AES Convention in Los Angeles in May 1972. Massenburg described a filter architecture in which center frequency, gain, and bandwidth were all independently controllable — a direct response to the limitations of the fixed-Q graphic equalizers and semi-fixed console tone controls that dominated studios in the late 1960s. His prototype hardware became the basis for the GML 8200, which Massenburg later refined into one of the most respected equalizer designs in professional recording history. His clients during the 1970s and 1980s included Earth, Wind & Fire, Linda Ronstadt, and Little Feat, and the transparency and precision of parametric EQ became central to the clarity that distinguished those recordings.

Before Massenburg's formalization, engineers had been working toward parametric-style control through parallel efforts. The Neve 1081 console EQ module, introduced in 1972, offered sweepable mid-range frequencies alongside fixed-Q settings, bridging the gap between simple tone controls and the fully parametric ideal. API's 550A (1967) provided fixed-frequency peaking filters with broad musical curves — closer to a graphic EQ but with a musical, program-dependent response that made it a studio staple. The SSL 4000 series consoles, first delivered in 1976, included semi-parametric EQ sections on every channel that became the defining sound of 1980s records: dense, punchy, and assertive. Engineers including Hugh Padgham, Alan Parsons, and Steve Hillage used SSL EQ not just correctively but as a deliberate sonic signature.

The introduction of the Neve 33609 and later the outboard Neve 8803 brought fully parametric midrange control to hardware racks, giving mastering engineers and mix engineers the ability to apply the same precision as console inserts to outboard processing chains. Simultaneously, Focusrite, founded by Rupert Neve in 1985, pursued a high-headroom, low-noise parametric EQ philosophy that culminated in the Focusrite ISA 110, still considered one of the finest-sounding hardware parametric EQs available. On the digital side, Digidesign's introduction of Pro Tools TDM plug-ins in the mid-1990s brought parametric EQ to the DAW era, with third-party developers like Waves and McDSP developing software equivalents of the hardware classics that engineers already trusted.

The plugin era redefined what parametric EQ could mean. FabFilter's Pro-Q series, first released in 2008, introduced a real-time spectrum analyzer integrated directly behind the EQ curve, allowing visual identification of resonances and masking — a workflow revolution. The Pro-Q 3 (2018) added mid-side per-band processing, dynamic EQ behavior, and inter-plugin spectrum matching, compressing what previously required racks of hardware and dedicated metering into a single instantiated plugin. Meanwhile, companies like Kirchhoff, TDR (Tokyo Dawn Records), and Eventide developed EQ plugins pushing the boundaries of filter topology and user interface design. Today, the parametric EQ is simultaneously a centuries-old concept in filter theory, a 50-year-old studio practice formalized by Massenburg, and an actively evolving software category where new releases continue to expand what the three core parameters can achieve.

06 How Producers Use It

On vocals, parametric EQ is used in two distinct stages: corrective and creative. The corrective pass typically involves a high-pass filter at 80–120 Hz to remove plosive rumble and stand vibration, a narrow cut somewhere in the 200–400 Hz range to remove the boxy, honky quality that plagues close-miked vocal recordings, and a surgical notch to address any room resonance or microphone proximity peak that the recording captured. The creative pass adds life: a gentle broad bell boost at 2–4 kHz for intelligibility and presence, and a high shelf lift at 10–16 kHz for air and sheen. The exact frequencies depend entirely on the singer's voice and microphone — no preset survives contact with a real vocal.

Drums benefit enormously from parametric EQ applied both per-track and on the overhead or room mics. On a kick drum, a high-pass at 30–40 Hz removes infrasonic energy that wastes headroom, a cut at 300–500 Hz removes the cardboard body tone common to many rooms, a boost at 60–80 Hz adds weight, and a boost at 3–5 kHz adds the beater click that cuts through dense arrangements. On snare, a cut at 400–600 Hz removes the ring, and a boost at 5–8 kHz adds crack. Overhead microphones often receive a high-pass at 100–200 Hz (since kick and snare are handled by close mics) and a presence boost to open the cymbals, a treatment pioneered on countless records by engineers like Steve Albini and Andy Wallace.

On bass guitar and synthesizer bass, parametric EQ is used to control the fundamental weight, the low-mid mud range (200–350 Hz), and the upper harmonic definition (700 Hz–2 kHz) that allows bass to translate on small speakers. A common technique is to carve a small dip in the bass at the same frequency where the kick drum is boosted, creating complementary frequency profiles between the two low-end anchors — a practice often called frequency stacking or kick-bass relationship carving. Electric guitar tracks typically receive a steep high-pass at 80–100 Hz (since guitars produce little useful content below this) and strategic mid cuts to prevent competing with vocals in the 1–4 kHz range.

At the bus and mastering stage, parametric EQ demands a lighter touch. Moves of 0.5–2 dB have audible consequences on program material, and boosting with a low Q (0.3–0.7) across broad frequency ranges shapes overall tonal balance without the surgical character that would sound wrong at full-mix level. Mid-side EQ at the bus level is particularly powerful: boosting the high shelf on the side channel opens the stereo image width, while cutting low frequencies in the side channel tightens the low end and mono compatibility simultaneously. Mastering engineers like Bob Ludwig and Emily Lazar have described using mid-side parametric EQ as one of their most reliable tools for translating mixes across playback systems.

AbletonEQ Eight is Ableton's built-in parametric EQ — eight fully parametric bands with mid-side mode. Enable the spectrum analyzer (the small graph icon) before making any cut to visually confirm the problem frequency. Use the Oversampling setting (right-click any band) to reduce aliasing when working with high-frequency shelves on the master bus.
FL StudioParametric EQ 2 is the stock fully parametric option — seven bands with adjustable filter types. Right-click the display to enable the spectrum analyzer. For surgical work, switch band type to Notch for deep narrow cuts; the default peak mode is more musical for broad moves. The Vintage mode in Parametric EQ 2 adds subtle harmonic coloring that works well on drums and buses.
Logic ProChannel EQ is Logic's primary parametric tool — eight bands with integrated real-time analyzer. The Analyzer Mode (pre/post) toggle is essential: set it to Pre to see the unprocessed signal and identify problems before applying correction. For mastering, use the Linear Phase EQ instead, which trades latency for phase-coherent processing across all bands.
Pro ToolsEQ III (seven-band) is the native parametric option in Pro Tools. While functional, most professional Pro Tools users install third-party plugins (FabFilter Pro-Q 3, Waves SSL, McDSP) for primary EQ work because EQ III lacks a real-time spectrum analyzer overlay. Use EQ III for quick corrective moves in AAX when plugin latency budgets are tight, as its processing overhead is minimal.
ReaperReaEQ is Reaper's built-in parametric — unlimited bands, low CPU overhead, and a clear frequency/phase display. While the interface is utilitarian, ReaEQ supports any filter type per band and updates curves in real time. Pair it with ReaPlugs' ReaFir in Subtract mode for noise reduction that works in concert with parametric shaping — a combination not available in any other DAW's stock tools.

07 In the Wild

Abstract knowledge becomes practical when you can hear it in music you know. These tracks demonstrate parametric eq used intentionally, at specific moments, for specific purposes.

Kendrick Lamar — "HUMBLE." (2017)
0:00–0:30 · Produced by Mike Will Made-It

The kick drum in 'HUMBLE.' demonstrates textbook parametric EQ on a 808-derived low end: the sub weight sits at approximately 55–65 Hz with almost nothing above 200 Hz on the drum bus, and a narrow presence notch appears to have been applied around 300–400 Hz to prevent the kick from accumulating low-mid mud in the dense mix. Listen on headphones — the clarity and separation between the kick and the vocal during the verses demonstrates how subtractive EQ creates space without thinning the sound.

Billie Eilish — "bad guy" (2019)
0:00–0:45 · Produced by Finneas O'Connell

Recorded largely in a bedroom studio, 'bad guy' is a master class in using parametric EQ to compensate for an acoustically non-ideal environment. The bass line sits unusually low and tight — almost certainly the result of aggressive high-pass filtering on everything except the bass and kick, creating a clean contrast between the sub-heavy elements and the dry, close vocal. The vocal itself has a distinctive lack of room reflection, suggesting a significant cut in the 200–300 Hz range to eliminate any bedroom ambience, combined with a presence boost that places the vocal right at the listener's ear.

Daft Punk — "Get Lucky" (2013)
0:30–1:30 · Produced by Daft Punk & Nile Rodgers

Nile Rodgers' rhythm guitar on 'Get Lucky' is a lesson in parametric EQ on a full-frequency source. The guitar occupies a carefully carved mid-range window — full in the 800 Hz–3 kHz presence zone, with apparent cuts above 10 kHz to prevent competing with the synthesizer arpeggios, and a high-pass aggressive enough to keep the low end clean for bass and kick. The result is a rhythm guitar that is simultaneously present and invisible in the mix, supporting rather than competing. Tom Elmhirst handled the mix; Mick Guzauski mastered.

Radiohead — "Everything in Its Right Place" (2000)
Full track · Produced by Nigel Godrich

The Thom Yorke vocal in this track, filtered and processed through what is audibly a narrow band-pass parametric EQ for sections of the arrangement, demonstrates how extreme parametric moves — Q values above 5, cutting most of the frequency range except a mid-band window — can become a compositional and emotional device rather than just a corrective tool. Nigel Godrich's use of EQ as timbre transformation rather than simple correction influenced a generation of electronic and alternative producers.

08 Types & Variants

Fully Parametric EQ
GML 8200 · Neve 8803 · Focusrite ISA 110

All three parameters — frequency, gain, and Q — are independently controllable on every band. This is the standard for professional outboard and plugin work, offering maximum precision for both corrective and creative applications. The GML 8200, designed by George Massenburg, remains the benchmark for transparent fully parametric processing.

Semi-Parametric (Quasi-Parametric) EQ
SSL 4000 channel EQ · API 3124 · Neve 1073

Frequency is sweepable, but Q is fixed (often at a musically useful broad setting) or switches between two or three preset values. Semi-parametric EQs are faster to use in console environments and often impart a recognizable sonic character — the SSL semi-parametric mid, for instance, is associated with the dense, punchy tonality of countless 1980s and 1990s productions. The fixed Q forces musical, broad-stroke decisions rather than the hyper-surgical approach possible with fully parametric designs.

Dynamic Parametric EQ
Weiss DS1-MK3 · SPL PQ (hardware); FabFilter Pro-Q 3, TDR Nova (software)

A dynamic parametric EQ applies gain only when the signal at the target frequency exceeds a set threshold — effectively a frequency-selective compressor or expander. This is ideal for controlling sibilance that only becomes harsh on loud syllables, taming resonances that only peak on certain notes, or applying broad tonal balance corrections that respond to the natural dynamics of the material rather than flattening them.

Linear-Phase Parametric EQ
Weiss EQ1 · TC Electronic System 6000; Fabfilter Pro-Q 3 (LP mode), Logic Linear Phase EQ

Linear-phase implementations apply the same amplitude response as minimum-phase parametric EQs but use FIR (finite impulse response) filters to eliminate phase rotation across the frequency range. The tradeoff is pre-ringing (audible as a brief pre-echo before transients) and significant latency. Linear-phase mode is appropriate for mastering and bus processing where phase coherence across a stereo mix is paramount and latency is not a constraint.

Mid-Side Parametric EQ
Dangerous Music Master · Elysia Museq; FabFilter Pro-Q 3 (M/S per band), iZotope Ozone

Mid-side EQ processes the center (mono sum) and sides (stereo difference) of a stereo signal independently, allowing frequency changes that would affect only the stereo width or only the mono center. Common applications include cutting low frequencies from the side channel to tighten mono compatibility, boosting the high shelf on the sides to open stereo width, and removing mono resonances without altering the stereo image.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    Boosting narrow Q to add body or warmth
    A high-Q boost (Q above 3) at a low-mid or bass frequency sounds unnatural because the ear perceives narrow amplitude peaks as resonances — the acoustical signature of a physical object vibrating at its natural frequency. Warmth and body in a mix come from broad, low-Q lifts that raise an octave or more of frequency content proportionally. If a narrow boost sounds pleasing in isolation but weird in context, lower the Q until the curve looks like a gentle hill rather than a spike.
  • ✕
    EQing in solo without checking in the full mix
    Frequencies that sound excessive when a track is soloed often fill critical gaps when the full arrangement is playing, and frequencies that sound perfect in solo can mask or clash with other elements in context. The professional standard is to make EQ adjustments while the full mix is playing. Solo can be used momentarily to identify a resonance frequency, but the final decision — how much to cut or boost — must always be made in context.
  • ✕
    Using a high-pass filter to 'fix' low-end problems without identifying the actual frequency
    Applying an HPF and sweeping it upward until the bass sounds tighter is a shortcut that frequently removes fundamental weight the mix needs for translation to large speakers. Instead, use a spectrum analyzer to identify the specific problem frequency (often a room mode between 50–200 Hz), apply a narrow parametric cut, and use the HPF only to remove content genuinely below the instrument's fundamental range. This preserves musical low end while eliminating the actual problem.
  • ✕
    Applying identical EQ curves across different takes or sessions
    Acoustic environments, microphone placement, and performance dynamics vary enough between sessions that a parametric EQ preset that worked on Tuesday's vocal session may not work on Thursday's. Presets are valid starting points, but every source requires re-evaluation with a spectrum analyzer before copying settings forward. The most common symptom of over-relying on presets is a mix that sounds tonally inconsistent across songs on an album.
  • ✕
    Overusing additive EQ on the mix bus
    Boosting multiple frequencies on the mix bus accumulates gain that can overload headroom and create inter-sample peaks that cause distortion in the mastering chain. If a mix bus EQ requires more than ±2–3 dB of correction on multiple bands, the problem likely lives in the individual tracks and should be addressed there. Reserve the mix bus EQ for broad tonal balance adjustments of 1 dB or less, and ensure the output is gain-compensated to match the unprocessed level.
  • ✕
    Ignoring phase rotation on minimum-phase EQs in parallel processing
    When a minimum-phase EQ is used on a parallel (wet/dry blend) path — such as in parallel compression or parallel saturation — the phase-rotated processed signal combines with the unprocessed dry signal, creating comb filtering that can hollow out certain frequencies. This is most audible on low-frequency heavy processing (large bass boosts or cuts). Use linear-phase EQ on parallel paths to avoid this, or move the EQ outside the parallel blend to the full signal path.

12 Frequently Asked Questions

A graphic EQ has a fixed set of center frequencies (typically 10 or 31) spaced at regular intervals, and each band's bandwidth (Q) is also fixed. You can only boost or cut at those predetermined points. A parametric EQ lets you freely choose the frequency, the amount of boost or cut, and the width of the affected region for each band. This makes parametric EQ far more precise and flexible for professional work, while graphic EQs remain useful for live sound applications where speed and visual feedback are prioritized.
Q is the ratio of center frequency to bandwidth — a high Q means a narrow, focused filter, a low Q means a wide, gradual filter. As a practical starting guide: use a Q of 0.5–1.0 for broad tonal shaping (adding warmth, brightness, or air), a Q of 1–3 for moderate corrections, and a Q of 4–12 for surgical removal of specific resonances or feedback frequencies. The commonly cited 'cut narrow, boost wide' rule exists because narrow boosts tend to sound like resonances to the ear, while narrow cuts are less audible as artifacts.
High-pass filters on non-bass tracks are nearly always useful for removing infrasonic content and low-frequency rumble that wastes headroom without contributing to the mix — but the cutoff frequency should match the instrument's actual range. A violin has no musical content below 196 Hz (its open G string), so an HPF at 150–180 Hz is appropriate. A piano, however, extends down to 27.5 Hz, so an aggressive HPF would remove musical fundamentals. Set HPFs by identifying where the instrument's useful frequency range actually begins, not by applying a blanket rule.
Minimum-phase EQ applies amplitude changes using IIR filters that introduce phase rotation as a byproduct — the natural behavior of analog circuits and most digital emulations. This phase shift is generally inaudible on individual tracks and is part of the character of analog-modeled EQs. Linear-phase EQ uses FIR filters to apply amplitude changes without phase rotation but introduces pre-ringing artifacts and processing latency. Use minimum-phase for individual tracks and most mixing tasks; use linear-phase on stereo buses, the mix bus, and mastering chains where phase coherence across the full signal matters more than latency.
Subtractive EQ means cutting problem frequencies rather than boosting desired ones. For example, instead of boosting 5 kHz to add vocal presence, you cut the frequencies that are masking the presence — often the 300–500 Hz 'mud zone' — allowing the natural presence to emerge. Subtractive EQ preserves headroom (cuts reduce total signal level rather than increasing it), tends to sound more transparent and natural than additive EQ, and forces the engineer to identify the root cause of a tonal problem rather than papering over it with a boost. Most experienced engineers do the majority of their corrective work subtractive and reserve boosts for deliberate creative enhancement.
The most reliable method is frequency sweeping: set a high-Q bell filter to a boost of +6 to +12 dB and slowly sweep the frequency control while the audio plays. When the boost makes the problematic ringing or harshness dramatically worse, you have found the resonance. Then switch the band to cut (typically −6 to −12 dB), raise the Q as high as needed to isolate the resonance without affecting surrounding frequencies, and lower the gain until the problem disappears without audibly thinning the sound. Always compare with bypass to confirm the cut is an improvement.
Parametric EQ can significantly reduce the audible effects of acoustic problems — room modes, comb filtering from reflections, proximity effect buildup — but it cannot completely reverse them. EQ changes amplitude as a function of frequency; it cannot address phase issues, stereo imaging artifacts, or the temporal smearing caused by severe room reflections. The correct hierarchy is: acoustic treatment and microphone technique first, EQ second. That said, a well-placed parametric notch at a room mode frequency can transform an unusable recording into a usable one, which is why it remains the most important corrective tool in post-production.
Dynamic EQ applies gain only when the signal at the target frequency exceeds a set threshold, making the filter response depend on the input signal level rather than being fixed. This is superior to static parametric EQ when the problem is intermittent — sibilance that only becomes harsh on loud syllables, a bass guitar string resonance at C# that only honks on specific notes, or a room mode that only activates when the drummer hits hard. Static EQ would over-correct these issues by attenuating the frequency at all times, even when it is behaving acceptably. Dynamic EQ is also preferable on program material (full mixes) where a static cut might remove energy from quiet passages that needed it.
