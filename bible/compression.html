Signal Processing

Compression

noun / signal processing tool · kəm-PREH-shən
Every record you've ever loved was shaped by compression you couldn't hear — and that invisibility is the entire point.
📅 2026-05-15 ⏱ 20 min read 📚 Producer’s Bible
Quick Answer

Compression reduces the dynamic range of a signal by attenuating peaks above a set threshold. The ratio determines how hard it clamps; attack and release control when the gain reduction engages and exits.

Common Misconception

Most producers believe that more compression equals a louder, bigger sound — that compressing harder makes a signal hit with more impact, and that a heavily compressed mix will sound more powerful than a dynamic one.

Here is what is actually happening: compression reduces gain. It makes peaks quieter, not louder. The perception of loudness from compression is entirely a function of makeup gain — the output level boost applied after gain reduction to compensate for the attenuation. A compressor applying 6 dB of gain reduction and 6 dB of makeup gain has not made the signal louder; it has made the loud moments quieter and brought the average level up. What it has changed is the ratio of peak to average — the crest factor. A lower crest factor allows a limiter downstream to apply more gain before hitting a ceiling, which is why compressed material can be made louder through a limiter. But compression itself does not add loudness. Heavy compression reduces it — and on streaming platforms that normalize playback level to -14 LUFS, a heavily compressed track with a crest factor of 4 dB will play back at exactly the same perceived volume as a dynamic track with a crest factor of 12 dB. The only difference is that the dynamic track sounds better. More compression does not make a bigger sound. It makes a denser, more controlled sound — which is sometimes what the material needs, and often what it doesn't.

What Is Compression?

Every record you've ever loved was shaped by compression you couldn't hear — and that invisibility is the entire point.

Compression is the process of automatically reducing the gain of a signal when its level exceeds a defined threshold, and restoring that gain when the signal falls back below it. The result is a narrowing of dynamic range — the distance between the quietest and loudest moments in a recording. A raw vocal take might swing 30 dB between a whispered verse and a belted chorus. A well-compressed vocal might swing 12 dB. The performance feels equally dynamic to the listener, but it sits in a mix without disappearing or clipping, and every word lands with authority. That gap between perceived and measured dynamics is where compression does its most important work.

The compressor is not a volume control, though it behaves like one — it is a volume control that reacts to the audio itself, in real time, with parameters that let the engineer define exactly how fast, how hard, and how long that reaction occurs. This is why compression is simultaneously the most powerful and most dangerous tool in a signal chain. Used correctly, it makes a kick drum feel like it's hitting you in the chest. Used incorrectly, it makes a mix feel airless, lifeless, and fatiguing — a wall of sound that seems loud but has no dimension. The difference between the two outcomes is often a matter of milliseconds on the attack control.

Compression operates on every level of a modern production simultaneously: on individual tracks to control erratic performances; on submix buses to glue groups of instruments into a cohesive sound; on the mix bus to add density and make the record feel finished; and in mastering to optimize loudness for distribution. Each application demands different settings, different compressor types, and a different understanding of what you are actually doing. A 1176 slammed on a room mic at 20:1 is a creative choice. The same settings on a mix bus are a catastrophe. Compression is context-dependent at every level.

What compression sounds like when it's working correctly is nothing. Or more precisely: it sounds like the performance itself, but with every moment of that performance equally present and equally audible. The snare doesn't disappear between the loud hits and the ghost notes. The bass doesn't vanish when the guitarist starts playing. The vocalist doesn't drop out in the verse and overwhelm the mix in the chorus. Compression is the infrastructure of modern recorded music — invisible, essential, and only noticed when it fails. When you hear a mix breathe unnaturally, when a drum room seems to surge between beats, when a vocal sounds strangled on its loudest notes, you are hearing compression working against the music rather than for it.

There is also the other face of compression: the creative, entirely audible, deliberate use of gain reduction as a textural element. The pumping sidechain compression in house and techno. The hyper-compressed drums of hip-hop — where the transient has been shaped into something more aggressive than the original hit. The 1176 in all-buttons-in mode on a guitar amp, creating a distortion-adjacent breakup that has nothing to do with subtlety. In these cases, compression is not a corrective tool — it is an instrument. The line between these two modes is something every professional learns to draw intuitively, and every beginner needs to understand explicitly before they can make that choice with purpose.

In 2026, compression matters differently than it did in 2006. The shift to streaming — with Spotify normalizing at -14 LUFS, Apple Music at -16 LUFS, and YouTube at -14 LUFS — has fundamentally changed the loudness-compression relationship. The arms race of the mid-2000s, which drove mastering engineers to crush records to -6 LUFS integrated, has receded. Platforms normalize playback level, so the heavily compressed record no longer sounds louder than the dynamic one — it just sounds worse. This has returned dynamic range to the conversation in a way that hadn't been true since the 1980s. Simultaneously, the proliferation of high-quality software compressors — accurate models of the 1176, LA-2A, SSL G-Bus, and API 2500 at a fraction of the hardware cost — means compression quality is no longer a function of budget. The barrier now is knowledge, not access. A producer working in a bedroom with stock plugins can achieve compression results that rival professional studios from twenty years ago — if they understand what they're doing. That understanding is what this entry provides.

Compression narrows the dynamic range of a signal by attenuating peaks above a threshold — the foundation of every professional mix, mastering chain, and drum sound in recorded music.

How Compression Works

At its mechanical core, a compressor is a voltage-controlled amplifier (VCA) — or its optical, FET, or tube equivalent — sitting in a signal path, with a detection circuit (the sidechain) measuring the incoming level and generating a control voltage that adjusts the amplifier's gain. When the signal measured by the sidechain crosses the threshold — say, -18 dBFS — the control circuit begins reducing gain according to the ratio. At 4:1, every 4 dB the signal exceeds the threshold produces only 1 dB of output increase. A signal hitting 10 dB over the threshold would normally produce 10 dB of output increase; the compressor reduces that to 2.5 dB. This gain reduction is expressed in dB and visible on the compressor's gain reduction meter. What the producer hears is the signal becoming denser and more controlled — the loudest moments are brought down, making the overall level more consistent without touching the quiet moments below the threshold.

Attack and release are the parameters that determine the temporal behavior of this gain reduction, and they are where compression transitions from a technical function to a perceptual one. Attack — the time it takes for the compressor to reach full gain reduction after the threshold is crossed — directly controls how much of the initial transient passes through unaffected. Set attack to 0.5 ms on a snare drum, and the compressor catches the stick impact before it can escape; the snap of the hit is reduced, and the body and sustain of the drum are pushed forward in the apparent sound. Set attack to 30 ms on the same snare, and the transient punches through completely before the compressor grabs the body — the crack of the hit is preserved, the sustain is controlled. This is not a subtle difference. It is the difference between a snare that cuts and a snare that thuds. Release time — the time for the compressor to return to unity gain after the signal drops below the threshold — determines whether the compressor recovers gracefully between hits or holds onto the gain reduction and makes the next hit feel strangled. At 120 BPM, with beats 500 ms apart, a release of 80–100 ms gives the compressor enough time to reset before the next downbeat. A release of 400 ms on the same material causes cumulative gain reduction — each hit arrives while the compressor is still recovering from the last, making the kit sound increasingly squashed as the measure progresses.

The sidechain — the detection circuit that feeds the control signal — is the part of the compressor architecture that most producers interact with last but understand first. In standard compression, the sidechain reads the signal being compressed. In sidechain compression (sometimes called ducking or keying), the sidechain reads an external signal — a kick drum, for instance — while the compressor acts on a different signal, such as a bass guitar or a synth pad. When the kick hits, the bass ducks by 3–6 dB for 60–100 ms, then returns. The result is a pocket in the low end that lets the kick punch through without masking conflict. This is not an effect — it is a frequency management technique masquerading as dynamics processing. The perceptual result is a low end that feels both full and clear simultaneously, because the two dominant low-frequency elements are never competing at full level at the same moment. This principle scales up to creative applications — sidechain compression driven by a kick at deep ratios creates the pumping effect in dance music, where the entire mix breathes rhythmically with the kick drum, producing a sense of motion and momentum that is as much a compositional element as it is a mixing technique.

A gain-reduction element (VCA, FET, optical, or tube) responds to a sidechain detection circuit — attack sets how fast it grabs, release sets how fast it lets go, and the ratio defines how hard it clamps everything above the threshold.

Compression — Key Parameters

Threshold

Technical: The dBFS level at which gain reduction begins. Signals below the threshold pass through unaffected; signals above it are attenuated according to the ratio. Every dB above the threshold is processed; every dB below is not. Perceptual: Set the threshold too high (say, -5 dBFS) and almost nothing triggers the compressor — the sound is untouched. Set it too low (-40 dBFS) and the compressor is clamping nearly every moment of the signal, including the quiet parts. For a snare with peaks hitting -6 dBFS, a threshold of -15 dBFS means the top 9 dB of every hit is being processed. That's the range where the compressor lives. Practically: start at the peak level of your signal and pull down until you see 4–8 dB of gain reduction on the loudest hits.

Ratio

Technical: Expresses the input-to-output gain relationship for signals above the threshold. A 4:1 ratio means for every 4 dB the signal exceeds the threshold, only 1 dB passes. At 2:1, an 8 dB overage becomes 4 dB. At infinity:1, nothing above the threshold gets through — limiting. Ratios below 4:1 are generally called compression; above 10:1 is typically limiting territory. Perceptual: Low ratios (2:1–3:1) feel transparent — the dynamics are gently narrowed but the signal still breathes. 4:1–8:1 adds audible control and density; peaks are clearly contained. 10:1 and above starts to sound like clamping — useful on drum rooms and parallel chains, destructive on a lead vocal. A 20:1 ratio on a snare gives you the crack without the inconsistency. Choose ratio based on how much the dynamic range is working for you vs. against you.

Attack

Technical: The time constant (in milliseconds) it takes for the compressor to reach full gain reduction after the signal crosses the threshold. At fast attack (0.1–1 ms), the compressor catches transients immediately. At slow attack (50–100 ms), the transient punches through before gain reduction engages. Many compressors use an exponential approach curve, meaning they reach approximately 63% of full gain reduction within the attack time. Perceptual: Attack is the single most impactful parameter for perceived punch and transient character. A slow attack (30–50 ms) on a kick drum lets the initial stick impact punch through before the compressor grabs, preserving attack while controlling the body. A fast attack (0.5–2 ms) on the same kick smooths the transient and pushes the low-end sustain forward. On vocals, 10–20 ms attack gives consonants their articulation; 1–3 ms attack makes the vocal feel controlled but slightly blunted. The punch of a drum mix lives almost entirely in attack time.

Release

Technical: The time constant (in milliseconds or seconds) for the compressor to return to unity gain after the signal falls below the threshold. Like attack, most analog compressors use an exponential curve, recovering approximately 63% within the release time. Auto-release circuits read the program material and vary the release time dynamically — fast release for transients, slower for sustained notes. Perceptual: Too-fast release (under 30 ms on most material) causes the compressor to pump — the gain recovery is audible as a swell or 'whoosh' between hits. Too-slow release (over 500 ms on a snare) means the compressor hasn't recovered before the next hit, causing cumulative gain reduction that makes the kit feel squashed and lifeless. For a kick at 120 BPM (500 ms per beat), a release of 80–120 ms lets the compressor recover before the next downbeat. For bus compression, release set to tempo often creates a musical breathing effect rather than an artifact.

Knee

Technical: Describes how abruptly the gain reduction curve transitions from unity gain to the set ratio at the threshold. A hard knee applies the full ratio immediately at the threshold. A soft knee distributes the ratio transition across a range of dB around the threshold (typically ±6 dB), so at the threshold itself only half the ratio is applied. Perceptual: Hard knee sounds more aggressive and assertive — ideal for drums, limiting, and situations where you want the compressor to behave like a brick wall. Soft knee sounds more transparent and musical — the compressor eases into gain reduction rather than snapping onto the signal. Most vocal compression and mastering compression uses a soft knee specifically because listeners don't hear the compressor engage; they just experience the vocal sitting in the mix more consistently. If a compressor sounds too obvious at moderate settings, try softening the knee before reaching for threshold or ratio.

Makeup Gain

Technical: A fixed output gain stage applied after the gain reduction circuit to compensate for the level lost during compression. If a compressor applies an average of 6 dB gain reduction, 6 dB of makeup gain restores the output to match the uncompressed input level. This is a linear gain boost — it does not itself affect dynamics. Perceptual: Makeup gain is not a 'fix' for poor compression — it's volume matching. The perceptual trap is louder sounds feel better, so when you add makeup gain without A/B matching levels, you'll always prefer the compressed version simply because it's louder. To evaluate compression honestly, engage a gain plugin after the compressor set to the negative of your average gain reduction. On a mix bus, incorrect makeup gain is one of the most common reasons a mix sounds different in the chain vs. soloed.

Threshold sets where the compressor starts working, ratio sets how hard it works, attack shapes the transient character, release determines rhythmic breathing, and knee controls how abruptly the gain reduction engages.

Signal Chain Position

Compression before EQ catches the raw dynamic range and shapes it; EQ after compression allows tonal shaping without re-triggering the gain reduction circuit — though placing a high-pass before compression is critical to prevent low-frequency energy from falsely triggering the detector.

Interaction Warnings

  • Compression interacts destructively with EQ when a boost (particularly in the 60–200 Hz range) is placed before the compressor: the boosted low-frequency energy continuously triggers the detector, causing the compressor to over-clamp the full signal every time the kick or bass plays. The result is audible pumping and unnatural gain reduction that affects the entire frequency range, not just the boosted band. Fix: place the low-frequency EQ boost after the compressor, or engage the compressor's sidechain high-pass filter (typically 80–100 Hz) to prevent low-end from dominating the detector.
  • Compression placed before a reverb or delay send creates a problematic interaction: the compressed signal feeds the send at a more consistent level, but when the compressor releases after a peak, the sudden return to unity gain can be heard as a level jump in the reverb tail. On vocal sends, this manifests as reverb that seems to 'surge' after loud phrases end. The fix is to place compression on the return bus of the reverb rather than (or in addition to) the send, or use a slower release time on the vocal compressor (100–200 ms) so the recovery is gradual enough to not excite the reverb unpredictably.
  • Applying heavy compression (ratio 8:1 or higher, fast attack under 5 ms) before saturation or distortion amplifies the saturation effect nonlinearly: the compressed signal has a flatter dynamic envelope, which drives the saturation stage more consistently into its clipping region. This can sound thicker and more aggressive than intended. The audible result is a loss of dynamic feel in the distorted sound — the front of every note sounds identical to its body, eliminating the natural harmonic bloom of a guitar amp or tape machine. Fix: place saturation before compression to allow the saturation's natural dynamic compression to do work, then use the compressor for final control; or reduce the drive on the saturation stage to account for the more consistent input level.

History of Compression

Western Electric engineer Harold Black, working at Bell Laboratories in 1937, is credited with the first practical application of automatic gain control circuits in broadcast transmission — a system designed to prevent transmitters from overloading when announcers raised their voices. The hardware was not a studio tool; it was infrastructure. But the fundamental principle — a circuit that reads its own output level and adjusts gain in real time — is identical to every compressor that has followed. By 1940, CBS's radio broadcast chain at 485 Madison Avenue in New York was using limiter circuits on every transmission path, and studio engineers were beginning to notice that these limiters did something interesting to the sound of recorded voices: they made them feel more present, more even, more intimate. The corrective tool was already revealing its creative potential before anyone had named it.

The first dedicated studio compressor designed for sound quality rather than transmission protection was the Fairchild 670, built by Rein Narma for Fairchild Recording Equipment Corporation in 1959. Narma's design used tubes and a variable-mu topology — the gain reduction element was a tube whose transconductance decreased as the control voltage increased, producing an inherently smooth, program-dependent compression character. The Fairchild 670 was installed at Capitol Studios in Hollywood, at Abbey Road in London, and at Columbia's 30th Street Studio in New York, and it defined what compression sounded like for an entire decade. When John Lennon's vocal appears on Revolver (1966), recorded at Abbey Road with engineer Geoff Emerick, the harmonic density and apparent intimacy of the sound is partly a function of the Fairchild 670 in the signal chain. The hardware cost $27,000 in 1959 — approximately $280,000 in 2026 dollars — and fewer than one hundred were built.

Urei's 1176 Peak Limiter, designed by Bill Putnam Sr. and introduced in 1967 at United Recording in Hollywood, changed the economics and the sonic character of compression in studios simultaneously. Where the Fairchild was tube-based, expensive, and gentle, the 1176 used a FET (field-effect transistor) as its gain reduction element — fast, aggressive, and capable of a transient response measured in microseconds. Putnam designed the 1176 to handle the increasingly percussive nature of rock recordings coming through United's rooms. The compressor's characteristic sound — a kind of aggressive, slightly distorted density — became synonymous with rock drumming and the sound of Motown. By the early 1970s, every major studio in Los Angeles had multiple 1176s, and engineers were discovering the "British mode" — pressing all four ratio buttons simultaneously to create a nonlinear, harmonically complex overload character that sounded more like a driven amplifier than a dynamics processor.

The SSL 4000 console, introduced by Solid State Logic in 1977 and installed at Townhouse Studios in London that same year, changed bus compression permanently. The G-series bus compressor built into the SSL master section — with its VCA-based gain reduction, fixed program-dependent attack and release, and a character that seemed to make everything placed through it cohere — became the default finishing tool for commercial music through the 1980s and 1990s. Engineers like Hugh Padgham at Townhouse discovered that the G-bus compressor, set to 4:1 with a slow attack and a fast release, created a sense of forward momentum in a full mix that was almost impossible to achieve otherwise. Every major pop and rock record of the 1980s was finished through an SSL G-bus compressor. The "glue" that the SSL provided — a slight reduction in micro-dynamic variation between elements, combined with a subtle harmonic coloration from the VCA gain reduction stage — is now one of the most emulated sounds in software, with dozens of plugins attempting to capture what Padgham and his contemporaries were doing at Olympic, Townhouse, and Air Studios throughout that decade.

From Bell Labs broadcast limiters in 1937 through the Fairchild 670, Urei 1176, and SSL G-bus, compression hardware defined the sound of recorded music for six decades before software models made every circuit topology universally accessible.

How Producers Use Compression

Drums: Drum compression is where most producers first learn what compression actually does, because the results are immediate and audible. On a kick drum, the goal is almost always to control the transient and shape the sustain — set threshold to catch the top 6–10 dB of every hit, use a 4:1 ratio, slow the attack to 25–40 ms to let the initial click of the beater through, and set release to match the tempo (at 120 BPM, 80–100 ms). The result is a kick that punches through the mix with its attack intact but whose sustain decays consistently, rather than varying with how hard the drummer hit. On a snare, parallel compression is often more effective than direct compression — send the snare to a parallel bus, compress it heavily (8:1, fast attack, medium release, 10–15 dB of gain reduction), and blend it under the unprocessed snare at roughly 30% wet. The blend preserves the crack while adding density and sustain from the compressed signal. Room mics and overhead compression work differently — slower attack (50–100 ms), medium ratio (4:1–6:1), and a longer release (200–400 ms) allow the room sound to build naturally before the compressor grabs it, preserving the sense of acoustic space while controlling the peaks.

Vocals and Bass: Vocal compression is the most nuanced compression task in a mix, because the compressor's behavior is audible in a way it isn't on a drum hit. Start with a soft-knee VCA or optical compressor, set a 3:1 ratio, position the threshold to catch the top 4–8 dB of the loudest phrases, and use a 10–20 ms attack that lets the consonants articulate before the compressor grabs the body of the vowel. Release should be long enough that it doesn't pump between words — 60–120 ms depending on the tempo of the delivery. For a doubled or stacked vocal, compress more aggressively (5:1–6:1) to glue the layers together; compression increases the perceived coherence of doubled vocals by evening out the micro-timing variations between takes. Bass guitar benefits from a different approach: use a fast attack (5–10 ms) to catch plucked transients, a 4:1 ratio, and a release of 50–80 ms to maintain note definition. The goal is not to make the bass feel compressed — it is to make it feel consistent, as though every note sustains at the same level regardless of pick pressure or string weight.

Bus and Mix Bus: On a drum bus, use 2:1–4:1 compression with a medium-slow attack (20–40 ms), a tempo-matched release, and aim for 2–4 dB of gain reduction on the peaks. This amount of bus compression is enough to glue the individual drum elements together without destroying the dynamic shape of the performance. The mix bus compressor follows the same logic at a lower ratio — 1.5:1 to 2:1, 4–6 dB of gain reduction at the very most, and often as little as 1–2 dB. The purpose is cohesion: the compressor reduces the micro-level variation between elements, making the mix feel like it was recorded in the same room rather than assembled from separate sessions. An SSL G-series type compressor with slow attack (30 ms), fast release (100 ms), and 2:1 is a reliable starting point for mix bus compression on almost any genre. Mastering compression is the most conservative application — rarely exceeding 1–2 dB of gain reduction, using the compressor almost entirely for its tonal character rather than its dynamic function.

DAW-Specific Notes: In Ableton Live: The stock Compressor device has a Lookahead parameter (up to 10 ms) that allows the compressor to begin gain reduction before the transient arrives — engage this on mix bus compression to prevent any overshoot. Use the Glue Compressor on drum groups; it models the SSL G-bus topology and responds identically. In Logic Pro: The Vintage VCA compressor (modeled on the dbx 160) is the most accurate bus compressor in the stock toolkit; use it on drum buses at 4:1 with the Distortion switch engaged for SSL-adjacent character. The Vintage FET models the 1176 faithfully; use it on room mics and parallel drum chains. In FL Studio: The Fruity Peak Controller as a sidechain source lets you drive compression from any internal signal — route it to the Fruity Peak Controller's source, set the tension and base parameters to define the depth and shape of ducking, then assign it to a channel volume knob. For transient-heavy material, Maximus provides multiband limiting with per-band compression that the stock Compressor cannot approximate. In Pro Tools: Use the BF-3A (Bomb Factory LA-3A model) on acoustic instruments and vocals — it lacks the flexibility of a full VCA compressor but its optical program-dependency makes over-compression nearly impossible. The Avid C6 multiband compressor on the mix bus adds frequency-targeted compression for tonal shaping that a broadband bus compressor cannot provide; use it before the broadband compressor, not after.

Drums need slow attack to preserve transients and tempo-matched release; vocals need soft knee and moderate ratio; bus compression at 2:1 with 2–3 dB of gain reduction glues a mix without squashing it.

Before and After

Without Compression

A raw lead vocal take exhibits a dynamic range of approximately 28 dB across a verse-to-chorus performance. The quietest verse phrases are hitting -32 dBFS; the loudest chorus peaks are hitting -4 dBFS. At a mix level where the chorus vocal sits correctly, the verse is 8–10 dB too quiet and disappears behind the instrumentation. At a mix level where the verse is audible, the chorus peaks are clipping the mix bus. The consonants at the start of words — 't', 'k', 'p' — spike 6–8 dB above the following vowels. The vocal is unmixable in its current state without automation or compression.

With Compression

After a 4:1 ratio, -20 dBFS threshold (catching the top 12 dB of the performance), 15 ms attack, 100 ms release, soft knee, and 4 dB of makeup gain: the vocal now ranges from approximately -22 dBFS to -8 dBFS — a 14 dB dynamic range. Verse phrases are present and audible; the chorus sits 6–8 dB louder in the mix, as intended. Consonant spikes have been reduced from 6–8 dB above the vowels to 2–3 dB above them, preserving articulation without harshness. The gain reduction meter shows 0 dB on quiet passages, 4–6 dB on verse phrases, and 8–10 dB on the loudest chorus peaks. The compressor is working only where it needs to. The vocal can be placed at a fixed fader position and it will remain intelligible and present throughout the entire performance.

Steely Dan — Aja (1977) · Produced by Gary Katz

0:00 — throughout; focus on snare at 1:12

Engineer Roger Nichols applied precision VCA compression to every element of this recording with an almost clinical exactness. The snare at 1:12 — played by session drummer Steve Gadd — has a controlled, almost porcelain crack: fast attack catching the body after the initial transient passes, 4:1 ratio, with gain reduction of roughly 6–8 dB visible on every hit. The result is a snare with consistent level from ghost note to backbeat, sitting in the same plane of the mix at all times. Notice how the snare never jumps or retreats — it occupies an exactly defined space.

Listening Guide

Put on headphones. Play from 1:10. Count the snare hits and listen for any variation in level between them. You will find almost none — every hit is within 2 dB of every other, across an entire performance of varying intensity. This is compression performing its primary corrective function with mastery: not changing the character of the drum, just eliminating the inconsistency.

0:00 — 0:05 kick/bass groove; throughout

Engineer Bruce Swedien's compression choices on this track are a masterclass in sidechain interaction. The kick drum and bass guitar are compression-paired — when the kick hits, the bass ducks by approximately 3 dB for 60–80 ms, creating a slot in the low end that allows both to exist at full apparent level without masking conflict. From 0:00 to 0:05, listen specifically to the moment of the kick hit and the recovery of the bass immediately after. The bass seems to bloom back into the mix in the space following each kick — that is the sidechain releasing.

Listening Guide

Play from 0:00. Focus below 200 Hz. On every kick hit, notice how the bass guitar's fundamental momentarily recedes, then returns. Then listen to the full groove from 0:00 to 0:30 and notice that you can hear both elements clearly at all times, even though their frequency ranges overlap almost entirely. This is the practical result of sidechain compression done correctly — two competing elements sharing a frequency range without stepping on each other.

0:00 — 0:08 guitar riff; drum room throughout

The drum room sound on this record — engineered and produced by Brendan O'Brien at One on One Studios, Los Angeles — is one of the most compressed drum room sounds in rock history. The room microphones are running through aggressive compression at 10:1 or higher, with a slow attack (60–80 ms) allowing the drum transients to punch through before the compressor clamps the room wash. The result is a room sound that surges in between hits — the compressor releases during the quiet gaps and catches the next transient, creating a rhythmic breathing that sounds almost like a second performance underneath the dry kit.

Listening Guide

Play from 0:00. Listen not to the close drum sounds but to the ambient wash underneath them. Between guitar hits at 0:00–0:08, notice the room sound rising in the brief silences — this is the compressor releasing its hold between transients. As the track builds, this breathing effect becomes increasingly pronounced, adding aggression and size to the drum sound that the close mics alone could not produce.

Daft Punk — One More Time (2000) · Produced by Daft Punk

The sidechain compression in 'One More Time' is not a corrective technique — it is the melodic content. The synth pad is routed through a compressor whose sidechain is driven by the kick drum, with a ratio of 8:1 or higher and a release time of approximately 200–300 ms tuned to the tempo. Every kick triggers 10–15 dB of gain reduction on the pad, followed by a gradual recovery that occupies the entire space between beats. The pad does not simply duck and return — it swells back in a way that is rhythmically expressive, creating forward momentum across the entire bar.

Listening Guide

Play from 0:10. Listen to the synth pad specifically — not the kick, not the vocal, just the pad in the background. On every kick hit, the pad disappears almost entirely. In the space between kicks, it swells back up. Now pay attention to the shape of the swell: it does not come back instantly, it curves upward over approximately 200 ms. That curve is the compressor's release time in action. This is compression as rhythmic composition — the release time is a musical parameter as deliberate as any note choice.

Kendrick Lamar — HUMBLE. (2017) · Produced by Mike Will Made-It

0:00 — 0:16 kick and snare; vocal at 0:30

Mike Will Made-It's drum processing on this track exemplifies modern hip-hop compression technique. The kick hits with a transient that has been sculpted by a very fast attack (under 1 ms) and high ratio (8:1–10:1), reducing the natural decay and creating an almost percussive click rather than a traditional kick boom. This is compression being used not to preserve dynamics but to reshape the transient envelope into something that didn't exist in the original sample. Kendrick's vocal at 0:30 demonstrates the opposite approach — moderate compression (4:1, 15 ms attack) that keeps the vocal audible and present while preserving the rhythmic aggression of his delivery.

Listening Guide

Play from 0:00. Listen to the kick drum's attack — it arrives almost instantaneously with no gradual onset, then stops. That abruptness is the result of heavy compression catching and containing the tail of the hit. Now play from 0:30 and listen to Kendrick's vocal. Notice that even his loudest, most aggressive syllables stay within a defined loudness range — the compressor is catching every peak above threshold, and the gain reduction is holding the vocal in its lane. Neither application sounds like compression. Both are essential to the record's character.

Amy Winehouse — Rehab (2006) · Produced by Mark Ronson

0:15 — 0:30 vocal compression; horns throughout

Mark Ronson and engineer Tom Elmhirst used optical-style compression on Amy Winehouse's vocal — the LA-2A or its software equivalent is the most likely candidate — with a program-dependent response that follows the natural dynamics of the performance without imposing a mechanical gain reduction curve. From 0:15 to 0:30, Winehouse's vocal swings between intimate near-whisper and full-throated delivery. The compression catches the peaks of the louder phrases, but the optical response of the circuit means the gain reduction eases in and out gradually, so the compression never draws attention to itself. The horns throughout demonstrate bus compression on a section — every horn has been individually compressed, then the section bus compressed again, creating a density and blend that makes the section sound like a single instrument.

Play from 0:15 with your eyes closed. Listen to Winehouse's vocal as if you were in the room with her. Notice that her voice feels consistently present — when she pulls back in delivery, the vocal doesn't retreat from the mix; when she pushes into the chorus, it doesn't jump. Now focus on the horn section at any point in the track and listen for the individual instruments within it. They are audible but they are also merged — that blend is bus compression doing its work. Compare the horn section to how an uncompressed brass section sounds — wide, inconsistent, with individual instruments jumping in and out of prominence.

It is one of the most emotionally effective moments in the record.

Types of Compression

VCA (Voltage-Controlled Amplifier)

SSL G-Bus Compressor, dbx 160, API 2500

The fastest-responding and most precise compressor topology. A control voltage derived from the detector circuit drives an amplifier that attenuates the signal — reaction times can reach below 0.1 ms. VCA compressors are the workhorses of drum buses, mix buses, and any application requiring tight control with minimal coloration. The SSL G-Bus on a drum bus at 4:1, 30 ms attack, 100 ms release, and 4–6 dB GR is the defining sound of 1980s–2000s rock and pop.

FET (Field-Effect Transistor)

Universal Audio 1176, Empirical Labs Distressor

FET compressors use a transistor in the signal path as the gain reduction element, giving them extremely fast transient response (attack as fast as 20 microseconds on the 1176) combined with a distinct harmonic coloration from the transistor. They impart a forward, aggressive character that glues without losing definition. The 1176 in 'all-buttons-in' mode (all four ratio buttons simultaneously) enters a nonlinear overload state that adds distortion and sustains — a deliberate misuse that became a classic technique on drums and room mics.

Optical

Teletronix LA-2A, Tube-Tech CL 1B

Optical compressors use a light source and photoresistor to control gain reduction — as the light source brightens (driven by the input signal), the photoresistor's resistance changes, attenuating the signal. The photoresistor has an inherently nonlinear release that slows as it recovers, creating a program-dependent 'musical' release curve. The LA-2A has no attack or release controls because they're determined by the optical element itself — attack ~10 ms, initial release ~40 ms with a long tail of several seconds. This makes it ideal for vocals and bass where the natural program-following feels transparent.

Variable-Mu (Tube)

Fairchild 670, Manley Variable Mu

Variable-mu compressors control gain by varying the bias of a tube, which changes its amplification factor. The result is the slowest and most program-dependent of all topologies — attack times rarely drop below 10 ms, and the ratio itself changes with the amount of gain reduction applied. These are mastering and bus tools, not dynamics controllers. They add warmth, air, and a sonic glue that has nothing to do with gain reduction numbers — the Manley Variable Mu is on virtually every major-label mastering chain because of the harmonic complexity it contributes, not just its GR.

Multiband Compressor

Neve 33609 (broadband, not multiband), Weiss DS1-MK3 (digital multiband)

A multiband compressor splits the signal into frequency bands using crossover filters, compresses each band independently, then recombines them. This allows a low-frequency transient (kick drum punching into a mix) to trigger gain reduction without affecting the midrange or high frequencies. The risk is phase artifacts at crossover points and unnatural timbral shifts if each band is compressed aggressively. Mastering engineers typically limit multiband use to 2–3 dB of GR per band with wide crossover slopes, using it to control resonances rather than as primary dynamic control.

Parallel (New York) Compression

Any compressor used in a send/return or wet/dry blend configuration

Not a compressor topology but a routing technique: the uncompressed signal is blended with an aggressively compressed copy. The transients of the dry signal are preserved while the sustain, density, and energy of the compressed signal are added underneath. On drums, this means the crack of the snare and the punch of the kick remain sharp while the room and shell sustain are pushed up 10–15 dB in apparent volume. Ratio settings of 10:1 or higher with very fast attack (1–5 ms) and short release (30–60 ms) work best in the wet path — the dry signal handles everything the compressor destroys.

VCA compressors are fast and precise for drums and buses; FET compressors are aggressive and colored for rock; optical compressors are program-dependent and musical for vocals; tube and variable-mu designs add warmth and harmonic richness for mastering and full-mix applications.

The Producer's Verdict

For most producers, start with: a 4:1 ratio, threshold set to catch 4–8 dB of gain reduction on peaks, attack at 20–30 ms, release at 100–150 ms, soft knee, and 1–2 dB of makeup gain. Apply this to individual tracks first and listen for 30 seconds before adjusting. This set of starting values will be correct for 70% of compression tasks on vocals, instruments, and buses — and it will never cause the catastrophic over-compression that beginners most commonly inflict on their mixes.

If you are doing parallel compression on drums, use extreme settings on the parallel chain — 10:1 ratio, 2 ms attack, 50 ms release, 15–20 dB of gain reduction — and blend the compressed signal at 20–40% under the unprocessed drum mix. The extreme settings on the parallel chain are what make it work; restrained parallel compression is just compression with extra steps.

Avoid mix bus compression until you understand what it sounds like bypassed versus engaged at 1 dB of gain reduction — that incremental comparison is the only way to calibrate your ear to the subtle changes bus compression makes. A producer who cannot hear 1 dB of bus compression gain reduction has no business applying 6 dB of it.

Common Mistakes with Compression

Attack Too Fast — Losing the Transient

Symptom: The drum hit sounds dull and undefined. The kick has no click. The snare has no crack. The mix sounds dense but flat, as if every element has been padded. Producers often compensate by adding high-frequency EQ, which makes things brighter but not more defined.

Cause: Attack time set below 5 ms on percussive material. The compressor is catching the initial transient — the 0–5 ms onset of the hit — and attenuating it before it can establish itself in the listener's ear. This is the opposite of what compression should do on drums.

Fix: Set attack to 20–40 ms on kick and snare. Slow the attack until the click or crack is audible again, then back off 5 ms to leave a small amount of transient control. For room mics, 50–80 ms attack. Verify by A/B bypassing the compressor — the transient character of the compressed signal should match the uncompressed one.

Release Too Fast — Pumping

Symptom: A rhythmic 'whoosh' or swell between beats. The mix seems to breathe in time with the kick drum but not musically — it is distracting, audible, and the listener perceives it as an artifact even if they cannot name it. Common on bus compression with a kick-heavy mix.

Cause: Release time set below 50 ms on full-mix or bus material. The compressor is recovering between every kick hit and the released gain is audible as the level surging upward in the gaps between hits.

Fix: Set release to match tempo. Calculate milliseconds per beat (60,000 / BPM) and target 70–80% of that value as the starting release time. At 120 BPM (500 ms per beat), start at 350–400 ms on bus compression. Engage auto-release if available — most program-dependent release circuits handle this more gracefully than a fixed time constant.

Threshold Too Low — Over-Compression

Symptom: The signal sounds lifeless and airless. Quiet passages have no dynamic distinction from loud passages. The performance loses emotional impact — a whispered phrase and a shouted phrase occupy the same apparent level. The mix sounds loud but has no sense of movement or breath.

Cause: Threshold set so low that the compressor is engaged on nearly every moment of the signal — including material that has no dynamic problem. At -40 dBFS threshold on a vocal with peaks at -6 dBFS, 34 dB of the signal is being processed at all times.

Fix: Raise the threshold until gain reduction meters show 0 dB on quiet passages and 4–8 dB on loud peaks. The compressor should be working on the loudest 15–20% of the material, not the entire performance. Verify: bypass the compressor on the quietest moment in the track — the quiet moment should sound unaffected.

Ratio Too High — Clamping

Symptom: The signal sounds strangled at its peaks. Loud notes or hits arrive with the expected energy and then suddenly stop — there is an unnatural ceiling that prevents the natural dynamic shape of the note from completing. Vocalists sound choked on their most expressive moments. Drums lose impact on the hardest hits.

Cause: Ratio set above 8:1 with a fast attack on material that needs dynamic breathing room — particularly vocals and acoustic instruments. A 20:1 ratio with a 2 ms attack is essentially a limiter; any transient above the threshold is clamped immediately.

Fix: Reduce ratio to 4:1 for most source compression tasks. Reserve 8:1 and above for limiting applications or deliberate creative effect on parallel compression chains. If the gain reduction meter is showing more than 10 dB of reduction on the loudest moments with a high ratio, lower the ratio to 4:1 and compensate by lowering the threshold slightly.

No Makeup Gain — Misreading Results

Symptom: The compressed signal sounds worse than the uncompressed signal — thinner, quieter, less present. Producer bypasses the compressor and immediately prefers the sound. Compression is abandoned as 'not working' on this source. The error is not the compression — it is the level comparison.

Cause: No output gain (makeup gain) applied after compression. Gain reduction lowers the average level of the signal. Without compensating output gain, the compressed version is being compared to the uncompressed version at a lower level, and quieter always sounds worse on first listen.

Fix: Match the perceived loudness of the compressed and uncompressed signals before making an A/B judgment. Use the compressor's output gain control to raise the compressed signal until it matches the bypass level — typically by the amount of average gain reduction visible on the meter. On a compressor applying 6 dB of gain reduction on peaks and 2 dB on average, add 2 dB of makeup gain. Then A/B. The judgment is now about compression character, not level.

Mix Bus Over-Compression — Squashing the Mix

Symptom: The mix sounds loud but fatiguing. Individual elements — particularly the kick and snare — lose their punch and distinctness in the context of the full mix, even if they sounded fine before the bus compressor was engaged. The mix bus compressor's gain reduction meter is showing 6–10 dB of gain reduction on the transient peaks.

Cause: Mix bus compressor ratio and threshold set too aggressively. 4:1 ratio with a threshold that causes 8 dB of gain reduction on every kick hit is compressing the entire mix by 8 dB on the loudest moments — every element ducks simultaneously with the kick, destroying the relative balance the mixing engineer built.

Fix: Reduce mix bus compression to 1.5:1 or 2:1. Target 1–3 dB of gain reduction on the loudest moments, never exceeding 4 dB. The bus compressor's job is cohesion, not loudness. If you need more loudness, use a limiter after the compressor. Mix bus gain reduction above 3–4 dB is almost always audible and almost always counterproductive.

Sidechain Not High-Pass Filtered — Low-Frequency Triggering

Symptom: The compressor seems to react unpredictably to the program material. In a full mix, the compressor appears to duck everything every time a kick or bass note hits, regardless of threshold settings. The mix breathes rhythmically in a way that feels uncontrolled.

Cause: The compressor's sidechain is reading the full-frequency signal, including low frequencies with high energy. A kick drum's fundamental at 60 Hz can generate 10–15 dB more energy than the high-frequency content of the same mix, causing the sidechain to react primarily to bass energy rather than the peaks the engineer is trying to control.

Fix: Engage the sidechain high-pass filter at 80–120 Hz. This removes the low-frequency content from the detection circuit without affecting the compressed signal, so the compressor responds to midrange and high-frequency transients rather than bass rumble. On the SSL G-bus compressor topology, this is the 'HP SC' button. In software, most compressors with sidechain access allow a filter to be placed on the detection signal.

Over-compression symptoms — pumping, transient loss, clamping, and squashed mixes — all trace back to a small number of specific parameter errors that are correctable once you know what you're hearing and why.

Red Flags and Green Flags

🔴 Red Flags

  • Pumping between kick hits at a threshold below −20 dBFS and ratio above 8:1 with release under 40 ms: the compressor is releasing before the next hit and the gain recovery is audible as a whooshing swell in the mix.
  • Vocal intelligibility loss — consonants (especially 'T', 'K', 'P') are soft or missing: attack is set faster than 5 ms, catching the transient before the compressor can distinguish it from sustained vowels. Raise attack to 15–25 ms.
  • Snare sounds like it's behind a blanket rather than cracking forward: attack is under 2 ms at a ratio of 6:1 or higher, crushing the initial stick transient. Slow attack to 20–40 ms or reduce ratio to 4:1.
  • The mix gets quieter when loud sections hit and doesn't recover until the section ends: release is set too slow (over 500 ms) on a bus compressor, causing cumulative gain reduction that builds through the chorus without recovering. Reduce release to 100–200 ms or engage auto-release.
  • Bass guitar loses its low-end weight on downbeats while the high-mids feel exaggerated: the compressor sidechain has no high-pass filter and the detector is responding to sub frequencies, causing extreme GR on low-end content and relative boosting of uncompressed high-mid content. Engage the sidechain HPF at 80–100 Hz.
  • A mix bus compressor causes the stereo image to narrow and shift when loud transients hit: the compressor is applying asymmetric gain reduction to left and right channels (common in some linked-stereo VCA designs under extreme gain reduction). Reduce ratio to 3:1 or below, or switch to a true stereo-linked compressor that applies identical GR to both channels.

🟢 Green Flags

  • The gain reduction meter shows 4–6 dB of GR consistently on the loudest peaks of a vocal, with the reading returning to zero between phrases — the compressor is catching peaks without compressing sustained content.
  • A/B comparing the compressed and uncompressed signal at matched loudness (compensated with makeup gain) and the compressed version sounds more present and controlled, not louder or smaller — the compressor is adding density, not just volume.
  • Drum bus sits in the mix without occupying excessive headroom: individual peaks are 6–8 dB lower than before compression, allowing the fader to come up 4–5 dB overall — average level is higher, headroom is maintained.
  • The mix bus compressor gain reduction meter moves rhythmically with the downbeats rather than random spiking — release is tuned to tempo and the compressor breathing is musical rather than chaotic.
  • Parallel compression blend (dry/wet) passes a transient ABX test: listeners consistently prefer a specific wet percentage (typically 20–40%) that adds density without audibly blunting the snare transient — the compression is contributing without imposing.
  • A vocal track that previously needed frequent fader automation (±6 dB rides every few words) now rides within ±2 dB between phrases with the same energy throughout — the compressor has done dynamic control work, reducing the manual labor required.

Your Progression with Compression

Beginner

When you first encounter compression, resist the instinct to use it everywhere immediately. Start with a single track — a vocal or a snare drum — and spend a full session learning what each parameter does in isolation. Set the threshold until you see 6 dB of gain reduction on the loudest moments. Set the ratio to 4:1. Leave attack and release at their defaults. Press play and listen. Now move the attack from its default to its fastest setting and listen to what happens to the transient. Move it to its slowest setting and listen again. Spend one hour doing nothing but moving one parameter at a time and listening. You are not making music in this session — you are building a perceptual vocabulary that every subsequent production decision will depend on. The moment you can hear the difference between a 5 ms and a 50 ms attack on a snare drum, you have graduated from this stage.

Intermediate

Once the parameter relationships click — once you hear attack as transient control and release as rhythmic behavior — start using compression intentionally rather than correctively. Apply parallel compression to a drum bus: set up a send to a compressed parallel channel with extreme settings (10:1, 2 ms attack, 40 ms release) and blend it under the dry kit. Apply sidechain compression between kick and bass. Start working with different compressor types — use an optical-style compressor on a vocal and a FET-style compressor on a room mic and listen for the difference in character. At this stage, you should be asking not just 'how much compression?' but 'which compressor?' The topology — VCA, FET, optical, tube — shapes the harmonic content and the response curve as much as the parameter settings do. Begin applying bus compression to the drum group at conservative settings (2:1, 2–3 dB of gain reduction) and train your ear to hear what the bus compressor adds before you add the individual track compressors.

Advanced

When you are ready for professional results, the compressor is no longer a device you apply to problems — it is a compositional element you choose based on the character you want to achieve before you start mixing. At this stage, you select a Fairchild-style compressor on the vocal bus because you want the program-dependent release to interact with the rhythmic phrasing of the performance. You use a 1176 in all-buttons-in mode on a guitar bus specifically for the harmonic distortion that mode introduces. You apply mix bus compression during mixing rather than after, so that every mixing decision is made in the context of the compressed bus, and the gain staging accounts for the makeup gain at every stage. You A/B bus compression at 0.5 dB increments and hear the difference. You understand that the sidechain of your mix bus compressor is reading a filtered version of the signal, and you adjust the filter cutoff to tune which frequencies drive the gain reduction. Compression at this level is not about controlling dynamics — it is about shaping the identity of a record.

Frequently Asked Questions

A compressor monitors the level of an audio signal and automatically turns it down when it exceeds a set threshold. The ratio determines how much it turns down: at 4:1, a signal that would be 4 dB over the threshold comes out only 1 dB over. Attack and release control how quickly the compressor reacts and recovers. The practical result is that loud peaks are reduced, quiet passages become relatively louder, and the overall dynamic range narrows — which lets you push the average level higher without clipping.

The clearest sign is pumping: you hear the compressor breathing, usually as a swell or whoosh between transients. A second symptom is the signal feeling lifeless — the transients have been caught and flattened, so a snare sounds like a thud instead of a crack. Check your gain reduction meter: if you're consistently seeing more than 10 dB on a single-source instrument compressor, you're likely over-compressing. The quick test is to bypass the compressor and listen — if the bypassed version sounds more exciting, pull back the ratio or raise the threshold.

It matters enormously, and the difference is audible. A VCA (like the SSL G-Bus or dbx 160) is precise, fast, and relatively neutral in color — it does exactly what you set it to do. An optical compressor (like the LA-2A) uses a light element and photoresistor whose response is nonlinear and program-dependent — it naturally slows down as the signal decays, which creates a release curve that follows the music rather than a fixed time constant. On vocals, this feels transparent; on drums, VCAs are more useful because you need control over exact timing. The Teletronix LA-2A became the defining vocal compressor precisely because its 'flaw' — program-dependent release — is musically appropriate for human voice.

Sidechain compression means the compressor's detector circuit is fed a signal different from the audio being compressed. The most common use is ducking: a kick drum feeds the sidechain of a bass compressor, so every kick hit briefly reduces the bass level by 2–4 dB, clearing space for the kick's low end without EQ cuts. The Daft Punk pumping effect is an extreme version: a kick drum triggers massive gain reduction on a synth pad at a ratio of 10:1 or higher, causing the 'pumping' swell that defines French house music. Use sidechain compression whenever two elements fight in the same frequency range and EQ alone can't solve the problem.

Squashed compression usually comes from too-fast attack catching every consonant, too-high ratio clamping variation that would otherwise sound natural, or a threshold set so low that even quiet syllables trigger heavy gain reduction. The reference track is likely using 2:1 to 3:1 ratio with a 15–20 ms attack (slow enough to let consonant transients through) and a threshold set to catch only the loudest 30–40% of the performance — meaning 6 dB or less of average gain reduction. Fix: raise the threshold until you only see gain reduction on the loudest 3–4 words in a phrase, not every syllable. Consider using two compressors in series at lower ratios rather than one at a high ratio.

Neither is universally better — they do different things. Serial compression shapes and controls the drums: it tightens the relationship between the kick and snare, controls room bleed, and creates a consistent groove. Parallel compression adds density, weight, and perceived loudness underneath the transient without destroying the initial crack. The professional approach is usually both: a moderate serial compressor (4:1, 10 ms attack, 80 ms release, 4–6 dB GR) to control the bus, with a parallel chain featuring an aggressive setting (10:1 or higher, 1–3 ms attack, 30–50 ms release) blended in at 20–40% wet. The 1176 in 'all buttons in' mode was invented for the parallel position.

On individual tracks, compression is surgical — you're solving a specific problem (inconsistent vocal level, flabby bass low-end, snare that disappears in the chorus). The settings can be aggressive because only one element is being processed. On a mix bus, compression is affecting the relationship between every element simultaneously: when the kick hits and triggers the bus compressor, every other element in the mix ducks slightly in sympathy. This is the 'glue' effect. Bus compression parameters must be gentler (2:1–4:1, 4 dB GR maximum, attack long enough to let the transients of the full mix through at 30–50 ms) and the release must be tempo-matched so the recovery becomes rhythmically musical rather than audibly distracting. Over-compressing a mix bus is one of the top five ways engineers ruin a mix that was working.

Not pointless, but it changes what you're optimizing for. Platforms like Spotify normalize to −14 LUFS integrated, meaning a hypercompressed master at −8 LUFS will be turned down 6 dB to match a dynamic master at −14 LUFS — and both will play at the same perceived loudness. The practical implication: extreme limiting and compression to chase loudness no longer helps the listener's experience, since the platform compensates. What heavy compression still does is shape the timbre (harmonic saturation from the compressor), control transients, and affect the density and energy of the sound — those are valid musical reasons to compress. The arms race reason — being louder than competing tracks — is largely obsolete on streaming. Masters at −12 to −14 LUFS with preserved dynamic range now sound better on these platforms than hypercompressed equivalents.

The terminal form of dynamic control.Parallel CompressionBlending a heavily compressed copy with the dry signal to add density without destroying transients.Multiband CompressionIndependent compression applied to separate frequency bands — surgical but risky if overused.Bus CompressionCompression applied to a group of signals simultaneously to create cohesion and glue in a mix.Gain StagingManaging signal levels through the chain so the compressor receives an appropriate input level and behaves predictably.Dynamic RangeThe difference in dB between the loudest and quietest moments of a signal — what compression narrows.Noise GateThe inverse process: attenuates signals below a threshold rather than above it.SaturationSoft clipping that compresses transients and adds harmonics — often described as a compressor with flavor.
