Compression

/kəmˈprɛʃ.ən/

Quick Answer Definition How It Works Parameters Quick Reference History How To Use It In The Wild Types Mistakes Related Further Reading FAQ

Compression is a form of automatic gain control that reduces the dynamic range of an audio signal by attenuating levels that exceed a set threshold. It evens out performances, adds sustain, controls transients, and glues mix elements together.

01 Definition

Every great-sounding record you have ever loved was shaped, in large part, by compression — and understanding it deeply is the single fastest way to close the gap between your mixes and the ones you are chasing.

Compression is a signal processing technique that automatically reduces the gain of an audio signal whenever its level surpasses a user-defined amplitude threshold. Once the signal exceeds that threshold, the compressor attenuates it at a rate determined by the ratio parameter — for example, a 4:1 ratio means that for every 4 dB the signal rises above the threshold, only 1 dB passes through. The result is a narrowed dynamic range: the loudest moments are brought closer in level to the quietest, producing a more controlled, consistent, and often more powerful-sounding signal.

Beyond simple level management, compression is a profoundly musical tool. The timing parameters — attack and release — govern exactly how and when the gain reduction is applied and removed, and manipulating them is what separates a compressor that merely tames peaks from one that actively sculpts the groove and energy of a track. A slow attack on a snare drum lets the initial transient punch through unaffected before gain reduction kicks in, preserving the snap and crack of the hit. A fast attack on a vocal smooths out aggressive consonants and creates the illusion of proximity and intimacy. These are not subtle differences; they are the difference between a track that feels alive and one that feels lifeless.

Compression also performs an important psychoacoustic function. Because the human ear perceives louder sounds as better-sounding, compressors are routinely paired with make-up gain — a compensatory boost applied after gain reduction — to restore apparent loudness while maintaining the dynamic integrity the compression achieved. This is why a well-compressed signal often sounds both more controlled and more present than its unprocessed counterpart. The loudness-perception relationship encoded in the equal-loudness contours (Fletcher-Munson curves) is something every experienced engineer exploits through make-up gain calibration.

In professional practice, compression operates at multiple points in the signal chain simultaneously. A vocal might receive subtle compression on its individual channel to tame peaks, then pass through a bus compressor that glues the lead with its backing harmonies, and finally encounter a limiter at the mastering stage. Each instance serves a distinct purpose, and the cumulative result is a polished, broadcast-ready signal that retains the feeling of dynamics even though its actual dynamic range has been significantly reduced. Understanding the purpose of compression at each stage is as important as knowing how to set the controls.

02 How It Works

At a circuit level, a compressor measures the level of an incoming signal — either the signal itself or a parallel sidechain signal — and compares it against the threshold you have set. When the incoming level exceeds the threshold, a gain control element (a VCA transistor, an optical cell, an FET, a tube, or a software gain multiplier in the digital domain) begins reducing gain. The speed at which that reduction ramps in is the attack time, measured in milliseconds. When the signal falls back below the threshold, the gain control element releases and allows gain to return toward unity; the speed of that recovery is the release time, also in milliseconds.

The ratio parameter determines the slope of gain reduction: at 2:1, a signal 10 dB over the threshold exits at only 5 dB over; at 10:1, that same 10 dB over exits at just 1 dB over — near-limiting behavior. Knee controls soften or harden the transition at the threshold point: a hard knee applies the full ratio immediately at the threshold, creating an abrupt inflection visible on a gain-reduction graph, while a soft knee spreads the onset of compression across a range of several dB below and above the threshold, producing a more transparent, gradual response. Soft knees are common in vocal and bus compressors where transparency is paramount.

The sidechain signal — the signal the compressor's detection circuit actually analyzes — can be filtered to make the compressor frequency-sensitive. High-passing the sidechain at around 80–120 Hz prevents low-frequency energy (kick drum bleed, bass notes) from triggering excessive gain reduction on full-mix or drum bus compressors, a practice so universal it is pre-wired into classic hardware like the SSL G-Bus compressor. Sidechain inputs can also accept an entirely external signal, enabling ducking and pumping effects central to modern electronic and pop production.

Program-dependent release circuits, found in classic compressors like the Fairchild 670 and the Urei 1176, automatically vary release time based on the complexity and duration of the incoming audio, using fast release for short transients and slower release for sustained notes. This adaptive behavior is a large part of what gives vintage hardware its musical quality — the gain reduction breathes with the performance rather than imposing a rigid, mechanical timing. Modern plugin emulations attempt to model these nonlinear release behaviors, and the most convincing ones do so using component-level circuit modeling rather than simple parameter matching.

Make-up gain (also called output gain) compensates for the gain reduction introduced by compression. Because a compressor by definition lowers the peak level of a signal, make-up gain raises the entire compressed signal so it sits at a comparable perceived loudness to the unprocessed signal. Properly calibrating make-up gain — using a level-matched A/B comparison at identical loudness — is the only reliable way to evaluate whether a compressor setting is genuinely improving the signal or simply fooling you with increased volume.

03 The Parameters

Every compression — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these and you can work with any implementation.

THRESHOLD
The level at which compression begins

Threshold sets the amplitude point, measured in dBFS, above which the compressor starts reducing gain. Lower thresholds engage compression on more of the signal; a vocal threshold might sit at −18 dBFS to catch consistent mid-level phrases, while a limiter threshold sits at −0.3 dBFS to catch only the very loudest peaks. Setting the threshold correctly is the most impactful decision in compression: too low and you crush the dynamics; too high and the compressor barely engages.

RATIO
The slope of gain reduction above the threshold

Ratio defines how aggressively the compressor attenuates signals above the threshold, expressed as input:output dB relationships. Common ratios: 2:1 for gentle, transparent bus compression; 4:1 for assertive vocal control; 8:1 for heavy limiting-style drum peak control; ∞:1 (brick-wall) for a true limiter. Ratios below 2:1 produce subtle dynamic control; ratios above 10:1 begin to produce the audible pumping and squashing associated with hard-limiting behavior.

ATTACK
How quickly gain reduction engages after the threshold is crossed

Attack time, measured in milliseconds (typically 0.1 ms to 300 ms), determines how much of the initial transient passes through before gain reduction kicks in. Slow attack (50–150 ms) on drums lets the initial transient snap through, preserving punch and hit definition. Fast attack (0.1–5 ms) on a vocal or bass smooths out peaks quickly but can cause distortion if set too short, because the compressor begins gain reduction mid-cycle of low-frequency waveforms. The attack setting is perhaps the most tonal and musical parameter a compressor offers.

RELEASE
How quickly gain reduction recovers after the signal drops below threshold

Release time, measured in milliseconds to seconds, controls how long the compressor takes to stop attenuating after the signal falls below the threshold. Fast release (30–80 ms) on a drum bus creates audible 'pumping' — the compressor breathes with the kick pattern — which can sound energetic in electronic music. Slow release (200 ms–1 s) on a vocal produces smooth, transparent compression that the listener never consciously notices. Auto-release modes, found in compressors like the SSL G-Bus and FabFilter Pro-C 2, vary release time intelligently with the program material.

KNEE
The curvature of gain reduction onset at the threshold point

Knee determines whether compression engages abruptly (hard knee) or gradually (soft knee) as the signal approaches and crosses the threshold. A hard knee is audible and punchy, ideal for limiting and parallel compression on drums. A soft knee, typically spanning 6–10 dB around the threshold, produces a gentle, curved transition that sounds transparent even at high ratios — standard practice for vocals, acoustic instruments, and bus processing. Most vintage emulation plugins offer adjustable knee width; 0 dB = hard, 6 dB = soft.

MAKE-UP GAIN
Output gain to compensate for gain reduction

Make-up gain (output gain) raises the overall output level after compression to compensate for the peak attenuation introduced by the compressor. If a compressor applies an average of 6 dB of gain reduction, adding 6 dB of make-up gain restores perceived loudness. Critical rule: always level-match with make-up gain bypassed before evaluating whether your compression settings genuinely improve the sound. Auto make-up gain features, found in many modern plugins, calculate compensation automatically but can mislead inexperienced engineers into over-compressing.

SIDECHAIN
The detection signal that triggers gain reduction

The sidechain is the signal the compressor's detection circuit analyzes to determine when and how much to compress. By default this is the same signal being processed (feed-forward or feedback topologies differ in where they sample it), but an external sidechain input allows an entirely different signal to trigger gain reduction on the main audio. High-pass filtering the sidechain at 80–120 Hz prevents low-frequency energy from over-triggering bus compressors — a standard technique on the SSL G-Bus channel. In modern production, sidechain compression from a kick drum triggering a bass or pad is a foundational EDM and pop technique.

05 History & Origin

The origins of dynamic range compression lie in broadcast radio engineering of the late 1930s. Western Electric and RCA engineers developed automatic gain control (AGC) circuits to manage the wide dynamic range of live orchestral broadcasts, preventing transmitter overload while keeping quieter passages audible to listeners on low-quality receivers. These early circuits were crude by modern standards — slow, noisy, and prone to pumping — but they established the fundamental concept of amplitude-sensitive gain reduction that all modern compressors inherit. The first dedicated audio compressors intended for studio use emerged in the early 1940s, when CBS and NBC began installing variable-mu tube limiters in their broadcast chains.

The variable-mu compressor — in which a control voltage applied to the bias of a vacuum tube increases the tube's mu (amplification factor) reduction — produced the first truly musical compression character. The Fairchild 670, introduced in 1959 and designed by Rein Narma, became the definitive variable-mu compressor. Priced at approximately $27,000 in 1959 dollars, it was used on nearly every major recording session at Capitol, Columbia, and Atlantic through the 1960s. The 670 appears on the Beatles' Revolver (1966), where engineer Geoff Emerick used it on the stereo bus and on John Lennon's vocals, contributing to the forward, intimate vocal character that defined the album. Original units now sell for over $35,000.

The Urei 1176 Peak Limiter, designed by Bill Putnam Sr. and introduced in 1967, fundamentally changed studio compression practice by using a field-effect transistor (FET) as the gain control element instead of a tube or optical cell. The FET's near-instantaneous response enabled attack times as fast as 20 microseconds, making the 1176 the first compressor fast enough to catch and reshape individual transients with precision. The notorious 'All-Buttons Mode' — engaging all four ratio buttons simultaneously, producing a ratio of approximately 12:1 to 20:1 with a specific harmonic character — was discovered accidentally by engineers at studios including Record Plant and Electric Lady, and became a standard tool for aggressive rock and hip-hop vocal compression. The 1176 has appeared on more hit records than perhaps any other single piece of hardware.

The 1970s and 1980s brought VCA-based compression, in which a voltage-controlled amplifier provides the gain control element with millisecond-accurate, highly repeatable response. The dbx 160 (1976) and the SSL G-Bus compressor (integrated into the SSL 4000 G console in 1983) defined this era. The SSL G-Bus compressor, designed with a program-dependent auto-release mode, became the canonical mix bus compressor for commercial pop and rock through the 1980s and 1990s, heard on records by Michael Jackson, U2, and Def Leppard. Its characteristic 'glue' — the way it coheres disparate mix elements into a unified sonic image — remains the reference standard against which all bus compressors are evaluated. The dbx 160 and 165A became studio and live standards for dynamic bass guitar and drum overhead control, prized for their clean, punchy gain reduction with minimal coloration.

06 How Producers Use It

Drums and percussion. Compression on drums serves two distinct purposes: transient shaping on individual elements (kick, snare, toms) and bus 'glue' across the full kit. On a kick drum, a fast-to-medium attack (5–20 ms) lets the initial beater click through while compressing the sustain body, producing the tight, controlled low end characteristic of modern pop and hip-hop. On a snare, a slow attack (30–60 ms) preserves the snap of the stick hitting the head — often the most important element of the drum sound — while the compressor works on the ring and body. A drum bus compressor (typically an SSL G-style unit at 4:1, 30–50 ms attack, auto release, 2–4 dB GR) turns a collection of individually recorded elements into a coherent, unified kit.

Vocals. Vocals are the most compression-intensive element in most mixes, for the simple reason that human performance is inherently dynamic — singers push harder in choruses, pull back in verses, move closer and farther from the microphone. A two-stage vocal compression approach is standard: a fast-acting compressor (FET or VCA, 4:1, 10–20 ms attack) catches the peaks in real time during tracking, followed by a slower, more musical optical compressor (LA-2A style, at 3–6 dB GR) during mixing to smooth out phrase-level dynamics. The LA-2A-style optical compressor is particularly valued for its program-dependent time constants, which track the natural cadence of speech and singing without sounding mechanical.

Bass guitar and synth bass. Low-frequency sources benefit from slightly longer attack times (20–80 ms) to let the fundamental of each note establish itself before compression engages, preserving the natural bloom and attack of the instrument. VCA compressors (dbx 160, API 2500) are preferred for clean, fast, precise bass compression; optical compressors add warmth and a musical softening of transients on recorded electric bass. Parallel compression — blending a heavily compressed signal back with the dry signal — is especially effective on bass, restoring the natural attack that full compression removes while retaining the sustain and density that compression provides.

Bus and mix processing. The mix bus compressor is perhaps the most strategically important compressor in a production chain. Applied gently — 1–3 dB of gain reduction at 1.5:1 to 4:1, slow attack, program-dependent release — it coheres the mix elements into a unified whole, creating the sense that all the sounds occupy the same acoustic space and were performed together. Many engineers insert the bus compressor before beginning fader balancing so that every gain decision is made in the context of a compressed mix — this prevents the mix from falling apart when the bus compressor is finally engaged at the mastering stage. The SSL G-Bus, API 2500, and Neve 33609 are the canonical hardware references; their plugin emulations (Waves SSL G-Master Buss Compressor, UAD API 2500) are among the most widely used in commercial production.

AbletonLive's stock Compressor plugin offers Feedback and Feedforward modes — use Feedback for vintage-style character closer to VCA/tube behavior. The Glue Compressor (SSL G-Bus emulation) is the go-to bus tool; set SC HPF to 100 Hz to prevent kick drum over-triggering. For parallel compression, use a return track or the device chain's Dry/Wet control locked at the output stage.
FL StudioFruity Peak Controller combined with Parametric EQ 2 is a DAW-native sidechain approach, but for standard compression, the Fruity Compressor covers basics with clean VCA-style response. Third-party plugins load seamlessly — many FL producers route parallel compression via a Mixer send track set to 100% wet, blending with the original channel at the master send level. The stock Maximus multiband is better suited to mastering and heavy limiting than transparent mix compression.
Logic ProLogic's stock Compressor plugin includes seven circuit-type emulations selectable by circuit button: Platinum Digital (transparent), Class A_R (optical), Class A_U (tube), VCA, FET, and Vintage styles. For vocal work, use the Vintage Opto mode and engage the soft knee for LA-2A-style behavior. The Vintage VCA mode with fast attack and medium release closely approximates SSL G-Bus character for bus work. Always enable the Gain Reduction History display to evaluate pumping behavior over time.
Pro ToolsPro Tools includes the BF-76 (1176 emulation) and the Smack! compressor natively, both of which are session-proven for aggressive vocal and drum work. For mix bus processing, most professional Pro Tools sessions insert a third-party plugin (Waves SSL G-Master, UAD Neve 33609, or Slate Virtual Mix Rack) on the stereo bus output. Use the Clip-Based Gain feature to pre-level vocals before compression — this dramatically reduces the workload on the compressor and produces more transparent results.
ReaperReaper's ReaComp is a highly configurable stock compressor with RMS and peak detection modes, adjustable lookahead (up to 1000 ms), and sidechain filtering built in. For bus compression, engage the RMS detection with a 50–100 ms window for a program-dependent response that approximates VCA glue behavior. Reaper's routing flexibility makes parallel compression particularly straightforward: set up a separate FX chain on the same track using a parallel processing container, or use a send-return chain with reamped compression returned at a lower level.

07 In the Wild

Abstract knowledge becomes practical when you can hear it in music you know. These tracks demonstrate compression used intentionally, at specific moments, for specific purposes.

The Beatles — "When I'm Sixty-Four" (1967)
0:00 – 0:15 intro · Produced by George Martin, engineered by Geoff Emerick

Geoff Emerick applied heavy compression to the clarinets using a REDD console limiting amplifier, giving them an unusually forward and present character atypical of orchestral woodwind recordings of the era. Listen closely to the sustain of each clarinet phrase — the notes bloom and sustain with an almost synthetic evenness that would be acoustically impossible without significant gain reduction. Emerick's philosophy was to compress and close-mic everything, breaking BBC recording orthodoxy, which is why Revolver and Sgt. Pepper's sound unlike any orchestral pop records before them.

Kendrick Lamar — "HUMBLE." (2017)
0:00 – 0:08 drum intro · Produced by Mike Will Made-It

The opening snare crack on HUMBLE. is one of the most studied compression examples in modern hip-hop production. Mike Will Made-It applied fast-attack, high-ratio compression with heavy limiting, then drove the signal into additional saturation, producing the snare's crushed, hyper-present quality. At the intro, notice how the snare lacks any natural room decay — the compressor has reduced the tail to near silence while pushing the initial transient to maximum perceived loudness. The kick drum exhibits the same treatment: it is more felt than heard, a subsonic slam shaped by a combination of compression and low-frequency saturation.

Daft Punk — "Get Lucky" (2013)
Full track · Produced by Daft Punk and Pharrell Williams

Get Lucky provides a masterclass in bus compression applied to a live-feel recording. The mix, recorded with live musicians including Nile Rodgers on rhythm guitar and Omar Hakim on drums, was processed through bus compression that glues the ensemble into a cohesive, room-filling sound. Listen to the sustain of Hakim's hi-hats and how they maintain consistent level across the stereo field — this is bus compression making individual dynamics invisible while the performance breathes naturally. The guitar strums retain their attack transients while the sustain sits evenly under the vocals, a balance achievable only with careful attack and release calibration at the bus stage.

Billie Eilish — "bad guy" (2019)
0:43 – 1:02 verse 2 · Produced by Finneas O'Connell

Finneas O'Connell's production on bad guy demonstrates extreme multiband and dynamic compression applied to an intimate vocal performance recorded in a bedroom environment. The lead vocal sits at a remarkably consistent level despite Eilish's deliberately whispered, close-mic delivery, which produces extreme proximity effect and dynamic variation. The compression is fast enough to kill the natural peaks of her consonants while slow enough to preserve the breathy, intimate character of the sustained vowels. The kick and bass relationship is also a textbook sidechain compression example — the bass visibly ducks with each kick hit, creating space for the low end to breathe without masking.

08 Types & Variants

VCA (Voltage-Controlled Amplifier)
SSL G-Bus · dbx 160 · API 2500 · SSL 4000 channel strip

VCA compressors use a transistor-based voltage-controlled amplifier as the gain control element, enabling extremely precise, fast, and repeatable gain reduction with attack times down to 0.1 ms. They are valued for their clinical precision and punchy character, making them the standard choice for drum bus processing, parallel compression, and mix bus glue applications. The SSL G-Bus compressor's VCA circuit — with its 'Auto' release mode — is the canonical reference for modern pop and rock bus compression.

FET (Field-Effect Transistor)
Urei 1176 · Purple Audio MC77 · Warm Audio WA76

FET compressors, pioneered by the Urei 1176 in 1967, offer the fastest attack times available in hardware compression — down to 20 microseconds — with a characteristically punchy, slightly aggressive harmonic character contributed by the transistor's nonlinear response. They are the first choice for lead vocals requiring assertive peak control, room microphones on drums, and any source needing fast transient shaping with audible character. The 1176's 'All-Buttons' mode, engaging all ratio settings simultaneously, produces a unique overdriven compression sound widely used in rock and hip-hop production.

Optical (Opto)
Teletronix LA-2A · Urei LA-3A · Tube-Tech CL1B

Optical compressors use a light source (typically an electroluminescent panel) coupled to a light-dependent resistor (LDR) as the gain control element. The physical properties of the LDR produce inherently program-dependent time constants — faster for loud, sustained signals, slower for quiet or transient ones — giving optical compression a naturally musical, breath-like quality that tracks vocal and instrumental performances intuitively. The Teletronix LA-2A, with its fixed ratio and program-dependent release, remains the most imitated compressor in plugin history and the standard reference for transparent vocal leveling.

Variable-Mu (Tube)
Fairchild 670 · Manley Variable Mu · Vari-Mu

Variable-mu compressors use vacuum tubes as the gain control element, varying the tube's amplification factor by adjusting its bias voltage. They are inherently slow — attack times typically range from 20 ms to several hundred milliseconds — which makes them transparent and musical rather than punchy. Their harmonic distortion profile, rich in even-order harmonics, adds warmth and density to program material that passes through them. Variable-mu compressors are the classic choice for mastering bus and stereo bus applications where subtle, transparent glue and harmonic enrichment are the goals.

Multiband Compression
Weiss DS1-MK3 · Neve 8803 · dbx 4820

Multiband compressors split the signal into two or more frequency bands using crossover filters and apply independent compression to each band, allowing the low, mid, and high frequencies to be controlled without triggering gain reduction across the entire spectrum. This makes them essential for mastering — controlling excessive low-end energy without dulling the high frequencies — and for de-essing and broadband dynamic control on challenging source material. In mixing, multiband compression is often used surgically on individual sources (kick drum, bass guitar) where a specific frequency range is dynamically problematic.

Digital / Software (Linear Phase)
FabFilter Pro-C 2 · Waves H-Comp · Cytomic The Glue

Software compressors operate in the digital domain, offering functionality impossible in hardware: lookahead detection (reading ahead in the signal buffer to apply gain reduction before the transient arrives), linear-phase processing, zero-latency modes, and exhaustive metering of gain reduction, input, output, and sidechain signals simultaneously. The FabFilter Pro-C 2 is widely considered the benchmark software compressor for precision mixing, offering eight algorithm modes from clean transparent VCA to vintage-style program-dependent optical. Digital compressors democratized access to compression quality previously available only to engineers with six-figure equipment budgets.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    Setting attack too fast and killing the transient
    A compressor with attack set below 3–5 ms on a drum or percussive source will engage before the initial transient has fully passed, squashing the very snap and crack that gives the sound its energy and definition. The resulting sound is dull and lifeless — the kick or snare loses its punch even though the compressor appears to be adding sustain. Fix this by increasing attack to 10–30 ms on drums and listening specifically to the initial hit character before evaluating the body and sustain. If the transient feels weak, the attack is almost certainly too fast.
  • ✕
    Evaluating compression at different loudness levels
    The single most common compression error in home studio production is comparing the compressed signal louder than the unprocessed signal, because the human auditory system perceives louder sounds as better. Without make-up gain compensation calibrated to match perceived loudness, every compressor setting will appear to improve the sound — because it is simply louder. Always use a gain-matched A/B comparison: reduce the output gain of the compressor until the bypassed and engaged signals are perceptually identical in volume, then evaluate the effect of the compression honestly.
  • ✕
    Over-compressing the mix bus from the start
    Applying heavy bus compression early in a mix session and then building the entire mix around it locks you into a heavily processed sound that may work against the material. When a mix bus compressor is removed at the end of the session, the mix often sounds unbalanced and unfinished — because gain decisions, automation moves, and effect levels were all calibrated to a compressed reference. Use 1–2 dB of gentle bus compression as a reference reference point rather than a heavy processor, and reserve more assertive bus treatment for the final mix consolidation stage.
  • ✕
    Ignoring the release time and causing distortion
    A release time set too short relative to the tempo and pitch of the material causes the compressor to attempt gain recovery within a single cycle of a low-frequency waveform, introducing intermodulation distortion and low-frequency pumping artifacts. On a bass guitar signal or kick drum with release set below 30–50 ms, you may hear a rough, grainy quality in the low end that resembles harmonic distortion but is actually gain modulation at audio frequencies. The fix is to increase release time until the distortion clears, or to use a compressor with an intelligent auto-release mode that prevents sub-audio gain modulation.
  • ✕
    Using too much compression as a substitute for editing
    Applying extreme compression ratios and low thresholds to a vocal or performance to compensate for poorly controlled dynamic variation — jumps of 20 dB or more between phrases — will never produce a professional result. The compressor's gain reduction meter will be constantly pegged, the sound will be over-compressed and lifeless, and artifacts like pumping and breathing will be audible. The correct approach is to use clip-based or region gain automation in the DAW to manually level the loudest outlier phrases to within 6–8 dB of the average before inserting any compression, giving the compressor a manageable, consistent dynamic range to work with.
  • ✕
    Sidechain over-triggering on the mix bus from low-frequency content
    A bus compressor without a high-pass filter on its sidechain will detect the large-amplitude low-frequency energy of kick drums and bass as its primary gain reduction trigger, causing the entire mix to pump rhythmically in response to the kick pattern. While this pumping is intentional in some EDM styles, in most contexts it causes midrange and high-frequency content to audibly duck with each kick hit, pulling the vocals and guitars out of the mix. Engage the sidechain high-pass filter at 80–120 Hz on any bus compressor processing a full mix, or use an external sidechain path filtered to remove sub-100 Hz energy.

LimitingLimiting is extreme compression at ratios of 10:1 or higher, designed to prevent a signal from exceeding a defined ceiling — the final stage in loudness control. SidechainSidechain processing uses an external signal to trigger a compressor's gain reduction, enabling rhythmic ducking effects central to modern pop and EDM production. 12 Frequently Asked Questions

Compression reduces the dynamic range of an audio signal by automatically turning down the gain whenever the signal level exceeds a defined threshold. This narrows the gap between the loudest and quietest moments in a performance, making the signal more consistent, controlled, and — when make-up gain is applied — louder without additional peaks. In practical terms, it makes vocals more intelligible, drums more punchy, and mixes more cohesive.
A reliable starting point for lead vocal compression is: threshold set so the compressor engages on medium-loud phrases (typically −18 to −14 dBFS on a well-gain-staged signal), ratio 3:1 to 4:1, attack 15–30 ms, release 80–150 ms, soft knee. Aim for 3–6 dB of gain reduction on peaks. Apply make-up gain to match the bypassed level before evaluating the result. If the vocal still has problematic peaks after this, use clip gain automation to reduce the loudest phrases before the compressor, rather than pushing the ratio higher.
Attack determines how quickly gain reduction engages after the signal crosses the threshold — a fast attack (1–5 ms) catches transients immediately, while a slow attack (50–150 ms) lets them pass through before compression kicks in. Release determines how quickly the compressor stops reducing gain after the signal falls back below the threshold. Together, attack and release control the timing 'feel' of compression: they determine whether the compressor sounds transparent or audible, and whether it enhances or destroys transient character.
The most common reason a compressed signal sounds worse is that the make-up gain is not properly compensating for the gain reduction, causing a false loudness comparison. Alternatively, the attack may be too fast (killing transients), the release may be too short (causing distortion or pumping), or the threshold may be set too low (over-compressing the entire signal). Bypass the compressor, match levels carefully, and re-evaluate. Also check the release: if the gain reduction meter is not returning to zero between notes or phrases, the release is too slow and the compressor is applying static gain reduction throughout.
Hardware compressors use physical circuit components — tubes, FETs, VCAs, or optical cells — each of which introduces its own harmonic character, noise floor, and nonlinear behavior that is difficult to replicate perfectly in software. Software compressors offer capabilities impossible in hardware, including lookahead detection, linear-phase processing, exhaustive metering, and recall precision. The gap between high-quality plugin emulations and hardware has narrowed dramatically since 2015, and for most mixing purposes the differences are less important than the operator's understanding of how to use compression musically.
Parallel compression — also called New York compression — involves blending a heavily compressed version of a signal with the original uncompressed signal. The compressed signal provides density, sustain, and level consistency; the dry signal preserves the natural attack transients and dynamics. The result is a signal that sounds both punchy (because the transients from the dry signal remain intact) and powerful (because the compressed signal fills in the sustain and brings up lower-level details). It is particularly effective on drums and bass, where the attack is rhythmically important but the sustain needs controlling.
Choose ratio based on how aggressively you need to control the signal's dynamics and how audible you want the compression to be. Ratios of 1.5:1 to 2:1 produce gentle, almost imperceptible leveling — appropriate for mix bus and mastering. Ratios of 3:1 to 4:1 are the standard working range for vocals and most instruments — effective without sounding obviously compressed. Ratios of 6:1 to 10:1 produce assertive, audible compression useful for special effects, heavy limiting, and parallel compression sends. Ratios above 10:1 are effectively limiting behavior.
The sidechain detection circuit of a compressor responds most strongly to the frequencies with the greatest amplitude in the input signal. On a full mix, this is typically the kick drum and bass — large-amplitude, low-frequency signals that, without filtering, cause the compressor to reduce gain aggressively with each kick hit, making the entire mix pump in time with the kick. High-pass filtering the sidechain at 80–120 Hz removes the triggering influence of sub-bass and kick drum energy, allowing the compressor to respond to the overall program dynamics instead. This is standard practice for mix bus compression in pop, rock, and R&B, and is pre-wired into most hardware bus compressors including the SSL G-Bus.
