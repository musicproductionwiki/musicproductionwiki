Signal Processing

Compression

noun / signal processing tool · kəm-PREH-shən
Every record you've ever loved was shaped by compression you couldn't hear — and that invisibility is the entire point.
📅 2026-05-16 ⏱ 18 min read 📚 Producer’s Bible
Quick Answer

Compression reduces the dynamic range of audio by turning down signals that exceed a set threshold by a defined ratio, then using makeup gain to restore level — controlling peaks and shaping transients.

Common Misconception

Most producers believe that adding compression always makes audio sound better, and that more compression means a more professional, polished result. The compressor is seen as a polishing tool — the more you use it, the more finished the signal sounds.

This is wrong. Compression is a dynamic reduction tool, and more of it means less dynamics — which means less emotional information in the signal. What makes compression appear to improve everything is almost always the makeup gain inflating the output level, not the gain reduction itself. In a scientifically controlled test — equal RMS levels, blind evaluation — many signals sound demonstrably worse with compression applied incorrectly than without it. The professional use of compression is not about applying it to everything at high ratios; it is about applying the minimum amount of gain reduction needed to solve a specific problem (inconsistent level, excessive transient peaks, lack of bus cohesion) while preserving or enhancing the natural dynamics of the performance. Many of the most praised mixes in modern and classic recording — particularly in jazz, acoustic, and folk production — use dramatically less compression than beginners assume. The goal is for the listener to never hear the compressor working. That requires knowing when not to compress as clearly as knowing how to compress.

What Is Compression?

Every record you've ever loved was shaped by compression you couldn't hear — and that invisibility is the entire point.

Compression is the process of automatically reducing the gain of an audio signal when it exceeds a defined threshold level, then restoring the average level with makeup gain. The result is a narrower dynamic range: the loudest moments are brought down, and the overall level is pushed up so that the quieter moments become more audible. In practice, this means a vocal that swings 20 dB between a whisper and a belt is tamed to a swing of 8 dB — present in the mix at every moment rather than lurching from inaudible to overwhelming. The compressor does not make audio louder in the sense of adding energy. It redistributes existing dynamic energy more evenly across time.

The perceptual effects of compression extend far beyond simple dynamic control. Because the attack and release time constants govern exactly when gain reduction engages and disengages relative to a transient, the compressor is simultaneously a transient-shaping tool. A slow attack on a snare drum lets the initial crack — the highest-amplitude, fastest-rising part of the signal — pass through unaffected before the compressor clamps down on the sustain and room. That choice transforms a flat, one-dimensional hit into a sound with snap and depth. Conversely, a fast attack on that same snare softens the crack, pushing the sustain forward in the mix. Neither setting is correct in isolation. Both are decisions about what the listener hears first and what they hear next.

Compression also functions as a glue tool at the mix and mastering stage. When all elements of a drum bus, or an entire mix, pass through a single compressor, the gain reduction responds to the combined signal. Loud moments in the kick cause slight gain reduction across the snare, hi-hats, and room — binding the kit into a coherent physical event rather than a collection of independent sounds. This phenomenon, often called bus compression, is one of the most underappreciated techniques in professional mixing. The effect is not obvious when you compare a frame of static audio with and without it; it becomes undeniable when you listen to four bars and feel whether the mix moves as one thing or as many things.

There are five compressor topologies in common use — VCA, optical, FET, variable-mu (vari-mu), and digital — each with distinct character arising from how its gain reduction circuit responds to the incoming signal. A VCA (Voltage-Controlled Amplifier) compressor like the SSL G-Bus or API 2500 responds with precision and speed, making it the standard choice for drum busses and mix busses where control is paramount. An optical compressor like the LA-2A uses a light source and photocell whose physical properties introduce a natural, program-dependent release that feels organic on vocals and bass. A FET compressor like the UA 1176 has a near-instantaneous attack and an aggressive, forward character that has defined rock vocals and drum room mics for sixty years. Variable-mu compressors like the Fairchild 670 and Manley Vari-Mu use tubes whose gain reduces as bias current decreases — they are slow, warm, and their inter-modulation adds a musical density impossible to replicate in software. Understanding which topology to reach for before touching a knob is the difference between a mix that sounds engineered and one that sounds produced.

In 2026, compression matters differently than it did in 2006 for three specific reasons. First, streaming normalization — LUFS-based loudness targets on Spotify (−14 LUFS integrated), Apple Music (−16 LUFS), and YouTube (−14 LUFS) — has eliminated the loudness war as a final-stage arms race. A master that once needed to be crushed to −6 LUFS to compete on CD now gets turned down to the platform target anyway, and the aggressive limiting that defined the 2000s now works against the producer, stripping dynamic energy that survives intact on properly compressed, more open masters. Second, the proliferation of high-quality software compressor emulations — UAD, Waves, Plugin Alliance, Slate Digital — means that every topology and every classic hardware unit is available at any budget, but the barrier to good results is no longer access to tools; it is understanding what each tool does to the signal. Third, modern genres — trap, lo-fi hip-hop, hyperpop — use compression as a deliberate aesthetic element rather than a transparent utility. The pumping, breathing, and saturation artifacts of heavy compression are signature textures in these genres, which means the modern producer needs to understand both how to make compression invisible and how to weaponize its artifacts on purpose.

Compression reduces dynamic range by attenuating signal above a set threshold, then restoring level with makeup gain — shaping transients, controlling peaks, and gluing elements into cohesion when used at the right ratio with the right time constants.

How Compression Works

At its core, a compressor contains two functional paths: a gain reduction element and a detector (also called a sidechain or control path). The audio signal passes through the gain reduction element — a VCA, optical cell, FET, or tube stage — which is capable of attenuating the signal by a variable amount. The detector continuously measures the signal's amplitude (in practice, an RMS or peak measurement of the level), compares it to the threshold setting, and sends a control voltage to the gain reduction element telling it how much to attenuate. When the signal is 10 dB above the threshold and the ratio is set to 4:1, the detector tells the gain reduction element to attenuate by 7.5 dB, allowing only 2.5 dB of the excess level to reach the output. This calculation — (excess dB above threshold) × (1 − 1/ratio) — defines the gain reduction at any moment. In a hard-knee configuration, the full ratio applies the instant the threshold is crossed. In a soft-knee configuration, the ratio ramps from 1:1 to the full setting across a dB range (typically ±3 to ±6 dB around the threshold), which is why soft-knee compression sounds transparent even at high ratios on signals that hover near the threshold.

The attack and release time constants control how fast the control voltage changes in response to threshold crossings. Attack time is defined, in most compressors, as the time required to reach 63% of the full gain reduction — not 100%. This means that a 10 ms attack setting does not fully engage the compressor until 20–30 ms after threshold crossing for typical program material. This matters enormously in practice: a 10 ms attack on a kick drum at 120 BPM (where the beat period is 500 ms) allows roughly 10–15 ms of uncompressed transient to pass before gain reduction reaches its working level. That window is what producers call the transient punch — the initial click and crack of the instrument that carries the perception of impact. Set the attack to 0.1 ms and that window closes; the click is attenuated and the kick loses its physical presence in the low-mids. Release time is defined symmetrically — the time for the compressor to recover 63% of the applied gain reduction after the signal drops below the threshold. Too-fast release on a mix bus (under 50 ms) causes the compressor to pump in time with the kick drum, audible as a surge in level every beat that sounds like the mix is breathing. Too-slow release (over 1 second on a bus) means the compressor never fully recovers between transients, and every subsequent hit arrives at a pre-compressed state, losing perceived punch. The sweet spot on most program material — where the compressor recovers fully between musical events without pumping — is typically in the 150–400 ms range, but the correct value is always relative to tempo and arrangement density.

Makeup gain — the output gain stage applied after gain reduction — is the mechanism by which compression produces its most seductive perceptual effect: loudness. A compressor applying 6 dB of gain reduction on peaks, followed by 6 dB of makeup gain, has not changed the peak level at the output. But the average level (the RMS level, which correlates most closely with perceived loudness) is now higher, because the valleys are not pulled down while the makeup gain raises everything. This is why a compressed signal sounds louder than an uncompressed one at matched peak levels — and it is the source of the most common evaluative error in mixing: bypassing a compressor and concluding it sounds better because the uncompressed version sounds quieter. The correct evaluation method is to null the output level before bypassing — set makeup gain to 0 and listen at matched RMS, not matched peaks. A well-set compressor should sound better, or at minimum, neutral, relative to the unprocessed signal at equal perceived loudness. If it sounds worse at equal loudness, the compression is audibly degrading the signal.

A detector circuit measures the signal against the threshold and sends a control voltage to a gain reduction element; attack and release time constants govern when that reduction engages and disengages, making the compressor simultaneously a dynamic and a transient-shaping tool.

Compression — Key Parameters

Threshold

Technical: The dB level at which the compressor begins to act. Signal above the threshold is subject to gain reduction; signal below passes through unchanged. A threshold of -20 dBFS means only the loudest 20 dB of headroom above that point is affected. Perceptual: Lower threshold = more of the signal gets compressed. Setting threshold at -10 dBFS on a vocal catches only the peaks; setting it at -30 dBFS means the compressor is working almost constantly, adding density and coloration throughout the phrase.

Ratio

Technical: The ratio of input level change to output level change above the threshold. At 4:1, a signal 8 dB above threshold emerges only 2 dB above it. At infinity:1 (limiting), no signal exceeds the threshold. Perceptual: Low ratios (1.5:1–3:1) feel transparent and musical — the compressor breathes with the performance. High ratios (8:1–20:1) clamp the signal visibly, creating the pumped, controlled sound on drum busses or heavily compressed vocals. Ratios above 10:1 are functionally limiting.

Attack

Technical: The time constant (in milliseconds) it takes for the compressor to reach full gain reduction after the signal crosses the threshold. Most compressors define attack as the time to reach 63% of full gain reduction, not 100%. Fast attack: 0.1–5 ms. Slow attack: 30–100 ms. Perceptual: Fast attack clamps transients immediately — drums lose crack and punch, but peaks are controlled tightly. Slow attack (20–50 ms on a snare) lets the initial transient through before gain reduction kicks in, preserving the hit and giving the sound presence and snap. This is the primary transient-shaping parameter.

Release

Technical: The time constant (in milliseconds or seconds) the compressor takes to return to unity gain after the signal drops below the threshold. Defined as time to recover 63% of gain reduction. Ranges: fast (20–50 ms), medium (100–300 ms), slow (500 ms–1 s+). Perceptual: Too-fast release (under 30 ms on a mix bus) causes distortion artifacts as gain pumps at audio frequencies. Too-slow release (over 800 ms on a drum bus) means the compressor never fully recovers between hits, reducing the perceived punch of the next transient. Program-dependent auto-release adapts to the signal, which is why it works well on busses.

Knee

Technical: Controls how abruptly the compression ratio is applied as the signal approaches and exceeds the threshold. Hard knee: full ratio applied immediately at the threshold. Soft knee: ratio is gradually applied over a dB range (typically ±3 to ±6 dB) centered on the threshold. Perceptual: Hard knee sounds more aggressive and obvious — you hear the compressor engaging. Soft knee is smoother and more transparent, particularly on vocals and acoustic instruments where a hard transition sounds unnatural. Most mastering-grade compressors default to a soft knee for this reason.

Makeup Gain

Technical: A fixed gain stage applied after the compressor's gain reduction circuit to compensate for the level lost during compression. If a compressor averages 6 dB of gain reduction, applying 6 dB of makeup gain restores the original peak level while the average level is now louder — the fundamental mechanism of perceived loudness increase. Perceptual: Makeup gain is not compression — it's amplification. Setting it incorrectly inflates output levels and makes the result appear louder (and better) during A/B comparison. Always null your output level when evaluating compression decisions.

Lookahead

Technical: A digital-only feature that delays the audio path by a set number of milliseconds (typically 1–10 ms) while allowing the detector circuit to analyze the signal in advance. This allows the gain reduction to begin before the transient arrives at the output, effectively achieving zero attack time without the artifacts of truly instantaneous gain reduction. Perceptual: Lookahead compressors can fully clamp transients that would otherwise escape a conventional fast-attack design. Used in mastering limiters where transparent transient control is critical. Not available on analog hardware without introducing latency.

Threshold sets where compression starts, ratio sets how much gain reduction is applied, attack and release determine the temporal shape of the compression, knee controls transition smoothness, and makeup gain restores level — each parameter changes a different sonic dimension of the result.

Signal Chain Position

Compression before EQ controls the dynamic range of the raw signal; EQ after compression shapes the tonal character of the now-stabilized signal — running EQ first then compressing risks the compressor reacting differently to boosted frequencies than to the original source.

Interaction Warnings

  • Compression interacts problematically with reverb when the compressor is placed after the reverb return in the signal chain: the reverb tail triggers continued gain reduction after the dry signal ends, causing the reverb to pump and swell audibly in the gaps between notes. The result is an unnatural breathing effect where reverb decay rises in level after the source stops. Fix: always compress the dry signal before the reverb send, or sidechain the reverb bus compressor to the dry source so the reverb tail does not trigger gain reduction on itself.
  • Heavy compression (above 6 dB GR) on a bass instrument interacts problematically with low-pass filter resonance or additive low-frequency EQ boosts placed after the compressor: the resonant frequencies or boosted sub content vary dynamically in level relative to the rest of the signal now that the compressor has reduced the overall dynamic range, causing certain notes to suddenly jump out in the low end when they trigger the resonance. The result is uneven low-frequency weight that sounds like a tuning or arrangement problem rather than a processing artifact. Fix: apply corrective EQ and resonance control before the compressor so the compressor acts on an already tonally balanced signal.
  • Sidechain compression triggered by a kick drum interacts problematically with a bass synthesizer whose low-end fundamental is below 40 Hz when the sidechain is not high-pass filtered: the compressor reacts to sub-bass content in the kick that is inaudible on small speakers, creating uneven ducking that sounds inconsistent across playback systems. The result is a mix that sounds well-pumped on subwoofers but has almost no apparent ducking on laptop speakers. Fix: high-pass the sidechain input at 60–80 Hz so the compressor responds only to the audible body of the kick, not its sub content.

History of Compression

Western Electric engineer Harold Black developed feedback amplifier theory in the late 1920s at Bell Labs, and it was this work that laid the conceptual groundwork for automatic gain control — the technical ancestor of audio compression. The first practical broadcast compressors emerged in the 1930s as purely utilitarian devices: NBC and CBS engineers in New York needed to prevent transmitter overload when announcers leaned into microphones or orchestras swelled unexpectedly. The Langevin AM-16, introduced around 1934, was among the earliest dedicated broadcast limiters, and its purpose was entirely defensive — keep the signal on the air without clipping the transmitter. No one was thinking about what these devices did to the feel of a performance; the goal was to stop the catastrophic failure of expensive RF equipment. The sonic consequences of gain reduction were a side effect to be minimized, not a tool to be exploited.

The creative application of compression as a deliberate sonic choice began in earnest with engineer Bill Putnam Sr. at Universal Recording in Chicago in the late 1940s and early 1950s. Putnam, who would go on to found United Recording in Hollywood (later United Western, now Ocean Way), was among the first engineers to treat the compressor not as a safety device but as a microphone preamplifier's partner in shaping the character of a vocal. The UA 175B tube compressor, which Putnam's company produced, was a variable-mu design that added warmth and density to a signal in a way that was audibly musical rather than merely functional. By the time Putnam was recording Nat King Cole and Frank Sinatra at United Western in the mid-1950s, compression had become a creative decision — which unit to use, how hard to push it, and how its particular topology colored the sound were all choices made in the service of the performance.

The 1176 Peak Limiter, designed by Universal Audio's Bob Clearmountain-era predecessor Bill Putnam and brought to market in 1967, defined a generation of rock and pop recording. The UA 1176LN — used at Sunset Sound, Record Plant, and dozens of other major studios throughout the late 1960s and 1970s — became the defining FET compressor, and its 4-button 'all-buttons-in' mode (where all four ratio buttons are engaged simultaneously, a technically incorrect use that creates a ratio of approximately 12:1 with wild harmonic behavior) became a standard drum room technique. John Bonham's room sound on Led Zeppelin's 1971 recordings at Headley Grange was shaped partly by 1176s on the room microphones, and the way those compressors responded to the enormous transients in that stairwell — initial attack getting through, compressor clamping hard, then recovering — is central to why that drum sound still sounds physically impossible fifty years later.

The SSL G-Bus compressor, introduced on the SSL 4000 G Series console in 1981, fundamentally changed what bus compression meant. Abbey Road, Townhouse Studio, and virtually every major London commercial facility installed SSL 4000 G consoles throughout the 1980s, and the G-Bus compressor on the stereo bus became the defining sound of a mixed record for that decade. Engineers at those studios — including Hugh Padgham, who mixed Peter Gabriel and Phil Collins there — discovered that running the bus comp continuously at 2:1 or 4:1, with a threshold set to achieve roughly 2–4 dB of gain reduction on peaks, transformed the way a mix held together. The kick drum's interaction with the bus compressor's release became a rhythmic pumping that defined the gated reverb era's sound — not accidental pumping, but a controlled, tempo-synced breathing that made the mix feel alive. The SSL G-Bus compressor clone is now the most widely emulated piece of hardware in software history, present in essentially every major DAW's built-in processing suite.

Compression moved from a defensive broadcast tool in the 1930s to a creative production instrument through engineers like Bill Putnam Sr. in the 1950s, and was codified as a standard studio technique by the UA 1176 in 1967 and the SSL G-Bus compressor in 1981.

How Producers Use Compression

Drums: On individual drum tracks, compression is primarily a transient-shaping and sustain-control tool. On kick drum, the standard starting point is a fast-to-medium attack (5–15 ms) to preserve the initial click and beater impact, a ratio of 4:1 to 6:1, and a release set just fast enough to recover before the next kick hit — at 120 BPM, that means a release of approximately 300–400 ms. Threshold should be set so the compressor is working 6–10 dB of gain reduction on the body of the kick, not just the transient peaks. On snare, slower attack (15–30 ms) is almost always preferable — letting the crack through before the compressor engages produces the snap that makes a snare cut through a dense arrangement. On the drum bus, a VCA compressor (SSL G-Bus style) set at 2:1 to 4:1 with a medium attack (10–30 ms) and an auto or 100–200 ms release, with threshold adjusted for 3–6 dB of gain reduction on the loudest hits, will glue the kit into a single physical event. Parallel compression — blending a heavily compressed (8:1 or higher, threshold set for 10–15 dB of gain reduction) version of the drum bus with the dry signal — adds density and sustain without sacrificing the transient punch of the uncompressed signal.

Vocals and Bass: On lead vocals, the LA-2A optical approach — or any compressor with a program-dependent release — is the industry default because the release adapts to the natural cadence of speech and singing rather than imposing a fixed timing. Start with a ratio of 3:1 to 4:1, set the threshold so the compressor is working 4–8 dB on the loudest peaks of the performance, and use a slow attack of 10–30 ms to preserve consonant clarity. Two-stage vocal compression — a first compressor at a low ratio (2:1) for general dynamic control, followed by a second compressor at a higher ratio (6:1 or more) for peak limiting — is standard practice on professional vocal chains. On electric bass, the optical approach also works well for its musicality and sustain enhancement. A ratio of 3:1 to 6:1 with a fast attack (5–10 ms) and medium release (150–300 ms) will lock the bass into a consistent level that doesn't fight the kick drum. For the modern trap-style 808 bass, where the sustain is the sound, a slow attack and fast release on an 808 bus will allow the fundamental to bloom before the compressor clamps down, preserving the sub-bass energy while controlling upper harmonic peaks.

Mix Bus and Mastering: Mix bus compression should be set first — before all other mix decisions are made, if possible — so that you're mixing into the compressor and the entire balance is shaped by its gain reduction from the start. The classic SSL G-Bus setting: 4:1, attack 30 ms, release auto, threshold for 2–4 dB of gain reduction on the loudest sections. This 2–4 dB of reduction sounds like almost nothing in bypass but transforms the perceived cohesion and forward momentum of the mix. For mastering, a vari-mu compressor (Manley Vari-Mu style) at very gentle settings — 1.5:1 to 2:1, 1–3 dB of gain reduction — adds density and low-end weight without audible pumping. The mastering compressor should increase RMS without the listener being able to identify that a compressor is present.

DAW-Specific Notes:
In Ableton Live: The built-in Compressor has a Glue Compressor mode (in the Glue Compressor device) that models SSL G-Bus behavior directly. Use the Glue Compressor on the drum bus with the 'Peak' detector, 4:1, attack at 0.5 ms, release auto, and ceiling at 0 dB for classic bus glue. The regular Compressor's 'Auto' release button is reliable enough for vocal chains at 3:1 with a 20 ms attack.
In Logic Pro: The Vintage VCA in Logic's compressor suite directly models the SSL G-Bus. The Vintage Opto models the LA-2A behavior and is the correct starting point for vocal compression in Logic's native tools. Set the Vintage Opto to 'Compress' mode, Comp knob at roughly 50%, Peak Reduction at 40–60% — the program-dependent behavior handles release automatically.
In FL Studio: The Fruity Peak Controller combined with the Parametric EQ 2 can approximate sidechain compression, but the dedicated Fruity Compressor is most usable with the 'Type' switch in 'Peak' mode for drums. For bus compression in FL, Edison or the Mixer's built-in Peak Controller routing is the correct approach. The Parametric EQ 2's sidechain input enables frequency-dependent compression when routed correctly.
In Pro Tools: The BF-76 (bundled) is a reliable 1176 model for drums and vocals. For mix bus, the SSL G-Bus Compressor plugin (Waves) is the standard. Pro Tools' own stock Dynamics III compressor is serviceable as a utility tool but has no meaningful character — use it for gain control only, not for color.

On drums, compression shapes transients and glues the kit; on vocals, it creates consistency and presence; on the mix bus, it binds the full arrangement into a coherent, moving whole — and the correct tool for each context depends on matching compressor topology to the required time constants.

Before and After

Without Compression

An uncompressed lead vocal track has a measured dynamic range of approximately 18–22 dB between the quietest, most intimate phrases (sitting around −30 to −28 dBFS) and the loudest, most intense moments (peaking at −10 to −8 dBFS). The loudest peaks stick out of the mix by 6–8 dB, requiring the overall vocal level to be set low enough that the quiet phrases are buried under guitars and synths. Consonants are bright and sharp on loud syllables but unclear and recessed on quiet ones. The performance is emotionally legible in solo but disappears and reappears in the full mix context.

With Compression

After applying an optical-style compressor (LA-2A emulation) at 3:1 ratio, threshold set at −20 dBFS for 5–8 dB of gain reduction on peaks, attack at 15 ms, program-dependent release, and makeup gain adjusted to null RMS level: the dynamic range narrows to 8–10 dB. The loudest phrases are 5–6 dB lower in level relative to the quiet phrases — the vocal now sits consistently in the mix through every moment of the song. Consonants retain their clarity because the 15 ms attack allows the consonant transient to pass before gain reduction engages. Quiet, intimate phrases are now audible in the mix context. The vocal appears to have been performed at a consistent level — and the listener's attention is directed to the words and the emotion rather than to the mechanics of the level.

Compression In The Wild

The Beatles — When I'm Sixty-Four (1967) · Produced by George Martin

0:00 — Throughout

The bass guitar was recorded direct into the console and compressed through an optical unit at EMI Studios. The result is a bass line with almost no dynamic variation between notes — every pitch sustains at the same level, giving it a synthesizer-like consistency that holds the track together without needing to be loud in the mix. This is optical compression doing what it does best: making an instrument sound like it is performing perfectly evenly, even when the player's touch is inconsistent.

Listening Guide

Put on headphones. Start from the very beginning. Focus exclusively on the bass line through the first verse. Notice how every note — regardless of where it sits on the neck, regardless of how it was plucked — has identical sustain and identical volume. There is no note that blooms louder than another, no note that dies away faster. This is not editing. This is heavy optical compression creating a perfectly even, instrument-like consistency that would be impossible to achieve on a live bass without it.

Led Zeppelin — When the Levee Breaks (1971) · Produced by Jimmy Page

0:00 — The drum introduction

John Bonham's kit was set up on the ground floor of the Headley Grange stairwell, with microphones placed on the first-floor landing above — capturing enormous room reflections. The room microphones were compressed with units that included 1176s; the compressor's response to the enormous transient of Bonham's kick and snare hitting the stone stairwell — initial transient escaping before the compressor clamps down, then the compressor releasing as the room decays — creates the sensation of a physical space being compressed and released in time with each hit. The result is not a drum recording. It is a geological event.

Listening Guide

Put on headphones. Play from 0:00. On the first downbeat, notice the split-second delay between the initial crack of the snare and the explosion of room sound that follows. That gap is the compressor's attack time — the 10–20 ms window before gain reduction fully engages. Then notice how the room sound blooms upward rather than decaying naturally — the compressor's release is pulling up the tail as the level drops below threshold. The entire character of this recording is the interaction between a massive acoustic space and a compressor's time constants.

Michael Jackson — Billie Jean (1982) · Produced by Quincy Jones

0:00 — Throughout

The kick drum is a textbook example of VCA compression applied for maximum control and consistency. Engineer Bruce Swedien used tight VCA compression on the kick — fast enough to clamp the body and tail, with just enough attack time (5–8 ms estimated) to let the initial transient click pass through. The result is a kick that has mechanical precision — every hit is exactly the same weight, the same sustain, the same presence — while retaining the snap of the beater impact. Listen for how the kick sits locked at a fixed level throughout the entire track without ever pushing forward or pulling back.

Listening Guide

Put on headphones. Start at 0:00. Focus on the kick drum through the first minute. Notice that every single kick hit — downbeat, upbeat, ghost hit — lands at identical volume with identical decay. There is no hit that punches harder than another, no hit that rings longer. This is VCA compression with near-perfect consistency. Now notice that despite this absolute dynamic uniformity, the kick still has a click at the front of each hit — that is the attack time letting the transient through before the compressor engages. That 5–10 ms window is the entire sonic identity of this kick.

Daft Punk — One More Time (2000) · Produced by Daft Punk

0:04 — Opening synth and throughout

The four-on-the-floor kick driving this track interacts with fast release times on the mix bus compression in a way that is entirely intentional — the mix breathes down slightly every time the kick hits, then surges upward in the gap between beats. This creates a pumping sensation that is both the most audible compression artifact in the track and one of the most effective rhythmic tools in electronic music production. The release time is tuned to the tempo (120 BPM = 500 ms per beat) so the compressor recovers almost fully between kicks, creating a cycle of compression and release that reinforces the groove.

Listening Guide

Put on headphones. Start at 0:04. Listen to the overall level of the track as the kick hits. When the kick lands, the rest of the elements — synths, voices, pads — dip very slightly in level. In the gap between kicks, they swell back upward. This is the mix bus compressor responding to the kick's amplitude and releasing in time with the tempo. Set a metronome to 120 BPM and you will hear the pumping align exactly with the beat. This is not a mistake — it was calibrated to be a rhythmic and textural element, not corrected away.

Kendrick Lamar — HUMBLE. (2017) · Produced by Mike Will Made-It

0:00 — Throughout

The kick drum on HUMBLE. is one of the most compressed elements in modern hip-hop production — enormous low-end body, precisely controlled sub-bass tail, and a fast punchy transient at the front that cuts through the sparse arrangement. The compression is allowing the initial click of the beater to pass through (slow enough attack, approximately 5–10 ms) before clamping the body of the kick aggressively, preventing the sub-bass from bleeding into the surrounding space. The combination of massive low-end impact and tight control is what makes this kick sound larger than the room it was produced in.

Listening Guide

Put on headphones, ideally with subwoofer or bass-forward headphones. Start at 0:00. On the first kick hit, notice the two distinct components: the initial attack click at the front (the beater impact, above 200 Hz, uncompressed), followed immediately by the massive low-end bloom (the sub-bass fundamental, below 80 Hz). Notice that the sub-bass has a hard, defined end — it does not ring or sustain into the next beat. That defined cutoff is the compressor clamping the tail. The click getting through while the body is controlled is the compressor's attack time doing exactly what it is designed to do.

Radiohead — Karma Police (1997) · Produced by Nigel Godrich

2:35 — The outro build

Beginning at approximately 2:35, the piano, guitars, and ambient elements begin compressing together on the mix bus in a way that makes the entire arrangement feel like a single organism swelling in intensity. Nigel Godrich's bus compression at this section — likely on an SSL 4000 at Corcovado or another London facility — is set to respond to the building density of the arrangement: as more elements layer in, the compressor works harder, bringing the overall level down slightly and pushing the average level upward, increasing perceived density without any individual element getting louder. This is the definition of bus compression increasing coherence.

Listening Guide

Start at 2:35. Focus on the overall impression of the mix rather than any individual instrument. Notice how, as the section builds over the following 60 seconds, the mix does not get louder in proportion to the additional elements — it gets denser. The piano and guitars do not separate into distinct layers; they compress into each other, feeling like one large sonic event. Take note of how the mix feels unified, like a single thing breathing, rather than layered tracks. That is bus compression responding to multiple instruments simultaneously and binding them into a coherent texture.

Adele — Rolling in the Deep (2010) · Produced by Paul Epworth

0:08 — Adele's vocal entry

When Adele's vocal enters at 0:08, it arrives with the character of an optical compressor that has a slow attack — the consonants of 'There's a fire' hit the listener sharply and clearly before the compressor engages, preserving the intelligibility and presence that make the performance feel immediate and unprocessed. The slow attack allows the initial consonant transients to pass through at full level before gain reduction reduces the sustained vowel sounds, which is the opposite of what over-compressed vocals do. The result is maximum clarity and presence without hardness or squashing.

Listening Guide

Put on headphones. Start at 0:08 and listen specifically to the consonants of Adele's first phrase — the 'Th' of 'There's', the 'f' of 'fire'. These consonant bursts are sharp, clear, and forward in the mix. Now listen to the sustained vowels that follow each consonant — 'air' and 'ire' — and notice that these feel slightly more controlled, slightly less aggressive than the consonants. The consonants are getting through before the compressor engages; the sustained sounds are being gently reduced. This is slow-attack optical compression preserving intelligibility and natural vocal character simultaneously.

From the synthesizer-like bass on When I'm Sixty-Four to the intentional pumping of One More Time, compression in professional recordings is either completely invisible or deliberately weaponized as a rhythmic texture — rarely accidental, never incidental.

Types of Compression

VCA (Voltage Controlled Amplifier)

SSL G-Bus Compressor, dbx 160, API 2500

VCA compressors use a voltage-controlled amplifier in the gain reduction path, making them fast, precise, and punchy. They respond quickly to transients and have a characteristically tight, controlled sound. Best for drums, drum busses, and any source where you want aggressive gain reduction without excessive coloration — this is the compressor that defined the sound of 1980s and 1990s rock and pop.

FET (Field Effect Transistor)

Urei 1176, Purple Audio MC77

FET compressors use a field effect transistor as the gain reduction element, allowing extremely fast attack times (down to 20 microseconds on the 1176) with a distinctive harmonic character. They impart a forward, aggressive quality that works especially well on vocals, room mics, and anything that needs presence and energy. The 1176 in All-Buttons-In mode — engaging all four ratio buttons simultaneously — creates a unique distorted pumping that has appeared on countless records.

Optical

Teletronix LA-2A, Tube-Tech CL 1B

Optical compressors use a light-sensitive resistor (photocell) and a light source whose intensity tracks the input signal. The inherent lag of the optical element creates a program-dependent attack and release that responds musically to the dynamics of the source. The result is smooth, transparent compression that is difficult to hear working even at significant gain reduction — ideal for vocals, bass, and acoustic instruments where maintaining natural dynamics matters.

Variable-Mu (Tube)

Fairchild 670, Manley Variable Mu

Variable-mu compressors use tubes operating in their nonlinear region to achieve gain reduction: as the tube is biased harder, it attenuates more. Attack and release times are inherently slow and program-dependent, making these compressors ill-suited for fast transient control but exceptional for gluing program material and adding harmonic richness. The Fairchild 670 stereo unit remains one of the most copied designs in mastering, valued for its ability to add warmth and dimension to a finished mix.

Multiband

Neve 33609 (sidechain EQ), Waves C6

Multiband compressors split the audio signal into frequency bands via crossover filters and compress each band independently. This allows targeted dynamic control — taming a resonant low-mid buildup without affecting the top end, for example. The crossover filters introduce phase issues and the interaction between bands can create unnatural-sounding results if pushed too far; experienced engineers use the minimum number of bands required to solve a specific problem, not as a default mixing tool.

Parallel (New York) Compression

Any compressor in a parallel configuration

Not a compressor circuit type but a routing technique: the dry, uncompressed signal is blended with a heavily compressed (often 10:1+, fast attack) version. The result retains the natural transients and dynamics of the dry signal while the compressed signal adds density, sustain, and apparent loudness underneath. Particularly effective on drums, where it adds weight and thickness without killing the attack of the kick and snare — the technique that defined the sound of large 1990s drum productions.

VCA compressors provide speed and precision for drums and busses; optical compressors provide musical, program-dependent response for vocals and bass; FET compressors provide aggressive forward character for rock and hip-hop; variable-mu compressors provide warmth and density for mastering.

The Producer's Verdict

For most producers, start with: a 4:1 ratio, threshold set for 4–6 dB of gain reduction on peaks, attack at 10 ms, release at 150–200 ms, and makeup gain nulled to match the uncompressed RMS level. Use this as the baseline on every source until you understand what each parameter is actually changing in the sound before deviating from it.

If you are working on a mix bus or drum bus and need cohesion rather than control, use the SSL G-Bus compressor model at 2:1 to 4:1, attack 20–30 ms, auto release, 2–4 dB of gain reduction — set it before you start mixing and mix into it rather than adding it at the end.

Avoid parallel compression until you understand gain staging: blending a heavily compressed signal with a dry signal will inflate your bus levels unpredictably, and if you don't understand why the mix bus is suddenly clipping, you will compensate in the wrong place and introduce problems that compound across the entire session.

Common Mistakes with Compression

Killing the Transient With Too-Fast Attack

Symptom: Drums sound flat, distant, and lifeless. The kick loses its click and beater impact; the snare has no crack. Cymbals sound dull. The mix feels like it is playing from the next room.

Cause: Attack time set below 2 ms on drum tracks or a drum bus. At 0.1–1 ms, the compressor engages before the transient has time to reach the output, clamping the highest-amplitude, fastest-rising portion of the signal — the very element that communicates impact and presence to the listener.

Fix: Set attack to 10–30 ms on snare, 5–15 ms on kick. Bypass the compressor, listen to where the transient begins, and set attack so the first 5–15 ms of the hit passes through uncompressed. Increase attack time by 5 ms increments until the crack and punch return.

Pumping and Breathing on the Mix Bus

Symptom: The entire mix surges and dips in level in time with the kick drum. Every kick hit is followed by an audible swell as the rest of the mix comes back up. At fast enough release times, this manifests as low-frequency distortion — a rhythmic wobble in the low-mids.

Cause: Release time set too fast on the mix bus compressor — typically under 80 ms. The compressor is fully recovering between kick hits, and the surge back to unity gain is audible as a positive-level event at beat frequency (1–3 Hz at typical tempos), which listeners perceive as pumping.

Fix: Increase release time to 150–400 ms on the mix bus. At 120 BPM, a release of 250 ms allows the compressor to recover roughly 85% of gain reduction between beats — enough to prevent audible pumping while still responding to musical dynamics. Use auto-release if available; it adapts to program material and eliminates the need to calculate tempo-matched release times.

Evaluating Compression With Mismatched Levels

Symptom: Every compressor sounds like it's improving the signal. You apply compression to everything because it always sounds better in bypass-A/B — but the mix sounds over-compressed, squashed, and lacking in dynamic life.

Cause: Makeup gain is inflating the output level above the dry signal level. When you bypass the compressor, the compressed version is 3–6 dB louder than the unprocessed signal. Louder almost always sounds better in a short A/B comparison — this is a psychoacoustic bias, not a sonic judgment.

Fix: Before evaluating any compression decision, null the output level. Reduce makeup gain until the compressed signal measures at the same RMS level as the unprocessed signal (use a VU meter or RMS meter on the output). Perform the A/B at matched levels. A well-set compressor sounds neutral or better at matched levels; if it sounds worse, the compression is hurting the signal.

Over-Compressing Vocals Into Lifelessness

Symptom: The vocal sits at a perfectly consistent level but feels mechanical, robotic, and emotionally flat. Every phrase has the same weight; dynamics that should communicate intimacy or urgency are absent. The performance sounds like it was played on a keyboard, not sung.

Cause: Threshold set too low (below −30 dBFS on a signal averaging −18 dBFS) and ratio set too high (above 8:1), so the compressor is working 15–20 dB of gain reduction throughout the entire phrase. The dynamic range of the performance — the very thing that communicates emotion — is being compressed out of existence.

Fix: Set threshold so the compressor is working no more than 6–10 dB on the loudest peaks of the performance, with minimal gain reduction (0–2 dB) on the quieter, more intimate moments. Lower the ratio to 3:1 to 4:1. If consistency is still needed, use clip gain or volume automation on the vocal before compression to tame the worst peaks by hand, then compress lightly — 2:1 to 3:1, 3–6 dB of reduction — for final polish.

Setting Ratio Too High and Calling It 'Limiting'

Symptom: The bass guitar sounds distorted and choked. Low-frequency notes that should sustain and breathe are cut off suddenly, with an audible 'pumping' as the compressor clamps and releases. The bottom end feels tight in a claustrophobic, suffocating way rather than a controlled one.

Cause: Ratio set to 20:1 or ∞:1 (limiting) on a bass guitar, where the signal contains sustained fundamental frequencies that interact with the compressor's time constants at audio frequency rates. Limiting on a low-frequency instrument causes the gain reduction to ride the individual cycles of the waveform, introducing low-frequency distortion that sounds like the bass is choking.

Fix: Reduce ratio to 4:1 to 6:1 for bass guitar control. If hard limiting is needed for peak control, use a second compressor or limiter after the primary compressor, set to catch only the absolute peaks (threshold at −3 to −1 dBFS relative to the primary compressor's output), leaving the primary compressor doing the musical dynamic work at a reasonable ratio.

Slow Release Killing Punch on a Drum Bus

Symptom: The drum bus has a consistent average level but every hit sounds soft and indistinct. The kick doesn't punch; the snare doesn't crack. The kit sounds like it's being played through a pillow. Increasing the individual track levels only makes the problem worse.

Cause: Release time set above 800 ms on the drum bus compressor. The compressor applies gain reduction in response to each kick or snare hit but does not recover fully before the next hit arrives. The next transient therefore arrives at a pre-compressed state — it is hitting a compressor that is already 4–6 dB into gain reduction — and its full dynamic impact is never realized.

Fix: Calculate the tempo-relative release. At 120 BPM, kick hits arrive every 500 ms; set release to 200–350 ms so the compressor recovers at least 80% of gain reduction between hits. At 100 BPM (600 ms between beats), a release of 250–400 ms is appropriate. Start with release at 200 ms and increase until pumping disappears while punch is maintained.

Using Makeup Gain as a Loudness Crutch

Symptom: The mix gets louder after compression but the headroom is gone. Everything hits −3 to −6 dBFS true peak before the mix bus. The master limiter is working 6–10 dB of limiting to hit the streaming LUFS target, introducing artifacts and smearing transients. The final master sounds loud but fatiguing.

Cause: Makeup gain on multiple compressors across the session — drum bus, vocal bus, mix bus — is adding cumulative level gain. Each compressor adds 3–6 dB of makeup gain; by the time the signal reaches the master limiter, the true peak is already high and the limiter is working too hard.

Fix: Audit every compressor in the session and confirm that makeup gain is compensating for the actual gain reduction applied, not inflating beyond it. On a drum bus applying 4 dB of gain reduction, makeup gain should be 4 dB — not 8 dB. Use gain staging discipline: keep the mix bus output at −6 to −3 dBFS headroom before the master limiter, allowing the limiter to do only 1–3 dB of peak limiting work.

The most common compression errors are killing transients with too-fast attack, creating audible pumping with too-fast release, and mistaking makeup gain inflation for sonic improvement — all three sound different, have specific identifiable causes, and have specific numerical fixes.

Red Flags and Green Flags

🔴 Red Flags

  • The mix sounds flat and two-dimensional with no sense of depth between near and distant elements: threshold is too low (below -30 dBFS) and ratio too high (above 6:1), compressing every element into the same dynamic plane — raise threshold until GR averages 2–4 dB only on peaks.
  • The snare drum sounds soft and pillowy with no attack or crack: attack time is under 3 ms and ratio above 6:1 — the compressor is clamping the transient before it develops. Increase attack to 15–25 ms and the snap will return.
  • The vocal sounds like it is breathing or gasping between phrases, with an unnatural swell in level in the gaps: release time is too fast (under 50 ms) for the source material's tempo. Slow the release to 100–200 ms or engage the auto-release mode.
  • The low end of the mix is distorting subtly but the individual tracks sound clean: release time on the mix bus compressor is under 30 ms, causing the gain reduction to pump at sub-audio frequencies and introduce low-order intermodulation distortion — increase mix bus release to 100 ms minimum.
  • Electric guitar sounds thin and harsh after compression where it sounded full before: a high ratio (above 4:1) with a fast attack on a guitar with strong mid-range transients is compressing the warm body of the tone more than the harsh attack, inverting the tonal character. Reduce ratio to 2:1–3:1 and increase attack to 20 ms.
  • The mix A/Bs loudly against the uncompressed version but loses energy when level-matched: excess makeup gain is inflating the perceived quality. Null the output by reducing makeup gain by the exact average GR in dB before A/B comparison — if it still sounds better compressed at equal loudness, the compression is working.

🟢 Green Flags

  • The gain reduction meter on the drum bus moves rhythmically with the groove — 4–6 dB on kick hits, recovering cleanly between beats — indicating the release time is correctly matched to the tempo and the compressor is breathing with the track rather than fighting it.
  • A vocal phrase that previously ranged from -18 dBFS on quiet notes to -3 dBFS on belted notes now sits consistently between -12 and -6 dBFS throughout, confirming threshold and ratio are working together to control the performance without audibly over-compressing.
  • Bypassing the compressor on the mix bus causes the mix to feel looser and slightly chaotic, and elements feel less like they belong together — this confirms the bus compression is providing cohesion (2–3 dB GR) without becoming the loudness tool.
  • The parallel compression blend on the drum bus adds body and density when the fader is raised but the attack and crack of the snare do not diminish — confirming the dry and compressed paths are correctly balanced and the series compressor's attack is set slow enough to preserve the transient.
  • After compression and level-matched A/B comparison, the compressed version of a bass guitar sounds more consistent in level across the fretboard (particularly in the upper register where uncompressed bass typically loses body) — confirming the compressor is controlling the natural acousto-mechanical inconsistency of the instrument.

Your Progression with Compression

Beginner

When you first encounter compression, the most important thing to understand is not how to set the parameters — it is what problem you are trying to solve. Before touching a compressor, identify the dynamic issue: is the vocal jumping 15 dB between quiet and loud phrases? Is the bass disappearing on lower-register notes? Is the kick drum inconsistent between hits? Every compressor decision starts with a specific diagnosis. Once you have a problem to solve, start with the simplest possible setting: 4:1 ratio, medium attack (20 ms), medium release (200 ms), threshold adjusted until you see 4–6 dB of gain reduction on the loudest moments, and makeup gain set to zero. Listen. Then raise the makeup gain until the compressed version is at the same perceived loudness as the bypass. Now you are hearing what the compression is actually doing to the sound, not what the louder level is doing to your perception of the sound. Spend time here — building the ability to hear gain reduction as a change in shape, not a change in level, is the foundational skill of compression.

Intermediate

Once you can hear gain reduction reliably at matched levels, the next layer of understanding is the transient relationship — specifically, how attack time determines which part of the signal gets compressed. Take a snare drum with no compression applied. Listen to the crack. Now set a compressor to 6:1, threshold so it's working 8 dB of reduction, and sweep the attack from 0.5 ms to 50 ms while the track plays. At 0.5 ms, the crack disappears — the compressor catches it before it reaches the output. At 50 ms, the crack comes through fully and the compressor catches only the sustain and room. Everything between those extremes is a decision about how much transient energy you want in the mix. This is where compression goes from a dynamic control tool to a sound-design tool. From here, explore release time: keep the attack fixed at 20 ms and sweep release from 50 ms to 1 second on the same snare. Below 100 ms, you will hear the compressor pumping — recovering so fast that the recovery itself is audible. Above 600 ms, the snare sounds compressed and slow. The sweet spot where the compressor breathes with the performance without audible artifacts is your target, and finding it by ear rather than by number is the skill that separates intermediate from advanced work.

Advanced

When you are ready for professional results, the focus shifts from individual tracks to system behavior — how compressors interact across a signal chain. Start working in series: a slow optical compressor (LA-2A style, 2:1, program-dependent release) before a faster VCA compressor (4:1, 10 ms attack, 150 ms release) on a vocal chain. The optical unit handles the macro dynamic variation across the performance — the 10–15 dB swings between verse and chorus energy — while the VCA handles the micro variation within phrases, the 3–6 dB peak-to-average variation within a single sung line. Neither unit does all the work; the system divides the labor by timescale and topology. Simultaneously, develop the practice of setting your mix bus compressor before beginning a mix. Commit to 2–4 dB of bus gain reduction and mix every element into that compressed bus — your balancing decisions will be calibrated to the final, glued sound rather than to an uncompressed state that will change when the bus comp is applied. Finally, learn to use the sidechain input to make one element drive the compression of another: the kick drum sidechaining the bass compressor is the oldest version of this, but frequency-selective sidechaining — high-passing the sidechain so only the low-end of the kick triggers the bass compression — is the technique that produces low-end lock without losing the body of the bass between hits.

Frequently Asked Questions

A compressor monitors the level of an audio signal and automatically turns it down whenever it rises above a set threshold, by a ratio you specify. When a vocal hits a loud note 8 dB above threshold with a 4:1 ratio, the output rises only 2 dB above threshold instead of 8 dB — the dynamic range is reduced. The makeup gain stage then raises the overall level, which means the average level is louder and the performance sounds more consistent. The perceptual effect ranges from invisible tightening to an obvious pumping and breathing sound, entirely determined by the parameter settings and circuit type.

The clearest audible symptom is the sound losing its sense of depth and three-dimensionality — everything feels flat and equally loud, with no sense of perspective between near and distant elements. On a drum bus, over-compression sounds like the kick and snare are behind a pane of glass: present, but lifeless. Metering tells part of the story: consistent gain reduction of more than 8–10 dB on a single source or more than 4 dB on a mix bus is usually too much. The diagnostic test is to bypass the compressor — if the uncompressed version sounds more alive and dynamic while still being usable, you are working too hard.

Use series compression when you need to control dynamic range for a technical reason — a vocal that clips downstream processors, a bass that moves too much in the mix, a drum bus that has erratic peaks. Use parallel compression when the source already has great dynamics you want to preserve but needs more density and weight underneath. The classic New York drum parallel technique sends the drum bus to a compressor at 10:1 or harder with a fast attack and blends only 20–30% of that compressed signal back under the dry drums — you get the punch of the uncompressed hit and the thickness of the compressed sustain simultaneously.

The 1176 (FET) has attack times as fast as 20 microseconds and responds aggressively to transients — it adds presence and forward energy, and its harmonic character is slightly gritty. Use it on anything that needs to be pushed to the front of a mix: lead vocals, room mics, electric guitars. The LA-2A (optical) has a program-dependent, inherently slow response that cannot be manually dialed — its attack and release adapt to the complexity of the signal. Use it on sources that need transparent density without an audible compressor sound: bass, acoustic guitar, smooth vocal styles. Both on the same vocal in series — 1176 first for peak control, LA-2A second for density — is a combination used on an enormous number of major-label records.

Attack time determines how much of the initial transient passes through before gain reduction engages. A fast attack (1–3 ms) clamps the transient immediately, reducing the crack of a snare hit but keeping the level tightly controlled. A slow attack (15–30 ms) lets the full transient through before the compressor reacts, preserving the snap and perceived punch of the hit. For a snare drum you want presence in the mix, start at 15–20 ms attack, 60 ms release, and 4:1 ratio — then shorten the attack only if peaks are causing problems downstream. The attack setting is the primary tool for transient shaping; treat it as a texture control, not just a protection setting.

Sidechain compression routes an external signal (or a filtered version of the input) to the compressor's detector circuit, so the gain reduction is triggered by one signal but applied to another. The most practical application is ducking: the kick drum signal is fed to the sidechain of a compressor on the bass guitar, so every time the kick hits, the bass ducks by 2–3 dB, creating space in the low end without EQ carving. This is necessary — not optional — in dense electronic and hip-hop productions where the kick and bass occupy the same sub frequencies. Sidechain EQ filtering within the compressor is equally critical: high-passing the sidechain at 80–100 Hz prevents low-frequency content from triggering excessive gain reduction on a vocal bus.

Because makeup gain is masking the damage. When a mix bus compressor applies 6 dB of gain reduction and you apply 6 dB of makeup gain, the output level is nominally the same but the average level is higher — the Fletcher-Munson curve makes louder material sound better to human hearing at the same monitoring volume. Null your output: set the compressor's output 6 dB lower than the input gain reduction to ensure you are evaluating compression decisions at equal perceived loudness. Mix bus compression should be set to no more than 2–4 dB of gain reduction, with a ratio of 2:1 or lower, and attack times of 30–50 ms to preserve the transient content of your drums. If you need more than 4 dB of GR on the mix bus, the dynamics problems exist in the mix itself, not at the bus stage.

Compression at mastering is used for tonal shaping, cohesion, and gentle dynamic control — typically 1–2 dB of gain reduction at a 2:1 ratio with slow attack and auto release, applied before the limiter. It sets the attitude and character of the master. Limiting is used for peak control and final loudness maximization — a brick wall at a true peak ceiling of -1 dBTP, often with 3–6 dB of gain reduction on modern commercial masters. They cannot replace each other: compression shapes the body of the signal and determines how the limiter will subsequently react to transients, while the limiter handles the final ceiling. Running a limiter without a compressor in front produces a brittle, over-limited sound because every peak hits the limiter independently; compression first smooths the input so the limiter engages more evenly across the program material.
