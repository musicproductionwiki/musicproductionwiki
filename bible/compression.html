Compression

noun / signal processing tool · kəm-PRESH-ən
*Every record you've ever loved was shaped by compression you couldn't hear — and that invisibility is the entire point.*
📅 2026-05-15 ⏱ 20 min read 📚 Producer’s Bible
Quick Answer

Compression reduces the dynamic range of an audio signal by attenuating levels above a set threshold. It controls peaks, adds sustain, glues elements together, and shapes transient punch when applied correctly.

Common Misconception

Most producers believe that more compression always makes a mix sound more professional, louder, and more polished — that the thick, dense sound of commercial records comes from aggressive compression on every channel, and that a mix without heavy compression is inherently amateurish or weak.

Here is what is actually happening: the dense, polished sound of professional records comes primarily from gain staging, arrangement decisions, and the strategic application of compression at 2–4 dB of gain reduction on bus elements — not from heavy per-channel compression. Many professional engineers use zero compression on individual channels and rely entirely on clip gain automation and a single well-set bus compressor. Compression does not add energy or richness; it redistributes and controls dynamics. Heavy compression on every channel simultaneously reduces micro-dynamic variation — the subtle loudness differences that make a performance feel alive and human — and replaces it with a static, flat loudness that sounds impressive on a reference track in isolation but becomes fatiguing and dimensionless in a mix. The records that sound the most powerful and dense in modern production — particularly in hip-hop and pop — achieve that density through arrangement (fewer competing elements, each sitting in its own frequency range), careful gain staging, and precisely targeted compression on specific elements, not through maximizing gain reduction on every track. When streaming platforms normalize loudness, a mix with more dynamic range and less compression will often sound more powerful at equivalent LUFS than an over-compressed mix, because its transients — the kicks, snares, and vocal consonants — remain sharp and impactful rather than being rounded off by excessive gain reduction.

What Is Compression?

Every record you've ever loved was shaped by compression you couldn't hear — and that invisibility is the entire point.

Compression is the process of automatically reducing the gain of an audio signal whenever it exceeds a set threshold level. In practical terms, it is a voltage-controlled amplifier with a gain reduction circuit that responds to the amplitude of the incoming signal: loud passages get turned down in real time, quiet passages are left alone, and the result is a signal with a narrower dynamic range. That narrower range is then typically boosted with makeup gain, which raises the average level without raising the peaks — producing the perception of something simultaneously louder, denser, and more controlled than the raw recording.

What separates compression from simply turning a fader down is its time-based, automatic, and continuous nature. A compressor is always listening to the signal and adjusting its gain reduction faster than any human hand could move a fader — sometimes in under a millisecond. That speed is what makes it useful on drums, where transients arrive and disappear in under 5ms. That same speed, misapplied, is what makes compression destructive when it pumps, breathes, or crushes the life out of a performance. The difference between a compressor that helps and one that hurts is almost always the producer's understanding of attack and release — the two parameters that govern the compressor's timing, and the two that most beginners ignore entirely.

Perceptually, compression does several things simultaneously, some of them intended and some of them side effects. It controls peaks, which creates headroom and prevents clipping. It increases sustain, because quieter portions of a note — the tail of a snare, the decay of a guitar chord — are raised relative to the attack. It shapes transient character, making hits feel harder or softer depending on whether you let the initial peak through or catch it. And when applied across a mix bus, it binds individual elements together into a unified sonic image, a phenomenon engineers call glue — a word that sounds mystical but describes something entirely mechanical: the subtle gain reduction applied simultaneously to every element of the mix reduces micro-level dynamic differences between them, making the mix feel cohesive rather than assembled.

Compression is not, despite common assumption, primarily about loudness. A heavily compressed signal sounds loud because its average level is high — the quiet parts have been raised toward the peaks. But compression itself does not add energy. It redistributes it. A limiter — which is a compressor operating at an extreme ratio, typically 10:1 or higher — is what actually prevents a signal from exceeding a ceiling. The distinction matters because producers who reach for compression as a loudness tool without understanding dynamics will consistently over-compress: the signal loses punch, the mix sounds flat, and no amount of EQ will fix what is fundamentally a dynamics problem.

The tool exists in every genre, every format, every production environment. It is on the master bus of every commercial release. It is on the vocal chain of every streaming hit. It is on the drum room microphone at Abbey Road, on the 808 in the trap beat, on the bass guitar in the funk record, on the acoustic guitar in the folk album. Understanding compression at a fundamental level — not just knowing where the threshold knob is, but understanding what the compressor is hearing, when it is acting, and what you want the result to sound like — is the single most transferable skill in music production.

In 2026, compression matters differently than it did in 2006 in two important ways. First, streaming loudness normalization has fundamentally changed the relationship between compression and perceived loudness. Platforms like Spotify normalize playback to approximately -14 LUFS and Apple Music to -16 LUFS. This means that a heavily over-compressed master that measures -7 LUFS will be turned down by the platform until it matches a well-dynamics-managed master at -14 LUFS — and because it has less dynamic range, it will sound worse at equal loudness, not better. The loudness war incentive for over-compression no longer exists on streaming, and producers who compress with restraint and intent now consistently outperform those who compress by habit. Second, the proliferation of high-quality software compressors — accurate emulations of the 1176, LA-2A, SSL G-Bus, Neve 33609, and dozens of others — means that the tonal character of hardware compression is no longer inaccessible. The question is no longer which tool you have access to, but whether you understand what each tool does and why you are choosing it.

Compression automatically reduces gain above a threshold to narrow dynamic range — the foundation of mix control, vocal presence, and the perceived density of every commercial record.

How Compression Works

At its core, a compressor is a voltage-controlled amplifier (VCA) whose gain is determined by a detector circuit monitoring the signal's amplitude. When the input signal crosses the threshold — measured in dBFS in the digital domain, or dBu in the analog domain — the detector sends a control voltage to the gain reduction element, instructing it to reduce the output by an amount determined by the ratio. A ratio of 4:1 means that for every 4 dB the signal exceeds the threshold, only 1 dB reaches the output. At a ratio of 8:1, an input 8 dB above the threshold produces only 1 dB of gain reduction output — at which point the compressor is approaching limiting behavior. At ratios above 10:1 with a fast attack, the compressor becomes a brick-wall limiter in practice. The knee parameter controls how abruptly this gain reduction kicks in: a hard knee engages the full ratio immediately at the threshold, while a soft knee begins gentle attenuation at a point several dB below the threshold and gradually increases to the full ratio, producing a smoother transition that is less audible on material where the signal hovers near the threshold.

The parameters that govern time — attack and release — are where the perceptual character of compression actually lives, and they are the parameters most misunderstood by producers at every level. Attack is the time, typically measured in milliseconds, that the compressor takes to reach full gain reduction after the signal crosses the threshold. A 1ms attack time means the compressor is at full gain reduction within 1 millisecond of the transient — which is fast enough to catch and reduce the initial impact of a snare hit or a plucked bass note. A 30ms attack time means the first 30 milliseconds of that transient pass through unaffected before the compressor clamps down. That 30ms window is what creates punch: you are deliberately letting the attack of the instrument through before the compression engages, which makes the hit feel more aggressive and three-dimensional. Release is the time the compressor takes to return to unity gain after the signal drops below the threshold. A release that is too fast causes the compressor to cycle on and off with the waveform itself, producing a distortion artifact called intermodulation distortion at low frequencies — perceptually this sounds like a chuffing or fluttering on bass and kick. A release that is too slow causes the compressor to hold its gain reduction through the space between notes, which suppresses the natural breath of the performance and produces that suffocated, flat sound that over-compressed vocals exhibit. Getting attack and release right — which means setting them relative to the tempo, the instrument's transient character, and the genre's dynamic expectations — is the actual skill of compression.

Makeup gain is the final element of the compression circuit. Because gain reduction by definition lowers the output level, a gain stage after the detector restores the overall level to approximately where it was before compression — or higher, depending on intent. The critical distinction is that makeup gain raises the floor and ceiling together by a fixed amount, while the compressor has already lowered the peaks by a variable amount based on gain reduction. The result: average level is higher, peak level is controlled, and the signal occupies more of the available dynamic range consistently. In digital environments, the gain reduction meter — the GR meter on any compressor plugin or hardware unit — shows exactly how many dB the compressor is attenuating at any given moment. Watching this meter while adjusting threshold and ratio is the fastest way to understand what the compressor is actually doing to the signal. On a vocal, 3–6 dB of gain reduction on the loudest phrases, with lighter reduction elsewhere, is a functional starting point. On a drum bus, 2–4 dB of gain reduction with a slow attack around 30ms and a musically-timed release is enough to produce glue without killing transients. On a limiter at the mastering stage, gain reduction should rarely exceed 3 dB on peaks, with 1–2 dB being preferable for transparent loudness maximization.

A detector circuit monitors amplitude and drives a gain reduction element when the signal exceeds the threshold; attack and release times determine how quickly the compressor responds, and these timing decisions define the entire sonic character of the effect.

Compression — Key Parameters

Threshold

Technical: The dBFS level at which the compressor begins attenuating the signal. Every decibel above the threshold is subject to gain reduction according to the ratio. A threshold of -18 dBFS means the compressor is inactive on any signal below that level. Perceptual: Setting the threshold lower engages compression on more of the signal, making the track sound more controlled and even. Too low, and the source loses all dynamics — vocals start sounding like they're being squeezed through a tube. A good starting threshold for a dynamic vocal is typically -20 to -14 dBFS, catching the louder phrases while leaving the quieter moments untouched.

Ratio

Technical: The ratio defines how many dB of input signal above the threshold produces 1 dB of output increase. At 4:1, a signal 8 dB above the threshold exits at only 2 dB above it. At infinity:1, the compressor behaves as a limiter — no signal escapes above the threshold regardless of input level. Perceptual: Low ratios (1.5:1 to 3:1) feel transparent — the listener can't identify compression, only a sense of consistent presence. Medium ratios (4:1 to 8:1) give audible control, common on drums and bass. High ratios (10:1 and above) cause the classic squashed, pumping character used deliberately in rock and electronic music. The threshold and ratio always work together — a high ratio with a high threshold may apply less actual gain reduction than a low ratio with a very low threshold.

Attack

Technical: The time constant (in milliseconds) it takes for the compressor to reach full gain reduction after the signal exceeds the threshold. Most compressors measure attack as the time to achieve 63% of the full gain reduction specified by the ratio. Fast attack (0.1–2 ms) catches transients almost immediately. Slow attack (30–150 ms) allows the initial transient to pass before compression clamps down. Perceptual: Attack is the single most powerful shaping tool in compression. A fast attack on a snare kills the crack — the transient is absorbed, the hit sounds soft and controlled. A slow attack (50 ms) on that same snare lets the stick impact through before the compressor catches the body, producing the punchy snap that defined 1980s rock drums. On vocals, attack around 10–30 ms preserves consonant intelligibility while smoothing sustained vowel dynamics.

Release

Technical: The time in milliseconds (or seconds) for the compressor to return to unity gain after the signal falls below the threshold. Most compressors define release as the time to recover 63% of the applied gain reduction. Values range from 20 ms (very fast) to 2000 ms (2 seconds, very slow). Perceptual: Release shapes the rhythm of compression. Too fast on a bass guitar (under 50 ms) causes pumping — the compressor opens and slams shut with each note, creating an unnatural surge. Too slow on a drum bus (over 500 ms) means the compressor never fully opens between hits, killing the groove. The sweet spot for drum bus compression is typically 100–200 ms, allowing the compressor to breathe with the tempo. Program-dependent or auto-release modes use a dual-stage algorithm that avoids both artifacts simultaneously.

Knee

Technical: The knee setting determines whether gain reduction is applied abruptly at the threshold (hard knee) or gradually across a range surrounding it (soft knee). A soft knee of 6 dB begins partial compression 3 dB below the threshold and reaches full ratio 3 dB above it, blending through a curved gain function. Perceptual: Hard knee sounds immediate and assertive — character compressors like the UA 1176 are known partly for this. Soft knee sounds more forgiving and natural, better suited for acoustic instruments and mastering where invisible compression is the goal. For a lead vocal that needs to sit consistently, a soft knee at 4–6 dB prevents the obvious 'clamp' sound at the transition point.

Makeup Gain

Technical: Compression attenuates the signal above the threshold, resulting in an overall reduction in average output level. Makeup gain is manual post-compression amplification to restore perceived loudness, typically adjustable from 0 to +30 dB. Some compressors offer auto-makeup gain that estimates compensation, but this is never perfectly neutral. Perceptual: Makeup gain is not just compensation — it changes how the compressed signal sits in the mix. Bringing a compressed vocal up 6 dB with makeup gain after 6 dB of average gain reduction raises the sustain and noise floor along with the signal, increasing the vocal's sense of presence and density. This is why a well-compressed vocal feels bigger, not just louder. Always level-match when comparing compressed to uncompressed to make fair sonic judgments.

Gain Reduction (GR)

Technical: Gain reduction is the actual attenuation being applied at any given moment, measured in dB, displayed on the compressor's GR meter. It is the real-time product of the threshold, ratio, and input signal level interaction. On a mix bus, 2–4 dB of GR is conservative glue. On a peak-heavy vocal, 6–10 dB of GR is medium-heavy control. Perceptual: Watch the GR meter, not just the output meters. A compressor showing 15 dB of gain reduction on a vocal is working too hard — the performance will sound strangled. The GR meter also reveals the rhythm of compression: on a drum bus, you should see it pumping at roughly the tempo of the track. If it's pinned flat, attack is too fast. If it barely moves, threshold is too high.

Threshold sets where compression begins, ratio sets how aggressively it attenuates, attack and release govern the timing of gain reduction engagement and recovery, knee controls the transition smoothness, and makeup gain restores overall output level after attenuation.

Signal Chain Position

Compression placed after input gain staging and corrective EQ responds to a balanced signal, producing more predictable and musical gain reduction; placing compression before EQ means tonal problems in the source will actively modulate the compressor's behavior.

Interaction Warnings

  • Compression interacts badly with reverb when the compressor is placed after a send-return reverb effect on the same bus: the compressor's gain reduction responds to the reverb tail as well as the dry signal, causing the tail to pump and swell audibly after each note as the compressor releases. The result is an unnatural reverb that appears to breathe in time with the dry source. Fix: place compression before the reverb in the signal chain, or use a dedicated send (not insert) for reverb so the compressor only sees the dry signal.
  • A compressor placed before a noise gate without careful threshold calibration produces an audible low-level pumping artifact: the makeup gain raises the compressor's output during quiet passages, pushing the noise floor up toward the gate's threshold, causing the gate to flutter open and closed between phrases. The result is an intermittent rush of noise at the end of sustained notes or between vocal phrases. Fix: lower the makeup gain by 2–3 dB and raise the gate's threshold to match the new noise floor level, or reverse the order so the gate removes noise before the compressor amplifies it.
  • Heavy compression (8:1 or above, 8+ dB GR) applied before saturation causes the saturator to receive an unnaturally consistent input level, producing constant harmonic distortion rather than the dynamic, level-responsive saturation that gives analog saturation its musical quality. The result is a hardened, statically distorted tone rather than the warm, touch-sensitive saturation character of tube or tape. Fix: reduce compression ratio to 3:1 or 4:1 before the saturator, or place a light saturation stage before compression so the saturator responds to the natural dynamics of the source.

History of Compression

Harold Leak at the BBC in 1934 filed one of the earliest practical designs for an automatic gain control device — a circuit that reduced amplifier gain when signal levels became too loud for early broadcast transmission equipment. This was not compression in the modern musical sense; it was a technical solution to a transmission problem. But it established the core principle: a circuit that monitors signal amplitude and responds by reducing gain automatically. The problem of uncontrolled dynamics on live radio, where a performer's shout could overload a transmitter and a whisper could disappear into noise, was the original commercial driver for every compressor that followed.

The RCA BA-6A leveling amplifier, deployed at NBC Radio in New York throughout the late 1940s, was the first device to bring something resembling musical compression into regular broadcast use. Engineers at 30 Rockefeller Plaza used it to manage the dramatic dynamic range of live orchestral broadcasts — not to make music louder, but to make it transmittable. What they discovered, without initially intending it, was that the processed signal also sounded more present and forward — closer, more intimate. This quality, the perceived closeness created by reduced dynamic range, would become one of compression's most deliberately exploited characteristics in the recording studio era that followed.

Bill Putnam Sr., working at Universal Recording in Chicago in 1957, was among the first engineers to deliberately apply compression as a creative and tonal tool rather than purely a technical correction. His use of the Fairchild 670 — a tube-based variable-mu compressor that cost more than most automobiles at the time — on vocal sessions for artists including Frank Sinatra established the idea that compression could shape a performance's emotional character. The Fairchild 670's slow, program-dependent release gave vocals a naturalness and warmth that faster, more aggressive compression destroyed. Putnam understood that the compressor's timing needed to breathe with the performance, not fight it — a principle that remains the correct approach seven decades later.

The Universal Audio 1176 Peak Limiter, released in 1967 and designed by Bill Putnam's son Bill Putnam Jr. at United Audio in Los Angeles, fundamentally changed what compression sounded like. The 1176 was the first commercially successful FET (Field Effect Transistor) compressor, and its transistor-based gain reduction stage was dramatically faster than tube-based variable-mu designs — capable of attack times as fast as 20 microseconds. This speed, combined with the characteristic harmonic distortion of the FET circuit, produced a sound that was aggressive, present, and punchy in a way that suited rock and soul recording. John Lennon's vocals on the Beatles' Abbey Road sessions, engineered by Geoff Emerick at Abbey Road Studios in London in 1969, were processed through 1176 units that shaped the intimate, slightly compressed sound of those recordings. The 1176 remains in constant use today — both the original hardware and its software emulations — on drums, vocals, bass, and bus applications across every genre.

The SSL G-Series Bus Compressor, introduced by Solid State Logic in the early 1980s and built into the SSL 4000 G console at studios including Britannia Row in London and The Hit Factory in New York, established what most producers now think of as the sound of a compressed mix. Engineers including Hugh Padgham, who used the SSL G-Bus compressor on Peter Gabriel's So in 1986 at Ashford Mill, and Chris Lord-Alge, who built his mix sound around SSL bus compression throughout the late 1980s and 1990s, popularized the use of a single, lightly applied compressor across the entire stereo mix bus. The SSL G-Bus compressor at a ratio of 2:1, attack of 30ms, release of Auto, and 2–3 dB of gain reduction became the de facto bus compression sound of commercial pop and rock for three decades. Its plugin emulation, available from SSL, Waves, UAD, and others, continues to be the most commonly reached-for bus compressor in 2026.

From BBC broadcast limiters in 1934 through the Fairchild 670, UREI 1176, and SSL G-Bus compressor, compression evolved from a technical necessity into the defining tonal tool of recorded music.

How Producers Use Compression

Drums: Compression on drums is primarily about transient control and sustain shaping. On individual drum hits — kick and snare specifically — the approach divides into two philosophies: letting the transient through for punch, or catching it for density. For punch on a snare, set the attack between 20–40ms so the initial crack passes uncompressed, then set the release to match the snare's natural decay — typically 50–150ms for a dry snare, longer for a roomy one. A ratio of 4:1 on a threshold set to catch only the loudest 6–8 dB of the hit is a solid starting point. For a thicker, more compressed snare sound — the classic hip-hop snare — shorten the attack to 1–5ms to catch the transient and increase the ratio to 8:1 or higher. On the drum bus, use bus compression with a slower attack (30–50ms), a musically timed release (set the release so the GR meter is recovering between hits — play at tempo and adjust until the needle breathes with the groove), a ratio of 2:1 to 4:1, and target 2–4 dB of gain reduction. This is where the kit becomes a kit rather than a collection of individual sounds.

Vocals: Vocals are the most compression-intensive instrument in most mixes because the human voice has the widest natural dynamic range of any instrument — a trained singer can move 20+ dB between a whisper and a belt. The standard vocal chain involves two stages: first, clip gain or volume automation to manually ride the performance so that the loudest and quietest phrases are within about 6 dB of each other before any compression touches the signal; second, a compressor (often two in series) to handle what automation cannot. For transparent vocal control, an optical compressor — LA-2A style, with its program-dependent timing — responds naturally to the rise and fall of a vocal performance because its release time slows down as the signal drops, preventing the pumping that a fixed-release VCA compressor would introduce on sustained notes. Set for 4–8 dB of gain reduction on the loudest phrases. For a more aggressive, present vocal sound — R&B, pop, rock — an 1176-style FET compressor at a fast attack (around 1–5ms), medium release (50–100ms), ratio of 4:1 to 8:1, gives the vocal an up-front, dense sound. Many engineers run an optical compressor for leveling followed by a FET compressor for character.

Bass guitar and 808: Bass needs compression that is fast enough to catch transients but releases slowly enough to avoid distortion artifacts on the low-frequency waveform. For live bass guitar, a VCA compressor with a 10–20ms attack and a 100–200ms release at a ratio of 4:1, with 4–8 dB of gain reduction on the peaks, keeps the low end consistent without audible pumping. For 808 sub bass in hip-hop and trap, sidechained compression from the kick drum is the defining technique: the 808 ducks on every kick hit and rises in the space between, creating that bouncing, rhythmic sub relationship that defines the genre. Set the sidechain release to match the tempo so the 808 is fully recovered before the next kick arrives — at 140 BPM, that means a release of approximately 200–250ms for a quarter-note kick pattern.

Mix bus and parallel compression: Bus compression applied to the stereo mix — 2:1 ratio, 30ms attack, Auto or musically timed release, 2–3 dB of gain reduction — provides glue without significantly changing the dynamics of the mix. This is the most common application of the SSL G-Bus compressor emulation. Parallel compression (blending compressed and uncompressed signals) is the technique for drums specifically when you want the density of heavy compression but the transient punch of the uncompressed signal. Send the drum bus to a parallel compression channel, apply aggressive compression (8:1 or higher, fast attack, 10–15 dB of gain reduction), and blend it under the dry signal at around 20–30% wet until the drums feel thicker without losing their snap. New York compression on drums is a variation of this technique using extreme settings — ratio at maximum (20:1), attack at fastest (0.1ms), threshold set so the compressor is working constantly — and the result blended at a very low level (10–15%) to add density and harmonic excitement to the drum bus.

DAW specifics:
In Ableton Live: The Glue Compressor (modeled after the SSL G-Bus) is the best built-in option for bus compression. Set it with the Soft Clip enabled for gentle limiting on peaks. For parallel compression on a drum rack, use a return track with heavy compression and blend with the Sends knob on the drum bus channel.
In Logic Pro: The Vintage VCA (modeled after the SSL G-Bus) and Vintage Opto (modeled after the LA-2A) are the two built-in compressors worth knowing deeply. The Vintage VCA's Mix knob enables parallel compression natively — no parallel routing required. Set it to 30–50% for drum bus parallel applications.
In FL Studio: Fruity Peak Controller can drive a Parametric EQ 2's gain parameter as a sidechain compressor — useful for frequency-dependent ducking. For standard compression, the Maximus multiband compressor has a full-band mode that functions as a transparent master bus compressor at low ratios.
In Pro Tools: The stock Dyn3 Compressor/Limiter is accurate and low-CPU but sonically neutral. For mix bus work, most Pro Tools engineers rely on UAD or Waves plugins — specifically the UAD SSL G-Bus Compressor or the Waves API 2500 — inserted on the Master Fader.

Compression is applied differently across drums (transient shaping and glue), vocals (leveling and presence), bass (consistency and sidechain ducking), and mix bus (cohesion), with DAW-specific implementations in Ableton, Logic, FL Studio, and Pro Tools.

Stereo image width can shift subtly during heavy gain reduction on hardware units due to channel imbalances in aging components.Plugin stereo compressors link channels mathematically precisely — both channels receive identical gain reduction regardless of content. This is more transparent for mastering but loses the subtle stereo movement of aged hardware.DAW stock stereo compressors are linked precisely by default. Some (Logic's Compressor) offer independent L/R or M/S operation, which is useful for mastering applications that hardware often requires dedicated units to achieve.
Overload CharacterWhen driven hard (20+ dB GR), hardware compressors clip transformers or saturate tubes in musically complex ways — the distortion is often even-order and harmonically rich. The 1176 driven into overload is a usable creative texture.UAD 1176 and FET plugin emulations model the overload behavior of the hardware. However, digital clipping at the plugin output stage (if overdriven past 0 dBFS internally) is harsher than analog saturation. Trim the output to avoid digital overload.Stock compressors clip digitally and cleanly when overloaded — no harmonic richness, just harsh clipping above 0 dBFS. Keep output levels below -3 dBFS when using stock compressors aggressively.

Before and After

Without Compression

An uncompressed lead vocal with clip gain applied but no dynamics processing: the signal averages around -22 dBFS with peaks hitting -6 dBFS on the loudest phrases — a dynamic range of approximately 16 dB within a single take. The loudest word in the chorus is 14 dB louder than the quietest line in the verse. In the mix, the loud phrases jump forward and compete with the lead instruments; the quiet phrases disappear under the guitars and keyboards. The consonants on hard syllables ('p', 't', 'k') produce sharp peaks that are 8–10 dB above the surrounding vowels. The vocal feels uneven and difficult to sit at a consistent place in the mix — raising the fader for the quiet sections makes the loud phrases unbearably forward.

With Compression

After two stages of compression: first, an optical-style compressor (LA-2A emulation) set with the Gain (threshold) control adjusted for 4–6 dB of peak GR, program-dependent release, reducing the most extreme dynamic jumps. Second, a FET compressor (1176 emulation) at 4:1 ratio, 3ms attack, 60ms release, threshold set for 2–4 dB of GR on the remaining peaks. Combined makeup gain of approximately +6 dB restores overall level. Result: the vocal now averages -16 dBFS with peaks at -9 dBFS — a dynamic range of 7 dB rather than 16. The loudest chorus phrase is now only 5 dB louder than the quietest verse line. In the mix, the vocal sits at a consistent position — fader set to -3 dB on the vocal channel, and it occupies the same relative space from verse to chorus without fighting the instruments on the loud phrases or disappearing on the quiet ones. The consonant peaks are controlled by the fast FET compressor, preventing harshness. The vocal sounds more present, more intimate, and more even — while retaining the natural performance dynamics that make it feel human rather than machine-leveled.

Compression In The Wild

Michael Jackson — Billie Jean (1982) · Produced by Quincy Jones

0:00 — Throughout

The kick drum at the opening of Billie Jean — recorded at Westlake Audio in Los Angeles by engineer Bruce Swedien — is one of the most studied examples of drum compression in recording history. The kick's attack is sharp and immediate, but its tail sustains far longer than a natural kick would. This is the result of compression with a medium-fast attack (approximately 10ms, letting the initial transient through for punch) and a slow release (around 300ms, allowing the decay to sustain). Listen for the way the kick body pushes forward in the mix without losing its initial impact — a result of the compressor raising the tail of the decay through makeup gain.

Listening Guide

Put on headphones. Play from 0:00. Focus entirely on the kick drum before any other instrument enters. Notice how the kick has a hard, defined click on the attack followed by a substantial sustained body. Count the milliseconds between the click and the fade — it is considerably longer than an uncompressed kick would decay. This is compression holding the tail up. Now notice how the kick sits in the mix when the bass enters at 0:29 — the compression has controlled its peak so it never obscures the bass fundamental, yet it is entirely present. This is Compression doing dynamic range management and sustain shaping simultaneously.

The Beatles — Come Together (1969) · Produced by George Martin

0:00 — 0:10 (bass intro)

Paul McCartney's bass at the opening of Come Together, recorded at Abbey Road Studios in London and engineered by Geoff Emerick, demonstrates classic DI bass compression using the UREI 1176. The bass has an even, singing quality across its entire dynamic range — rapid picked notes and sustained notes sit at nearly the same perceived volume. The 1176 at a ratio of 4:1 with a fast release is catching each note's peak and releasing between notes, producing an even, forward-sitting bass line with no note disappearing into the track.

Listening Guide

Put on headphones. Play from 0:00. Listen to the bass in isolation during the intro — notice how each note has a similar perceived volume despite the naturally varying pick dynamics of a bass guitar. A similar uncompressed bass line would have dramatic volume differences between a hard-picked attack and a light finger-plucked note. The evenness you hear is compression working. Now at 0:30 when the full band enters, notice how the bass still cuts through without any note disappearing. This is Compression doing leveling — reducing the dynamic range so every note occupies the same space in the mix.

Pharrell Williams' vocal on Get Lucky, mixed by Mick Guzauski, is an example of transparent optical-style compression on a pop vocal. The vocal sits at a remarkably consistent level through phrases of varying intensity — Pharrell's natural delivery varies considerably in dynamics, yet in the mix every word is equally present. The compression is working hard — likely 6–8 dB of gain reduction on the loudest phrases — but the program-dependent release of the optical-style compressor (or its emulation) prevents any audible pumping or breathing.

Listening Guide

Put on headphones. Play from 1:13 as Pharrell's vocal enters. Listen through the entire verse and notice how the vocal level does not waver despite the melodic phrases rising and falling in pitch and intensity. Sing the melody yourself — notice how much louder you naturally get on the high notes. Now listen to the recording again. Those dynamic swings have been compressed into a consistent, forward-sitting performance. This is Compression doing vocal leveling. Pay specific attention at 1:40 when the phrase 'lucky' ends — the word does not disappear or get swallowed; the compressor's release has kept the tail of the word present.

Kendrick Lamar — HUMBLE. (2017) · Produced by Mike WiLL Made-It

0:00 — 0:04 (kick hit)

The opening kick of HUMBLE. is one of the most recognizable compressed drum sounds in contemporary hip-hop. Produced by Mike WiLL Made-It, the kick has an extreme transient — a sharp, almost painful attack — followed by a very fast, tight decay. This is a compressed, clipped kick sample processed for maximum impact. The attack time on the compressor is extremely fast (under 1ms), catching and limiting the peak, while the release is equally fast (under 50ms), allowing the compressor to recover before the next hit. The result is a kick with enormous perceived density despite a very short physical duration.

Listening Guide

Put on headphones. Play from 0:00 and listen specifically to the kick drum's shape. Notice the extremely sharp attack transient — like a fist hitting a table — and how immediately it stops. Compare this mentally to a natural acoustic kick drum, which has a longer, more gradual attack and a longer decay. The artificial sharpness and density of this kick is the result of heavy compression and limiting applied to a sample. At 0:30 when the vocal enters, notice how the compressed kick cuts through without competing with Kendrick's voice in the frequency spectrum. This is Compression being used as a sound-design tool, not just a dynamic control tool.

3:45 — Bass and drum outro

The iconic bass and drum section that opens the final section of The Chain, recorded at the Village Recorder in Los Angeles and engineered by Ken Caillat, demonstrates bus compression at a time when SSL consoles were beginning to define the sound of professional rock recording. The drums and bass in this section are compressed together, creating the locked, driven feeling of the section. The bus compression reduces the micro-dynamic differences between John McVie's bass and Mick Fleetwood's kick drum, making them feel like a single rhythmic organism. Listening Guide

Put on headphones. Play from 3:45 as the bass-and-drum section kicks in. Listen to the kick drum and bass simultaneously — notice how they feel physically locked together, like they are occupying the same rhythmic and dynamic space rather than competing for it. This is bus compression doing glue. Now try to hear the individual dynamic variations — notice how the bass does not jump forward on harder-plucked notes or disappear on lighter ones. Now notice how the entire section has a sense of forward motion and energy that is inseparable from the compression applied. This is what glue compression does at the bus level.

From the kick drum of Billie Jean to the bus compression of Fleetwood Mac's The Chain, professional compression is defined by specific, intentional gain reduction that is rarely audible as compression but always audible as quality.

Types of Compression

VCA (Voltage-Controlled Amplifier)

dbx 160A, SSL G-Bus Compressor, API 2500

VCA compressors use a voltage-controlled amplifier in the gain reduction path, enabling very precise, fast, and repeatable gain control. Character is clean and punchy with minimal coloration in transparent modes, but the SSL G-Bus and API 2500 introduce harmonic density that glues a mix bus without sounding obviously processed. Ideal for drum buses, mix buses, and any source needing aggressive, controlled compression at fast time constants — the SSL G-Bus at 4:1, 30 ms attack, auto release, with 2–4 dB of GR is one of the most copied bus compression moves in professional mixing.

FET (Field-Effect Transistor)

Universal Audio 1176LN, 1176AE

FET compressors replicate the behavior of tube circuits using transistors, producing a fast, aggressive response with distinctive harmonic coloration — particularly a slight odd-harmonic saturation under heavy gain reduction. The 1176 is defined by its near-instantaneous attack (as fast as 20 microseconds), making it ideal for sources with sharp transients like vocals, bass DI, and snare. The 'All-Buttons-In' mode on the 1176 (engaging all four ratio buttons simultaneously) creates a chaotic, distorted compression character used deliberately on room mics, buses, and vocals when aggression is the goal.

Optical

Teletronix LA-2A, UA LA-3A

Optical compressors use a light-emitting element and photocell to control gain reduction — the light dims or brightens in response to the input signal, and the photocell's response time is inherently nonlinear and program-dependent. This produces a naturally musical, smooth compression character that is difficult to over-apply on vocals and acoustic instruments. The LA-2A has no attack or release knobs; it sets its own time constants based on the incoming audio, which means it responds differently to a guitar strum than to a sustained piano chord. This self-regulating behavior is precisely why it has remained the default vocal compressor for six decades.

Variable-Mu (Tube)

Fairchild 670, Manley Variable Mu, Vari-Mu

Variable-mu compressors use tubes whose amplification factor decreases as the grid bias increases — the tube itself is the gain reduction element, with no VCA, FET, or optical element in the path. This produces an exceptionally warm, three-dimensional compression character with slow time constants (attack typically 20–70 ms, release 50–500 ms) that make it unsuitable for transient control but ideal for adding density to a stereo bus or full mix at the mastering stage. 1–3 dB of gain reduction through a well-calibrated Manley Variable Mu is used by mastering engineers to add commercial weight to a mix without any audible compression artifacts.

Multiband

Weiss DS1-MK3, TC Electronic MD3

Multiband compressors divide the frequency spectrum into two or more bands (typically 3–5), applying independent compression to each. This allows dynamic control of a problematic low-mid buildup without affecting the high-frequency compression, or de-essing a vocal through frequency-specific gain reduction. Multiband is enormously powerful and proportionally dangerous for inexperienced users — it can fundamentally alter the spectral balance of a source in ways that are difficult to reverse downstream. In mastering, 3–5 dB of gain reduction in the 200–400 Hz range of a dense mix using a Weiss DS1 is precise surgery; on an individual track, the same move sounds unnatural without meticulous crossover calibration.

VCA compressors are fast and precise for transient control, FET compressors are fast and colored for aggressive character, optical compressors are smooth and program-dependent for natural vocal leveling, and variable-mu tube compressors are warm and slow for transparent glue applications.

The Producer's Verdict

For most producers, start with: a compressor on the drum bus set to 2:1 ratio, 30ms attack, auto release, threshold adjusted for 2–3 dB of gain reduction on the loudest hits, and a compressor on the lead vocal set to 4:1 ratio, 10ms attack, 100ms release, threshold adjusted for 3–6 dB of gain reduction on the loudest phrases. Learn these two applications until you can set them by ear without looking at the GR meter. Everything else in compression is a variation of these two contexts.

If you are working on electronic music — specifically techno, house, or any genre where the kick-bass relationship is the rhythmic foundation — use sidechain compression from the kick into the bass and the lead synth, set with a fast attack (1–5ms), a release timed to the tempo (so the sidechain ducking creates a rhythmic pumping that feels intentional), and a ratio of 4:1 to 8:1. This is not a correction — it is a compositional device, and the pumping effect is the point. Set it deliberately, not accidentally.

Avoid bus compression on the mix bus until you understand why your mix sounds the way it does without it. Bus compression is not a problem-solver. It makes a good mix better. Applied to a mix with level, arrangement, or frequency problems, it makes those problems harder to identify and fix because it changes the overall character and makes A/B comparison unreliable. Get your mix sounding the way you want it at the fader level first, with EQ if needed, before inserting a bus compressor. When you insert it and the mix immediately feels more cohesive and energized at 2–3 dB of GR, you will understand what it is doing — and that understanding is the prerequisite.

Common Mistakes with Compression

Attack Too Fast — Killing the Transient

Symptom: Drums sound flat, lifeless, and small. The kick and snare lose their punch and feel more like thuds than hits. The mix sounds squashed even at moderate gain reduction levels.

Cause: Attack time set too fast — typically under 5ms on a drum bus or individual drum channel — allowing the compressor to clamp down on the initial transient before it reaches the output. At 1ms attack on a snare, the first 1ms of the hit is the actual crack; catching it with compression removes the defining impact of the sound.

Fix: On drums, start the attack at 30ms and adjust downward only if you specifically want a more compressed, dense sound. For the drum bus, 20–40ms attack is the functional range for preserving punch while achieving glue. Listen for when the snap and impact return to the hits as you slow the attack — that is the correct setting for most applications.

Release Too Fast on Bass — Low-End Distortion

Symptom: The bass sounds distorted, buzzy, or fluttery — especially on sustained low notes. This is not a saturation or clipping artifact; it has a cyclic, churning quality that correlates with the bass note's fundamental frequency.

Cause: Release time set faster than the period of the lowest fundamental frequency in the signal. At 60 Hz (low B string), one period is approximately 16ms. If the release is set to 10ms, the compressor is cycling open and closed multiple times per waveform cycle, producing intermodulation distortion. This is particularly destructive on 808 sub bass, which can go below 40 Hz.

Fix: Set the release to a minimum of 50ms on bass guitar and a minimum of 100ms on 808 or sub bass. As a rule: the release must be longer than the period of the lowest note you expect the compressor to process. For sub bass at 40 Hz, the period is 25ms — set release to at least 50–75ms to avoid distortion artifacts.

Over-Compression on the Mix Bus

Symptom: The mix sounds loud but lifeless. Individual elements — particularly the kick, snare, and lead vocals — feel buried or pushed back rather than forward. The mix lacks air and space. When you A/B against the uncompressed mix, the processed version sounds like it is under a blanket.

Cause: Too much gain reduction on the mix bus — typically more than 6 dB — combined with a fast attack that is catching and reducing the transients of every drum hit across the entire mix. At this point the compressor is no longer gluing; it is flattening.

Fix: On the mix bus, target 2–3 dB of gain reduction maximum on the loudest moments. If the compressor is showing 6+ dB of GR consistently, the individual track levels need to be addressed before the bus compressor is applied. Reduce individual track levels or pull back elements that are overloading the bus, then re-engage the bus compressor with lighter settings.

Threshold Too Low — Compressing Everything

Symptom: The track sounds flat and lacks dynamics. Quiet, intimate moments are the same apparent volume as loud, intense moments. The performance loses its emotional arc. On vocals specifically, soft breaths and loud phrases feel identical in level.

Cause: Threshold set too low — often at -30 dBFS or below on a signal whose average level is around -18 to -20 dBFS. At this setting, the compressor is in constant gain reduction on virtually every portion of the signal, including the quietest passages that should remain uncompressed.

Fix: Set the threshold so the compressor is only engaging on the loudest 20–30% of the signal. On a vocal with an average level of -18 dBFS and peaks at -6 dBFS, a threshold of -12 to -15 dBFS targets only the loud peaks. Verify with the GR meter: the needle should be moving only on the loudest phrases, sitting at zero on the quieter passages.

Release Too Slow — The Suffocated Mix

Symptom: The mix sounds dark and flat after the loudest moments. Following a chorus or a loud vocal phrase, the mix seems to dim and lose energy — then gradually return to normal over a second or two. This is audible pumping: the compressor pulled down on the loud moment and is recovering slowly through the quieter passage that follows.

Cause: Release time set too long — typically 500ms or longer — on a bus compressor or vocal compressor with significant gain reduction. The compressor applied 6 dB of reduction on the loud section and is still holding some of that reduction through the beginning of the quiet section, raising it out only gradually.

Fix: Set the release so the compressor fully recovers between the loudest moments in the program material. On a mix bus at 120 BPM with a 4/4 pattern, a release of 100–150ms typically allows recovery between kick hits. Use the GR meter: watch for the needle returning to zero between hits. Many compressors offer an Auto release mode that calculates a musically appropriate release time from the program material — this is a valid starting point, particularly on bus applications.

Ignoring Makeup Gain — Comparing at Different Levels

Symptom: The compressed signal always sounds better than the uncompressed signal in A/B comparisons. The producer concludes that more compression is always better and continuously increases settings. The final mix is over-compressed.

Cause: A compressed signal with makeup gain applied is louder than the uncompressed signal. Louder sounds better to human perception at any volume level — this is a psychoacoustic fact, not a judgment. When producers A/B compressed vs. uncompressed without matching levels, the compressed version wins every time regardless of whether the compression is actually beneficial.

Fix: Before any A/B comparison, match the output level of the compressed signal to the uncompressed signal using the makeup gain control. Both should measure the same average level (LUFS) on a meter. At matched levels, you are comparing the sonic character of the compression, not its loudness. If the compressed signal still sounds better at matched levels, the compression is adding something. If it sounds worse or identical, you may not need it.

The six most damaging compression errors — killing transients with a fast attack, inducing low-end distortion with a fast release, over-compressing the mix bus, setting the threshold too low, using a release too slow for the tempo, and comparing compressed signals at mismatched levels — all produce specific, identifiable symptoms with specific, correctable causes.

Red Flags and Green Flags

🔴 Red Flags

  • Pumping on a vocal (the room noise rises audibly between words): release time is too fast, typically under 40 ms — raise release to 80–150 ms and the surging will diminish or disappear.
  • Kick drum sounds soft and pillowy with no initial impact even though it felt punchy in solo: attack is too fast (under 5 ms) on the drum bus, absorbing the transient. Raise attack to 30–50 ms and the punch returns within seconds.
  • Bass guitar loses note definition between pitches — notes blur together at moderate GR of 6 dB: release is too slow (over 400 ms) for the playing tempo; the compressor is not recovering fully between notes. Reduce release to 100–150 ms.
  • Vocals sound strangled or choked on loud phrases despite only moderate ratio (4:1): threshold is set too low, engaging compression on nearly all of the signal. The compressor is working continuously rather than catching peaks — raise threshold by 4–6 dB so GR only occurs on the loudest 20% of the performance.
  • Mix bus compressor causes the snare to disappear when the full mix hits: sidechain is responding to the low-frequency energy of the kick drum, triggering disproportionate gain reduction that affects the entire mix including the snare. Fix: engage the sidechain HPF (typically 80–120 Hz) to prevent the kick from over-triggering the detector.
  • Acoustic guitar sounds thin after compression: too much GR (10+ dB) at a fast attack (under 10 ms) is squashing the pick transient and the body resonance together, removing the instrument's natural spatial character. Back off to 4–6 dB GR with a 20–30 ms attack.

🟢 Green Flags

  • The gain reduction meter moves rhythmically with the tempo — on a drum bus at 120 BPM, seeing the needle dip and recover approximately in time with the groove confirms the release is calibrated to the track's pulse rather than fighting it.
  • A vocal sits at a consistent level throughout a verse without a single automation move — 3–6 dB of GR on the loudest phrases and near-zero on quieter ones means the compressor is working only when needed.
  • When you bypass the mix bus compressor, the mix sounds slightly wider, less cohesive, and lighter — this contrast confirms the compressor was adding glue and density without audible artifacts.
  • After adding makeup gain to compensate for 6 dB of average gain reduction on a vocal, the level-matched compressed signal sounds more present and forward in the mix than the dry signal at the same peak level — this is the hallmark of correctly applied compression increasing perceived loudness without clipping.
  • The kick drum has both a defined transient click and sustained body when compressed with a 30–50 ms attack: hearing both elements distinctly confirms the attack is letting the initial hit through before the compressor engages.
  • Parallel compression blend at 30–40% adds sustain and density to a drum bus without making the overhead cymbals sound crushed — the cymbals retain air while the room and toms gain weight, which is the primary goal of the New York compression technique.

Your Progression with Compression

Beginner

When you first encounter compression, the instinct is to hear it working — to set the threshold low enough and the ratio high enough that the gain reduction is obvious and audible. Resist this. The first skill to develop is not hearing compression; it is hearing the problems that compression solves. Spend time listening to an uncompressed vocal take with the level automation stripped out — notice how the loud phrases jump forward by 10–15 dB, how the quiet phrases disappear into the track, how the performance is uneven. Now apply a compressor at a 4:1 ratio with the threshold set so you see 4–6 dB of GR on the loudest phrases, a 10ms attack, and a 100ms release. Adjust the makeup gain so the output level matches the input level. Compare: the vocal is not louder overall, but it is more consistently present. That consistency is what compression does. Begin with one compressor per application — drums, vocal, bass — and set it to achieve a specific, audible result: even the vocal, control the kick peaks, thicken the bass sustain. Learn what 3 dB of gain reduction looks like and sounds like on a GR meter before you pursue more.

Intermediate

Once the basic control functions are clear, the intermediate stage is about developing an ear for the timing parameters and building the discipline to match compression to the material rather than applying a fixed setting to everything. Set up a drum session with no compression and learn to hear the individual dynamic variations between hits — the way a snare ghost note sits 15 dB below the backbeat, the way a kick varies in level between a hard hit and a softer groove hit. Now apply bus compression and adjust the attack until the snare backbeat has the punch you want, and the release until the compressor recovers cleanly between hits. Do this by ear, checking the GR meter only to confirm what you hear. Practice parallel compression on drums: route the drum bus to a parallel channel, apply heavy compression (8:1 or higher), and blend it at different levels while listening for the point where density increases without the snap being reduced. Develop opinions about different compressor types — run an 1176 emulation against an LA-2A emulation on the same vocal and learn to articulate why each sounds different. The 1176 is faster and more colored in the mids and highs; the LA-2A is slower and smoother with a rising low-mid warmth as it compresses harder. Understanding why you reach for one over the other is the intermediate plateau.

Advanced

At the advanced level, compression becomes a compositional and tonal tool rather than a correction tool. You are making decisions about which compressor type — VCA, FET, optical, variable-mu — suits the tonal character of the source and the genre, and about whether the gain reduction is creating a rhythmic relationship with the tempo. You are using sidechain compression for frequency-specific ducking — not just kick-to-bass sidechaining, but ducking the low-mids of a rhythm guitar when the bass plays a note in the same frequency range, or ducking the reverb return when the dry signal plays so the reverb breathes rather than accumulates. You are using multiband compression surgically — not as a master bus safety net, but as a band-specific dynamics tool that addresses specific frequency ranges with different timing characteristics. You are measuring the loudness of your masters in LUFS and making informed decisions about the relationship between compression, dynamic range, and streaming normalization — understanding that a master at -14 LUFS with 8 LU of dynamic range will be played back at the same level as a master at -9 LUFS with 3 LU of dynamic range, and that the former will often sound more powerful and impactful because its transients remain intact. At this level, you are not asking what compression does. You are asking what this specific compressor does to this specific signal at this specific moment in this specific song — and you are answering that question with your ears.

Frequently Asked Questions

A compressor automatically reduces the gain of a signal whenever it exceeds a set threshold level. The result is a smaller difference between the loudest and quietest parts of the signal — a narrower dynamic range. This makes it easier to place a vocal in a mix without it disappearing on quiet phrases or jumping out on loud ones. Think of it as an automated hand on a fader, pulling down only when the signal gets too loud, at a speed and aggressiveness you define.

Watch the gain reduction meter and watch for two specific symptoms: the source loses its sense of movement — everything feels the same volume — or you hear an audible 'pumping' where the background noise surges up between notes. On a vocal, over-compression above 10 dB of continuous GR sounds strangled, like someone is gripping the throat. The corrective move is either raising the threshold (so compression engages less often) or lowering the ratio (so engagement is gentler). A healthy vocal compressor session rarely needs more than 6–8 dB of gain reduction on the loudest moments.

Attack determines how quickly the compressor responds after the threshold is crossed; release determines how quickly it lets go. Ratio tells you how hard compression is applied — attack and release tell you when. For most sources, attack and release are the variables that determine whether compression sounds natural or mechanical. On a kick drum, an 80 ms attack at 4:1 sounds punchier than a 2 ms attack at 2:1 because the slower attack passes the transient through unaffected. Nail the attack and release first; only then fine-tune the ratio.

Compress after EQ in the majority of cases. If a source has a problematic low-mid buildup at 250 Hz, that buildup will repeatedly trigger the compressor and color the entire dynamic response before you have a chance to shape it. Cutting 250 Hz first with a high-pass or parametric EQ, then compressing, means the compressor responds to a more balanced signal. The exception: if you want EQ to shape the compressed sound — adding high-end brightness after compression to avoid the HF content from triggering faster gain reduction — then EQ post-compression is valid. Many engineers use both: a surgical pre-compression EQ and a broad, musical post-compression EQ.

Pressing all four ratio buttons simultaneously on the 1176 (originally a hardware error mode) causes the compressor to engage an undocumented, chaotic gain reduction behavior — the unit operates outside its normal ratio curves, often producing 12–20 dB of gain reduction with a highly distorted, saturated character. It is a deliberate effect, not a neutral compression setting. It was used famously on vocals by Chris Lord-Alge and on room mics by engineers seeking a wrecked, blown-out room sound. On a modern plugin emulation (UA's own 1176 plugin faithfully reproduces this mode), push the threshold down so you're seeing 15+ dB of GR and blend it in at 30–50% parallel wet for a vocal crush that retains intelligibility.

Mix bus compression is glue, not control. It should not be working hard enough to be heard as compression — its job is to make the elements of the mix feel like they were recorded together, not assembled from separate sessions. Typically: 1.5:1 to 2:1 ratio, slow attack (30–50 ms) to let the mix breathe, auto or 200 ms release, threshold set for 1–3 dB of GR. Track compression on a vocal or kick might be driving 6–10 dB of GR with a 4:1 ratio — the compressor is actively shaping the source. Mix bus compression does it job best when you bypass it and the mix sounds wider, lighter, and slightly less cohesive — confirming it was working, invisibly.

Almost never on a full-range audio source, and only deliberately on specific transient material with a sonic intention. Very fast attack and very fast release (both under 5 ms) on a broadband signal causes intermodulation distortion — the gain reduction is modulating the signal at audio rates, producing harmonic artifacts audible as a rough, grainy texture. This is sometimes called 'distortion compression' and is used intentionally on room mics or a full drum bus to add saturation character. If you are trying to control dynamics cleanly and hearing a gritty crunch under the signal, slow the release to 80–150 ms as a first corrective move.

No, and applying compressors by default is one of the most common workflow failures in mixing. Synthesizers with consistent, programmed dynamics rarely benefit from compression — a pad sustaining at -18 dBFS across four bars has no dynamic problem to solve. Percussion samples triggered at fixed velocities are already dynamically controlled before they hit the compressor. The question is always: does this source have a dynamic problem that is making the mix harder to balance? If not, the compressor adds noise floor, phase shift, and processing load without contributing to the mix. Reserve compression for sources that actively need it.
