Compression

/kəmˈprɛʃ.ən/

Definition How It Works Parameters Quick Reference History How To Use It In The Wild Types Mistakes Related FAQ

Compression is a dynamic processing technique that automatically reduces the volume of audio when it exceeds a set threshold — catching loud peaks, evening out the dynamic range, and making signals sit more consistently in a mix.

01 Definition

Every record you've ever loved was shaped by compression. Most producers who use it daily couldn't explain exactly what it's doing — and that gap between using it and understanding it is exactly what this entry closes.

Compression is the most-used processor in modern music production. Every major release you've heard in the last 50 years — every snare crack, every vocal phrase, every kick drum punch — was shaped by compression at some point in the production chain. Understanding it is not optional. It's the difference between a mix that holds together and one that doesn't.

At its core, compression is gain reduction that happens automatically. You set a threshold — a level in dBFS — and whenever the audio exceeds that threshold, the compressor turns it down. How much it turns it down is determined by the ratio. How fast it reacts is the attack. How fast it lets go is the release. What it does after reducing gain is controlled by makeup gain. Those five parameters — threshold, ratio, attack, release, makeup gain — are the entire language of compression.

The goal is dynamic control: making the loud moments and the quiet moments closer together in volume, without making the signal sound flat or lifeless. A well-compressed vocal sits in the mix rather than jumping out of it every time the singer hits a high note. A well-compressed kick drum has consistent punch through the whole track rather than peaking on some hits and disappearing on others. A well-compressed mix bus sounds cohesive — like all the elements were recorded together — rather than like separate tracks playing at the same time.

Done wrong, compression destroys energy. It kills transients, suffocates dynamics, and makes music feel fatiguing. The goal is never more compression — it's the right compression, in the right amount, at the right time in the signal chain.

02 How It Works

Inside every compressor — hardware or plugin — is a gain computer. This circuit continuously monitors the incoming signal level and compares it to your threshold setting. The moment the signal crosses the threshold, the gain computer calculates how much to reduce the gain based on your ratio setting, then applies that reduction to the output.

The attack control determines how quickly the gain reduction kicks in after the threshold is crossed. A fast attack (1–5ms) catches transients immediately — good for taming peaks, bad for drums where you want the initial crack to pass through. A slow attack (30–100ms) lets the front of the signal through before compression engages — preserving the natural punch of a drum hit or the consonant of a vocal.

The release controls how quickly the compressor lets go of gain reduction after the signal drops back below the threshold. Too fast and you hear the compressor breathing — the volume pumps in an unnatural way. Too slow and the compressor stays engaged between words or hits, reducing the perceived loudness of the track overall.

The knee setting determines how abruptly compression engages at the threshold. A hard knee switches from no compression to full compression the instant the signal crosses the threshold — transparent and precise. A soft knee begins gently reducing gain slightly below the threshold and gradually increases compression as the signal moves further above it — smoother, more musical, harder to hear working.

03 The Parameters

Every compressor — hardware or plugin, vintage or modern — operates on the same five core parameters. Know these completely and you can operate any compressor ever made.

Threshold
The level at which compression begins

Set in dBFS. When the signal exceeds the threshold, compression engages. Lower the threshold and compression engages more often and on more of the signal. Raise it and only the loudest peaks get caught. A threshold of −18dBFS on a vocal means the compressor is working most of the time. A threshold of −6dBFS means it only catches the loudest peaks.

Ratio
How much compression is applied

Expressed as X:1. A 4:1 ratio means for every 4dB the signal exceeds the threshold, only 1dB comes out the other side — the other 3dB are reduced. A 2:1 ratio is gentle and transparent. An 8:1 ratio is assertive. Anything above 10:1 is effectively limiting — the signal is prevented from significantly exceeding the threshold.

Attack
How fast compression engages

Measured in milliseconds. A fast attack (1–5ms) catches transients immediately — useful for taming unpredictable peaks on bus compression, damaging on drums where you want the initial hit to punch through. A slow attack (30–100ms) lets the transient pass before compression engages, preserving the punch and crack that give drums and plucked instruments their energy.

Release
How fast compression disengages

Also in milliseconds. Too fast and you hear the compressor breathing — the volume rises and falls in an unnatural, pumping way between notes or drum hits. Too slow and the compressor stays engaged through quiet sections, pulling the average level down and making the track feel lifeless. The sweet spot is often tempo-related — a release that completes between the beats feels natural and musical.

Makeup Gain
Volume added after compression

Compression reduces level. Makeup gain restores it. If your compressor is applying 6dB of gain reduction on average, adding 6dB of makeup gain brings the perceived volume back to where it started — but now the dynamics are controlled. The most common beginner mistake is forgetting makeup gain and concluding the compression "isn't doing anything" because the output sounds quieter than the input.

Knee
How gradually compression engages at the threshold

Hard knee: full compression ratio engages the instant the threshold is crossed — precise, transparent, clinical. Soft knee: compression begins gradually below the threshold and reaches full ratio above it — smoother, more musical, harder to hear working. Soft knee is generally preferable for vocals and acoustic instruments. Hard knee suits buses and situations where you want surgical control.

05 History & Origin

Compression was invented out of necessity, not creativity. In the early days of radio broadcasting, engineers needed a way to prevent transmitters from being overloaded by loud audio signals. The solution was an automatic gain control circuit — a device that would reduce the gain of the signal when it got too loud. These early limiters were crude, slow, and not particularly musical. But they worked.

The first compressors designed specifically for recording arrived in the 1950s. The Fairchild 670, introduced in 1959 and used extensively on Beatles recordings at Abbey Road, was a variable-mu tube compressor that remains one of the most sought-after pieces of audio hardware ever made. Its character — warm, slow, and deeply musical — defined what compression could sound like rather than just what it could do.

The Teletronix LA-2A optical compressor arrived in 1962. Optical compression uses a light-dependent resistor (LDR) that changes its resistance based on the intensity of a small light source. The result is an inherently program-dependent response — the compressor naturally behaves differently on different material, producing a smoothness that fixed time constants can't replicate. The LA-2A and its descendants define the sound of compressed vocals on virtually every major recording from the 1960s onward.

The UREI 1176, introduced in 1967, brought FET (field-effect transistor) technology to compression and changed the sound forever. Where the LA-2A is smooth and musical, the 1176 is fast and aggressive. Its ability to catch transients that the LA-2A would miss made it the standard for drums, bass, and any source where punch and control needed to coexist. The "all-buttons-in" mode — engaging all four ratio buttons simultaneously, an undocumented feature — produces a distorted, aggressive compression character that has appeared on thousands of records.

The SSL G-Bus compressor, introduced in the late 1970s as part of the SSL 4000 console, defined the sound of mix bus compression for the next four decades. Its VCA (voltage-controlled amplifier) design is fast, clean, and transparent at low ratios — perfect for gluing a mix together without audibly processing it. The SSL G-Bus is the compressor sound of 1980s and 1990s commercial music. Every major compressor plugin manufacturer has made an emulation of it.

By the 1990s, digital audio workstations brought compression to every producer with a computer. The democratization of the tool that once required tens of thousands of dollars in hardware meant that compression — once the domain of professional engineers with decades of experience — was now available to anyone. The challenge shifted from access to understanding.

06 How Producers Use It

On Drums

Drum compression is about punch and consistency. The goal on a kick drum is to control the dynamic variation between hits — the quieter hits come up, the louder hits come down — while preserving the initial transient that gives the kick its attack. Slow attack (10–20ms), moderate release (50–80ms), 4:1 to 8:1 ratio. On a snare, faster attack can work, but be careful — catch the transient too aggressively and the crack disappears. Parallel compression is the preferred technique for drums: heavy compression blended with the uncompressed signal preserves the attack while adding density and sustain underneath.

On Vocals

Vocal compression is about consistency and presence. A dynamic singer can vary 20–30dB across a performance. Without compression, the quiet passages disappear in the mix and the loud passages jump out uncontrollably. A 3:1 to 4:1 ratio with a medium attack (15–25ms) and medium release (80–150ms) brings the performance into a controllable range without sounding processed. Two-stage compression — a gentle compressor first, a harder compressor second — often produces more transparent results than one compressor doing all the work.

On Bass

Bass compression is about control and punch. The fundamental frequencies of bass guitar and 808s live in the low end where human hearing is already less sensitive to dynamics. Compression on bass brings the notes together in volume, prevents low-end peaks from consuming headroom, and tightens the relationship between the bass and the kick drum. Slow attack (30–60ms) to let the attack of each note through, moderate release (80–150ms), 4:1 to 6:1 ratio. Sidechain compression where the kick triggers the bass compressor — causing the bass to briefly duck every time the kick hits — is the technique behind the low-end clarity of most modern trap and hip-hop production.

On the Mix Bus

Mix bus compression — also called glue compression — is applied to the stereo output of the mix. The goal is cohesion, not control. Settings are gentle: 2:1 to 4:1 ratio, slow attack (50–100ms) to preserve transient energy, slow release (150–300ms) tuned to the tempo, 1–3dB of gain reduction. The effect is subtle — individual elements shouldn't sound different, but the mix as a whole should feel like it breathes together. Most engineers commit to mix bus compression early in the session so every mixing decision is made in the context of the glue.

Ableton Stock Compressor device. Fully featured with all standard parameters plus a visual transfer curve and auto-release. Auto mode for attack and release is useful for learning — switch to manual once you understand what the auto settings are doing. The Glue Compressor (SSL G-Bus emulation) is included and excellent for bus work.
FL Studio Fruity Peak Controller + parametric EQ for basic compression; Fruity Compressor for straightforward tasks. The Maximus multiband compressor/limiter is powerful and precise. Many FL producers prefer third-party plugins — the stock compression is functional but the interface is less intuitive than dedicated compressor designs.
Logic Pro Logic's Compressor plugin includes seven circuit models (Platinum Digital, Studio VCA, Studio FET, Classic VCA, Vintage VCA, Vintage FET, Vintage Opto) selectable from the same interface — effectively seven different compressors in one. The Vintage VCA (SSL G-Bus emulation) and Vintage Opto (LA-2A emulation) are excellent and widely used by professional engineers.
Pro Tools Stock Compressor/Limiter III is capable for basic tasks. BF-76 (1176 emulation) and BF-2A (LA-2A emulation) are included in many configurations and are genuinely useful tools. Most professional Pro Tools sessions use third-party plugins — Waves CLA-76, FabFilter Pro-C 2, and Slate Digital Virtual Bus Compressors are common choices.
Reaper ReaComp is functional and free with clean parameter control. ReaXComp for multiband work. Reaper's routing flexibility makes parallel compression particularly easy — a single send to a compressed bus blended back into the dry signal is a two-step process. Most Reaper users supplement with third-party VSTs given the stock compressor's clinical character.

07 In The Wild

Abstract compression knowledge becomes practical when you can hear it in music you know. These tracks demonstrate compression used intentionally, at specific moments, for specific purposes.

Daft Punk — "One More Time" (2000)
Throughout · Produced by Daft Punk

The most famous example of bus compression used as an aesthetic rather than a technical tool. The entire mix is compressed aggressively enough that it pumps audibly with the kick drum — every element ducking slightly on each beat. What would be considered a mistake in most contexts is here the defining sonic character of the record. The pumping creates a rhythmic energy that makes the track feel like it's breathing. Thomas Bangalter and Guy-Manuel de Homem-Christo were not trying to hide the compression — they were making it a feature.

Dr. Dre ft. Snoop Dogg — "Still D.R.E." (1999)
0:00 – throughout · Produced by Dr. Dre, Scott Storch

The kick drum on this record is a masterclass in compression for punch. Each hit has a clear, defined attack followed by a sustained body — a product of parallel compression on the drum bus. The uncompressed signal provides the crack; the heavily compressed signal underneath provides the weight and sustain. The result is a kick that simultaneously cuts through and sits deep in the mix. Listen on headphones and notice how consistent every kick hit is — that consistency is compression, applied with enough care that it sounds like natural dynamics.

Nirvana — "Smells Like Teen Spirit" (1991)
0:00 – throughout · Produced by Butch Vig

The drum room sound on this record — cavernous, explosive, larger than life — is partly a function of extremely aggressive room microphone compression. Producer Butch Vig compressed the room mics hard enough to bring up the room ambience to near the level of the close mics, creating a drum sound that feels like it's happening in a much larger space than it was. The compression is not transparent — it's clearly working — and that evidence of working is precisely what makes the drums sound so enormous. The attack of Dave Grohl's playing punches through; the compressed room follows it.

The Weeknd — "Blinding Lights" (2019)
0:46 – vocals enter · Produced by Max Martin, Oscar Holter, DaHeala

The vocal compression on this track is a study in maintaining energy while controlling dynamics. The Weeknd's voice stays remarkably consistent in level through the verses — sitting in exactly the same place in the mix on every phrase, regardless of how quietly or loudly he's singing — without ever sounding processed or over-compressed. This is the result of careful multi-stage compression and likely automated threshold or ratio adjustments between verse and chorus. When the chorus hits, the vocal expands into the mix rather than sitting on top of it, a choice that required deliberate decisions about where the compression ends and where the natural dynamics of the performance take over.

J Dilla — "Workinonit" (2006)
Throughout · Produced by J Dilla

Dilla's production on Donuts — the album this track opens — uses compression not for control but for texture. Samples run through hardware samplers and beat-up outboard gear accumulate compression artifacts that become part of the aesthetic. The lo-fi pumping, the inconsistent dynamics, the sense that the mix is alive and unstable — these are not production failures corrected in post. They are the sound. Understanding that compression can add character rather than remove it is one of the most important conceptual expansions a producer can make.

08 Types of Compression

Compressor circuit topology determines character more than any other factor. The same settings on different circuit types produce different results — sometimes subtly, sometimes dramatically. Understanding the four primary types tells you which tool to reach for before you've turned a single knob.

VCA
SSL G-Bus · dbx 160 · API 2500

Voltage-controlled amplifier. Fast, clean, transparent. The most common type in modern DAWs. Excellent for buses and situations requiring precise control. Can sound punchy and aggressive at higher ratios. The workhorse compressor character.

FET
UREI 1176 · Universal Audio 1176

Field-effect transistor. Fast attack capability (down to 20 microseconds), aggressive character, adds harmonic coloration. The sound of compressed drums and rock vocals. Every major plugin manufacturer has a version. Distinctive in "all-buttons-in" mode.

Optical
Teletronix LA-2A · Universal Audio LA-3A

Program-dependent response via light-sensing circuit. Naturally musical, smooth, and nearly impossible to make sound bad. Slow attack means transients pass through. Ideal for vocals, bass, and acoustic instruments. The sound of classic recordings.

Variable-Mu
Fairchild 670 · Manley Variable Mu

Tube-based. The slowest circuit type. Warm, thick, and deeply musical. The Fairchild 670 was used on virtually every major recording at Abbey Road in the 1960s. Prohibitively expensive in hardware form. Plugin emulations are widely available and highly regarded.

Modern plugin compressors emulate all four circuit types. A well-built optical emulation (FabFilter Pro-C 2 in Optical mode, Universal Audio LA-2A) behaves differently from a VCA emulation (Waves SSL G-Master Buss Compressor) and different still from a FET emulation (Waves CLA-76, Universal Audio 1176). The circuit type is not window dressing — it changes how the compressor responds to your material.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    Over-compressing everything
    The most common beginner mistake. More compression does not mean better sound — it means less dynamics, less energy, and a mix that feels fatiguing after 30 seconds. If your gain reduction meter is constantly showing 10dB or more on individual tracks, you are working against the music. Compression should be noticed when it's absent, not when it's present.
  • ✕
    Fast attack killing the transient on drums
    Setting a 1ms attack on your drum compressor catches the transient — the initial crack of the snare, the punch of the kick — before it can pass through. The result sounds dull, like someone put a blanket over the kit. Unless you're deliberately going for a squashed, retro sound, slow the attack down until the punch returns. The transient is what gives drums their impact.
  • ✕
    Forgetting makeup gain
    Compression reduces level. Without makeup gain, the compressed signal is quieter than the bypassed signal. When you A/B the compressor in and out, the uncompressed version sounds "better" simply because it's louder — not because it actually is. Always match levels when A/B testing compression. This single habit eliminates the most common source of bad compression decisions.
  • ✕
    Using compression to fix a bad recording
    Compression controls dynamics — it cannot fix a vocalist who was too close to the microphone, an acoustic guitar recorded in a reflective room, or a bass recorded with poor gain staging. Compressing a bad recording more aggressively just makes a bad recording with less dynamics. Fix the source problem first. Compression is a tool for controlling a good signal, not correcting a problematic one.
  • ✕
    Watching the meter instead of listening
    The gain reduction meter tells you how much compression is happening. It does not tell you whether that compression sounds good. Two different compressors applying 6dB of gain reduction to the same signal will sound different because of their circuit character, attack and release curves, and knee behavior. Set parameters by ear, use the meter to understand what you're hearing, not to decide whether it's right.
  • ✕
    Wrong release time creating pumping
    When the release time is too short for the tempo, the compressor fully releases between hits and then re-engages aggressively on the next transient — creating an audible pumping or breathing artifact. Set release times in relation to your BPM: at 120BPM there are 500ms between beats on a quarter-note grid. A release time of 400–500ms that completes between beats feels natural. Shorter release times work for specific effect; longer ones suit slower material.

12 Frequently Asked Questions

A compressor monitors the level of an audio signal and automatically turns it down whenever it exceeds a set threshold. The amount it turns down is determined by the ratio — a 4:1 ratio means every 4dB over the threshold gets reduced to 1dB. The result is a signal with less dynamic range: the loud parts are closer in volume to the quiet parts, making the overall signal easier to control in a mix.
For vocals, start with a ratio between 2:1 and 4:1. A 2:1 ratio is transparent and gentle — ideal for controlled performances. A 4:1 ratio is more assertive and works well for dynamic singers or rap vocals. Anything above 6:1 starts to sound obvious and can make vocals feel unnatural unless that's the aesthetic you're going for.
A limiter is a compressor with a very high ratio — typically 10:1 or higher, often described as infinity:1. Where a compressor reduces gain proportionally above the threshold, a limiter effectively prevents the signal from exceeding the threshold at all. Compression controls dynamics. Limiting enforces a ceiling. Both use the same parameters but limiting is absolute where compression is graduated.
Over-compression sounds like lifelessness — the signal loses its natural ebb and flow and starts to feel flat, suffocated, or fatiguing. Specific signs: drums lose punch (the attack is being caught), vocals feel robotic, the mix sounds loud but not energetic. The gain reduction meter is a guide — more than 6dB of GR consistently on a single source is usually too much. Use your ears first, not the meter.
Both approaches are valid. EQ before compression means the compressor responds to the EQ'd signal — boosted frequencies will trigger more compression. EQ after compression means you're shaping the tone of an already-controlled signal. Most engineers do both: a pre-compression EQ to remove problem frequencies that would trigger unwanted gain reduction, then a post-compression EQ to shape the final tone.
Parallel compression blends a heavily compressed version of a signal with the original uncompressed signal. The compressed signal adds density and sustain while the original preserves transients and dynamics. It's used most on drums — the attack and crack of the uncompressed signal sits on top of the thick, sustaining compressed signal underneath. The result sounds both controlled and natural, with energy that straight compression removes.
The best compressor for beginners is the stock compressor in your DAW. Ableton's Compressor, Logic's Vintage VCA, and Reaper's ReaComp are all capable and free. Learning on a simple interface teaches the concept before plugin character matters. Once the concept is solid, FabFilter Pro-C 2 is widely considered the best all-purpose plugin compressor for its visual feedback and flexibility. Browse compressor plugins →
Mix bus compression (glue compression) is applied to the stereo output of a mix to tie all elements together into a cohesive whole. Settings are gentle: 2:1 to 4:1 ratio, slow attack (30–100ms) to preserve transients, moderate release (100–300ms) tuned to the tempo, and only 1–3dB of gain reduction. The effect is subtle — the mix breathes together rather than feeling like separate elements playing simultaneously.
