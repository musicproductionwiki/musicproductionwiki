Compression

noun / signal processing tool · kəm-PREH-shən
*Every record you've ever loved was shaped by compression you couldn't hear — and that invisibility is the entire point.*
📅 2026-05-15 ⏱ 18 min read 📚 Producer's Bible
Quick Answer

Compression reduces the dynamic range of audio by attenuating signals above a set threshold by a defined ratio, then applying makeup gain — controlling peaks, adding sustain, and gluing tracks together.

Common Misconception

Most producers believe that more compression always makes a mix louder, punchier, and more professional — and that the compressor's GR meter showing constant gain reduction is a sign that the compression is working hard and therefore working well.

Here is what is actually happening: a compressor that is always reducing gain is not working — it is suffocating. Gain reduction that is constant rather than dynamic is simply volume reduction with extra steps. The power of compression comes from its contrast: peaks being pulled down so that makeup gain can bring the entire signal — peaks and quieter moments alike — up by the same amount. When the GR meter never returns to zero, there are no quiet moments being lifted. There is only loud being made quieter. A compressor should breathe: GR goes up when the signal is loud, returns to zero when the signal is quiet. That cycle — reduction and recovery — is what shapes dynamics. Constant gain reduction flattens dynamics entirely, removes musical phrasing, and creates the fatiguing, lifeless quality that characterizes amateur over-compression. The professional's test is simple: if the GR meter never moves off its maximum reading, raise the threshold until it does. Compression works by contrast, not by constant application.

What Is Compression?

Every record you've ever loved was shaped by compression you couldn't hear — and that invisibility is the entire point.

Compression is the process of automatically reducing the dynamic range of an audio signal — the difference between its loudest and quietest moments — by attenuating any signal that exceeds a defined amplitude threshold. A compressor is, at its core, an automatic volume control: it listens to the incoming level, and when that level crosses the threshold, it turns the signal down by a ratio you specify. Once the gain reduction is applied, makeup gain is added to bring the overall level back up. The result is audio that sits more consistently in a mix, that sustains longer, that punches harder, and that integrates with other elements in a way that raw, unprocessed audio simply cannot.

But the technical description misses what compression actually does to music. Compression shapes time. It changes how fast a sound arrives at the listener's ear and how long it lingers. A snare drum hit without compression can spike 18 dB above the body of the sound in two milliseconds, then decay instantly. Run that same snare through a compressor with a 10 ms attack and a 60 ms release, and you've made a decision about what the listener perceives as impact versus what they perceive as sustain. You've told a story with a transient. That is not a technical process — that is an artistic one. Compression is the tool that makes that artistic decision systematic and repeatable.

There are four primary parameters that define any compression event: threshold (the level at which compression begins), ratio (how aggressively the signal is attenuated above that threshold), attack (how quickly the compressor responds to a signal crossing the threshold), and release (how quickly it stops compressing once the signal falls back below it). A fifth parameter — knee — determines whether the transition into compression is abrupt (hard knee) or gradual (soft knee). Makeup gain then compensates for the gain reduction that compression imposes. Every compressor ever built — analog hardware or digital plugin — operates on these five variables. The art is in how each circuit or algorithm implements them, and how a producer chooses to deploy them.

In the context of a mix, compression is the difference between elements that fight for space and elements that coexist. A bass guitar and a kick drum both live in the 60–120 Hz range. Without compression on either, their peaks collide randomly — sometimes the kick cuts through, sometimes it gets buried, depending on where each note in the bass line lands in time relative to the kick transient. Sidechain compression solves exactly this: the compressor on the bass is triggered by the kick signal, ducking the bass by 3–6 dB every time the kick hits, then releasing before the next musical event. The result is a low end that breathes in sync with the groove. This is not a correction — it is a production choice that defines the rhythmic feel of the record.

Compression also does something more subtle and more profound: it controls perceived loudness without changing peak levels. Because human hearing responds to sustained energy rather than instantaneous peaks, bringing up the RMS (root mean square, or average) level of a signal makes it sound louder even if the peak hasn't moved. Compression reduces peaks, makeup gain brings the whole signal up, and the result is more average energy hitting the listener's ear — more presence, more weight, more forward momentum. This is why compressed audio sounds more powerful in a car speaker or a phone speaker than uncompressed audio at the same peak level. It is also why over-compression sounds exhausting: there is no dynamic contrast left for the listener's ear to interpret as musical phrasing.

In 2026, compression matters differently than it did in 2006 for three specific reasons. First, streaming normalization has shifted the strategic calculus: platforms like Spotify (targeting −14 LUFS integrated) and Apple Music (targeting −16 LUFS) will turn down loud masters, meaning the old arms-race logic of hyper-compression for competitive loudness is now actively counterproductive. Producers who understand compression's musical function — rather than its loudness function — are making records that survive normalization with their dynamics intact and their energy preserved. Second, the proliferation of high-quality plugin emulations means every producer now has access to hardware-accurate VCA, FET, and optical compression character at zero cost, raising the baseline but also the noise level of poorly applied processing. Third, the rise of lo-fi, bedroom pop, and intentionally compressed aesthetic genres has made deliberate over-compression a creative tool rather than a mistake — but only for producers who understand what they are doing and why. That distinction — between compression applied with knowledge and compression applied by default — is the exact line this entry is written to clarify.

Compression automatically reduces dynamic range by attenuating signals above a threshold and applying makeup gain — making audio more consistent, present, and powerful without changing its peak level.

How Compression Works

At the electrical level, a compressor's gain control element sits in the signal path and reduces gain in response to a control voltage (CV) generated by the detector circuit. The detector — which can monitor the main signal path (feed-forward) or the output of the gain element (feed-back) — measures the incoming amplitude and compares it to the threshold. When the signal exceeds the threshold, the detector generates a control voltage proportional to the overage, and the gain element — whether it's a VCA, an optical attenuator, a FET transistor, or a tube circuit — reduces gain accordingly. The ratio parameter defines the relationship: at 4:1, every 4 dB above threshold becomes 1 dB at the output. At 10:1, every 10 dB above threshold becomes 1 dB — behavior approaching limiting. At 1:1, nothing happens. What the producer hears at 4:1 on a snare drum is the transient spike being pulled back toward the body of the sound: the initial crack is tamed, and the ring of the drum head becomes more audible relative to the hit. The snare sounds wider, longer, more present in the room.

Attack and release are the parameters most directly responsible for the perceived character of compression. Attack time — measured in milliseconds, typically ranging from 0.1 ms to 300 ms depending on the unit — determines how much of the initial transient passes through before the compressor clamps down. A fast attack of 1 ms on a kick drum will attenuate the click of the beater hitting the head, softening the initial impact but preserving the low-frequency body. A slower attack of 30 ms lets the transient through entirely and compresses only the sustained portion, which increases the perceived punch of the drum while controlling the tail. This is the attack paradox that confuses most beginners: slower attack = more punch. Release time determines how quickly the gain returns to 1:1 once the signal drops below threshold. Too fast a release (under 20 ms on program material) creates pumping: the gain snaps back audibly between transients, creating a rhythmic breathing artifact. Too slow a release (over 500 ms on a fast source) means the compressor never fully recovers between hits, resulting in sustained gain reduction that flattens the dynamic between events. The sweet spot on a 120 BPM session for kick drum release is typically 80–150 ms — fast enough to recover before the next hit, slow enough not to breathe audibly.

The knee parameter governs how the compressor transitions into and out of its compression zone. A hard knee means compression begins abruptly the instant the signal crosses the threshold — audible on dynamic sources if the threshold is set too close to the average program level. A soft knee means the ratio increases gradually across a defined dB range on either side of the threshold (typically ±6 dB or ±12 dB), making the onset of compression transparent and more musical. Optical compressors — like the Teletronix LA-2A — have an inherently soft, program-dependent knee because the light-source-and-photoresistor circuit responds with a lag that is a function of both the attack content and the signal's history. This is why the LA-2A sounds smooth and invisible on vocals: its knee, attack, and release are all reactive rather than fixed, adapting to the musical phrase rather than responding to every individual peak with the same mechanical action. For the producer, the audible difference is this: hard knee compressors sound decisive and punchy; soft knee compressors sound like the dynamics are simply becoming more even, with no audible processing artifact. Both are correct. Neither is better. They are different tools for different goals.

A detector circuit compares the input level to the threshold and drives a gain control element to reduce signal by the set ratio, with attack and release times determining how the compressor responds to the arrival and departure of loud transients.

Compression — Key Parameters

Threshold

Technical: The level in dBFS at which the compressor begins to act. Signal below this point passes through unaffected at a 1:1 ratio. Signal above this point is reduced according to the set ratio. Common starting points: -18 dBFS for drums, -24 dBFS for vocals in a dense mix. Perceptual: Threshold determines how often you hear the compressor working. Set too high (e.g., -6 dBFS on a vocal that peaks at -8 dBFS) and you get almost no gain reduction. Set it so your GR meter reads 3–6 dB on peaks and you're in the zone where the signal starts feeling controlled without sounding squeezed.

Ratio

Technical: Expresses the attenuation slope above the threshold. A 4:1 ratio means that for every 4 dB the signal exceeds the threshold, only 1 dB passes. At infinity:1 (limiting), no signal passes beyond the threshold ceiling. Ratios 1.5:1 to 3:1 are considered transparent; 4:1 to 8:1 are corrective; 10:1 and above are limiting territory. Perceptual: Low ratios (2:1) feel like the mix breathes — you notice dynamics still moving. A 6:1 ratio on a snare clamps transients and adds punch. Ratios above 10:1 on a vocal produce that clamped, breathless sound beloved in modern pop production.

Attack

Technical: The time constant in milliseconds for the compressor to respond once the signal crosses the threshold. Faster attack = more of the transient is caught. Most program-dependent compressors specify attack in ms from 0.1ms (very fast, essentially limiting) to 300ms (letting nearly all transients through). Perceptual: Attack is the knob that shapes punch. A 30–50ms attack on a snare lets the crack through before gain reduction hits — preserving the snap. Set attack to 1ms on the same snare and the stick transient disappears, the drum feels like it's wrapped in cotton. On buses, slow attack (50–100ms) is what creates the sense of tracks breathing together rather than being crushed.

Release

Technical: The time in milliseconds (or seconds) for the compressor to stop attenuating after the signal drops back below the threshold. Too fast a release causes the gain reduction to pump with the signal's rhythm. Too slow a release and the compressor doesn't recover in time for the next transient, creating progressive gain reduction buildup. Perceptual: Release is the most underrated parameter. A 50–80ms release on kick drum in a 120 BPM track syncs the pump to tempo. Set release to 400ms on that same kick and the compressor is still working when the snare hits, reducing both. Auto-release modes — found on the SSL G-Bus compressor and many emulations — vary release time based on program content, often the safest choice for bus work.

Knee

Technical: Defines the transition zone around the threshold. A hard knee applies the full ratio immediately at the threshold. A soft knee (typically 6–10 dB wide) gradually increases the ratio as the signal approaches and crosses the threshold, so at 3 dB below threshold the effective ratio might be half the set ratio. Perceptual: Hard knee sounds like a wall — it's decisive and works well on transient sources like drums where you want a clean, defined clamp. Soft knee sounds more natural on vocals and acoustic instruments because the gain reduction creeps in — listeners don't hear a sudden change in level, they just hear evenness.

Makeup Gain

Technical: Gain applied after the compression stage to compensate for the overall level reduction caused by attenuation above the threshold. If a compressor averages 6 dB of gain reduction, adding 6 dB of makeup gain restores the perceived loudness while the dynamics have been permanently altered. Perceptual: Makeup gain is where producers fool themselves. Because more volume sounds better to untrained ears, adding excessive makeup gain makes compression seem to improve everything. Use a level-matched bypass — the compressed signal at the same RMS level as the uncompressed — to hear what the compressor is actually doing to the tone and dynamics, not just the volume.

Lookahead

Technical: Found on digital compressors only. Introduces a latency buffer (typically 1–20ms) so the compressor can begin gain reduction before the signal actually reaches the threshold. This allows attack times effectively faster than 0ms without the audible distortion that true 0ms hardware attack would produce on low-frequency content. Perceptual: Lookahead is almost always invisible when working correctly. It prevents transient overshoot on sources where fast attack is needed — mastering limiters use it extensively. Set it above 10ms on a drum bus and you may begin to notice a slight pre-transient softening that sounds unnatural.

Threshold, ratio, attack, release, knee, and makeup gain are the six variables that define every compression event — attack and release are the most musically consequential and the most commonly misunderstood.

Signal Chain Position

Compression position matters because any tonal shaping before it changes what the compressor's detection circuit responds to — low-end-heavy signals will trigger more gain reduction, so a pre-compression HPF on vocals often makes compression more even and predictable.

Interaction Warnings

  • Compression interacts badly with reverb sends when compression is placed after a send junction: the reverb tail from a snare hit will not be compressed, but the dry snare signal will be — creating a situation where the reverb sounds unnaturally louder than the source during compression events. The result is an audible bloom or wash after each snare hit. Fix: place compression before the send point, or use parallel compression so the reverb is consistently seeded from the dry level.
  • Compression applied to a bass track interacts destructively with a low-pass filter set below 200 Hz when the compressor's sidechain monitors the filtered signal: the compressed bass will exhibit gain pumping driven entirely by sub frequencies below the musical content, causing the upper harmonics of the bass (the part you actually hear in mix context) to pulse rhythmically with no relation to the playing. Fix: use a sidechain high-pass filter at 80–120 Hz to remove sub energy from the detection circuit, so compression responds to the musical body of the bass, not the subsonic fundamentals.
  • Heavy mix-bus compression (above 6 dB GR) applied before a brickwall limiter creates a compressor-limiter interaction where the limiter sees a signal that has already had its transient peaks altered — the compressor's slow release means the limiter receives a signal with elevated average level but reduced peak headroom. The result is the limiter triggering constantly and introducing inter-sample distortion even at conservative ceiling settings like -1 dBFS. Fix: reduce mix bus compression GR to 2–3 dB maximum, reserve limiting for true peak control, and avoid using compression to push loudness into the limiter stage.

History of Compression

James O. Landon's 1936 patent for a variable-mu tube amplifier at Bell Laboratories established the theoretical basis for gain-reduction circuitry — but it was Bob Cato's work at CBS Radio in 1939 that put a hardware limiter into a broadcast chain for the first time, preventing transmitter overload on live radio in New York. The device was crude by modern standards, with response times measured in seconds rather than milliseconds, but the foundational logic was identical to every compressor built since: when the signal gets too loud, reduce the gain. The problem those early engineers were solving was purely practical — protect the transmitter. The music was incidental. The irony is that in solving a technical problem, they created the most musically significant signal processing tool in the history of recorded sound.

The first compressor to cross from broadcast protection into deliberate musical use was the Fairchild 660, developed by Rein Narma for Fairchild Recording Equipment Corporation in New York in 1959. Narma's design used multiple variable-mu tube stages with six discrete time-constant positions rather than continuously variable attack and release controls — a circuit decision that created the distinctive Fairchild character: fast, transparent on transients, with a release that had program-dependent multi-stage behavior. By 1965, every major session at Capitol Studios in Hollywood had a Fairchild on the mix bus, and the unit's particular sonic fingerprint — a subtle harmonic richness from the tube gain stages combined with extraordinarily transparent gain control — was effectively the sound of American pop recording through the mid-1960s. A working pair today sells for $30,000–$40,000, and every major plugin developer has emulated it precisely because its sound remains unrepeatable by any other means.

Bill Putnam Sr. changed the trajectory of studio compression in 1968 when he designed the UREI 1176 Peak Limiter at United Recording Electronics Industries in Hollywood. Where the Fairchild was tube and program-dependent, the 1176 was solid-state FET — a field-effect transistor in the signal path that could respond in 20 microseconds, far faster than any tube circuit. Putnam set the minimum attack time at 20 μs (0.02 ms), giving engineers genuine transient control for the first time. The 1176 became the workhorse of rock recording precisely because its fast attack tamed the transient edge of electric guitars, snare drums, and vocals without the warmth-destroying harshness of simple peak limiting. Its four ratio buttons — 4:1, 8:1, 12:1, and 20:1 — and the legendary "all buttons in" mode (where pressing all four simultaneously creates an undocumented and musically pleasing overdriven compression mode) gave engineers a range of responses from gentle dynamic control to aggressive limiting. John Lennon's lead vocal on the Beatles' recordings from Abbey Road in 1969 was tracked through a modified version of this circuit topology. The 1176 remains, sixty years on, the most emulated hardware compressor in the world.

Dave Blackmer's invention of the VCA — voltage-controlled amplifier — at dbx Inc. in 1971 in Newton, Massachusetts, democratized compression by replacing expensive tube stages and FET networks with a single integrated circuit that could provide precise, repeatable gain control for a fraction of the cost. The dbx 160, released in 1976, brought VCA compression into every mid-tier studio and opened the door for the SSL G-Bus Compressor — designed by Colin Sanders at Solid State Logic's Oxford facility in 1976 for the SSL 4000 series console — which put VCA bus compression inside every console in every major studio on earth by 1982. The SSL G-Bus Compressor's specific characteristic — a 4:1 ratio with attack 30 ms, release Auto, and roughly 2–4 dB of gain reduction — became so ubiquitous as a mix glue tool that its sound is now baked into listeners' expectations of what a professional record sounds like. Tchad Blake, Jacqueline Shave, and effectively every mixer who worked on major-label releases through the 1980s and 1990s used it on their mix bus without thinking twice. The sound of compression at 2 AM in the control room of Air Studios, Metropolis, or Electric Lady was, overwhelmingly, the sound of the SSL G doing its quiet work.

Andy Wallace's mix of Nirvana's Nevermind at Scream Studios in Studio City, California in 1991 marks the moment compression crossed from a technical tool into an aesthetic signature. Wallace used heavy bus compression — reportedly 6–8 dB of gain reduction on the mix bus using an SSL 4000 G-series — not to transparently control dynamics but to create a specific crushed, urgent energy that matched the music's emotional content. The result was a record that sounded louder, more aggressive, and more physically present than anything that had come before it in mainstream rock, and it triggered the loudness wars that dominated mastering practice for the following two decades. The response in the digital era — the algorithmic compressors in early DAW software, the Waves C1 and Renaissance Compressor that appeared in every Pro Tools rig by 1998, and eventually the plugin emulations that now replicate every hardware unit ever built — all trace their commercial logic back to the question Wallace's mix raised: what does compression sound like when it becomes the point, rather than the solution?

From Bell Labs broadcast limiters in 1936 through the Fairchild 660, the UREI 1176, the SSL G-Bus, and the loudness wars triggered by Andy Wallace's Nevermind mix, compression's history is the history of the recorded sound itself.

How Producers Use Compression

Drums: Kick and snare compression is about transient shaping, not dynamic control. On kick drum, the standard starting point is an 1176-style FET compressor: ratio 4:1, attack 40–60 ms (letting the initial transient through to preserve punch), release 80–100 ms (fast enough to recover before the next hit), threshold set for 4–6 dB of gain reduction. The slower attack means the compressor clamps the body and the ring, not the click — the click passes through unaffected, the sustain is controlled. On snare, faster attack (5–10 ms) and a harder ratio (6:1–8:1) with 6–8 dB of gain reduction brings up the ring and the room tone, making the snare sound bigger in the mix. Parallel compression — blending a heavily compressed version (20:1, −20 dB threshold, 3 ms attack) with the dry signal at roughly 30–40% wet — preserves the natural transient while adding thickness in the body. This is the "New York Compression" technique, and it is the reason why drums on records from Questlove (The Roots), J Dilla, and Mark Ronson sound simultaneously explosive and sustained.

Vocals and Bass: Vocal compression serves a different function: it's about consistency across a performance, not transient shaping. Optical compression (LA-2A topology or its plugin equivalents) is the standard choice because its program-dependent response smooths out the peaks of a dynamic performance without making the gain reduction audible as a mechanical event. Typical settings: threshold at −18 dBFS, ratio 3:1–4:1, attack as slow as the compressor allows (optical units handle this automatically), release auto or program-dependent. The goal is 3–5 dB of gain reduction on average, with peaks hitting 8–10 dB on the loudest phrases. Stacking two compressors in series — a fast VCA (SSL or 1176) to catch peaks, followed by an optical for general leveling — gives both control and transparency. On bass guitar, a slow attack (30 ms) preserves the pick or finger attack, while a ratio of 4:1–6:1 and 6–8 dB of GR controls the body and tail, locking the bass into the pocket with the kick drum. For bass, release time synced to tempo is critical: at 120 BPM (500 ms per beat), a release of 150–200 ms prevents the compressor from pumping on eighth-note patterns.

Bus and Mix: Mix bus compression is applied at the end of the signal chain, across the stereo output, and its function is glue: making individually compressed elements cohere into a single, unified sonic image. The SSL G-Bus Compressor is the canonical tool, but the Neve 33609, the Alan Smart C2, and plugin equivalents (Waves SSL G-Master Buss Compressor, UAD Neve 33609) all achieve similar results. Standard settings that a professional mixer reaches for first: ratio 4:1, attack 30 ms, release Auto, threshold set for 2–4 dB of gain reduction on peaks. The attack of 30 ms is deliberate — it is slow enough to allow transients from kick and snare to pass through the bus compressor without being attenuated, while still catching the sustained energy of the mix and holding it together. More than 4 dB of gain reduction on the mix bus is generally a mistake in isolation — but referencing against the bypass is the only objective test. If the bypassed version sounds bigger and more present, the compression is working. If the compressed version sounds smaller, the threshold is too low or the ratio too high.

DAW-Specific Notes: In Ableton Live: The stock Compressor device in "Peak" mode with lookahead enabled (0–5 ms) is excellent for transparent program compression; switch to "Glue Compressor" for mix bus work — it models the SSL G-Bus circuit with program-dependent release. The Glue Compressor's "Soft Clip" ceiling prevents inter-sample peaks post-compression without a separate limiter. In Logic Pro: The Vintage VCA (modeled on the SSL G-series), Vintage FET (modeled on the 1176), and Vintage Opto (modeled on the LA-2A) in the compressor plugin dropdown give you the three essential compression characters without leaving the DAW. The Platinum Analog Tape setting in the Vintage VCA is a useful quick-start for bus compression. In FL Studio: The Maximus multiband compressor is frequently misused as a broadband compressor — for single-band compression, use the Fruity Peak Controller sidechain approach or third-party plugins (Waves, FabFilter) inserted on mixer channels. The Parametric EQ 2's built-in sidechain filter is useful for high-pass filtering the sidechain signal on a kick-duck bass setup. In Pro Tools: The stock BF-76 (1176 emulation) and Dyn3 Compressor/Limiter are reliable starting points, but the real workflow in professional Pro Tools sessions involves hardware inserts on key channels (SSL channel strip, 1176, LA-2A) via a hardware interface with outboard, using the plugin chain for everything below bus level.

Drums use fast FET compression for transient shaping and parallel techniques for density; vocals use optical or stacked VCA-optical chains for transparent leveling; the mix bus uses slow-attack VCA compression at 2–4 dB GR for glue.

Before and After

Without Compression

An uncompressed lead vocal tracked at −18 dBFS average has peak-to-average variation of 18–22 dB across a typical verse-to-chorus performance. Individual syllables spike 10–14 dB above the phrase average. Quiet, breathy passages drop to −36 dBFS. Consonants register 8–12 dB below adjacent vowels. At a mix level where the chorus is audible, the verse is buried; at a level where the verse sits, the chorus clips the bus. The vocal competes with and loses to every other element during its quiet moments, and overrides every other element during its loud ones. There is no consistent presence in the mix — the vocal is either too much or not enough, and the listener's attention follows the volume rather than the performance.

With Compression

After a two-stage compression chain — UAD 1176 (4:1 ratio, 20 ms attack, 100 ms release, −18 dBFS threshold, 4–6 dB average GR) catching fast peaks, followed by a UAD LA-2A optical (50% peak reduction, 25% gain setting) leveling phrase-to-phrase variation — the peak-to-average ratio is reduced to 6–8 dB. The vocal sits at a consistent −14 to −16 dBFS across both verse and chorus. Consonants are within 3 dB of adjacent vowels. The quiet breathy phrases are now at −22 dBFS rather than −36 dBFS. Makeup gain of +4 dB brings the average to −12 dBFS. The vocal has a single, consistent presence in the mix across its entire dynamic range: every word, phrase, and breath registers at roughly the same perceived level. The emotional dynamic of the performance — the vocal getting louder or softer for musical reasons — is preserved, but the technical dynamic variation that would have caused the vocal to disappear or overpower is eliminated.

Compression In The Wild

The Beatles — "Come Together" (1969) · Produced by George Martin, Engineered by Geoff Emerick

0:00–0:10 (intro bass riff)

Paul McCartney's Rickenbacker bass enters with a level of sustain and mid-range presence impossible on uncompressed magnetic tape of the era. Emerick ran the bass through a UREI-style limiting circuit, heavily compressing the signal — estimated 8–10 dB of GR — so the note at the end of each phrase blooms upward instead of decaying. The low-end thump arrives first, then the note swells in the room. This is compression extending the natural decay envelope of the instrument far beyond its acoustic reality.

Listening Guide

Put on headphones. Play from 0:00. Listen to the first two notes of the bass riff. Notice how the note doesn't decay — it seems to actually get louder as it sustains, then cuts off. That swell is the compressor releasing: after the transient, gain reduction is easing, allowing the body of the note to come up in level. This is compression's release time creating perceived sustain that the instrument itself cannot produce.

Daft Punk — "Get Lucky" (2013) · Produced by Daft Punk and Pharrell Williams

1:15–1:45 (first verse)

Nile Rodgers' rhythm guitar sits in an impossibly consistent dynamic window — every chord strum hits at the same perceived level despite the natural variation of a live performance. This is optical-style compression (likely an LA-2A or equivalent) with a 3:1 ratio and approximately 6 dB average GR, combined with the natural compression of analog tape emulation. The guitar never pokes out of the mix and never disappears — it maintains a fixed pocket relationship with the snare on beats 2 and 4.

Listening Guide

Put on headphones. Play from 1:15. Focus only on the rhythm guitar. Try to hear any individual strum that is louder or quieter than the others. You cannot. Every hit arrives at the same level with the same attack character. This is compression functioning as a precision leveling tool — not limiting peaks, but creating absolute consistency across a performance that was, in reality, dynamically varied.

Dr. Dre — "Still D.R.E." (1999) · Produced by Dr. Dre and Scott Storch

0:00–0:30 (intro piano and kick)

The kick drum on this record is one of the most analyzed pieces of compression work in hip-hop production. The kick has a near-instant transient followed by a massive, sustained low-frequency bloom that holds for 200+ ms before decaying. This is achieved through parallel compression: a dry kick blended with a version processed at extreme ratio (20:1 or greater), fast attack (1–2 ms), slow release (300–400 ms), bringing up the room and the sub-harmonic tail. The result is a kick that sounds both sharp and enormous — a physical paradox that only compression makes possible.

Listening Guide

Put on headphones. Play from 0:00. Listen to the kick drum. Notice that the initial click arrives sharp and fast, but then a second wave of low-end weight follows 50–80 ms later, as if the room itself is being pressed into the listener. That secondary bloom is the compressed parallel signal, its slow release bringing up the sub content that follows the transient. This is compression revealing energy that exists in the recording but would be masked without gain reduction on the peaks.

Amy Winehouse — "Rehab" (2006) · Produced by Mark Ronson

0:08–0:30 (vocal entrance)

Winehouse's vocal enters with a presence and forward weight that is characteristic of heavy VCA compression followed by optical smoothing — likely 8–10 dB of gain reduction on peaks with a 4:1 ratio VCA catching fast syllables, then a slower optical stage controlling phrase-to-phrase variation. The consonants ('N-N-No') arrive with the same energy as the vowels, which is physiologically impossible in an unprocessed human voice. The compressor is catching the natural drop-off after consonants and filling them in, making the vocal feel punched directly into the listener's chest.

Listening Guide

Put on headphones. Play from 0:08. Listen to how each syllable of the word 'No' arrives — the N consonant, the vowel, the end of the word. In a natural, unprocessed voice, the vowel would dominate and the consonant would be 10–12 dB quieter. Here they are within 2–3 dB of each other. That evenness is the VCA compressor at work on every peak, filling in the dynamic valleys so that every phoneme registers with equal weight.

Billie Eilish — "bad guy" (2019) · Produced by FINNEAS

0:47–1:00 (first chorus drop)

The chorus of this record is built entirely around the perceptual impact of extreme mix bus compression used creatively. FINNEAS has described using heavy limiting and compression on the mix bus to create a claustrophobic, airless sonic environment that matches the lyrical content. At the chorus drop, the sub-bass and the vocal occupy the same compressed dynamic ceiling — there is no room, no air, no separation. Every element is pushed against the ceiling of the compressor simultaneously. This is not a mistake. It is a designed aesthetic state, and it works because it serves the song.

Listening Guide

Put on headphones. Play from 0:40. Notice how much sonic space exists in the verse — relatively open, with distance between elements. Now listen as the chorus hits at 0:47. The whole mix seems to collapse inward. The bass, kick, and vocal are all fighting for the same compressed ceiling. This is intentional heavy bus compression as a textural choice: the record sounds suffocating because it is supposed to.

Rage Against the Machine — "Killing in the Name" (1992) · Produced by Brendan O'Brien

3:55–4:20 (final repeated chorus)

The electric guitar across this entire record — but most audibly in the final section — shows textbook 1176-style FET compression at high ratios (12:1–20:1 estimated) with a fast attack (1–3 ms) and medium release (100–150 ms). Tom Morello's guitar is physically incapable of producing the level of sustained aggression heard here without compression: every pick attack, every chord, and every muted chug hits at the same perceived level, which creates the overwhelming wall-of-sound effect that made the record sonically groundbreaking. The compressor is making one guitar sound like five.

Listening Guide

Put on headphones. Play from 3:55. Focus on the guitar — try to identify any dynamic variation between the softest and loudest moment in this 25-second window. There is almost none: the guitar maintains a single, relentless pressure level throughout. This is high-ratio FET compression removing dynamic variation so completely that the instrument sounds mechanical and inescapable. It is one of the most effective uses of compression as an emotional intensifier in rock history.

From the sustained bass bloom on Come Together to the suffocating bus compression on Billie Eilish's bad guy, compression's fingerprint is audible on every significant record of the past six decades once you know what to listen for.

Types of Compression

VCA (Voltage-Controlled Amplifier)

dbx 160A, SSL G-Bus, API 2500

VCA compressors use a voltage-controlled amplifier in the gain reduction path, allowing very precise, fast, and consistent response. They sound relatively clean and punchy — the dbx 160 on a kick drum or the SSL G-Bus on a mix bus are canonical examples. Use VCA compressors when you need control and predictability: bus compression, drum buses, and any source where you want the effect to be felt but not necessarily heard as a tonal change.

FET (Field-Effect Transistor)

Urei 1176LN, Empirical Labs Distressor

FET compressors use field-effect transistors to emulate the behavior of tube circuits at faster speeds, resulting in an aggressive, forward character with an inherent harmonic color. The 1176's ability to be pushed into 'all-buttons-in' mode (a technically incorrect operating state) produces a thick, saturated sound that has appeared on countless rock and hip-hop vocals. Use FET compressors for character — they add something to the signal, not just control it.

Optical

LA-2A, LA-3A, Tube-Tech CL 1B

Optical compressors use a light element and photocell to control gain reduction — the light responds to the signal level and the photocell's resistance changes accordingly, creating a program-dependent, nonlinear response that no fixed time constant can replicate. This results in a musical, forgiving quality that works exceptionally well on vocals and bass. The LA-2A on a lead vocal is arguably the most recorded compressor setting in history — its slow, program-dependent release means it never sounds aggressive.

Variable-Mu (Tube)

Fairchild 670, Manley Variable Mu, Vari-Mu

Variable-mu compressors use the gain properties of vacuum tubes that change with bias current — there are no conventional attack/release knobs on early designs like the Fairchild 670. The response is extremely program-dependent and almost always described as 'glue,' a quality where disparate mix elements begin to cohere. Best used at the mastering stage or on full mixes in small amounts: 1–2 dB of gain reduction from a Manley Variable Mu can transform a flat mix into something that sounds like a record.

Multiband Compression

Neve 33609 (broadband), Weiss DS1-MK3 (digital multiband)

Multiband compressors divide the frequency spectrum into bands (typically 3–5) and apply independent compression to each band. This allows a low-end heavy kick to trigger compression in the low band without affecting the high-mid vocal compression — impossible with a full-band compressor. Use with care: multiband compression on a master bus can destroy the interaction between instruments that makes music feel live, but applied surgically (e.g., controlling 60–120 Hz buildup in a drum bus), it solves problems nothing else can.

Parallel (New York) Compression

Any compressor used in a send/return configuration

Not a compressor type but a technique: the dry uncompressed signal is blended with a heavily compressed version (often 20:1 ratio, threshold slammed, no makeup gain). The result preserves the natural attack and transients of the original while adding density and sustain from the compressed layer underneath. Pioneered in New York recording studios in the 1970s–80s, it remains the primary technique for getting drum compression that sounds powerful but never squashed.

VCA compressors are fast and precise for transient control; optical compressors are smooth and program-dependent for transparent leveling; FET compressors are aggressive and characterful for drums and rock sources; variable-mu tube compressors are warm and musical for bus and mastering applications.

The Producer's Verdict

For most producers, start with: an 1176-style FET compressor on individual drums and a clean VCA (SSL G-Bus topology) on the mix bus. On drums, set ratio 4:1, attack 30–50 ms, release 80–120 ms, threshold for 4–6 dB of GR on peaks. On the mix bus, set ratio 4:1, attack 30 ms, release Auto, threshold for 2–4 dB of GR only on the loudest transients. Learn these two contexts until you can set them without thinking. Everything else is a variation on this foundation.

If you are working on dense electronic music, hip-hop production, or any genre where the low end is the primary driver of the track's energy, use parallel compression on your drum bus: send the full drum group to a return channel, compress it at 20:1 with 15–20 dB of GR and a 1–3 ms attack, then blend it back at 30–40% under the uncompressed group. This technique preserves the natural transient dynamics of the uncompressed signal while adding the sustained weight and thickness of the compressed version — giving you punch and density simultaneously without sacrificing one for the other.

Avoid bus compression on your mix until you understand what it sounds like without it. Add the mix bus compressor only after the mix is otherwise complete, and always A/B it at level-matched volumes against the uncompressed mix. Until you have internalized the difference — which takes dozens of mixes — you will be unable to judge whether your bus compressor is helping or destroying your work, and the psychoacoustic bias toward louder-sounds-better will mislead you every time.

Common Mistakes with Compression

Attack Too Fast — Killing the Transient

Symptom: Drums or percussion sound flat, dull, and lacking punch. The kick drum has no 'click' and the snare has no 'crack' — they seem to appear in the mix rather than hit it. The track lacks forward momentum and energy at the rhythmic level.

Cause: Attack time set below 5 ms on a transient-heavy source like kick, snare, or acoustic guitar. The compressor is clamping down before the initial peak has time to reach the listener, attenuating the very part of the signal that defines the instrument's attack character. On a kick drum, an attack of 1 ms will reduce the beater click by 6–10 dB.

Fix: Increase attack time to 20–50 ms for kick drum, 10–30 ms for snare. Use your ears: bypass the compressor and listen to the unprocessed transient, then engage and adjust attack until the transient's character returns while the sustained portion remains controlled. The compressor should be reacting to the body, not the peak.

Release Too Fast — Pumping and Breathing

Symptom: The mix or track has a rhythmic 'pumping' sound — a periodic surge and dip in volume that follows the tempo but is not a musical element. On vocals, individual words seem to swell artificially after each breath. On a mix bus, the whole mix seems to pulse in and out of loudness in time with the kick drum.

Cause: Release time set too short for the program material — typically under 30 ms on a mix bus or under 50 ms on sustained sources like pads or vocals. The compressor is releasing so quickly that the gain snaps back to unity between every transient, creating an audible surge each time the signal drops below threshold.

Fix: Increase release time until the pumping disappears. For mix bus at 120 BPM, start at 300–500 ms and adjust by ear, watching the GR meter return smoothly between kick hits. For vocals, the 'Auto' release setting on most compressors is an appropriate starting point — it calculates release based on signal content. For creative pumping effects (EDM sidechain), this 'mistake' is intentional: use a 100–200 ms release and a fast attack with a sidechain trigger on the kick.

Threshold Too Low — Over-Compression

Symptom: The processed source sounds lifeless, flat, and fatiguing. Vocals lose their emotional dynamics — loud and soft phrases arrive at the same level. Drums lose all sense of groove because every hit is uniformly compressed regardless of musical emphasis. The GR meter is pinned at 10–15 dB of reduction constantly.

Cause: Threshold set so low that even the quietest parts of the signal trigger compression. Instead of acting on peaks, the compressor is acting on the entire dynamic range of the signal continuously. A ratio of 4:1 with a threshold at −30 dBFS on a vocal that averages −18 dBFS will create constant, heavy compression that destroys all dynamic phrasing.

Fix: Raise the threshold until the GR meter shows 0 dB during quiet passages and 3–6 dB during moderate passages, peaking at 8–10 dB only on the loudest phrases. The compressor should be acting on peaks and accents, not on the program as a whole. A useful rule: the GR meter should be moving, not static. If it is reading constant gain reduction, the threshold is too low.

Too Much Makeup Gain — Confusing Louder for Better

Symptom: Every time compression is engaged, the track sounds 'better' — but what has actually happened is that it is louder. When level-matched against the bypassed signal, the compressed version may sound smaller, duller, or thinner. Decisions made with this bias active produce over-compressed mixes that sound good in the DAW but collapse in mastering.

Cause: Makeup gain added in excess of the average gain reduction, causing the compressed output to be 2–4 dB louder than the input. The human auditory system consistently judges louder sounds as better-sounding, regardless of processing quality. This is one of the most persistent cognitive biases in audio engineering.

Fix: After setting compression, reduce the output level (makeup gain) until the bypassed and compressed signals are level-matched at the same perceived loudness — use a VU meter or a short-term LUFS measurement to verify. Make your compression decisions at matched levels. If the compressed version still sounds better at matched levels, the compression is working. If it sounds equal or worse, reconsider the processing.

Hard Knee at Too-High Threshold on Dynamic Material

Symptom: Compression sounds mechanical and obvious — you can hear the compressor 'grab' each time a loud phrase hits, then hear it let go. On vocals, the effect is most audible as a sudden density increase at the start of loud syllables, followed by a release surge. The processing sounds like a door opening and closing rather than a transparent dynamic event.

Cause: Hard knee mode with a threshold set close to the average program level. Because a hard knee compressor transitions from 1:1 to the set ratio instantaneously at the threshold, a threshold too close to the average level means the signal is constantly crossing in and out of the compression zone — each crossing is audible as an abrupt tonal and density shift.

Fix: Switch to soft knee mode, which applies the ratio gradually across ±6 dB of the threshold, making the onset of compression transparent. Alternatively, lower the threshold to −20 dBFS or below so the compressor is working more consistently, or raise it to −10 dBFS so it is acting only on clear peaks rather than the main body of the signal.

Ignoring Sidechain Frequency Content — Low-End Triggering on Mix Bus

Symptom: A bus or mix compressor seems to pump even with a release time set to 500 ms or longer. The low end of the mix triggers the compressor disproportionately — every kick or bass note causes the entire mix to duck, including the midrange and high-frequency elements that had nothing to do with the triggering event. The vocals and guitars seem to breathe with the kick drum.

Cause: The compressor's detector circuit is responding to the full-range sidechain signal, which includes strong sub-bass energy below 100 Hz. These low-frequency peaks are disproportionately large in amplitude relative to their perceived loudness, so the compressor reads them as loud signals and compresses accordingly — even when the mix does not perceptually need it.

Fix: Apply a high-pass filter to the compressor's sidechain signal at 80–120 Hz. Most compressors with an external or internal sidechain filter allow this. This tells the compressor to base its gain reduction decisions on the midrange content (100 Hz and above), not the sub bass. The gain reduction becomes more perceptually appropriate and the low-end-triggered pumping disappears. On SSL-style bus compressors, this filter is often a dedicated 'HPF' button in the sidechain section.

The six most costly compression errors — killing transients with fast attack, pumping from fast release, over-compression from low threshold, level bias from excess makeup gain, hard-knee audibility, and low-end sidechain triggering — all have specific audible symptoms and specific numerical fixes.

Red Flags and Green Flags

🔴 Red Flags

  • Drums lose their stick attack entirely — you can't hear the initial transient of the kick or snare, just body and sustain. Cause: attack time below 5ms on a drum bus or individual drum channel. Fix: increase attack to 20–40ms until the transient reappears.
  • Vocals breathe unnaturally — the ambience and room noise in the vocal recording audibly swell between syllables as the compressor releases. Cause: release time below 50ms on a vocal with significant room sound, or ratio above 6:1. Fix: increase release to 80–150ms and reduce ratio to 3:1–4:1.
  • The mix 'pumps' on every kick drum hit in an obvious, non-intentional way. Cause: bus compressor with fast release (under 80ms) and no sidechain HPF, causing low-frequency kick energy to dominate the detection circuit. Fix: engage sidechain HPF at 100–150 Hz, or increase release to 200–400ms.
  • Bass guitar sounds inconsistent across a phrase — some notes jump out while others disappear into the mix — despite having 8–10 dB of gain reduction showing on the meter. Cause: compressor attack is fast enough to clamp transients but release is too slow, causing gain to build up cumulatively across sustained notes and then release audibly during rests. Fix: try a two-compressor stack: one fast with 2:1 for transient control, one slow optical at 3:1 for sustain.
  • The stereo image collapses or shifts left/right unpredictably during loud passages. Cause: stereo compressor running in dual-mono mode (independent channels) rather than stereo-linked mode, causing the left and right channels to receive different amounts of gain reduction when the signal is not perfectly centered. Fix: engage stereo link on the compressor — most hardware defaults to linked; many plugins default to dual-mono.
  • High-frequency detail sounds dull and muffled after compression, particularly on cymbals and acoustic guitar. Cause: a VCA compressor with fast attack (under 5ms) on a bus, catching and attenuating high-frequency transient energy. Fix: increase attack to 20–30ms or switch to an optical compressor whose slow response inherently preserves high-frequency content.

🟢 Green Flags

  • The gain reduction meter consistently reads 3–6 dB on vocal peaks during the chorus, then recovers fully between phrases — the compressor is working exactly where the performance demands it and resting everywhere else.
  • A loudness-matched bypass comparison (output matched to input level within 0.5 dB) reveals that the compressed signal sounds 'glued' or 'bigger' without being louder — a confirmed sign the compression is adding cohesion rather than just volume.
  • The kick drum in the full mix has both a defined transient click at the front and a sustained thud afterward — the attack time (20–30ms) is letting the transient through while the ratio (4:1) is controlling the body.
  • A drum bus shows 2–4 dB of gain reduction during chorus sections and releases fully during sparse verses — the compressor is responding musically to the arrangement, not clamping constantly.
  • A vocal sits in the mix consistently across a 3-minute track without a single automated level adjustment needed — the compressor's average 4–5 dB of GR has done the work of 20 automation points.
  • The integrated LUFS reading of the compressed mix is 2–3 LU higher than the uncompressed version at the same peak level, confirming that perceived loudness has increased without adding any distortion or peak overshoot.

Your Progression with Compression

Beginner

When you first encounter compression, your only goal is to understand what it sounds like in its most obvious application. Take a snare drum sample — any snare. Put a compressor on it with ratio 8:1, attack 10 ms, release 100 ms, and drag the threshold down until the GR meter shows 10 dB of gain reduction. Listen. Then set attack to 1 ms and listen again. Then set attack to 100 ms and listen again. Do not move on until you can hear the difference in those three settings without looking at the GR meter. The next exercise: set attack to 30 ms, release to 50 ms, and slowly raise the release time to 500 ms while the snare plays on a loop. Hear the pumping begin and then smooth out. These two exercises — attack on transient shaping, release on dynamic recovery — are the entire foundation of compression technique. Everything else is applying these two insights in different contexts. Do not put compression on a mix bus. Do not use it on a full mix. Learn it on one sound, one parameter at a time.

Intermediate

Once you can hear what attack and release are doing in isolation, the intermediate challenge is learning compression by instrument type and understanding why different sources demand different approaches. Spend a session with an LA-2A plugin on a lead vocal: set it to reduce 5 dB on the loud phrases and 0 dB on the quiet ones. Notice how the vocal sits in the mix more consistently without sounding processed. Then try the same vocal with an 1176 at 20:1 and fast attack — hear how different the character is. The technical parameters are similar; the musical result is completely different because the circuit behavior is different. At intermediate level, you should also be learning parallel compression: set up a parallel drum bus, compress it heavily, and practice the blend ratio until the compressed signal adds density without removing punch. The target is that someone listening to the processed track cannot identify that compression is present — they only feel that the drums are bigger and more present than they were before. When you reach the point where bypassing the compressor makes the mix feel smaller rather than feel more natural, you have crossed the threshold into functional compression technique.

Advanced

When you are ready for professional-level compression work, the focus shifts from individual parameters to compression as a system. Advanced compression practice means understanding how gain reduction in one part of the signal chain affects decisions in every other part: the threshold on the vocal compressor changes how much headroom is available on the mix bus; the release time on the drum bus compressor changes whether the kick and bass lock rhythmically or fight; the sidechain filter frequency on the bus compressor determines whether low-end information drives gain reduction decisions or whether midrange content does. At this level, you are managing a chain of dynamic decisions from the first recorded track through the mastering limiter, and every decision is in relation to every other. Advanced compression also means understanding when not to use it: a perfectly performed acoustic guitar phrase, a classical piano take with intentional dynamic phrasing, a singer-songwriter vocal where the dynamic variation between loud and soft is the entire emotional content of the performance — these are sources that compression can destroy if applied without judgment. The professional mark is not knowing how to compress everything. It is knowing what each sound actually needs, and having the experience to recognize when the right answer is no compression at all.

Frequently Asked Questions

A compressor monitors the level of incoming audio and, when that level crosses a set threshold, reduces the gain by the ratio you specify. A 4:1 ratio means a signal 8 dB over threshold will only be 2 dB over threshold at the output. The result is that loud peaks are pulled down, and when you add makeup gain to restore the overall level, the quieter parts become louder relative to the loudest peaks — the dynamic range narrows. Perceptually, this means sustained notes last longer, transients can be shaped, and the element sits more consistently in a mix.

The most reliable indicator is that the source loses its sense of life — drums feel like they're hitting you through a pillow, vocals lose their natural breath and emphasis, and bass guitar sounds more like a synthesizer than a string instrument. Technically, if your gain reduction meter is averaging more than 6 dB on a drum bus or more than 8 dB on an individual vocal through the body of a phrase, you're likely over-compressing. The loudness-matched bypass test is non-negotiable: match the compressed output level to the input level using the makeup gain, then bypass — if the bypass sounds more real and present, pull back the ratio or raise the threshold.

Attack controls how quickly the compressor clamps down after the signal crosses the threshold — this determines how much of the transient you preserve. A 30ms attack on a snare lets the initial crack pass before compression engages, keeping the punch. Release controls how quickly the compressor lets go after the signal falls back below the threshold. Too fast a release (under 30ms) causes the gain to bounce up and down with the signal's waveform, creating distortion or pumping. Too slow (over 500ms on a drum) means the compressor is still holding from the kick when the snare arrives. Getting release right — especially tuned to the tempo of the track — is what separates usable compression from great compression.

For most lead vocals, start with an optical compressor (LA-2A or a quality emulation like the UAD LA-2A). Its program-dependent release and inherently slow response are forgiving on speech-rate dynamics — it smooths without sounding processed. If the vocal is aggressive and you want to add edge, stack a FET compressor (1176) after the optical: the optical handles the big dynamic swings, the FET adds presence and character. Use a VCA on a vocal only when you need precise, repeatable control — broadcast work, mastering a vocal for loudness normalization, or when a vocal is so inconsistent that the program-dependent behavior of optical becomes unpredictable.

Pumping is caused by the gain reduction oscillating audibly with the rhythm of the material — most commonly triggered by kick drum or bass energy on a bus compressor. The fix depends on the cause: if pumping is rhythmically random, your release time is too fast for the tempo — set release to approximately 500–800ms or switch to auto-release. If pumping is rhythmic and you want to reduce it, switch to a sidechain high-pass filter set around 100–150 Hz so low-frequency energy doesn't dominate the compressor's detection circuit. If pumping is intentional (as in house music sidechain pumping), you're not fixing anything — you're tuning the release to lock to the tempo.

Individual channel compression is corrective — you're controlling a problem dynamic on a specific source: a vocalist who goes from a whisper to a shout, a bassist whose note-to-note level varies by 10 dB. Bus compression is cohesive — you're applying a uniform response to a group of instruments that causes them to interact with each other's dynamics, creating the psychoacoustic effect called 'glue.' The trap is using bus compression to compensate for poor individual channel compression: an SSL G-Bus at 4:1 on a drum bus cannot fix a kick with 15 dB of dynamic range — fix the individual source first, then add 2–3 dB of bus compression to unify.

Compression before EQ means the compressor responds to the full, unmodified frequency content of the source — a bass-heavy vocal will trigger more compression than a balanced one, because the compressor's detection circuit is reacting to the sum of all frequencies. Compression after EQ means you've shaped the tonality first and the compressor sees a more refined signal, typically producing more predictable and even gain reduction. In professional practice: use EQ before compression to fix problems that would cause uneven compression (excessive low end, resonant peaks), and EQ after compression to shape final tone. Many engineers run both — one EQ pre and one post — which gives complete control.

Mastering compression operates on the full stereo program — every decision affects every instrument simultaneously, which demands far more restraint. Mastering engineers typically target 1–3 dB of gain reduction maximum, use very slow attack times (50–150ms) to preserve the transient energy of the mix, and prefer variable-mu or optical character over VCA aggression. The ratio is almost always 1.5:1 to 3:1. The primary goal at mastering is not dynamic control — that should have been handled in the mix — but tonal shaping through the harmonic character of the compressor and subtle temporal cohesion. A Manley Variable Mu at 1.5:1 with 2 dB of GR can make a technically finished mix sound like a record in a way that no EQ can replicate.
