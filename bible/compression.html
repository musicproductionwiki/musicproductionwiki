Compression

/kəmˈprɛʃ.ən/

Quick Answer Definition How It Works Parameters Quick Reference History How To Use It In The Wild Types Mistakes Calculator Related Further Reading FAQ

Compression is a form of automatic gain control that reduces the dynamic range of an audio signal by attenuating levels above a set threshold. It shapes transients, adds density, and glues elements together in a mix.

01 Definition

Every great mix you've ever loved was held together by compression you couldn't hear — and destroyed by compression you could.

Compression is a dynamic processing technique that automatically reduces the gain of an audio signal whenever that signal exceeds a user-defined amplitude level, called the threshold. The result is a narrowed dynamic range — the distance between the quietest and loudest moments in a recording. Unlike static gain adjustment, which raises or lowers a signal uniformly, a compressor responds in real time to the incoming signal's behavior, making it one of the most context-sensitive tools in audio production. When applied with intention, it renders performances more consistent, gives instruments a sense of power and presence, and allows elements to sit cohesively in a dense mix.

The concept of dynamic range control is older than digital audio by decades. In the analog domain, compressors were essential engineering tools long before they became creative ones. Early broadcast and recording engineers used them to prevent transmitters from clipping and to protect tape and disc cutting machines from overshoots that would cause physical damage. Over time, engineers began to notice that certain compressors didn't just control peaks — they imparted a characteristic color, a sense of weight and body, that enhanced the recordings. The transition from corrective to expressive tool is what defines compression's central role in modern music production.

At its most fundamental level, a compressor operates by measuring an incoming signal, comparing it to a threshold, and applying gain reduction proportional to how far the signal exceeds that threshold — a relationship governed by the ratio parameter. A 4:1 ratio means that for every 4 dB above the threshold, only 1 dB passes through. At extreme ratios — 20:1 and beyond — the compressor functions as a limiter, allowing virtually no signal to exceed the threshold. Between these poles lies the entire expressive range of the tool: gentle, barely perceptible loudness evening at low ratios and ratios, dramatic peak-catching at higher ones.

What distinguishes a skilled compressor user from a novice is not an understanding of the parameter definitions but an understanding of timing. Attack and release — the speed at which gain reduction engages and disengages — determine whether a compressor enhances or destroys the transient character of an instrument. A fast attack on a snare drum flattens its crack; a slow attack lets the initial hit pass through and then catches the body, adding apparent punch. This counterintuitive relationship between attack speed and perceived impact is responsible for more mixes being improved or ruined by compression than any other single factor.

Beyond corrective and tonal applications, compression is used as a rhythmic and textural tool. Parallel compression — blending a heavily compressed signal with its uncompressed original — became a defining technique in the work of engineers like Andy Wallace and Tom Lord-Alge, adding density and energy without sacrificing dynamics. Sidechain compression, where the compressor's gain-reduction circuit is triggered by a separate signal rather than the audio being compressed, underpins the pumping four-on-the-floor aesthetic of electronic dance music and provides transparent ducking in broadcast. In mastering, multiband compression allows engineers to address dynamic problems in discrete frequency regions without affecting the overall tonal balance. Compression is not a single technique — it is an entire discipline.

02 How It Works

A compressor's signal chain consists of two parallel paths: the audio path and the detection path, sometimes called the sidechain. The audio path carries the actual program material and contains the variable gain element — historically a variable-mu tube, a VCA (Voltage-Controlled Amplifier) chip, an optical cell, or an FET (Field-Effect Transistor). The detection path continuously measures the level of the incoming signal, compares it to the threshold, and generates a control voltage that instructs the gain element how much to reduce gain. The speed and character of this control voltage is shaped by the attack and release time constants. In most modern compressors, an RMS (root mean square) or peak detector is used in the detection path — RMS detection averages level over a short window and responds more naturally to program material, while peak detection reacts to instantaneous amplitude spikes and is better suited to transient control.

When the input signal rises above the threshold, the compressor begins applying gain reduction at a rate determined by the attack time. A 10 ms attack means the compressor takes approximately 10 milliseconds to reach the target gain reduction. During that 10 ms window, the signal passes through unaffected — this is precisely how slow-attack compression preserves and even enhances transients. Once the signal falls back below the threshold, the gain element begins returning to unity gain at a rate set by the release time. If the release is too fast, the gain element pumps visibly with the rhythm of the music, causing audible artifacts; if too slow, the compressor fails to recover between transients and causes sustained gain reduction that dulls the overall dynamics.

The knee parameter describes how the compressor transitions between uncompressed and compressed behavior around the threshold. A hard knee applies the full ratio immediately the moment the signal crosses the threshold — a precise, audible onset of gain reduction well-suited to peak limiting and clinical control. A soft knee begins applying gentle gain reduction several decibels below the threshold and gradually increases to the full ratio over a range of typically 6–10 dB, producing a smoother, more natural onset that is generally more musical for program material like vocals and full mixes. Many hardware compressors — particularly optical and variable-mu designs — have an inherent soft knee built into their electromechanical response curves, which contributes to why they sound more transparent than hard-clipping digital limiters at equivalent gain reduction amounts.

Makeup gain compensates for the overall level reduction caused by compression. Because gain reduction by definition lowers the average output level, a makeup gain control — typically placed after the gain element — allows the engineer to restore the compressed signal to the same perceived loudness as the uncompressed input, enabling valid A/B comparisons. This is critically important: a louder signal almost always sounds better to the human ear regardless of quality, so without level-matched comparison, it is essentially impossible to evaluate whether a compressor is improving or damaging a signal. Some compressors and plugins offer auto-makeup gain, which attempts to compensate automatically, but this convenience comes at the cost of masking the true effect of large gain-reduction values.

Modern DAW compressor plugins model these analog behaviors with varying degrees of accuracy, from simple mathematical implementations to sophisticated circuit emulations that replicate transformer saturation, VCA nonlinearity, and program-dependent time constant behavior. The practical implication for producers is that two compressors with identical parameter settings can sound dramatically different based on their detection topology, gain element type, and the nonlinear behaviors baked into their designs. Understanding the physics of the underlying process — not just the controls — is what allows a producer to choose the right compressor for a given task and to recognize when a problem lies in the wrong tool rather than wrong settings.

03 The Parameters

Every compression — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these and you can work with any implementation.

THRESHOLD
The level above which gain reduction begins

Threshold sets the amplitude in dBFS at which the compressor starts attenuating the signal. Every decibel the input exceeds the threshold is subject to gain reduction according to the ratio. On vocals, a threshold around −18 to −12 dBFS typically catches peaks without over-compressing the average level; on a bus, −6 dBFS or higher is common to catch only the loudest moments.

RATIO
The degree of gain reduction applied above threshold

Ratio is expressed as input change : output change above the threshold. A 4:1 ratio means a signal 8 dB above threshold exits at only 2 dB above threshold — 6 dB of gain reduction. Ratios of 1.5:1 to 3:1 are considered gentle and musical; 4:1 to 8:1 are moderate and effective for drums and bass; 10:1 and above approach limiting territory and can sound aggressive or punishing on program material.

ATTACK
How quickly gain reduction engages after threshold crossing

Attack time, measured in milliseconds, determines how fast the compressor clamps down after the signal exceeds the threshold. Slower attack times (50–100 ms) let transients pass through intact, adding punch and presence — a critical setting for kick and snare. Faster attacks (1–10 ms) reduce or eliminate transient peaks, adding sustain and body. On vocals, 15–40 ms typically balances intelligibility with control.

RELEASE
How quickly gain reduction disengages after the signal falls below threshold

Release determines how fast the compressor returns to unity gain once the signal drops below the threshold. Too-fast release (under 50 ms on slow-moving material) causes audible pumping as the gain element chases the signal. Too-slow release (over 500 ms on dense material) causes sustained gain reduction that robs the mix of energy. Auto-release modes, found on compressors like the SSL G-Bus and many modern plugins, adjust release time dynamically based on the program material.

KNEE
The transition character at the threshold point

Knee controls how abruptly gain reduction begins as the signal approaches and crosses the threshold. Hard knee applies full ratio the instant the signal hits threshold — precise and assertive, suited to limiting and clinical peak control. Soft knee begins applying partial gain reduction up to 6 dB below the threshold, gradually ramping to the full ratio, producing a more natural and transparent result on complex program material like vocals and full mixes.

MAKEUP GAIN
Output gain to compensate for the overall level reduction

Because compression reduces average output level proportional to the gain reduction applied, makeup gain restores the signal to the target operating level. Proper makeup gain setting is essential for honest A/B evaluation: without it, the compressed signal will sound quieter and therefore inferior regardless of its actual quality. A useful shortcut is to set makeup gain so the output VU meter reads approximately the same as the input on average-level passages.

LOOKAHEAD
Predictive processing to catch fast transients before they occur

Lookahead introduces a small delay (typically 1–10 ms) into the audio path, allowing the detection circuit to see the signal slightly before it arrives at the gain element. This allows the compressor to begin gain reduction before a transient peak actually occurs, achieving zero-overshoot performance impossible with conventional feed-forward designs. Lookahead is common in digital limiters and mastering compressors; it introduces latency equal to the lookahead time but produces cleaner, more transparent peak control.

05 History & Origin

The history of audio compression begins with broadcast engineering in the 1930s. Western Electric's Level Amplifier, developed in the early 1930s to maintain consistent signal levels in telephone transmission, established the conceptual foundation for automatic gain control in audio. By the mid-1930s, NBC and CBS engineers were deploying feedback-based gain-riding amplifiers to prevent transmitter overloads, and the first dedicated broadcast compressors — large, hand-built units using Western Electric triode tubes — appeared in network radio infrastructure. These early devices were purely corrective; musicality was not a concern. Their core function was protecting equipment and maintaining legally mandated broadcast levels.

The introduction of magnetic tape recording in the late 1940s — facilitated by captured German Magnetophon technology — created new urgency for dynamic control. Tape saturation and oxide noise floors imposed a practical ceiling and floor on recordable dynamics, and engineers needed tools to manage signals within that window. The 1950s saw the emergence of the first commercially significant audio compressors: Fairchild's 660 and 670 variable-mu units (introduced around 1959) used tubes whose gain varied with the bias voltage generated by the input signal, producing an inherently program-dependent, musical compression character that engineers at Capitol Records, Abbey Road, and Columbia's 30th Street Studio quickly adopted. The Fairchild 670 — a stereo unit priced at $27,000 when new — remains one of the most coveted pieces of studio hardware ever built.

The 1960s and 1970s produced the compressor topologies that define the sound of recorded music. Bill Putnam's Universal Audio 1176LN, released in 1967, used a Field-Effect Transistor as its gain element, allowing attack times as fast as 20 microseconds — far beyond what tubes could achieve. The 1176's distinctive aggressive character, its program-dependent release, and its infamous All-Buttons-In mode (engaging all four ratio buttons simultaneously for a dense, overdriven compression) made it ubiquitous on vocals, bass, and room microphones. The Teletronix LA-2A optical compressor, also associated with Universal Audio and refined in the mid-1960s, used a T4 electro-optical cell (an LED and a light-dependent resistor) whose slow, program-dependent response made it ideal for vocals and bass. These two units — the 1176 and the LA-2A — appear on more hit records than any other pair of compressors in history.

The 1970s and 1980s introduced VCA-based compression and the bus compressor era. dbx's 160 (1976) brought VCA technology — faster, quieter, and more controllable than tubes or FETs — into common studio use. SSL's G-Series console incorporated a bus compressor (the SSL G-Bus Compressor, based on the dbx VCA) that became synonymous with the sound of 1980s and 1990s pop and rock: tight, controlled, punchy. Engineers including Bob Clearmountain, who mixed records by Bruce Springsteen, Bryan Adams, and the Rolling Stones, built their sound around the G-Bus compressor's ability to glue a mix into a coherent whole. The UREI 2-LA and API 2500 extended the VCA palette into different tonal characters. By the time digital audio workstations arrived in the 1990s, compression was no longer merely a corrective tool — it was a core element of production aesthetics, with specific hardware units associated with specific sonic signatures that producers actively sought.

06 How Producers Use It

On drums, compression serves two distinct purposes that often require separate compressors at different stages. On individual drum hits — kick, snare, toms — FET-based compressors like the 1176 or its plugin emulations are deployed to shape transients. A slow attack (20–40 ms) lets the initial transient crack pass through, then grabs the body of the hit, adding punch and sustain that makes drums feel powerful in the low-mid frequencies. On the drum bus, a fast-ratio VCA unit like the SSL G-Bus compressor — with a relatively slow attack (30–50 ms), auto release, and a 4:1 ratio — creates the characteristic tight, controlled bus sound associated with a decade of hit records. Many engineers employ both: individual compression for tone shaping and bus compression for cohesion.

Vocal compression is where the art form becomes most nuanced. The human voice has a dynamic range of 30 dB or more between a whisper and a belt, and a raw vocal recording is rarely usable without dynamic control. Most professional engineers begin with a gentle optical compressor (hardware LA-2A or plugin equivalent) set to 3–6 dB of gain reduction with a slow, program-dependent release to even out the fundamental dynamic variation. A second compressor or clipper may then catch remaining peaks. The goal is a vocal that sits in the mix consistently without sounding clamped or lifeless — the listener should never be aware of the compression, only of a performance that locks into the track. Engineers like Michael Brauer frequently use multiple compressors in series and parallel on a single vocal channel, each contributing a small amount of gain reduction.

On bass — both electric bass and synthesizer bass — compression prevents low-frequency peaks from dominating the mix and eating headroom, while simultaneously adding sustain that gives bass lines melodic presence. A moderate ratio (3:1–6:1), medium attack (20–40 ms to preserve pick attack), and a medium release (100–200 ms) is a common starting point. The interplay between bass and kick drum is a critical consideration: many engineers use a sidechain from the kick to the bass compressor, causing the bass to duck slightly each time the kick hits, creating separation and clarity in the low end without manual volume automation.

Bus and mix compression — the application of a single stereo compressor across an entire mix or submix — is one of the most consequential uses of the tool. The purpose is not primarily to reduce dynamics but to create a sense of the mix breathing and moving together: instruments become interdependent rather than isolated, and the overall presentation gains density and cohesion. Gain reduction of 1–3 dB on a mix bus is typically sufficient; more than 4 dB usually indicates a mix that has not been properly managed at the individual channel level. Critically, many engineers print the mix with the bus compressor engaged from the earliest stages, allowing them to make creative decisions in the context of the compressed sound rather than discovering at the end of the session that the bus compressor changes the character of the mix significantly.

AbletonAbleton's stock Compressor is a capable dual-topology device (peak and RMS detection, feedback and feedforward modes). Use it in Feedback mode with the Optical emulation setting for LA-2A-style vocal work; switch to Feedforward/Peak for punchy drum bus compression. The Glue Compressor — an SSL G-Bus emulation — is the go-to for bus and drum-group cohesion; engage the Soft Clip option to add subtle harmonic saturation on heavier compression settings.
FL StudioParametric Compressor offers clean, transparent VCA-style compression suitable for most tracking and mixing tasks. For character compression, reach for Maximus in single-band mode for mix bus or mastering work — its lookahead limiter and transient control are particularly effective on drum buses. Fruity Peak Controller can be used creatively for sidechain-style gain reduction without a dedicated compressor plugin.
Logic ProLogic's bundled Vintage VCA (SSL G-Bus emulation), Vintage FET (1176 emulation), and Vintage Opto (LA-2A emulation) are genuinely high-quality models — use the correct topology for the source. Vintage FET with All-Buttons mode engaged adds aggressive harmonic character to room mics and parallel drum compression. 07 In the Wild

Abstract knowledge becomes practical when you can hear it in music you know. These tracks demonstrate compression used intentionally, at specific moments, for specific purposes.

Red Hot Chili Peppers — "Give It Away" (1991)
0:00–0:08 intro, throughout · Produced by Rick Rubin, engineered by Brendan O'Brien

The drum performance by Chad Smith is one of the most compressed major-label rock recordings of its era. O'Brien used a heavy-ratio FET compressor on the snare with a medium-fast attack, allowing the initial crack to pass before the compressor clamps the body — producing that distinctive oversized, almost synthetic snare sound despite being entirely live. Listen specifically to how the snare sustains unnaturally long and how the hi-hats pump subtly in the background as the compressor releases; this is a textbook example of intentional inter-element pumping used as a rhythmic device. The bass guitar similarly displays obvious compression artifacts that contribute to the track's abrasive energy rather than detracting from it.

Daft Punk — "One More Time" (2000)
0:48 vocal entry, throughout chorus · Produced by Daft Punk

Romanthony's vocoded lead vocal is processed through heavy compression before the vocoder, resulting in an extremely consistent, dense signal that the vocoder tracks with uniform accuracy across the dynamic range. The mix bus compression across the full track creates a characteristic pumping interaction with the kick drum — at 1:00 in the chorus, listen to how the entire mix breathes downward with each kick hit. This is deliberate sidechain-style pumping via aggressive mix bus compression, a technique that became definitional for the progressive house and French filter house genres Daft Punk helped establish.

The Beatles — "Come Together" (1969)
0:04–0:12 bass intro · Produced by George Martin, engineered by Geoff Emerick

Paul McCartney's Rickenbacker bass through an Ampeg amplifier was recorded direct-injected and compressed through the EMI REDD console's limiting circuits and subsequently through an LA-2A-style optical limiter, creating the thick, smooth, almost synth-like bass tone that opens the track. This is a canonical example of optical compression used tonally rather than correctively — the limiter was not controlling problematic peaks but actively shaping the harmonic character and sustain of the instrument. The way each note blooms and sustains evenly, without audible pick attack, is the direct result of the optical limiter's slow, program-dependent attack behavior.

Kendrick Lamar — "HUMBLE." (2017)
0:00–0:06 drum intro, throughout · Produced by Mike WiLL Made-It

The kick and snare on 'HUMBLE.' are processed with extreme transient compression — a very fast attack compressor set to crush the body of each hit, then a parallel uncompressed signal blended back for impact. The result is a snare with almost no natural decay, sounding closer to a clap or a handclap sample than a live drum. The contrast between the controlled, clipped-sounding percussion and Kendrick's relatively dynamic vocal delivery is compositionally intentional. The 808 kick also exhibits clear sidechain ducking in the low end — the sub bass briefly drops with each kick hit, creating the sense of low-end space despite the track's density.

08 Types & Variants

VCA (Voltage-Controlled Amplifier)
SSL G-Bus Compressor · dbx 160 · API 2500 · Neve 33609

VCA compressors use an integrated circuit whose gain is controlled by a DC voltage derived from the detection path. They are the fastest and most controllable type, capable of extremely precise attack and release times and consistent, repeatable behavior. The SSL G-Bus VCA character — tight, punchy, slightly aggressive in the high-mids — became the defining sound of mix-bus compression in commercial music from the 1980s onward, and VCA compressors remain the most widely used type across all stages of professional mixing.

FET (Field-Effect Transistor)
Universal Audio 1176LN · UA 1176AE · Empirical Labs Distressor

FET compressors use a transistor whose conductance is modulated by the control signal, allowing attack times measured in tens of microseconds — far faster than any other topology. The 1176LN's characteristic sound includes a subtle harmonic distortion from the FET circuit itself and a program-dependent release behavior that responds musically to varying signal content. FET compressors are the standard choice for aggressive peak control on vocals, room microphones, and parallel drum compression where both speed and sonic character are desirable.

Optical
Teletronix LA-2A · LA-3A · Tube-Tech CL 1B

Optical compressors use a light source (historically an incandescent lamp or electroluminescent panel, now typically an LED) and a photoresistor to control gain. Because the light source and photocell both have inherent physical response curves — slower to illuminate and slower to dim — optical compressors exhibit program-dependent attack and release behavior that is widely considered the most transparent and musical of any compressor topology. The LA-2A's two-knob simplicity (Peak Reduction and Output) and its inability to produce harsh artifacts even under heavy gain reduction make it the default choice for tracking vocals.

Variable-Mu (Vari-mu)
Fairchild 660/670 · Manley Variable Mu · Altec 436

Variable-mu compressors use triode or pentode vacuum tubes whose amplification factor (mu) decreases as the bias current increases, creating gain reduction directly in the tube gain stage. The result is compression with an inherently soft knee, program-dependent timing, and the harmonic richness associated with tube circuitry. Vari-mu units are typically the slowest of the major topologies — poorly suited to fast transient control — but excellent for gentle, transparent program compression on full mixes and mastering applications where tonal enhancement alongside dynamic control is the goal.

Multiband
Weiss DS1-MK3 · TC Electronic MD3 · Neve 8803

Multiband compressors split the input signal into frequency bands (typically 3–6) using crossover filters and apply independent compression to each band before recombining. This allows frequency-specific dynamic control — compressing a problematic 200 Hz buildup in a bass guitar without affecting the high-frequency transient content, for example. Multiband compression is the dominant dynamic control format in mastering and broadcast processing, and is used surgically in mixing to address sources with frequency-specific dynamic problems that broadband compression cannot resolve without collateral damage.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    Over-compressing: crushing dynamics until the track feels lifeless
    The most common beginner mistake is applying too much gain reduction — often 10 dB or more on individual tracks — in pursuit of perceived loudness or consistency. The result is a track where all the natural dynamics of the performance are eliminated, leaving something that feels flat, static, and exhausting to listen to. The fix is to aim for 3–6 dB of gain reduction on most sources and to use your ears rather than the GR meter as your primary reference. If you can hear the compressor working, it is likely working too hard.
  • ✕
    Wrong attack time: killing the transient on a snare or kick
    Setting a very fast attack (1–3 ms) on drums flattens the initial transient of the hit — the crack or thump that makes a drum feel physical and present. The compressor reaches full gain reduction before the transient peak exits the gain element, effectively removing the snap. This produces a dull, padded drum sound when punch was the goal. The fix: slow the attack to 20–50 ms and observe the GR meter — you should see it respond after the peak, not before or simultaneous with it.
  • ✕
    Ignoring release time: audible pumping on non-dance material
    A release time set too fast on program material that doesn't call for pumping causes the compressor's gain element to hunt constantly, producing an audible wavering or breathing on sustained notes and between syllables in vocals. This is different from intentional pump-and-release in dance music — unintentional pumping draws attention to the compression rather than the performance. The fix is to lengthen the release time until the gain element recovers smoothly between transients, or to use an auto-release mode that adapts to the source.
  • ✕
    Not compensating for makeup gain during A/B comparison
    Because a compressed signal is quieter than the uncompressed original (before makeup gain is applied), bypassing the compressor for comparison will make the uncompressed signal sound immediately better — louder, more dynamic, more present. This is a perceptual illusion driven by the loudness-equals-quality bias in human hearing. Always match levels before comparing: reduce the bypass signal or increase the compressed output until both read the same average loudness on a VU meter or a loudness meter calibrated to LUFS.
  • ✕
    Using compression to fix a performance problem it cannot solve
    Compression reduces dynamic range — it cannot fix out-of-tune notes, timing errors, or a performance that is inconsistent in tone rather than just level. A common mistake is reaching for a compressor to try to mask a vocal that moves too much in pitch or a bass line with uneven tone between positions. The correct tool for pitch inconsistency is tuning correction; for tonal inconsistency, EQ and re-recording are more appropriate. Applying heavy compression to a flawed performance often makes the problem more audible by sustaining and emphasizing the offending notes.
  • ✕
    Compressing everything by default, regardless of whether the source needs it
    The habit of inserting a compressor on every channel as the first mixing move — without auditing whether the source actually has a dynamic problem — leads to mixes that feel uniformly squashed and lack the contrast between dynamic and non-dynamic elements that creates interest. Some sources, such as a well-played acoustic guitar, a carefully performed piano, or a programmed pad with intentional dynamic swells, may not need compression at all. Always listen to the unprocessed source first and ask what specific problem compression would solve before inserting it.

12 Frequently Asked Questions

A compressor automatically reduces the gain of an audio signal whenever it exceeds a set threshold level. The amount of reduction is controlled by the ratio parameter, and the speed of that reduction is controlled by attack and release. The practical result is a narrower dynamic range — loud peaks are brought down, making the overall signal more consistent in level. After compression, makeup gain is applied to restore the average level, and the net effect is a signal that sounds more controlled, dense, or present than the unprocessed original.
Compression and limiting are the same process at different ratio settings. Compression typically operates at ratios of 1.5:1 to 8:1 and is used to shape and even out dynamics over time. Limiting uses ratios of 10:1 and above — sometimes infinity:1 — to prevent a signal from exceeding the threshold at all, functioning as a hard ceiling on amplitude. Limiters typically also use very fast (or lookahead) attack times to catch instantaneous peaks that slower compressors would miss. In practice, the distinction is a matter of degree and intent: limiting is extreme, corrective compression.
The clearest sign of over-compression is a loss of dynamics so complete that the signal sounds flat, lifeless, or fatiguing to listen to — elements no longer have a sense of energy or movement. Technically, you can check the gain reduction meter: more than 6–8 dB of constant gain reduction on a single source is often excessive. The most reliable test is an A/B comparison at matched levels: if the compressed version sounds worse than the uncompressed version when levels are equal, you are compressing too much. Also listen for pumping, breathing, or a sense of the track 'gasping' — these are signs of fast release times combined with excessive ratios.
Attack is the time it takes for the compressor to reach full gain reduction after the signal exceeds the threshold — typically measured in milliseconds. A slow attack lets the initial transient peak pass through unaffected before clamping down. Release is the time it takes for the compressor to return to zero gain reduction after the signal falls below the threshold. A slow release means the compressor holds on longer; a fast release means it lets go quickly. Attack shapes the character of transients; release governs how the compressor interacts with the rhythm and flow of the material.
Parallel compression (sometimes called New York compression) is a technique where a heavily compressed copy of a signal is blended with the original uncompressed signal. The compressed signal adds density, sustain, and body in the mid-level content of the signal, while the uncompressed signal preserves the natural transient peaks and dynamic feel. The result is a sound that has both impact (from the transients that the heavy compressor would otherwise destroy) and fatness (from the boosted sustain and body in the compressed path). It is widely used on drums, room microphones, and full mixes.
Beginners should start with the compressor bundled in their DAW — every major platform ships with at least one capable compressor that can teach the fundamentals without introducing the variable of hardware or plugin quality. In Ableton, use the stock Compressor or Glue Compressor; in Logic, the Vintage VCA or Vintage Opto; in FL Studio, the Parametric Compressor. Learn to hear what each parameter does in isolation before worrying about hardware emulations or character compressors. Once you can predict what a compressor will do to a given source before you move a knob, then exploring SSL, 1176, or LA-2A emulations becomes musically productive.
Sidechain compression routes the detection circuit of a compressor to a separate 'key' signal rather than the audio being compressed. When the key signal exceeds the threshold, the compressor reduces the gain of the audio signal — even if the audio signal itself is quiet. In electronic music, the most common application is routing the kick drum as the sidechain key into a compressor on the bass or the full mix bus. Each kick hit causes the bass (or the mix) to duck briefly and then recover — the classic pumping, breathing pulse of four-on-the-floor dance music. The technique originated in broadcast (for voice-over ducking over music beds) and was adopted in electronic music production as a rhythmic and textural device.
Each topology imposes different timing behavior, harmonic distortion characteristics, and program-dependency on the compression process. VCA compressors (SSL, dbx) are fast, precise, and relatively neutral — they compress what you tell them to compress, when you tell them to, with minimal added color. FET compressors (1176) are extremely fast and add a specific harmonic edge and density from the transistor circuit's nonlinearity. Optical compressors (LA-2A) have inherently slow, program-dependent responses that make them difficult to push into harshness — the optical cell can't react fast enough to produce sharp-edged artifacts. Variable-mu compressors (Fairchild, Manley) are the slowest and most colored, adding tube warmth and soft-knee behavior suited to mastering and gentle program compression. Choosing a topology is an aesthetic decision as much as a technical one.
