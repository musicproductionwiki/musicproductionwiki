FM Synthesis

/ˌɛf ˈɛm ˈsɪnθəsɪs/

Quick Answer Definition How It Works Parameters Quick Reference History How To Use It In The Wild Types Mistakes Calculator Related Further Reading FAQ

FM Synthesis is a method of sound generation where one oscillator's frequency is modulated by another, creating complex, harmonically rich timbres from simple sine waves. It is the engine behind the electric pianos, basses, and metallic textures defining pop and electronic music since the 1980s.

01 Definition

Every producer who has ever dialed in that glassy DX7 electric piano or sculpted a punishing FM bass has stood at the edge of something mathematically precise yet sonically infinite — FM synthesis rewards the curious and humbles the impatient, and once it clicks, nothing else quite sounds like it.

Frequency Modulation (FM) synthesis is a method of audio synthesis in which the instantaneous frequency of one oscillator — called the carrier — is varied periodically by the output of a second oscillator called the modulator. Unlike subtractive synthesis, which begins with a harmonically rich waveform and carves away unwanted frequencies with filters, FM synthesis begins with simple sine waves and generates complex spectra through the interaction of oscillator ratios and modulation depth. The result is a sound that can range from gentle, bell-like shimmer to harsh, inharmonic clangor — all without a single filter in the signal path.

At its core, FM synthesis is governed by a deceptively simple equation: when a sine wave modulator at frequency fm drives a carrier at frequency fc, the output contains sidebands at frequencies fc ± n·fm, where n is any positive integer. The number and relative amplitude of these sidebands is determined by the modulation index (I), defined as the peak frequency deviation divided by the modulator frequency. A low modulation index produces a handful of sidebands and a clean, bell-like tone; a high index floods the spectrum with dozens of partials and generates dense, distorted textures. This mathematical regularity is what gives FM synthesis its distinctive character: spectra that feel structured and purposeful even at their most extreme.

In practical synthesizer design, individual oscillators are called operators. Each operator is typically a sine wave oscillator paired with its own amplitude envelope. Operators are arranged in algorithms — predetermined routing diagrams that define which operators act as carriers, which act as modulators, and how they feed into each other. A six-operator FM synthesizer like the Yamaha DX7 offers 32 algorithms, each producing a fundamentally different timbral character before a single parameter is adjusted. Stacking operators in series creates FM chains where each stage adds another layer of spectral complexity; branching operators in parallel allows multiple independent timbral threads to be mixed at the output.

The relationship between carrier and modulator frequencies — the frequency ratio — is arguably the most musically decisive parameter in FM synthesis. Integer ratios (1:1, 1:2, 2:1, 3:2) produce harmonic spectra with a clear, pitched quality. Non-integer ratios produce inharmonic spectra with a metallic, bell-like, or percussive character. This single principle explains why FM synthesis is uniquely suited to both the warm shimmer of a Rhodes-style electric piano and the atonal clang of a struck metal bar. The ratio is where FM's musical identity lives, and fluency with ratio selection is what separates practitioners from preset-browsers.

Modern producers encounter FM synthesis across a broad range of tools: hardware FM synthesizers from the 1980s that have never left professional studios, dedicated FM software instruments such as Native Instruments FM8 and Arturia DX7 V, and the FM operator sections embedded in modular synthesizers and hybrid workstations. Understanding FM at the parameter level — not just recalling presets — unlocks a synthesis method whose expressive ceiling has never been fully mapped, decades after its commercial introduction.

02 How It Works

The signal flow in a basic two-operator FM patch is straightforward: the modulator oscillator generates a sine wave at its own frequency and amplitude. This signal is added directly to the carrier oscillator's frequency input rather than to its audio output. The carrier then outputs a sine wave whose instantaneous pitch is being continuously pushed and pulled by the modulator — not its volume. This is the critical distinction between FM and AM (amplitude modulation): FM alters pitch continuously, and because the human auditory system interprets rapid pitch fluctuations as changes in timbre rather than as distinct pitch events, we perceive sideband frequencies rather than pitch wobble. At audio rates (above roughly 20 Hz), modulation stops sounding like vibrato and becomes tone color.

The modulation index (I = Δf / fm, where Δf is peak frequency deviation and fm is modulator frequency) controls how many sidebands are present and how loud they are relative to the carrier. At I = 0, only the carrier frequency is audible. As I increases toward 1, the first few sidebands emerge and the sound gains warmth and brightness. At I = 3–5, the spectrum is dense enough to produce convincing brass and electric piano imitations. At I above 10, the carrier fundamental can actually disappear beneath its own sidebands, producing noise-like or completely inharmonic tones. Enveloping the modulator's amplitude over time — so that the modulation index starts high and decays — recreates the natural spectral evolution of struck and plucked instruments, which are brightest at the moment of attack and dull rapidly thereafter.

Multi-operator FM extends this logic by chaining modulators. Operator B modulates Operator A (which modulates the carrier Operator C). Each stage multiplies the spectral complexity. A modulator feeding back into itself — a feedback loop — produces progressively noisier, saw-wave-like spectra as feedback amount increases, giving FM synthesizers access to harmonically rich timbres without a second modulator. In the DX7's architecture, one operator per algorithm is permitted to feed back into itself, and this single operator is responsible for much of the instrument's ability to produce aggressive, reedy, or noisy timbres. Feedback is often overlooked by FM beginners but is a primary color in professional FM programming.

Operator envelopes in commercial FM synthesizers — the DX7's Rate/Level system being the canonical example — use a four-stage rate-and-level format rather than the standard ADSR attack/decay/sustain/release model. Each stage specifies both a rate (how fast the envelope moves) and a level (the target amplitude), giving designers fine control over the multi-segment transient shapes that make FM electric pianos, marimbas, and bells feel acoustically authentic. The carrier envelope controls the overall amplitude contour of the note; each modulator envelope independently sculpts the brightness trajectory of the sound across time.

The output stage of most FM synthesizers passes the carrier audio signal through a global amplitude envelope and optionally through a low-frequency oscillator (LFO) for vibrato or tremolo. Some implementations — including the DX7's successor the SY77 and modern instruments like the Korg Opsix — add filters, effects, and additional modulation sources after the FM core, creating hybrid architectures that combine FM's spectral precision with subtractive shaping. Understanding the pure FM signal path first, however, is essential: filters applied after the fact cannot replicate the dynamic spectral behavior of a well-designed FM algorithm, but they can refine and polish it.

03 The Parameters

Every fm synthesis — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these and you can work with any implementation.

MODULATION INDEX (I)
Controls spectral density and brightness

Defined as peak frequency deviation divided by modulator frequency (I = Δf / fm). At I=0, only the carrier is audible; at I=1–2, a few sidebands add warmth; at I=5–10, the spectrum is dense and metallic. Enveloping I over time (bright attack decaying to warm sustain) is the key to natural-sounding FM patches. In most hardware synths, modulator output level directly maps to I.

FREQUENCY RATIO (fc : fm)
Determines harmonic or inharmonic character

The ratio of carrier frequency to modulator frequency sets the tonal character of the sound. Integer ratios such as 1:1, 1:2, or 3:2 place sidebands on harmonic series positions, producing pitched, musical tones. Ratios like 1:1.41 or 1:3.14 scatter sidebands off-harmonically, yielding metallic, bell-like, or percussive textures. This is the most musically expressive parameter in FM synthesis — learn it before all others.

OPERATOR LEVEL
Sets output amplitude of each operator

For a carrier operator, output level controls volume. For a modulator operator, output level controls how strongly it modulates the carrier — effectively acting as the modulation index control in most hardware implementations including the DX7. A modulator at full level produces maximum spectral complexity; pulled back to zero, it goes silent and removes its contribution to the timbre. Automating modulator levels is the primary FM performance gesture.

OPERATOR ENVELOPE (RATE / LEVEL)
Shapes the amplitude trajectory of each operator over time

Commercial FM synthesizers typically use a multi-stage Rate/Level envelope rather than classic ADSR. Each stage specifies a target level and the rate at which the envelope reaches it, allowing complex, multi-breakpoint contours. The DX7 uses a four-stage system (R1/L1 through R4/L4 plus release). Carrier envelopes control overall dynamics; modulator envelopes sculpt brightness over time, which is what gives FM patches their characteristic spectral animation.

ALGORITHM
Defines routing topology of operators

An algorithm is a fixed wiring diagram specifying which operators are carriers, which are modulators, and how they connect. The DX7's 32 algorithms range from simple two-operator pairs to complex six-operator chains and parallel stacks. Algorithm choice determines the fundamental timbral architecture before any parameter is adjusted — Algorithm 5 (three parallel carriers each with its own modulator) is ideal for organs and pads, while Algorithm 1 (a six-deep serial chain) produces the most extreme harmonic generation.

FEEDBACK
Routes an operator's output back into its own frequency input

Feedback causes a modulator (or carrier) to modulate itself, progressively transforming a pure sine wave into a sawtooth-like waveform as the feedback amount increases. At low feedback values, the operator gains subtle harmonic content. At high values, it becomes a rich, buzzy tone approaching white noise. On the DX7, one operator per algorithm is feedback-capable, and it is commonly used as a noise source for snare hits, the reed-like core of wind patches, or the gritty edge in FM bass sounds.

TRANSPOSE / DETUNE
Sets operator frequency offset from the played pitch

Operators can be set to track the keyboard at a fixed ratio (harmonic mode) or locked to a fixed frequency regardless of pitch (fixed mode). Fixed-mode operators are essential for percussion synthesis — a kick drum's thump frequency stays constant regardless of which key triggers it. Coarse detune shifts the operator by semitones; fine detune by cents, enabling unison-style thickening when two operators are detuned slightly against each other.

05 History & Origin

FM synthesis was developed mathematically by composer and electrical engineer John Chowning at Stanford University's Center for Computer Research in Music and Acoustics (CCRMA) in 1967–1973. Chowning discovered that modulating one audio-rate oscillator with another produced complex, musically useful spectra, and that varying the modulation index over time closely replicated the spectral evolution of natural acoustic instruments — particularly brass, percussion, and bells. Stanford filed a patent in 1975 (US Patent 4,018,121), and after IBM and other American companies declined to license the technology, it was licensed exclusively to Yamaha in 1973 in what became one of the most lucrative university patent deals in history, ultimately generating over $20 million for Stanford.

Yamaha's first FM hardware was the GS-1, released in 1981 — a concert-grand-sized, $16,000 professional instrument that found its way into Japanese broadcast studios and academic settings. The accessible breakthrough came with the Yamaha DX7 in 1983, priced at $1,995 and offering six operators, 32 algorithms, 16-voice polyphony, and MIDI implementation in a stage-ready package. The DX7 was an immediate commercial phenomenon: it sold over 200,000 units in its first three years, making it the best-selling professional synthesizer to that point in history. Its factory presets — particularly the Rhodes-style electric piano (Preset 1, "E.PIANO 1"), the marimba, the bass guitar, and the brass ensemble — defined the sonic palette of 1980s pop, R&B, and jazz fusion. The DX7's electric piano alone appears on thousands of commercially released recordings between 1983 and 1992.

The DX7 was followed by an extensive family: the four-operator DX9, DX21, DX27, and DX100 brought FM to budget buyers; the eight-operator Yamaha TX816 rack system became a studio standard for producers wanting denser FM textures; and the Yamaha DX7II (1986) introduced a second bank of sounds, a built-in chorus, and fractional-scaling envelopes. Competing manufacturers responded — Casio's Phase Distortion synthesis in the CZ-101 (1984) was a related but distinct approach marketed at the consumer level, while Korg's DW-8000 layered sampled attack transients over FM-like digital wavetables. By the late 1980s, Yamaha had introduced AFM (Advanced FM) in the SY77 and SY99, combining FM operators with AWM2 sample playback and a resonant filter, a hybrid architecture that pointed toward the instrument design philosophy of the following decade.

FM synthesis fell commercially from prominence in the early 1990s as ROM-based ROMpler instruments and later sample-based workstations offered more immediately accessible sonic variety. However, FM never disappeared from professional use: the Yamaha FS1R (1998) expanded FM to 88 operators with formant-based shaping; the Yamaha Montage (2016) and MODX re-introduced eight-operator FM-X synthesis alongside AWM2 samples. Software rekindled widespread interest — Native Instruments FM7 (2000) and FM8 (2007) made DX7-compatible FM programming available in any DAW with a modern visual interface. Arturia's DX7 V (2017) added modulation matrix depth and effects that the original hardware lacked. Korg's Opsix (2021) introduced what Korg termed "Altered FM" synthesis, blending FM operators with additional waveshaping, filter, and ring modulation modes, while simultaneously teaching a new generation of producers the fundamental FM workflow through a hardware interface designed for hands-on exploration.

06 How Producers Use It

Electric piano and keys: The most historically significant FM application, the DX7 electric piano preset remains in active use on contemporary records across soul, neo-soul, and pop. Producers programming from scratch typically use a two-operator pair per voice (modulator at a 1:1 ratio to carrier), set modulator level to produce I ≈ 2–3 at the note onset, and apply a relatively fast modulator envelope decay (300–600 ms) so the sound transitions from bright attack to warm sustain naturally. Adding light velocity sensitivity to modulator level introduces expressive dynamics. Slight carrier detuning against a second parallel carrier thickens the timbre without heavy unison detune artifacts.

Bass synthesis: FM bass is a defining element of house, techno, electro, and countless R&B productions from the 1980s onward. The approach involves a carrier tuned to the fundamental pitch and a modulator at a 1:3 or 1:5 ratio driving significant spectral complexity into the midrange (I = 3–7). A fast attack on the modulator envelope with quick decay produces the characteristic "click" that cuts through a mix at low volume, a perceptual trick that subtractive bass patches without transient FM articulation cannot easily replicate. For deeper sub presence, a fixed-frequency operator tuned to 50–80 Hz can be layered as an additional carrier in a parallel algorithm branch.

Percussion and drums: FM is exceptionally suited to percussion because non-integer ratios produce natural inharmonic decay profiles. A kick drum patch uses two operators: a carrier at the target pitch with a carrier pitch envelope falling rapidly from 150–200 Hz to 50–60 Hz over 40–80 ms, and a modulator at a fixed frequency (80–120 Hz) with a fast decay (20–50 ms) and moderate modulation index that produces the punchy transient click. Snare and hi-hat sounds use high-feedback operators and inharmonic ratios to generate noise-like bursts. The Yamaha RX11 and RX5 drum machines from 1985–1986 used FM-based synthesis for their percussion voices and appear on countless recordings from that period.

Pads, bells, and textures: FM's inharmonic capabilities are its most distinctive creative resource for sound designers. Bell tones are generated with non-integer ratios (carrier:modulator = 1:1.4 or 1:2.756), long modulator decays, and low-to-moderate index. The result is a naturally decaying tone with beating partials that no subtractive patch can replicate. For evolving pads, modulation index is assigned to a slow LFO or drawn-in automation, causing the spectrum to open and close over time. Layering multiple FM voices with slightly different algorithms and ratios creates rich, complex textures well-suited to atmospheric film, ambient, and neosoul production contexts.

AbletonOperator is Ableton's native four-operator FM synth. Use the Algorithm view to select your routing, then focus on envelope stages A, D, S, R for each operator independently. Drag the Ratio dials while holding Shift for fine-tuning. For DX7 compatibility, load .syx files via third-party converters into FM8 run as a plug-in alongside Operator.
FL StudioSytrus is FL Studio's stock FM engine — it offers six operators, a matrix-based routing grid, and built-in unison, filter, and effects stages. Start with the Operator routing matrix rather than the mod/car labels, and use the FX column per operator for per-operator distortion and filtering. Sytrus can import some DX7 SysEx data via community patch converters.
Logic ProLogic's FM synthesizer is Alchemy in FM mode: set Source A or B to Additive, enable FM modulation routing in the mod matrix, and use Alchemy's extensive modulation sources (Env, LFO, MIDI) to animate index over time. For classic DX7 emulation, third-party plug-ins (FM8, DX7 V) are recommended; Alchemy's FM implementation is hybrid and best used for experimental or layered patches.
Pro ToolsPro Tools ships no stock FM synthesizer; the recommended workflow is to use Xpand!2 (included) with FM-style presets as a starting point, then supplement with third-party AAX instruments such as Native Instruments FM8 or Arturia DX7 V. Route FM instruments to dedicated Instrument tracks and print to audio early — FM patches with high operator counts can be CPU-intensive in larger sessions.
ReaperReaper ships no native FM synthesizer. The recommended approach is the free, open-source Dexed plug-in (VST2/VST3) — a cycle-accurate DX7 emulator that accepts original SysEx patch banks and provides a visual interface for all 32 algorithms and six operators. Run Dexed on an FX instrument track; use Reaper's built-in parameter modulation to automate any operator level or envelope in real time.

07 In the Wild

Abstract knowledge becomes practical when you can hear it in music you know. These tracks demonstrate fm synthesis used intentionally, at specific moments, for specific purposes.

Whitney Houston — "I Wanna Dance with Somebody" (1987)
0:00 intro / throughout · Produced by Narada Michael Walden

The opening keyboard riff and the sustained pad throughout are DX7-based FM patches layered with a Yamaha DX5. Listen at 0:04 for the electric piano voicing — the characteristic midrange sheen and fast spectral decay on each note attack are textbook high-modulation-index FM. The bass line locking with the kick uses FM bass programming (operator ratio 1:3) that was Narada Michael Walden's signature production fingerprint through this period. The entire track is a master class in how FM synthesis textures sit in a densely arranged pop mix.

Aphex Twin — "Xtal" (1992)
0:00–2:00 / throughout · Produced by Richard D. James

Richard D. James (Aphex Twin) was an early adopter of FM synthesis for ambient and IDM contexts. The opening arpeggiated chime figure on 'Xtal' uses inharmonic FM ratios to produce a bell texture with natural, non-periodic beating between partials — an effect that samples and subtractive patches cannot replicate with the same physical plausibility. The attack transient on each note is particularly instructive: a sharp, high-index burst that decays to a quieter, cleaner sustain, demonstrating FM's modulator envelope decay technique in a non-pop context.

Toto — "Africa" (1982)
0:00 intro chime / 0:42 synth bass · Produced by David Paich and Steve Porcaro

The iconic opening mallet/chime sequence was performed by David Paich on a Yamaha GS-1 — one of the earliest commercial FM synthesizers — months before the DX7's release. Listen to the natural inharmonic decay of the marimba-like tones: the way upper partials fade before the fundamental is a function of the FM modulator envelope, not reverb or EQ. The underlying synth bass at 0:42 uses an FM bass algorithm that became a template for DX7 bass programming throughout the decade. The production illustrates FM synthesis in its earliest commercial form.

Kali Uchis — "After the Storm" (2018)
0:26 electric piano entrance · Produced by Kaytranada

Kaytranada's production on this track employs a DX7-style electric piano — programmed with a velocity-sensitive modulation index so the instrument brightens with harder key hits and darkens to a smooth, muffled tone at low velocities. This dynamic spectral response (bright attack, warm sustain, dynamics tied to modulator level) is specifically an FM capability; sample-based electric pianos at the same velocity sensitivity sound qualitatively different because they capture a fixed snapshot of the acoustic instrument's spectrum rather than synthesizing it dynamically. The FM timbre provides an organic quality that anchors the track's neo-soul aesthetic.

Burial — "Archangel" (2007)
Throughout · Produced by Burial (William Bevan)

Burial is known for using Yamaha FM synthesizers alongside time-stretched samples. The melodic synth motif running through 'Archangel' employs FM operator feedback to generate a slightly noisy, reed-like quality in the upper harmonics — identifiable by the gentle roughness that persists through the sustain phase of each note and does not correlate with reverb or distortion applied after synthesis. Studying this track against the DX7's feedback parameter (0 to 7) illustrates how a single parameter creates the difference between a clean sine-based tone and the gritty atmospheric textures Burial makes central to his sound.

08 Types & Variants

Classic 6-Operator Hardware FM
Yamaha DX7 · Yamaha DX7II · Yamaha TX816

Six sine-wave operators arranged in 32 algorithms, controlled by the Rate/Level envelope system and a single feedback-capable operator per algorithm. Sound character is clean, precise, and highly dynamic — the spectrum changes character with every velocity and every millisecond of the envelope trajectory. This is the canonical FM voice and the reference point for all subsequent FM implementations.

4-Operator Budget FM
Yamaha DX9 · Yamaha DX21 · Yamaha DX27 · Yamaha DX100

Four-operator FM with a reduced algorithm set (typically 8 algorithms versus 32). Sonically similar to the DX7 but with less spectral complexity and fewer layering possibilities. The DX21's velocity-to-modulation-index routing and its two-layer architecture make it a practical sound design tool; its lower cost makes it common in vintage gear collections. The simpler interface is often recommended for FM beginners.

Advanced / Hybrid FM
Yamaha SY77 · Yamaha FS1R · Korg Opsix · Elektron Digitone

FM architectures expanded beyond the DX7 template with additional operator types, built-in filters, formant shaping, or alternative modulation modes. The FS1R adds formant-sequence operators for vocal-like timbres; the Opsix provides operator types including waveshaping and ring modulation alongside standard FM. These instruments are suited to producers who find classic DX7 FM limiting and want to integrate FM spectral design into broader hybrid signal chains.

Software FM Emulation
Native Instruments FM8 · Arturia DX7 V · Ableton Operator · FL Studio Sytrus

Software implementations of FM synthesis range from cycle-accurate DX7 emulators (FM8, DX7 V) to expanded reimaginings with modern modulation matrices and effects (Operator, Sytrus). Software FM advantages include DAW parameter automation, recall, and DAW integration; disadvantages include the loss of the tactile, hands-on workflow that makes hardware FM programming faster for experienced users. FM8's graphic operator display and matrix routing made FM programming more accessible to a generation of producers who had no hardware FM background.

Modular FM
Intellijel Dixie II+ · Mutable Instruments Braids · Make Noise DPO

In Eurorack and modular synthesis contexts, FM is applied using discrete oscillator modules with dedicated FM input jacks and CV-controllable frequency. Linear FM (modulating frequency in Hz) versus exponential FM (modulating in semitones) is a critical distinction in modular — linear FM is used for timbral modulation at audio rates, exponential FM for pitch-stable vibrato and melodic pitch modulation. Modular FM patches are not limited to sine wave operators and commonly use any waveform, creating hybrid FM textures unavailable in operator-based synthesizers.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    Ignoring the modulator envelope — treating FM like a static texture
    The most common FM programming mistake is setting the modulator to a sustained level and leaving it there, producing a tone that is bright throughout with no spectral evolution. Real acoustic instruments — and convincing FM patches — have a modulation index that is highest at the note attack and decays rapidly. Start by giving every modulator a distinct envelope with a peak at attack and a decay shorter than the carrier's sustain. Even a 200 ms modulator decay transforms a static FM tone into something that breathes.
  • ✕
    Using non-integer ratios on pitched instruments by accident
    Non-integer carrier-to-modulator ratios produce inharmonic sideband positions that cause tuning instability and beating artifacts across the musical scale — frequencies that sound slightly 'off' on certain notes, especially in the upper register. This is intentional for bells and percussion but destructive for bass lines, lead synths, or anything meant to sit in a harmonic mix. Always confirm that ratios are integer values (or simple fractions like 0.5, 1.5) for pitched material. Most FM synthesizers display ratios clearly; check them before assuming a preset is correctly tuned.
  • ✕
    Maxing out feedback to add 'character' and creating noise
    Feedback is a powerful FM parameter, but above roughly 50–60% on most hardware, the operator output becomes white-noise-like and masks the fundamental. Producers new to FM often increase feedback hoping for harmonic richness but instead get an undefined hiss that muddies the mix. Use feedback at moderate settings (20–40%) to add harmonic bite, then use EQ and filtering to shape the result. Reserve maximum feedback for intentional noise textures like snare transients or specific harsh textural effects.
  • ✕
    Applying heavy post-synthesis EQ instead of adjusting the algorithm
    FM synthesis produces highly specific spectral shapes that are set at the synthesis stage. Boosting or cutting frequencies with EQ after the fact is less effective than adjusting the ratio, index, or algorithm — an EQ boost at 2 kHz applied to an FM patch that lacks sideband energy at 2 kHz will find nothing to boost and may instead emphasize adjacent artifacts. Learn to read the spectrum of your FM patch and address timbral problems at the synthesis level. EQ on FM outputs should be primarily corrective (removing harshness, shaping low end) rather than tonal (adding character).
  • ✕
    Confusing linear and exponential FM in modular contexts
    In modular synthesis, patching audio-rate FM into the exponential FM input rather than the linear FM input causes pitch to shift across the frequency range of the carrier — the modulation index effectively changes with carrier pitch, making patches that play consistently at one pitch sound completely different two octaves up or down. Linear FM inputs are correct for timbral audio-rate modulation; exponential FM inputs are correct for pitch modulation (vibrato, portamento). This distinction does not arise in dedicated FM synthesizers like the DX7 because their architecture handles it internally.
  • ✕
    Copying DX7 presets without understanding them
    Loading factory or downloaded DX7 presets is a valid starting point but produces producers who cannot modify them when a sound doesn't fit the session. A preset that sounds right in isolation may be too bright in a dense mix, or the bass response may be wrong for the key of the track. Without understanding which operator is the modulator, what the ratios are, and where the modulation index sits, adjustments are guesswork. Before using a preset in a commercial session, spend five minutes mapping its algorithm, identifying its carriers and modulators, and adjusting modulator level and envelope decay to taste.

12 Frequently Asked Questions

Subtractive synthesis starts with a harmonically rich waveform (sawtooth, square, or noise) and removes frequencies using filters, envelopes, and amplifiers to arrive at the desired sound. FM synthesis starts with simple sine waves and generates harmonic complexity through frequency modulation — no filters required. The key practical difference is that FM spectral content changes dynamically with every envelope stage and velocity, while subtractive synthesis timbral evolution is controlled by filter cutoff movement. FM excels at naturally evolving attack transients; subtractive synthesis is more intuitive for broad, sweeping filter movements.
Not deeply, but understanding two core concepts dramatically accelerates FM learning: modulation index (more = more sidebands = brighter/more complex) and frequency ratio (integer = harmonic/pitched, non-integer = metallic/inharmonic). Beyond those two concepts, FM synthesis is best learned empirically — adjust one parameter at a time and listen. The underlying Bessel function mathematics that describes sideband amplitudes is not operationally necessary. Most professional FM programmers learned entirely by ear and parameter experimentation, using the math only as a post-hoc explanation for what they were hearing.
An FM operator is a self-contained synthesis unit consisting of a sine wave oscillator and its own amplitude envelope. In commercial FM synthesizers, multiple operators are connected in algorithm configurations where some operators (modulators) modulate the frequency of others (carriers). The term 'operator' comes from the mathematical operation they perform. A six-operator synth like the DX7 has six of these units available, which can be arranged into dozens of different modulator/carrier routing configurations via the algorithm selection.
FM electric pianos synthesize the timbre dynamically — the spectrum is generated anew on every note, and modulator envelope decay produces a brightness contour that follows the physics of a vibrating tine. A sampled electric piano captures a fixed snapshot of the acoustic instrument at specific velocities and pitches; the spectrum is static within each sample. The FM version brightens and darkens continuously in response to envelope stages and velocity-to-modulation-index routing, which is why FM electric pianos feel 'alive' in a subtly different way from even high-quality multisampled equivalents. Each approach has its place; many producers layer both.
Algorithm 32 on the Yamaha DX7 routes all six operators as independent carriers — none modulate each other. This means each operator outputs its own sine wave, and the algorithm becomes a six-sine additive synthesizer rather than an FM synthesizer. It is useful for organ-style sounds (six harmonics mixed to taste), soft sustained pads where inter-modulation artifacts would be unwanted, and as a diagnostic starting point when learning FM — with no modulation active, you can hear each carrier clearly and introduce modulation connections one at a time.
FM synthesis can produce convincing approximations of certain acoustic instruments — electric pianos, brass, marimba, vibraphone, bells, and plucked strings particularly — where the natural spectral evolution closely parallels what FM modulator envelopes produce. It is less successful at instruments with noise-dominated attacks (bowed strings, breathy flutes) unless a feedback or noise operator is added, and physically complex instruments like acoustic piano require more operators and algorithmic sophistication than most dedicated FM synthesizers provide. The DX7's success came precisely from targeting instruments whose natural physics mapped well onto FM's strengths.
The modulation index (I) is defined as the ratio of peak frequency deviation to modulator frequency: I = Δf / fm. It controls how many sidebands are generated and their relative amplitudes. In most hardware FM synthesizers including the DX7, you do not set I directly — instead, modulator output level acts as a proxy for I. Higher modulator output level = larger Δf = higher I = more sidebands. The relationship is not perfectly linear across all implementations, but the principle holds: increase modulator level to add brightness and complexity, decrease it to thin and clean the sound. In software FM instruments like FM8 and Operator, the modulation depth control is often labeled directly as modulation index or depth.
FM patches can sound brilliant in isolation but harsh or cluttered in a dense arrangement for two reasons: excessive high-frequency sidebands at high modulation index, and inharmonic partial content that clashes with other instruments. To address this, first reduce modulator levels by 20–30% from the isolated patch setting — mix context typically requires less brightness than headphones suggest. Second, use a gentle high-shelf EQ cut above 8–10 kHz to remove the uppermost sidebands without dulling the midrange character. Third, use velocity sensitivity to reduce modulation index at lower velocities — quieter notes naturally produce fewer sidebands and sit under louder elements without competing. Finally, check tuning carefully: FM patches with non-integer ratios can produce partials that create audible beats against other pitched instruments in the mix.
