Resonance

/ˈrɛz.ə.nəns/

Quick Answer Definition How It Works Parameters Quick Reference History How To Use It In The Wild Types Mistakes Calculator Related Further Reading FAQ

Resonance is the emphasis of a specific frequency band, most often the boost at a filter's cutoff point controlled by the Q or resonance parameter. High resonance creates a pronounced peak that can reach self-oscillation.

01 Definition

Every producer has heard it — that singing, whistling peak that turns a static filter sweep into something alive. Resonance is not a flaw to manage; it is a voice waiting to be tuned.

In music production, resonance describes the sustained or amplified vibration of a system at a particular frequency — its resonant frequency. The term appears in two closely related but distinct contexts: as a parameter on analogue and digital filters that controls the height of a peak at the cutoff frequency, and as the acoustic phenomenon whereby an enclosure, string, column of air, or circuit preferentially amplifies certain frequencies over others. Both phenomena share the same physics: energy is stored and re-released at a rate dictated by the physical or electronic properties of the system, causing a narrow band of frequencies to accumulate amplitude far beyond its neighbors.

On a synthesizer or resonant EQ, the resonance control (also labeled Q, emphasis, or peak on various hardware) governs how aggressively the filter feeds back energy around its cutoff point. At low resonance settings the filter rolls off smoothly with no audible peak. As resonance increases, a narrow boost forms at the cutoff, narrowing in bandwidth and rising in amplitude. At extreme settings — typically above 90–100% of the control's range on analogue designs — the filter enters self-oscillation, generating a pure sine tone at the cutoff frequency independent of any input signal. This is not an artifact to be feared; it is a precise pitch that can be played chromatically by automating the cutoff, a technique foundational to acid bass, electro, and industrial music.

Acoustically, resonance describes the tendency of physical objects to vibrate most freely at their natural frequencies. A snare drum shell resonates at a frequency determined by its diameter and depth; a vocal tract shapes timbre through a series of resonant cavities called formants; a recording room reinforces certain bass frequencies depending on its dimensions. For the mixing engineer, unwanted acoustic resonances manifest as build-ups — boomy low-end in under-treated rooms, ringing in specific drum shells, honky mid peaks in wooden instrument bodies — that must be identified and addressed with narrow notch EQ or physical treatment. Understanding resonance as physics makes the diagnostic process systematic rather than guesswork.

The word itself derives from the Latin resonare, to resound, and entered scientific use in the seventeenth century through the study of pendulums and vibrating strings. In audio engineering it gained its modern technical meaning through the development of analogue filter circuits in the mid-twentieth century. The Moog ladder filter, the Roland transistor ladder, the Oberheim SEM state-variable filter, and the Korg MS-20's Sallen-Key design each implement resonance differently at the circuit level, producing the tonal personalities that define entire genres. Understanding that those sonic signatures are expressions of the same underlying physics connects synthesis, mixing, acoustic design, and sound design into one coherent discipline.

02 How It Works

At the circuit level, resonance in an analogue low-pass filter is created by a feedback path that routes a portion of the filter's output back to its input, inverted and phase-shifted. The classic four-pole transistor ladder filter, as used in Moog synthesizers, achieves this by tapping a portion of the output from the fourth pole and summing it back before the first. When the amount of feedback is small, it gently shapes the passband. As feedback increases, the loop gain at the cutoff frequency approaches unity, at which point the filter sustains oscillation indefinitely — self-oscillation. In a state-variable filter topology (as in the Oberheim SEM or Dave Smith instruments), the resonance is controlled by a variable resistor in a different feedback topology, allowing simultaneous low-pass, high-pass, and band-pass outputs from the same resonant peak, each phase-shifted by 90 degrees relative to its neighbor.

In digital signal processing, resonance is implemented using recursive IIR (Infinite Impulse Response) filter coefficients. The biquad filter — a standard second-order section — is the most common building block. Its transfer function includes poles and zeros in the z-plane; moving the poles toward the unit circle increases Q and narrows the resonant peak. High-order digital filters chain multiple biquad sections, just as the analogue ladder chains multiple RC stages. The Q factor (Quality factor) is the precise mathematical expression of resonance: Q = f₀ / Δf, where f₀ is the center frequency and Δf is the bandwidth measured at the −3 dB points on either side of the peak. A Q of 0.707 (1/√2) produces a maximally flat Butterworth response with no peak; a Q of 1 produces a mild peak; Qs above 10 produce very narrow, surgical peaks audible as a distinct ringing tone.

In acoustic spaces, resonance follows from the wave equation applied to room boundaries. A rectangular room supports standing waves (modes) at frequencies where the room's dimensions are multiples of a half-wavelength. The three types — axial (between two parallel surfaces), tangential (involving four surfaces), and oblique (all six surfaces) — cluster densely in the low frequency range below the Schroeder frequency (typically 200–300 Hz in professional rooms). These modes cause certain bass notes to sustain unnaturally long after a sound source stops, an effect called room resonance or modal ringing. Bass trapping at room corners reduces modal energy by converting acoustic energy to heat via friction in porous absorbing material. The same principle — stored energy re-released at a preferred frequency — applies equally to a filter circuit and a concrete room.

Resonance interacts with adjacent parameters in critical ways. Raising resonance while simultaneously reducing cutoff frequency intensifies the effect, because more of the filtered signal's energy concentrates at a lower, louder part of the spectrum. In envelope-modulated filter sweeps, the speed of the cutoff movement determines whether the resonant peak sounds as a smooth vocal formant or a sharp, percussive click. Very fast attacks with high resonance against a transient-rich signal (a drum hit through a Minimoog-style filter, for instance) produce the characteristic pluck or thwack of analogue synthesis. Slower sweeps with moderate resonance create the classic wah or vowel formant motion used in funk and soul. Understanding this interaction is what separates mechanical knob-turning from deliberate sonic design.

Self-oscillation deserves special attention as a creative tool. When a filter self-oscillates, it generates a near-perfect sine wave at whatever pitch the cutoff frequency is tuned to. Because the cutoff is voltage-controlled (in analogue hardware) or automatable (in software), the self-oscillating filter becomes a playable sine oscillator with its own pitch tracking, envelope, and modulation possibilities. Producers who understand this use it to generate pure sine sub-bass tones, metallic tones by tuning the oscillation into the mid range, and harmonically rich textures by combining self-oscillation with a second signal. The technique appears throughout electronic music from Robert Moog's earliest demonstrations to modern hyperpop and ambient production.

03 The Parameters

Every resonance — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these and you can work with any implementation.

RESONANCE / Q
Height and sharpness of the peak at the cutoff frequency

Controls the amount of feedback within the filter circuit, creating a boost at the cutoff point. A Q of 0.707 yields a flat Butterworth response; Q values of 1–4 suit musical filter sweeps; Q above 8–10 on analogue designs typically triggers self-oscillation. On hardware the label varies — Moog uses 'Amount of Feedback', Roland uses 'Resonance', Korg uses 'Peak' — but all describe the same parameter.

CUTOFF FREQUENCY
The frequency at which the resonant peak is centered

Sets the pitch of the resonant peak and the corner frequency of the filter roll-off simultaneously. Expressed in Hz (20–20,000 Hz) or as a normalized 0–100 value on older hardware. The interaction between cutoff and resonance defines the filter's fundamental voice: sweeping cutoff with high resonance traces a sine-tone glide through the frequency spectrum, the basis of the acid bass sound.

FILTER SLOPE
Rate of attenuation beyond the cutoff, in dB per octave

Common values are 12 dB/oct (2-pole) and 24 dB/oct (4-pole). A steeper slope concentrates more energy in the resonant peak for a given Q setting, making 4-pole filters sound more dramatic and piercing at equivalent resonance levels. The Moog ladder and Roland TB-303 both use 24 dB/oct slopes, contributing to their characteristic aggressive peak. A 6 dB/oct 1-pole filter has no meaningful resonance.

FILTER TYPE
Low-pass, high-pass, band-pass, or notch — determines where the peak sits relative to passband

In a low-pass filter, the resonant peak sits at the top edge of the passband, just before the roll-off. In a band-pass filter, the peak is centered in a narrow pass region; bandwidth is the inverse of Q. A notch (band-reject) filter inverts the concept, creating a narrow null rather than a peak; 'notch depth' is the resonance equivalent. State-variable designs like the Oberheim SEM provide all modes simultaneously from the same resonant circuit.

ENVELOPE MODULATION AMOUNT
Depth to which an ADSR envelope modulates the cutoff frequency over time

Not technically a resonance parameter, but inseparable from the resonance result in practice. With high resonance and a fast attack envelope, the cutoff sweeps rapidly upward, ringing the peak against the transient to produce a percussive pluck or click. With a slower decay and moderate resonance, the result is a formant-like vowel motion. Most synthesizers provide a dedicated filter envelope with independent ADSR controls and a modulation depth (or 'envelope amount') knob.

KEYBOARD TRACKING
Links cutoff frequency to MIDI note pitch so the resonant peak tracks melodic content

At 100% tracking, each semitone of MIDI input shifts the cutoff by one semitone, keeping the resonant peak at a constant harmonic relationship to the fundamental. Without tracking, the peak becomes proportionally darker on high notes and brighter on low notes as you play across the keyboard. Partial tracking (33–67%) is often the most musical compromise. Self-oscillating filters with 100% key tracking become a fully chromatic pitched oscillator.

05 History & Origin

The scientific study of resonance began in earnest with Galileo Galilei's observations of pendulums around 1602, and Marin Mersenne's foundational work on vibrating strings, published in Harmonie Universelle (1636), established that a string's resonant frequency is determined by its length, tension, and mass per unit length — relationships still encoded in every physical modeling synthesizer. Hermann von Helmholtz's On the Sensations of Tone (1863) systematized acoustic resonance further, describing the Helmholtz resonator (a hollow sphere with a small opening that amplifies a single narrow frequency) and explaining how the vocal tract uses formant resonances to shape phoneme identity. Helmholtz resonators remain a standard tool in studio acoustic design today.

Electronic resonance in audio circuits emerged with the development of early radio technology. The LC resonant circuit — an inductor and capacitor in a loop — selectively amplifies a narrow frequency band with a Q determined by the circuit's resistive losses, and was the basis of tuning in broadcast receivers from the 1910s onward. Audio engineers in the 1940s and 1950s began applying similar principles to tone controls in mixing consoles and public address equipment. The Pultec EQP-1A, introduced in 1951, used passive inductor-capacitor networks that produced musical resonances and inter-frequency interactions that passive resistor networks could not replicate — a quality still prized in modern Pultec emulations. Rupert Neve's console EQ designs of the 1960s and 1970s, implemented with custom-wound inductors and transformer coupling, produced smooth resonant peaks that became synonymous with 'analogue warmth.'

The decisive moment for resonance as a creative performance parameter was Robert Moog's 1964–1965 development of the transistor ladder filter, described in his landmark AES paper of 1965. Moog's insight was to use four cascaded transistor stages with a feedback path from output to input; adjusting the feedback amount varied resonance continuously from zero to self-oscillation. The circuit was voltage-controlled, allowing the resonance amount and cutoff frequency to be modulated by envelope generators, LFOs, and keyboard pitch signals simultaneously. Don Buchla developed his own low-pass gate around the same period using vactrols (optocoupler resistors), yielding a softer resonance character more suited to percussive plucking sounds. Roland's BC-1 Transistor Ladder variant, used in the TB-303 Bass Line (1981), introduced a slightly different resonance character — brighter, more nasal — that Spanky, DJ Pierre, and the originators of Chicago acid house would exploit systematically after 1986. The TB-303's ability to sequence cutoff, resonance, and accent per step made filter resonance a compositional element rather than a performance timbre, fundamentally changing how producers thought about the parameter.

From the mid-1990s onward, digital synthesizers and software emulation transformed resonance from an analogue circuit property into a mathematical calculation. Native Instruments Reaktor (1996) allowed producers to model resonant filter circuits at the patching level; Propellerhead's ReBirth RB-338 (1997) brought TB-303-style resonance to the desktop in software. The ongoing challenge of digital resonance implementation — achieving the non-linear saturation behavior of analogue transistor junctions at the moment of self-oscillation without generating aliasing artifacts — drove significant DSP research through the 2000s and 2010s. Companies like Cytomic (The Drop, 2012), u-he (Diva, 2011), and Arturia (filter emulations in the V Collection series) developed non-linear, oversampled filter models that reproduce not just the frequency response but the harmonic distortion signature of the original circuits. This distinction — between a resonance curve that is mathematically accurate and one that saturates and distorts musically under signal — remains the central debate in analogue-modeled synthesis.

06 How Producers Use It

In synthesis and sound design, resonance is most commonly deployed as a dynamic parameter — swept, modulated, or automated rather than set statically. The foundational technique is the filter envelope sweep: an ADSR envelope with a fast attack and medium decay modulates the cutoff frequency from a high starting point downward (or vice versa), while resonance is set to taste to give the sweep character. With Q around 2–3, the result is an organic, vowel-like motion useful for lead synth sounds, bass plucks, and pad attacks. With Q at 6–8, the peak dominates the sweep, producing the nasal, nagging quality of classic analogue lead lines. With resonance near self-oscillation (90%+), the sweep traces a descending sine-tone glide, useful for pitch percussion, zap effects, and the pitched drops common in UK garage and grime production.

For bass synthesis specifically, the relationship between resonance and the sub-bass region requires careful gain management. A resonant low-pass filter with cutoff in the 80–200 Hz range and high Q creates a prominent bump that can significantly boost low-frequency energy, causing clip on downstream compressors and limiters and translating poorly on smaller speakers. The standard solution is to reduce the filter input gain by 3–6 dB before raising resonance, compensating with make-up gain after the filter stage or in the amplitude envelope. On hardware, this is described as 'pre-filter gain padding'; in software, it is simply reducing the oscillator level before the filter insert. The result is the same resonant character with controlled headroom. Engineers like Chris Lord-Alge and Andrew Scheps frequently describe managing this interaction when mixing bass-forward records.

In mixing and EQ work, resonance manifests as the Q parameter on parametric equalizers. Narrow Q settings (Q 5–10) are used for surgical notching of problem frequencies — the ring of a snare at 200 Hz, the nasal honk of a guitar cabinet at 1.5 kHz, the room mode in a low-mid build-up at 120 Hz. Wider Q settings (Q 0.5–1.5) produce broad, musical shelving-like boosts or cuts used for tonal shaping. On bell-curve (peaking) EQ bands, very high Q with positive gain creates a narrow emphasis that can add 'clang' to metal percussion, 'bite' to synth leads, or 'air' to vocals by accentuating harmonic content at a specific frequency. The inverse — high-Q cut — is one of the most powerful mixing tools available, capable of removing a single problematic partial from a recording without audibly affecting adjacent frequencies.

In acoustic treatment, understanding resonance guides both problem-solving and room design. Identifying a room mode requires either a real-time analyzer (RTA) with a measurement microphone or a simple sweep tone test: play a sine sweep from 20 to 200 Hz while monitoring the recording in a treated listening position and note frequencies that sustain noticeably after the sweep passes. Those frequencies are candidates for bass trap placement. Corner broadband traps (minimum 4-inch thickness, floor to ceiling at front corners) reduce axial and tangential modes most effectively. Helmholtz resonator panels, tuned to a specific frequency using the resonator's air column length and opening diameter, address individual stubborn modes that broadband traps cannot fully control. Studios such as Electric Lady (New York) and Abbey Road's Studio Three were designed around mastery of room resonance to achieve a controlled yet musical low-frequency environment.

AbletonUse Operator's Filter section (LP24 mode) for classic 24 dB/oct resonant sweeps; Simpler and Sampler both include resonant multi-mode filters — set to LP24 and automate Freq with a MIDI Envelope or clip envelope for TB-303-style acid lines. For notching in mixing, use EQ Eight with a bell band set to a very high Q (above 8) and a negative gain.
FL Studio3xOsc, Sytrus, and Harmor all expose resonance prominently in their filter sections; in 3xOsc, the CUT and RES knobs on each oscillator layer allow per-voice resonance textures. In the mixer, use Parametric EQ 2 with a narrow bell band (Q scrolled far right) for surgical notching. For dynamic resonance effects, automate CUT and RES via a Fruity Peak Controller linked to an audio-triggered envelope.
Logic ProES2's multi-mode filter (LP, HP, BP, Notch, Peak modes) provides one of the most versatile resonant filter implementations in any stock DAW synthesizer; use the ENV2 → Cutoff modulation with high resonance for textbook sweeps. In Channel EQ and Linear Phase EQ, drag Q handles to narrow bands for problem-frequency removal. Sculpture's resonant waveguide synthesis uses physical resonance as its primary tone-shaping mechanism — an advanced but powerful tool for organic resonant timbres.
Pro ToolsPro Tools' stock MIDI instruments are limited, but the bundled Xpand!2 includes a resonant filter section suitable for basic sweeps. For surgical EQ work, use the 7-band EQ3 or Pro-Q3 (if licensed) with narrow Q settings; Pro Tools' automation system allows precise per-sample automation of EQ frequency and Q for dynamic resonance control during mixdown. ReWire-linked Reason or third-party AUs/AAXs like u-he Diva are the standard workflow for resonant synthesis.
ReaperReaEQ provides full parametric Q control for all band types — set a band to Band Pass with a very high Q for an extreme resonant peak effect, or to Low Shelf/High Shelf with moderate Q for analogue-style soft-knee EQ curves. For synthesis, use ReaSynth or ReaSynDr with the filter cutoff and resonance parameters exposed in the plugin UI; both support MIDI CC automation directly from a controller. Reaper's native JS plugin scripts also include several resonant filter implementations for custom sound design chains.

07 In the Wild

Abstract knowledge becomes practical when you can hear it in music you know. These tracks demonstrate resonance used intentionally, at specific moments, for specific purposes.

Phuture — "Acid Tracks" (1987)
0:00–full track · Produced by DJ Pierre, Spanky, Herb J

The foundational document of resonant filter as melody. The Roland TB-303's resonance is pushed to near self-oscillation throughout, creating the nasal, speaker-tearing quality that defined acid house. Listen specifically to how the resonance interacts with the programmed accents — accented steps drive the filter slightly harder, increasing resonance amplitude momentarily. The cutoff automation is sequenced per step alongside pitch, making resonance a rhythmic as much as timbral element. No track in production history demonstrates filter resonance as compositional device more directly.

Massive Attack — "Teardrop" (1998)
0:00–0:30 intro · Produced by Massive Attack, Neil Davidge

The opening harpsichord-like instrument (a Mellotron processed through a resonant low-pass filter sweep) demonstrates mid-range resonance used for emotional rather than functional effect. As the filter cutoff opens over the first eight bars, the resonant peak traces upward through the 400–1200 Hz range at a Q around 3–4, creating the sense of the sound 'waking up' from darkness. Elizabeth Fraser's vocal enters as the filter reaches its open position, timing the resonance sweep to the emotional architecture of the track — a masterclass in using automation to serve arrangement.

Daft Punk — "Da Funk" (1995)
0:08 onwards · Produced by Daft Punk (Thomas Bangalter)

The bass-lead synthesizer line uses a resonant 24 dB/oct low-pass filter with a decay-only envelope modulating cutoff — no attack, pure decay — producing the iconic 'wah' pluck on every note. Resonance is set around 65–70% of maximum (short of self-oscillation but aggressive enough to add a distinct peak at approximately 400 Hz per note). The key production detail is that the resonance is kept constant while the envelope depth controls the sweep range per step: tighter on shorter notes, wider on held tones. This dynamic creates the groove illusion that the bass is expressively played rather than sequenced.

Nine Inch Nails — "Closer" (1994)
0:00–1:00 · Produced by Trent Reznor, Flood

The distorted, modulated bass synthesizer uses very high filter resonance approaching self-oscillation, combined with hard clipping distortion after the filter stage. This ordering — filter resonance before saturation — causes the resonant peak to be disproportionately amplified by the clipper, producing a harsh metallic harmonic structure at the peak frequency. The result is a tone that occupies both sub-bass and aggressive upper-mid space simultaneously, a technique widely replicated in industrial and aggressive electronic production. Compare the result with a chain that places distortion before the filter to hear how ordering changes the resonance behavior entirely.

Flying Lotus — "Zodiac Shit" (2010)
0:00–1:10 · Produced by Flying Lotus

The opening melodic synth line illustrates slow resonance automation synchronized to the phrase structure rather than note-level. The filter cutoff and resonance are both automated on a 4-bar cycle, gradually opening and narrowing the resonant peak in counterpoint to the melodic content. At its narrowest, the resonance peak is around Q 6, isolating a narrow band at 1.8 kHz and giving the sound a distant, telephone-filtered quality. At its widest (Q ~1.5), the filter opens fully, revealing the full harmonic content. This phrase-level resonance automation — distinct from note-triggered envelope sweeps — exemplifies the texturally sophisticated filter work characteristic of Flying Lotus's production.

08 Types & Variants

Transistor Ladder Resonance
Moog Minimoog Model D · Roland TB-303 · Sequential Prophet-5

Four cascaded transistor stages with a global negative feedback path from output to first stage. As resonance increases, the harmonic content of the peak develops odd-order distortion products from the transistor junctions, giving the peak warmth and body not present in purely linear digital implementations. Self-oscillation is a pure sine with slight harmonic loading from the transistors. Best suited to bass lines, leads, and sweeps where a warm but aggressive resonant peak is desired.

State-Variable (SVF) Resonance
Oberheim SEM · Dave Smith Prophet-08 · Korg MS-20 (dual SVF)

Two integrators and a summing amplifier in a feedback loop providing simultaneous LP, HP, and BP outputs. The resonance character is smoother and more 'open' than ladder designs, with the BP output providing a pure resonant band-pass function identical to a peaked filter. Self-oscillation on an SVF tends toward a slightly cleaner sine tone. Highly valued for polyphonic pads, string-like textures, and situations where the resonance peak must remain musically transparent rather than dominating.

Sallen-Key / OTA Resonance
Korg MS-20 (second filter section) · Roland Juno-106 · ARP Odyssey

Operational transconductance amplifier (OTA) designs use current-controlled transconductance to vary cutoff and resonance, producing a brighter, thinner resonant character with faster self-oscillation onset than transistor ladders. The Roland Juno-106's Sallen-Key-based CEM3350 filter produces the characteristically wide, shallow resonance peak associated with the Juno's 'chorus pad' sound. At high resonance without self-oscillation, the sound is glassy and cutting — useful for high-frequency synth leads and arpeggiated textures.

Digital Linear Resonance (Biquad IIR)
Access Virus TI · Waldorf Blofeld · Most software synthesizers

Mathematically precise implementation using biquad filter coefficients. Resonance peaks are accurate to the specified Q with no non-linear saturation unless explicitly added. Self-oscillation, if implemented, generates a theoretically perfect sine wave free of harmonic loading. The weakness is the absence of the saturation-at-the-peak that gives analogue filters their character under heavy modulation. Modern implementations by developers such as Cytomic, u-he, and Arturia add non-linear stages explicitly to simulate analogue behavior.

Formant / Vocal Resonance
Electro-Harmonix Talking Machine · Moog MF-102 Ring Mod with filter · Software: iZotope Nectar Harmony

Multiple band-pass filters with individual resonance peaks tuned to approximate the first two formant frequencies of vocal phonemes (F1: 300–800 Hz; F2: 800–2500 Hz). Moving the relative positions of the two peaks morphs the perceived vowel sound from 'oo' through 'ah' to 'ee'. Used in talk-box effects, formant-shifting vocoders, and vocal character processors. The creative application in synthesis involves morphing between formant positions in synchrony with a melody, effectively singing through a synthesizer without a microphone.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    Setting high resonance without compensating for filter gain boost
    Raising resonance adds significant gain at the cutoff frequency — potentially +12 to +18 dB on 4-pole designs near self-oscillation. This causes the output of the filter stage to clip or overload downstream compressors unexpectedly. The fix is to reduce the oscillator or pre-filter gain by 6–12 dB before adding resonance, then restore perceived loudness with post-filter volume or amplitude envelope level. Many hardware synthesizers lose several dB of overall output at high resonance by design; account for this in your gain-staging chain.
  • ✕
    Using a narrow-Q boost to 'fix' a dull mix element
    Inserting a very high-Q boost (Q 8–12) to make a dull bass or pad sound more present creates a resonant coloration that identifies a specific frequency as artificially emphasized — the mix element sounds 'ringy' or 'honky' rather than full. This is the EQ version of over-resonance. The correct approach is to use a wider Q (0.7–2) for additive boosts and reserve high Q for subtractive notching. If a source lacks presence, address it at the source or with harmonic saturation rather than a sharp EQ peak.
  • ✕
    Ignoring self-oscillation pitch when automating cutoff
    When a filter reaches self-oscillation while the cutoff is moving (automated or modulated), the oscillation pitch follows the cutoff, producing an audible pitch glide independent of the musical key. If unintentional, this clashes with tonal centers and sounds out of tune. The solution is either to keep resonance below self-oscillation threshold during cutoff sweeps, or to deliberately tune the self-oscillation pitch to a note consonant with the key — typically the root, fifth, or octave — before beginning the sweep.
  • ✕
    Removing room resonances with too-broad a notch
    Attempting to fix a 100 Hz room mode with a broad EQ dip (Q 0.5–1) affects 2–3 octaves of low-end content, thinning the entire bass register rather than addressing the specific offending mode. Room modes are narrow — they build up at specific frequencies within ±5–10 Hz — and require Q values of 4–10 to notch precisely. Use a measurement microphone and RTA to identify the exact mode frequency, then apply a narrow notch at that frequency before broadening to taste.
  • ✕
    Assuming all resonant filter types are interchangeable in the same role
    A TB-303-style transistor ladder at 80% resonance and a Juno-106-style OTA filter at 80% resonance produce completely different sounds at the same settings because of the underlying circuit topology and saturation behavior. Switching plugin filter types mid-project without rebalancing resonance and cutoff often breaks a carefully tuned sound. Treat each filter model as a distinct instrument and calibrate resonance settings from scratch when changing filter type.
  • ✕
    Over-resonating high-pass filters on bus or master
    Applying a resonant high-pass filter to a mix bus or master to 'clean up' low frequencies with Q above 1.5 creates a low-frequency boost just above the cutoff — exactly in the region where sub-bass and kick energy live. This paradox (cutting below 40 Hz causes a boost at 55 Hz) is invisible on a spectrum analyzer unless the curve display is active. High-pass filters on buses should use Q values of 0.5–0.7 (Butterworth or Linkwitz-Riley alignment) to achieve clean roll-off with no resonant peak.

12 Frequently Asked Questions

Resonance on a synthesizer filter is a parameter that controls the height of a narrow frequency peak at the cutoff point. It works by feeding a portion of the filter's output back into its input; more feedback means a more pronounced peak. At low settings it adds subtle brightness; at high settings it produces the nasal, singing quality associated with acid bass and sweep-style sounds.
Q (Quality factor) is the mathematical ratio of a filter's center frequency to its bandwidth: Q = f₀ / Δf. A high Q means a narrow bandwidth, producing a sharp, resonant peak or notch. On an EQ, narrow-Q boosts create ringing emphasis at a specific frequency (resonance in practice), while narrow-Q cuts are used surgically to remove problem frequencies. Q and resonance describe the same underlying phenomenon from different contexts — synthesis and equalization.
Self-oscillation occurs when a resonant filter's feedback loop reaches unity gain at the cutoff frequency, causing the filter to sustain oscillation indefinitely with no input signal. The result is a pure sine wave pitched at the cutoff frequency. Creatively, you can play self-oscillating filters chromatically by automating the cutoff to track MIDI note data, generating pitched sine-bass tones, metallic pings, or laser-sweep effects. Tune the oscillation to your track's key before recording to avoid clashing with harmonic content.
Use a narrow-Q parametric EQ band (Q 8–12) with a large positive boost (+12 dB or more) and slowly sweep it through the problem frequency range while listening for the resonance to become dramatically louder or ringy. Once you identify the frequency, flip the gain to a narrow cut (-6 to -12 dB) and reduce Q to just wide enough to address the issue without affecting adjacent content. Measurement microphones and real-time spectrum analyzers (RTAs) accelerate this process significantly.
High resonance on a low-pass filter concentrates energy in a narrow peak at the cutoff frequency while simultaneously attenuating frequencies above the cutoff. If the cutoff is set in the mid-bass range (150–400 Hz), the fundamental and sub-bass frequencies below the cutoff receive the resonant boost, but the overall perceived fullness depends on the filter's pre-resonance gain. Reduce input gain before the filter by 6 dB, then re-tune the cutoff upward slightly to restore body. Alternatively, layer the resonant filtered tone with an unfiltered sine wave for sub foundation.
On a low-pass filter, resonance creates a peak at the top of the passband (at the cutoff), with the signal rolling off above it. On a band-pass filter, Q directly controls bandwidth — a high Q produces a narrow band of frequencies that pass through while rejecting everything else, which is inherently a resonant behavior. The band-pass output of a state-variable filter at high Q is essentially a resonant peak without any associated low or high-pass content, making it ideal for wah effects and formant simulation.
Analogue filter resonance includes non-linear saturation from transistor junctions or OTA circuits, meaning the resonant peak clips softly under heavy signal drive, adding harmonic distortion to the peak frequency. Digital biquad filters are mathematically linear unless saturation is explicitly added — the peak is precise but can sound sterile under high signal levels. For modern production, the difference matters most in bass synthesis and filter sweep work where the analogue saturation characteristic gives the peak warmth and 'weight.' High-quality analogue-modeled plugins (u-he Diva, Cytomic The Drop) add non-linear stages specifically to replicate this behavior and are worth the investment for resonance-critical work.

In mixing, narrow-Q boosts (Q 4–8 with +2 to +4 dB gain) at a drum's fundamental shell resonance frequency can reinforce the natural acoustic resonance of the instrument, making it sound more physically present. Resonant high-pass filters with Q slightly above 0.707 add a gentle low-frequency warmth to the cutoff region, useful for adding thickness to samples that were recorded in overly-treated (anechoic) environments.
