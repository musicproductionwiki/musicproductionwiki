Envelope

/ˈɛn.və.loʊp/

Quick Answer Definition How It Works Parameters Quick Reference History How To Use It In The Wild Types Mistakes Related Further Reading FAQ

Envelope is a time-varying control signal that shapes how a synthesizer parameter — most commonly amplitude or filter cutoff — changes from the moment a note is triggered through its release. The classic four-stage ADSR model defines attack, decay, sustain, and release.

01 Definition

Every sound you have ever loved — the slow swell of a pad, the snap of a snare, the blooming pluck of a Juno chord — was carved from silence by an envelope. Master it and you stop designing patches; you start designing feelings.

In synthesis and audio engineering, an envelope is a time-varying control signal that describes how a parameter evolves from the onset of a note or event to its conclusion. The term borrows from physics and acoustics, where the envelope of a waveform refers to the smooth curve traced along the peaks of an oscillating signal over time. Inside a synthesizer, an envelope generator (EG) produces this curve as a control voltage (CV) or, in the digital domain, as a modulation signal that is then applied to a target — most commonly amplitude, filter cutoff frequency, or pitch. Without an envelope, every synthesized note would begin instantly at full level and sustain indefinitely until key release, producing a tonally static, unnatural result.

The dominant model is the ADSR envelope, first systematized by engineer Bob Moog and subsequently adopted across virtually every analog and digital synthesizer from the early 1970s onward. ADSR stands for Attack, Decay, Sustain, and Release — four stages that collectively describe the complete lifecycle of a note from key-down to key-up and beyond. Attack governs the time taken to rise from zero to peak level; Decay sets how long the signal falls from peak to the Sustain level; Sustain holds at a fixed level for the duration of the held note; and Release determines the fade time after the key is lifted. Each stage is independently adjustable, giving composers and sound designers an enormous range of expressive possibilities from the sharpest percussive transient to the longest orchestral swell.

Envelopes are not confined to amplitude control. A filter envelope routes the same ADSR shape into a synthesizer's low-pass or band-pass filter, producing the characteristic vowel-like opening sweep found on classic Minimoog bass lines and Oberheim pad sounds. A pitch envelope momentarily bends the oscillator frequency at note onset, generating the 'dip' typical of marimba and plucked string transients. Modern synthesizers may offer dedicated envelopes for every modulatable parameter, with additional stages such as Hold, Delay, and multiple curve-shape controls extending the ADSR into DAHD, AHDSR, or multi-segment formats. The modular synthesis world expands this further still, treating the envelope as a free-standing voltage source applicable to any signal in the patch.

From a producer's practical standpoint, the envelope is the primary tool for controlling a sound's perceived attack transient — that initial energy burst critical to the intelligibility of percussion and plucked instruments in a dense mix. A snare with a 2 ms attack envelope retains its crack on small speakers; stretched to 50 ms, the same patch becomes a ghost-like whoosh. Similarly, a synth pad with a 2-second attack envelope blends harmonically into a track without masking other elements, while a 5 ms attack competes aggressively with every transient in the arrangement. Understanding envelope behavior is therefore inseparable from understanding mix dynamics, arrangement density, and the emotional pacing of a production.

02 How It Works

An envelope generator operates by outputting a unipolar or bipolar control signal whose amplitude changes according to a programmed time sequence triggered by a gate signal. In analog hardware, this gate is a sustained voltage — typically +5 V — produced by a MIDI-to-CV converter or a keyboard's key contact for as long as the note is held. When the gate goes high, the EG enters its Attack phase, charging an internal capacitor toward a peak voltage through a resistor network whose value is set by the Attack knob. The RC time constant determines the attack time: lower resistance equals faster attack. When the capacitor reaches the peak — or when the gate drops before that, in the case of very short note lengths — the EG begins the Decay phase, discharging toward the Sustain reference voltage. Sustain is simply a DC level held for the gate's duration, not a time parameter. When the gate goes low (key release), the EG ramps down to zero through the Release RC network regardless of which stage it was in.

In digital synthesizers and plugin instruments, the same behavior is modeled numerically. Each sample period, the envelope generator increments or decrements an internal accumulator value at a rate determined by the current stage's time constant, which is typically expressed in milliseconds and stored as a coefficient. Many digital implementations use exponential curves by default rather than linear ramps, because exponential decay more closely matches the acoustic behavior of natural resonators — a struck string loses energy faster at first and more slowly as it approaches silence. Switchable curve shapes (linear, exponential, logarithmic, S-curve) are now standard in serious plugin synthesizers and allow designers to tailor the perceptual 'feel' of each stage independently. A linear attack into an exponential decay, for instance, produces the characteristic sharp onset and natural tail of a piano hammer strike.

The envelope's output is typically multiplied (for amplitude envelopes) or added (for pitch or filter envelopes) to the audio or control signal. In a standard subtractive synthesizer voice architecture, the audio chain flows: Oscillator → Filter → VCA (Voltage Controlled Amplifier). The amplitude envelope feeds directly into the VCA's control input, scaling oscillator output from silence to full level and back. The filter envelope feeds the VCF's cutoff control input, sweeping frequency upward or downward from a base cutoff value by a depth set by the envelope amount knob — sometimes called 'Env Mod' or 'Envelope Depth.' Negative envelope amounts reverse the sweep direction, useful for sounds that open bright and then darken, such as certain bass plucks and reverse-envelope effects.

Modern synthesizers frequently add a Velocity-to-Envelope routing, where MIDI note velocity scales the envelope's peak output level or modulates individual stage times. Harder key presses produce brighter, more dynamic sounds by routing velocity to filter envelope depth or VCA envelope peak, approximating the velocity sensitivity of acoustic instruments. This single routing is responsible for the 'playability' feel of most high-quality virtual instruments. Beyond velocity, envelope generators in modular and semi-modular systems can be retriggered mid-cycle by a second gate pulse, looped to function as a low-frequency oscillator (LFO), or time-synced to host BPM, expanding the envelope's role far beyond its conventional note-shaping function.

The practical takeaway is that envelope behavior is always a product of three interacting variables: stage time, curve shape, and destination modulation depth. A slow attack on a VCA is perceptually different from a slow attack on a VCF even when both are set to identical millisecond values, because the ear integrates amplitude changes and frequency changes through different psychoacoustic pathways. Producers who internalize this distinction can shape timbral evolution and dynamic contour simultaneously using nothing more than the four classic ADSR knobs — a deceptively simple interface concealing enormous expressive range.

03 The Parameters

Every envelope — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these and you can work with any implementation.

ATTACK
Time from note-on to peak amplitude

Attack sets how long the envelope takes to rise from zero to its peak value after a gate/trigger is received. Values range from sub-millisecond (0.1–2 ms for percussive transients) to several seconds (2–8 s for orchestral swells). Slow attacks reduce a sound's perceived loudness and transient energy in a mix, making them useful for blending pads without masking drums or vocals.

DECAY
Time from peak to sustain level

Decay governs the fall time from the peak amplitude reached at the end of the attack stage down to the sustain level. On plucked and struck instruments with no held sustain (sustain set to 0), the decay stage defines the entire audible body of the note — a 200 ms decay produces a tight pluck; 1.5 s produces a resonant, guitar-like sustain. For bass patches, decay times between 100–400 ms typically yield the most defined low-end in a dense arrangement.

SUSTAIN
Held amplitude level while key is depressed

Unlike the other three parameters, Sustain is an amplitude level (0–100%), not a time value. The envelope remains at the sustain level for as long as the gate is high — i.e., the key is held. At 0%, the sound behaves like a plucked or struck instrument that fully decays; at 100%, there is no decay at all and the sound holds at peak level indefinitely. Most lead and pad sounds live in the 50–85% sustain range.

RELEASE
Fade time after key is lifted

Release determines how long the envelope takes to fall from the current sustain level to zero after the gate goes low (key-up). Short releases (5–30 ms) produce an abrupt cutoff useful for staccato playing styles and tight bass lines; long releases (500 ms–4 s) allow notes to bloom out naturally after release, critical for piano emulations and ambient pads. Releases set too long on low-pitched bass patches can cause audible pitch bleed and muddy the harmonic foundation of a mix.

ENVELOPE DEPTH / AMOUNT
Degree of modulation applied to the target parameter

Found on filter and pitch envelopes, this knob scales how strongly the envelope signal moves the target parameter. A filter envelope depth of zero means the envelope has no effect on cutoff; maximum depth may sweep the filter from fully closed to fully open. Negative depth values invert the envelope direction, producing darkening attacks and brightening releases — a technique used for downward pluck sounds and reverse-envelope pads.

CURVE SHAPE
Contour of each stage (linear, exponential, logarithmic)

Many modern synthesizers and DAW envelope editors allow selection of the mathematical curve applied to each stage. Exponential decay (the natural acoustic curve) sounds more realistic for amplitude envelopes because human hearing perceives loudness logarithmically. Linear attack into exponential decay approximates piano and marimba behavior. S-curve (sigmoid) shapes on attack produce a natural, breath-like onset preferred for string and choir patches.

05 History & Origin

The concept of the amplitude envelope in electronic music predates the synthesizer itself. Early electronic organs of the 1930s and 1940s, including the Hammond B-3 (1935), produced sounds with effectively instantaneous attack and infinite sustain — a tonally unnatural but musically functional approximation that composers worked around through performance technique. The real breakthrough came with the RCA Mark II Sound Synthesizer (1957–1958), developed at Columbia-Princeton Electronic Music Center by Harry Olson and Herbert Belar, which allowed programmers to specify amplitude and filter characteristics over time via paper roll control, producing some of the earliest examples of programmatic envelope shaping in electronic composition.

The ADSR envelope as a unified, real-time-controllable module was crystallized by Bob Moog in the late 1960s. Moog's 911 Envelope Generator module, introduced for the Moog modular system around 1967, provided independent Attack, Decay, Sustain, and Release controls in a format directly informed by conversations with composers Vladimir Ussachevsky and composer-performer Keith Emerson. The 911 became the architectural template for virtually every synthesizer EG to follow. When Moog miniaturized the circuit for the Minimoog Model D (1970), the two hardwired ADSR envelopes — one for the VCF, one for the VCA — placed precise envelope control in the hands of performers for the first time at a commercially accessible price point. Keith Emerson's and Rick Wakeman's prominent use of the Minimoog in early 1970s progressive rock brought the ADSR envelope's expressive capabilities to mass audiences.

The 1970s saw rapid proliferation and refinement of envelope design. ARP Instruments' ARP 2600 (1971) offered a slew-rate limiter approach alongside traditional ADSR, while the Oberheim SEM (1974) introduced the first ADSR with variable curve shaping. Sequential Circuits' Prophet-5 (1978), the first fully programmable polyphonic synthesizer, gave each of its five voices two independent ADSR envelopes stored in patch memory — a feature so influential that it established the dual-envelope (filter + amp) standard that persists today. Engineers Dave Rossum and Scott Wedge at E-mu Systems subsequently developed contour generators for samplers, adapting ADSR behavior to one-shot sample playback on the Emulator II (1984), which found widespread use on records by Peter Gabriel, Herbie Hancock, and Tears for Fears throughout the mid-1980s.

The MIDI era accelerated envelope complexity. Yamaha's DX7 (1983), the best-selling synthesizer in history, used operator-level envelope generators with six independently timed stages per operator — enabling the characteristic 'glassy piano' attack of the DX7 electric piano preset that appears on countless pop recordings from 1983 through 1990. Software synthesizers of the late 1990s and 2000s, beginning with Native Instruments' Generator (later Reaktor, 1996) and Propellerhead's Reason (2000), translated all analog EG behavior into digital precision, adding millisecond-accurate display, curve selection menus, and MIDI CC real-time control of every parameter. Contemporary software instruments such as Serum (2014) and Vital (2021) offer multi-point envelope editors with freely drawable curves, loop modes, and sample-accurate timing, representing the full maturation of a concept whose essential architecture has remained stable for more than fifty years.

06 How Producers Use It

Drums and percussion: On synthesized drums, the envelope is the primary sculpting tool. A kick drum's punch is entirely defined by its amplitude and pitch envelopes — attack times of 0.5–2 ms preserve the initial transient click, while decay times of 300–600 ms control the body. Setting the pitch envelope with a fast attack and 30–80 ms decay produces the characteristic downward sweep from a high fundamental frequency to the sustained low tone, a technique at the heart of every 808 bass drum sound from Roland's TR-808 (1980) to modern trap productions. Snare rimshots benefit from two-stage filter envelope treatment: a short high-pass sweep on attack followed by a slow low-pass release softens the crack without losing articulation.

Bass and keys: Synth bass lines depend on tight envelope timing to carve space in the low-frequency spectrum. Classic Minimoog bass technique keeps attack under 5 ms and decay between 150–300 ms with sustain around 50%, allowing each note to speak clearly without sustaining into the next — a crucial distinction in fast 16th-note bass lines at 130+ BPM where overlapping sustain creates mud. The filter envelope is equally important: a moderate positive depth (40–70%) with a 200 ms decay produces the 'talking' formant-like tone of classic acid bass (TB-303, 1981), while negative filter envelope depth on a piano patch produces the characteristic brightness-before-warmth behavior of struck strings.

Pads and strings: Slow-attack envelopes (500 ms–4 s) are the defining characteristic of pad sounds in virtually every electronic genre. By delaying amplitude onset, a slow-attack pad fills harmonic space without competing with lead elements and naturally ducks around transients without any compression needed. In practice, most professional pad sounds layer two envelope stages: a main amplitude envelope with a 1–3 s attack and a separate filter envelope with a slightly shorter attack (600 ms–2 s), so the timbre brightens marginally after the amplitude has already started opening — creating the subtle internal movement that distinguishes a living pad from a static one.

Leads and plucks: Pluck sounds with sustain set to zero and a 200–800 ms decay rely entirely on decay shape to feel realistic. Exponential decay curves produce the most natural plucked-string behavior, while linear decays sound mechanical and artificial. For lead synths in electronic music, moderate attack times (5–25 ms) combined with maximum sustain and a release of 150–300 ms allow expressive legato playing without the blunt, transistor-gate feeling of zero-attack patches. Velocity-to-filter-envelope routing on a lead makes every note nuance audible — a technique central to the expressiveness of Howard Jones's and Jan Hammer's lead playing in 1980s pop-electronic music.

AbletonAbleton's Analog and Wavetable instruments both offer dual ADSR envelopes (Amp and Mod) with curve shape control. In Wavetable, right-click any envelope segment to switch between linear and exponential — use exponential on decay for natural-feeling pads. The Envelope MIDI Effect (in Max for Live) can modulate any parameter on any device in real time from incoming MIDI velocity or CC.
FL StudioFL's 3xOSC and Harmless expose full ADSR per voice with the ENV/LFO target selector. The Envelope section in the instrument panel includes Tension controls that bend each stage's curve — positive tension on decay approximates exponential behavior. For precise millisecond values, right-click any knob and type the target value directly rather than dragging.
Logic ProES2 provides three assignable envelopes (Env 1–3), each with ADSR plus a Decay Vel (velocity-to-decay) parameter — a critical control for piano and keys realism. Alchemy goes further with MSEG (multi-segment envelope generator) editors per source. For quick transient shaping on audio clips, Logic's built-in Transient Shaper plugin adjusts the effective attack and sustain of recorded audio without re-synthesizing.
Pro ToolsPro Tools' built-in Xpand!2 offers basic ADSR per patch but limited curve control. The more powerful workflow uses Avid's Structure sampler or third-party instruments. For audio envelope shaping, Smack! (legacy) and the Avid Dynamics III compressor's Attack/Release controls are the primary tools — set Attack to 30–60 ms to preserve transients on drum buses without clip gain automation.
ReaperReaSynth, Reaper's stock synthesizer, offers simple ADSR but points toward third-party instruments for deep envelope work. Reaper's real strength lies in its track envelope automation system — any plugin parameter can be drawn as a time envelope in the arrange view with precise point placement, enabling sample-accurate parameter automation that functions like a sequenced envelope generator for any effect.

07 In the Wild

Abstract knowledge becomes practical when you can hear it in music you know. These tracks demonstrate envelope used intentionally, at specific moments, for specific purposes.

Donna Summer — "I Feel Love" (1977)
0:00–0:30 · Produced by Giorgio Moroder

The pulsing Moog bass pattern that opens this track is a masterclass in decay-dominant envelope design. Giorgio Moroder set the Minimoog's filter envelope decay to approximately 150–200 ms with sustain near zero, creating a rhythmic 'breath' on each repeated note. The amplitude envelope's attack is sub-5 ms, preserving the articulation of every 16th note at 126 BPM. Listen on headphones and notice how no two notes blur together despite being played at full rate — a result of precise envelope timing rather than gate length control.

Depeche Mode — "Just Can't Get Enough" (1981)
0:04–0:20 · Produced by Daniel Miller

The lead synth melody on Vince Clarke's Roland SH-101 demonstrates positive filter envelope applied aggressively. Each note attack shows a rapid brightness increase (short attack, high depth on the filter EG) followed by a 300 ms decay back to a warmer sustained tone. This gives every note a vowel-like articulation — an 'a' on attack opening to 'o' on sustain — that is entirely envelope-driven and requires no additional harmonic processing.

Boards of Canada — "Roygbiv" (1998)
0:00–0:45 · Produced by Boards of Canada

The main melodic sequence features a deliberately degraded envelope with a soft 20–40 ms attack and a sustain-dominated amplitude shape, producing the woozy, slightly late-entry feel that characterizes Boards of Canada's aesthetic. The filter envelope here is mild but inverted — notes begin slightly darker and brighten slowly, inverting the typical synth brightness contour. This subtle inversion creates the sense of 'breathing from the inside' that makes their pads feel organically alive.

Kendrick Lamar — "HUMBLE." (2017)
0:00–0:15 · Produced by Mike Will Made-It

The 808 kick that drives this track is defined almost entirely by its pitch envelope: a very fast attack to a high-pitched transient followed by a steep exponential decay over 400–600 ms sweeping downward to the sustained sub-bass tone. The amplitude envelope extends well past 1 second, allowing the sub-frequency energy to build in the room before the next hit. Producers can recreate this with any TR-808 plugin by increasing pitch envelope depth to +8 semitones and setting decay to approximately 450 ms.

Brian Eno — "An Ending (Ascent)" (1983)
0:00–1:00 · Produced by Brian Eno

This ambient composition from the Apollo soundtrack is almost entirely constructed from DX7 and Oberheim OB-Xa pads with attack times exceeding 3 seconds. Each note enters so gradually that individual pitch changes are perceived as harmonic color shifts rather than melodic events. The release envelopes are equally extended (4–6 s), meaning multiple notes sustain simultaneously even during rests, producing the perpetual shimmer that defined the ambient genre's envelope vocabulary.

08 Types & Variants

ADSR (Attack–Decay–Sustain–Release)
Moog 911 Module · Roland Jupiter-8 · Oberheim OB-Xa · Sequential Prophet-5

The universal standard, introduced by Bob Moog in 1967 and replicated in virtually every synthesizer since. Four parameters cover the complete lifecycle of a held note from key-down to key-up. Ideal for all sustained instrument types and the default starting point for any sound design. Its limitation — no differentiation between the initial peak and the decay phase's start — led to the development of extended formats.

AHDSR (Attack–Hold–Decay–Sustain–Release)
Yamaha Motif series · Korg M1 · Native Instruments Kontakt

Inserts a Hold stage between Attack and Decay, maintaining the peak level for a fixed duration before beginning the decay ramp. This is essential for modeling piano behavior, where the hammer blow creates a brief sustained peak before the string's natural decay takes over. Also widely used in sampler instruments to prevent decay from beginning before a looped sample has fully established its timbre.

DAHD (Delay–Attack–Hold–Decay)
Eurorack modules (Make Noise Maths) · Buchla 281 Function Generator

A sustain-free format with an initial pre-attack delay, designed for modular synthesis contexts where the envelope is triggered by a clock pulse rather than a keyboard gate. The Delay parameter creates rhythmically offset modulation useful for polyrhythmic filter sweeps, staggered voice entries in generative music, and auto-arpeggiation effects without an arpeggiator. The absence of a sustain stage makes it inherently percussive.

Multi-Segment / MSEG Envelope
Xfer Serum · Apple Logic Alchemy · Waldorf Blofeld · E-mu Proteus series

Allows the user to define an arbitrary number of breakpoints along a time axis, creating envelope shapes of any complexity — upward-curving attacks, mid-sustain swells, multiple decay phases, and looping sections. MSEGs effectively unify the envelope and LFO into a single time-domain modulation source. They are the envelope format of choice for evolving pads, cinematic scoring sounds, and any patch requiring internal timbral movement over time.

Looping Envelope
Buchla 281 · Make Noise Maths · Roland Juno-106 (chorus LFO derivation)

An envelope configured to repeat automatically after completing its cycle, functioning as a complex LFO with non-sinusoidal shape. When attack and release times are set very short and the envelope loops rapidly, the result is a buzzing, sawtooth-like modulation; at slower loop rates, it creates rhythmic filter or amplitude tremolo effects. Looping envelopes are foundational to Buchla-style West Coast synthesis and are increasingly found in Eurorack and software modular environments.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    Zero-millisecond attack on VCA causing digital clicks
    Setting attack to its absolute minimum (0 ms) in a digital synthesizer creates a hard discontinuity in the waveform — the amplitude jumps from zero to full level in a single sample, generating a broadband click at the note onset. This is a well-known artifact of digital synthesis absent from analog hardware, where RC time constants prevent truly instantaneous transitions. Fix it by setting attack to 0.5–1 ms minimum, which is still perceptually instantaneous but eliminates the click artifact.
  • ✕
    Confusing Sustain (level) with Decay (time)
    Beginner synthesists frequently attempt to make a note 'last longer' by increasing the Sustain parameter, not realizing that Sustain is a level, not a duration. The note's perceived duration is governed by the key hold time, the Decay setting (for plucked sounds with zero sustain), and the Release time. When a student increases Sustain and the sound doesn't lengthen as expected, the confusion deepens. Internalizing the level vs. time distinction for each stage is the single most clarifying conceptual step in learning ADSR.
  • ✕
    Release too long on low-frequency bass patches
    A release time of 500 ms or more on a bass patch set to a 60–80 Hz fundamental means the sub-frequency continues resonating well into the next bar, adding uncontrolled low-end energy that compressors and limiters will pump against. The result is a low-end that feels inconsistent and overpowering on systems with subwoofers. Keep bass release at 80–200 ms for most uptempo music, and monitor on a subwoofer or check low-end behavior with a spectrum analyzer before finalizing.
  • ✕
    Identical envelope settings on every layer of a stacked patch
    When stacking multiple oscillators or samples into a layered patch, applying the same ADSR to every layer produces a monolithic, texturally flat sound. Professional sound designers deliberately offset envelope times across layers — a 10 ms attack on layer 1, 50 ms on layer 2, 200 ms on layer 3 — so that the patch reveals different timbral characters sequentially through the note's lifetime. This internal motion is the defining quality of expensive preset packs and professional instrument libraries.
  • ✕
    Applying a slow-attack filter envelope without compensating filter cutoff base
    Setting a large positive filter envelope depth with a slow attack causes the filter to stay closed during the entire attack stage, producing a muffled onset regardless of how bright the oscillator's base tone is. If the filter's base cutoff frequency is set too low to pass useful harmonic content before the envelope opens it, the sound starts nearly silent in the high-mid range. Compensate by raising the base cutoff to ensure some harmonic content passes at the zero-envelope state, then let the envelope sweep further upward.
  • ✕
    Ignoring velocity routing on amplitude and filter envelopes
    Many producers program entire patches with MIDI velocity set to a fixed value or with velocity routed only to volume, then wonder why the patch feels robotic and unresponsive. Routing velocity to both amplitude envelope peak and filter envelope depth — even at modest depths (20–40%) — dramatically increases expressiveness and makes recorded MIDI performances feel human and dynamic. This is the most overlooked setting in stock synthesizer presets and the first thing most professional sound designers modify.

12 Frequently Asked Questions

Release is the time it takes to fall from the Sustain level to zero after you let go of the key. If you have Sustain set to 100%, there is effectively no audible decay phase — the sound holds at peak until you release the key, at which point the Release stage takes over.
