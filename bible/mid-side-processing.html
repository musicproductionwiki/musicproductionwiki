Mid-Side Processing

/mɪd saɪd ˈprɒsɛsɪŋ/

Quick Answer Definition How It Works Parameters Quick Reference History How To Use It In The Wild Types Mistakes Related Further Reading FAQ

Mid-Side Processing is a stereo manipulation technique that splits a stereo signal into a Mid channel (center, mono-compatible content) and a Side channel (stereo width information), allowing independent processing of each before re-encoding to stereo.

01 Definition

The stereo field isn't a mystery you manage from the outside — Mid-Side processing hands you the keys and lets you walk straight in.

Mid-Side (M-S) processing is a technique for manipulating stereo audio by first converting the conventional left/right (L/R) stereo signal into two new components: the Mid channel, which contains all audio information shared equally between left and right — the mono-compatible center image — and the Side channel, which contains only the difference signal between left and right, representing the stereo width and spatial information. Once separated, each channel can be processed entirely independently — equalized, compressed, saturated, or otherwise treated — before being recombined through the same matrix transformation to yield a processed stereo signal. The technique is applicable at every stage of production, from tracking and recording through mixing and mastering.

The mathematical relationship is straightforward: Mid = (Left + Right) / 2, and Side = (Left − Right) / 2. Reconstruction is the inverse: Left = Mid + Side, Right = Mid − Side. Because the transformation is lossless — mathematically exact — no information is destroyed during encoding or decoding. This means any processing applied to the Mid and Side channels in isolation translates with full fidelity back into the stereo domain, giving producers surgical control unavailable through standard L/R approaches. Even modest adjustments, such as a 2 dB high-shelf boost on the Side channel only, can dramatically open the perceived width of a mix without touching the center image.

The practical value of M-S processing derives from the natural structure of most commercial recordings. Kick drums, bass guitars, lead vocals, and snares are typically anchored in the center of a stereo image, while reverb tails, stereo synthesizer pads, room microphones, double-tracked guitars, and audience ambience tend to live predominantly in the Side channel. This natural separation means that a low-cut applied to the Side channel alone can clean up muddy low-frequency stereo content — a common mastering move — without affecting the punch or weight of a bass-dominant center. Conversely, a high-frequency boost on the Side channel can add perceived air and width without making the vocal or lead instrument harsh.

M-S processing exists as both a recording microphone technique and a post-processing paradigm. In the recording context, an M-S microphone array uses a cardioid or hypercardioid capsule facing forward (the Mid mic) and a figure-eight capsule oriented perpendicular (the Side mic), whose outputs are later decoded into a stereo pair with adjustable width. In the mixing and mastering context, M-S processing is applied entirely in the digital or analog signal chain using dedicated plug-ins, encoder/decoder hardware, or DAW routing, without any connection to the original recording method. Both applications share the same underlying matrix mathematics but serve distinct purposes in the production workflow.

M-S processing is particularly prevalent in mastering, where engineers routinely use M-S EQ to address specific spectral problems in the stereo field — reducing low-frequency side energy that destabilizes playback on mono systems, enhancing high-frequency air in the width to increase perceived openness, or gently compressing the Side channel to glue stereo information without squashing the center image. In mixing, producers use M-S approaches on stereo bus groups, individual stereo instruments, and room microphone pairs to achieve a level of spatial precision that conventional stereo processing cannot offer.

02 How It Works

The M-S matrix is a sum-and-difference operation applied to the two channels of a stereo signal. Given a standard stereo input with a Left channel (L) and a Right channel (R), the Mid signal is derived by summing both channels: M = (L + R). The Side signal is derived by subtracting one from the other: S = (L − R). Note that in practice many implementations omit the 0.5 scaling factor at the encode stage and compensate at decode, which is mathematically equivalent. The critical point is that M and S are orthogonal — they carry entirely different information. The Mid signal is perfectly mono-compatible; if you fold a stereo mix to mono, you hear only what was in the Mid channel. The Side signal, when played back in mono, cancels to silence, because by definition it represents only the difference between channels.

Processing then occurs on the decoded M and S signals as if they were independent mono tracks. An equalizer inserted on the Mid channel affects only the center image: vocals, kick, bass, and anything panned centrally. An equalizer on the Side channel affects only the stereo width information: room ambience, panned reverb tails, stereo chorus effects, and spatial cues. Similarly, a compressor on the Side channel will control the dynamics of the stereo field independently of the center, allowing an engineer to tighten the width without touching center dynamics — a powerful mastering maneuver. After processing, the M-S matrix is applied in reverse: L = (M + S) and R = (M − S), reconstructing a standard stereo output that carries the effect of whatever processing was applied in the M-S domain.

In DAWs, M-S processing is typically implemented in one of three ways. First, dedicated M-S plug-ins such as FabFilter Pro-Q 3, iZotope Ozone's EQ module, or Brainworx bx_digital handle the encode/decode matrix internally, presenting the engineer with separate Mid and Side bands or channels within a single interface. Second, producers can build manual M-S routing using utility plug-ins: a Mid-Side encoder (often a free utility or a built-in DAW device) sends Mid and Side to separate aux tracks, where any mono plug-in can be inserted, before a decoder re-sums the result. Third, some DAWs — particularly Reaper and Logic Pro — support native M-S routing through their channel matrix or surround panning tools, enabling fully custom M-S processing chains. Each approach is sonically identical, provided the encoder and decoder math is correct.

A critical technical consideration is gain compensation. When decoding back to L/R, the summing and differencing operations can introduce up to 6 dB of level change depending on implementation. Reputable plug-ins handle this transparently, but when building manual M-S chains, producers must account for this gain offset to avoid misleading level comparisons between the processed and bypassed signal. Additionally, extreme Side boosts — particularly in the low frequencies — can introduce out-of-phase energy that causes audible problems on mono playback systems, including Bluetooth speakers, clubs with mono PA systems, and streaming services that apply mono downmix normalization. Checking mono compatibility after any M-S processing session is not optional; it is a professional standard.

The elegance of M-S processing lies in its reversibility and precision. Because the transformation is a linear matrix operation, it introduces no coloration, distortion, or frequency-dependent phase shift of its own — any artifacts introduced are solely those of the processing applied to the Mid and Side components. This makes M-S a transparent framework for extremely targeted spectral and dynamic surgery, giving producers and mastering engineers a vocabulary for stereo manipulation that is both mathematically grounded and immediately audible in practice.

03 The Parameters

Every mid-side processing — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these and you can work with any implementation.

MID GAIN / LEVEL
Controls the amplitude of the center image signal

Adjusting Mid gain raises or lowers all mono-compatible content — kick, snare, lead vocal, bass — simultaneously. A +1.5 dB Mid boost on the master bus can bring perceived loudness and focus to a mix that feels diffuse. Conversely, a −1 to −2 dB Mid reduction in a dense mix can create headroom and allow stereo elements to breathe.

SIDE GAIN / WIDTH
Controls the amplitude of the stereo difference signal

Increasing Side gain widens the perceived stereo image; reducing it narrows the field toward mono. A common mastering move is to reduce Side gain by 1–3 dB below 100–120 Hz to tighten low-frequency mono compatibility. Excessive Side gain boost — beyond +4 to +6 dB — often introduces phase cancellation artifacts and out-of-phase energy that collapses on mono playback.

MID EQ BANDS
Frequency-specific processing applied exclusively to the center image

Mid EQ allows targeted tonal shaping of the vocal, kick, and bass without affecting reverb tails or stereo spread. A common application is a 2–4 dB low-mid cut (200–400 Hz) on the Mid channel to reduce boxiness in the center without dulling the width. High-shelf boosts on the Mid channel add presence and clarity to lead elements at 8–12 kHz without artificially widening the image.

SIDE EQ BANDS
Frequency-specific processing applied exclusively to the stereo difference signal

Side EQ is the workhorse of mastering stereo management. A high-pass filter set at 60–120 Hz on the Side channel removes low-frequency phase issues that cause mono playback problems and streaming normalization errors. A gentle high-shelf boost (+1.5 to +3 dB above 8 kHz) on the Side channel opens perceived air and width without touching center-image sharpness.

MID COMPRESSION THRESHOLD / RATIO
Dynamic control applied only to the center image content

Compressing the Mid channel tightens the dynamics of centrally panned elements — vocals, kick transients, and bass — while leaving stereo width untouched. Typical mastering ratios are gentle: 1.5:1 to 2:1 with a slow attack (15–30 ms) to preserve transient impact. Heavier ratios (4:1+) can push the center forward aggressively, useful in certain heavily processed electronic music contexts.

SIDE COMPRESSION THRESHOLD / RATIO
Dynamic control applied only to the stereo difference signal

Side compression manages the width dynamics of a mix, preventing the stereo field from expanding uncontrollably on loud passages. A ratio of 1.5:1 to 2:1 with a medium attack (20–40 ms) and moderate release (150–250 ms) smooths out width variation. Overcompressing the Side channel narrows the mix noticeably; use a gain reduction meter and target no more than 3–4 dB of Side-channel gain reduction during peaks.

M-S BALANCE (MID/SIDE RATIO)
Global weighting between center and width energy

Many dedicated M-S processors include a single balance or width control that simultaneously raises Mid while lowering Side (narrowing) or raises Side while lowering Mid (widening). This is different from gain staging each channel individually — it acts as a cross-fade between a mono-compatible and a wide stereo presentation. Values of ±15% are typically the outer boundary for transparent master-level adjustments.

05 History & Origin

The origins of Mid-Side processing trace to 1934, when Bell Telephone Laboratories engineer Alan Blumlein — the pioneering figure behind stereophonic sound itself — described the sum-and-difference matrix as part of his foundational British patent no. 394,325 on stereo recording. Blumlein recognized that a stereo signal could be decomposed into a coincident mono component and a directional difference component, enabling adjustable stereo width at the decoder stage. His M-S microphone technique, which paired a forward-facing cardioid capsule with a sideways-oriented figure-eight mic, became one of the defining stereo recording arrays, used extensively at the BBC and by EMI's Abbey Road studios through the 1950s and into the present day.

The commercial use of M-S processing as a mastering and mixing tool gathered momentum through the 1970s and 1980s as the concept migrated from recording microphone technique into the analog signal-processing domain. German broadcast engineer and equipment designer Manfred Reetz at IRT (Institut für Rundfunktechnik) helped codify M-S decoding standards for European broadcasting, where the format's mono compatibility was particularly valued for simultaneous AM and FM transmission. In professional mastering, engineers at facilities such as Sterling Sound and Masterdisk began using custom-built sum-and-difference networks to apply independent analog equalization to mid and side components, a practice that spread gradually through the mastering community throughout the 1980s.

The digital era enabled M-S processing to become a standard tool rather than a specialist's technique. In 1998, the Brainworx bx_digital plugin — developed by Dirk Ulrich and released commercially in the early 2000s — was among the first dedicated M-S equalizer plug-ins to gain widespread mastering adoption, with its distinct Mid and Side EQ bands, a variable mono maker frequency control, and a stereo width parameter. Its interface design became a reference model for how M-S processing should be presented to engineers. Concurrent advances in DAW architecture allowed producers to build bespoke M-S chains using routing matrices, bringing the technique within reach of mix engineers and not just dedicated mastering suites.

Through the 2010s, M-S processing became a standard feature in virtually every professional EQ plug-in. FabFilter's Pro-Q 2 (2013) and Pro-Q 3 (2018) included per-band Mid-Side mode alongside the standard stereo and left/right modes, allowing producers to address specific frequency-range problems in the center or side independently without needing a dedicated M-S encoder/decoder. iZotope's Ozone series incorporated M-S capabilities across its EQ, compressor, imager, and exciter modules. By 2020, M-S processing had become a routine part of undergraduate audio engineering education, appearing in textbooks including David Gibson's The Art of Mixing and Mike Senior's Mixing Secrets for the Small Studio, cementing its status as a foundational professional technique.

06 How Producers Use It

Mastering and master bus. M-S processing is most commonly encountered at the mastering stage, where engineers apply it to finalize the spectral balance and spatial characteristics of a complete stereo mix. The most universal mastering application is a high-pass filter on the Side channel — typically set between 80 and 120 Hz — which removes low-frequency phase discrepancies that accumulate in the stereo difference signal during tracking and mixing. This single move alone often improves perceived punch and mono playback compatibility. A complementary high-shelf boost on the Side channel (1–2 dB above 8–10 kHz) opens perceived air and width without affecting the vocal or lead instrument sharpness in the center image. Dynamic M-S processing on the master bus — particularly Side-only compression — controls width variation, keeping the stereo field from expanding unpredictably on loud sections and collapsing on quiet ones.

Drum bus and room mics. On a stereo drum bus, M-S EQ allows producers to address the snare or kick in the Mid channel — boosting attack at 5–8 kHz or cutting mud at 200–300 Hz — without affecting the overhead-captured room ambience in the Side channel. Room microphones recorded as a stereo pair are an ideal candidate for M-S processing: cutting the Side channel's low-mid frequencies (160–400 Hz) removes muddy buildup while the spatial character and cymbal splash of the overhead image remain intact. M-S compression on the drum bus can control the dynamics of the room sound independently of the center-panned kick and snare.

Stereo synthesizers and pads. Wide stereo synthesizer pads — particularly those generated by chorus, ensemble effects, or stereo detuning — often contain significant energy in the Side channel that can wash out a mix's center. M-S EQ with a low-cut on the Side channel (set to 200–400 Hz for pads, even higher for brighter textures) focuses the pad's low-mid warmth into the center image while retaining the stereo shimmer in the highs. Narrowing the Side gain slightly (−1.5 to −3 dB) on a pad bus can reclaim center space for vocals and lead instruments without making the pad sound mono.

Acoustic guitars and room-heavy sources. Acoustic guitar recordings made with a stereo pair — spaced omnis, XY, or ORTF configurations — frequently exhibit width imbalances and low-frequency phase accumulation. M-S processing allows the Mid channel to be equalized for tonal balance (cutting 200–300 Hz boxiness, boosting 3–5 kHz pick attack) while the Side channel is trimmed or filtered to control room contribution. This is particularly useful when acoustic guitar needs to sit in a dense mix and the room character threatens to muddy the mid-range of the full arrangement. A modest Side gain reduction of −2 to −4 dB can focus the guitar without killing the stereo depth that gives it life.

08 Types & Variants

M-S EQ (Mid-Side Equalization)
Brainworx bx_digital · Maselec MEA-2

The most common application of M-S processing: applying independent equalization curves to the Mid and Side channels. M-S EQ allows spectral shaping of center and width independently, with common uses including Side low-cuts for mono compatibility, Mid low-mid cuts for center clarity, and Side high-shelf boosts for added air and width. The Maselec MEA-2 is a reference hardware M-S equalizer used in top mastering facilities worldwide, while the Brainworx bx_digital defined the template for software M-S EQ interfaces.

M-S Compression (Mid-Side Dynamics)
Elysia Alpha Compressor · Weiss DS1-MK3

Dynamic processing applied independently to Mid and Side signals, controlling the loudness and transient behavior of center and width components separately. Side compression is particularly effective at stabilizing a mix that has uneven width dynamics — pads and reverbs expanding too far on loud passages. The Elysia Alpha Compressor is a celebrated hardware unit with discrete M-S compression channels, used extensively in high-end mastering. The Weiss DS1-MK3 combines M-S dynamics with limiting and de-essing, representing the standard of modern digital hardware mastering processing.

M-S Saturation and Harmonic Excitation
Dangerous Music Liaison · Softube Harmonics

Applying saturation or harmonic distortion to the Mid or Side channels independently adds density and character to specific spatial zones of the stereo image. Saturating only the Mid channel adds harmonic richness and apparent loudness to center elements — vocals, kick, bass — while leaving the stereo reverb and pad information clean. Conversely, adding gentle excitation to the Side channel can make the stereo field shimmer with additional harmonic content without densifying the center image.

M-S Microphone Technique (Recording)
Neumann U87 (cardioid) + Royer R-121 (figure-eight) · AKG C414 (cardioid) + AKG C414 (figure-eight)

The original application of M-S processing: a recording technique using a forward-facing capsule (Mid mic, typically cardioid or omnidirectional) combined with a sideways-oriented figure-eight capsule (Side mic) positioned at the same point in space. The two outputs are decoded via the M-S matrix at the mixing stage to produce a stereo image whose width is continuously adjustable — wider or narrower — without moving microphones. This technique is particularly valued for broadcast, orchestral recording, acoustic guitar, and any application requiring post-production stereo width adjustment.

Mid-Side Wideners and Imaging Processors
Aphex Aural Exciter Type C · iZotope Ozone Imager

Dedicated stereo width processors that use M-S matrixing as the underlying mechanism for widening or narrowing the stereo image. These tools typically present a simplified interface — a width knob or multiband width controls — rather than exposing separate Mid and Side channels. The iZotope Ozone Imager adds multiband M-S width control, allowing independent width adjustment at different frequency ranges, which is a powerful tool for managing the relationship between low-frequency mono content and high-frequency stereo spread.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    Boosting the Side channel below 100 Hz — mono compatibility disaster
    Boosting low-frequency content in the Side channel introduces out-of-phase low-end energy that cancels completely when the stereo signal is summed to mono. On club PA systems, Bluetooth speakers, and broadcast monitoring — all of which produce mono or near-mono low-frequency output — the bass will vanish. Always apply a high-pass filter to the Side channel set between 60 and 120 Hz before making any other Side adjustments. Check your mix in mono after every M-S processing session.
  • ✕
    Over-widening the Side channel without gain compensation
    Raising Side gain significantly (more than +4 to +6 dB) without compensating the overall output level results in a mix that sounds dramatically louder and wider than the unprocessed version, making A/B comparison misleading. Many producers mistake the apparent width and loudness increase for genuine improvement, when it is largely a psychoacoustic artifact of increased level. Always match perceived loudness when bypassing and re-engaging M-S processing to evaluate whether the changes are genuinely beneficial.
  • ✕
    Applying identical M-S EQ curves to every source regardless of content
    The specific frequencies to cut or boost in the Mid and Side channels depend entirely on the spectral and spatial content of the source. A universal Side low-cut at 120 Hz is sensible for a dense mix master but may be far too aggressive for a sparse acoustic guitar recording or a single stereo synth pad. Approach each source with fresh ears and use the Mid-solo and Side-solo monitoring buttons available in most M-S plug-ins to understand what content is actually present before making EQ decisions.
  • ✕
    Ignoring phase issues introduced by M-S processing on non-minimum-phase EQ curves
    Linear phase EQ in M-S mode can introduce pre-ringing and latency issues that create audible smearing on transient-rich sources, particularly at mastering. Standard minimum-phase EQ curves introduce phase rotation, which at large boost/cut amounts in M-S mode can cause subtle phase misalignment between the reconstructed Left and Right channels. Use gentle EQ moves — rarely more than ±3 dB per band — and verify the reconstructed stereo output sounds phase-coherent by checking the correlation meter on the output bus.
  • ✕
    Forgetting to monitor the Mid and Side channels in isolation during processing
    Without using the solo monitoring functions (Mid-solo and Side-solo) available in tools like Brainworx bx_solo, producers often apply M-S processing without fully understanding what they are affecting. Seemingly minor Side EQ adjustments at 300 Hz may be cutting reverb tail warmth that was not apparent in the full stereo playback context. Regularly soloing each channel during the processing session reveals exactly what content is being shaped and prevents unintended sonic consequences.
  • ✕
    Using M-S processing to fix mix problems that should have been addressed at the source
    M-S processing is a precision finishing tool, not a solution to poorly panned or phase-problematic individual tracks. If the mix has comb filtering from a badly placed overhead mic, conflicting stereo guitars, or a vocal that drifts off-center, M-S processing on the master bus will manage symptoms rather than correct causes. Address spatial and phase problems at the individual track and bus level first; apply M-S processing to optimize an already well-constructed stereo image.

A correlation meter on the master bus should ideally read above +0.5; values approaching 0 or going negative indicate significant phase issues.
A standard stereo widener works by increasing or decreasing the Side channel level relative to the Mid channel, essentially adjusting the M-S balance with a single knob. M-S processing is the broader category of which widening is one application — it also includes frequency-specific Mid and Side EQ, independent compression of Mid and Side dynamics, saturation applied to individual channels, and any other processing that uses the M-S matrix to isolate and treat center and width components. M-S processing is more surgical and powerful than a simple width control.
As a general rule, Side channel level boosts above +4 to +6 dB on the master bus begin to introduce audible phase issues on mono playback and create an unnatural stereo image that fatigues listeners. More importantly, any Side boost should be verified against the mono fold — if the perceived bass or vocal level changes significantly when switching to mono after a Side boost, the boost is too large or is crossing into the low-frequency range. The most effective M-S mastering adjustments are typically subtle: +1 to +3 dB on the Side is often the full range of what is needed for a professional result.
Yes, and this is a valuable exercise for understanding the technique at a technical level. The standard approach is to insert a free M-S encoder utility (available as JSFX in Reaper, or as downloadable freeware such as DDMF Metaplugin configurations) on a stereo track to split the signal into Mid and Side, route Mid and Side to separate auxiliary tracks, insert any standard mono plug-ins on each auxiliary, then decode back to stereo with the inverse matrix on the output. The critical requirements are: the encoder must output Mid on the left channel and Side on the right (or vice versa, consistently), and the decoder must exactly inverse the encoding matrix, including any gain compensation. This approach is fully supported in Reaper using its native JS M-S utility plug-ins at no additional cost.
