Low-Pass Filter

/ˈloʊ pæs ˈfɪltər/

Quick Answer Definition How It Works Parameters Quick Reference History How To Use It In The Wild Types Mistakes Calculator Related Further Reading FAQ

A low-pass filter is a circuit or algorithm that allows frequencies below a set cutoff point to pass through while attenuating everything above it. It is one of the most fundamental tools in synthesis, mixing, and sound design.

01 Definition

Every great mix has silence in the right places — and the low-pass filter is how you carve that silence with surgical precision or sweeping drama, depending on what the track demands.

A low-pass filter (LPF) is a frequency-selective network — analog or digital — that permits signal energy below a defined cutoff frequency to pass through largely unaffected while progressively reducing the amplitude of frequencies above that point. The name is purely descriptive: low frequencies pass, high frequencies do not. In practice, no filter creates a perfectly sharp boundary; instead, attenuation increases gradually across a transition band whose steepness is determined by the filter's order and topology. The cutoff frequency — often labeled Fc or simply "Cutoff" on hardware — is conventionally defined as the point at which the output level has dropped by 3 dB relative to the passband, sometimes called the −3 dB point or the half-power frequency.

Low-pass filters appear in virtually every stage of audio production. In synthesis, they are the engine of timbre evolution: a slowly descending cutoff transforms a bright sawtooth wave into something dark and intimate, while a resonant sweep through the same range creates the iconic "wah" effect heard across decades of electronic music. In mixing, an LPF rolled onto a hi-hat bus removes the upper fizz that causes ear fatigue without touching the body of the sound. On a room microphone, a gentle LPF above 12 kHz can glue an ambience signal into the low-mid wash of a drum kit rather than competing with the overhead detail. These are not academic distinctions; they are the practical daily decisions that separate a dense, exhausting mix from one that breathes.

The parameter that gives a low-pass filter its character beyond cutoff is resonance, also called Q or emphasis. Resonance applies a boost in a narrow band immediately around the cutoff frequency before the attenuation slope begins. At low resonance values, the filter sounds smooth and transparent. As resonance increases, the peak becomes more pronounced, coloring the sound with a nasal, vocal quality. At self-oscillation — the point where a resonant analog filter feeds back enough energy to sustain a sine tone without any input signal — the filter becomes a pitch-stable oscillator in its own right, a trick exploited by producers from Aphex Twin to Hudson Mohawke to inject whistling tones directly from the filter stage of a synth.

In the broader EQ context, a low-pass filter is sometimes called a high-cut filter, reflecting a perspective shift: rather than describing what passes, the label describes what is removed. Both terms refer to the same operation. Most parametric equalizers include a dedicated high-cut shelf or LPF section with selectable slopes, and many channel strips incorporate a fixed-frequency high-cut switch — typically 18 kHz or 20 kHz — designed to remove ultrasonic content before it causes aliasing or inter-modulation artifacts in the summing bus. Understanding that "high-cut" and "low-pass" are synonymous prevents confusion when moving between DAW EQ plugins, analog consoles, and synthesizer architecture documentation.

The creative and technical reach of the low-pass filter extends far beyond any single instrument or processing stage. Sidechain-modulated LPFs create pumping, frequency-ducking effects. Tempo-synced LPF automation produces filter-sweep builds standard in house and techno. Formant filtering — stacking multiple resonant LPFs and band-pass filters — generates the vowel shapes of vocoders and talkboxes. Even the natural acoustics of rooms and speaker cabinets can be modeled as low-pass filters with particular slopes and resonance profiles. Mastering the low-pass filter means mastering one of the most versatile, deeply rooted tools in the entire signal-processing vocabulary.

02 How It Works

At the electronic level, the simplest low-pass filter is a single-pole RC (resistor-capacitor) network. A resistor in series with the signal path and a capacitor shunted to ground form a voltage divider whose impedance ratio changes with frequency. At low frequencies, the capacitor presents high impedance and passes the signal to the output largely intact. As frequency rises, capacitive reactance decreases, shunting more energy to ground and reducing the output amplitude. This first-order RC filter produces a slope of −6 dB per octave (equivalently, −20 dB per decade) above the cutoff. Each additional filter pole — achieved by cascading RC stages or using inductors — adds another −6 dB/octave of slope. A four-pole filter therefore rolls off at −24 dB/octave, which is why the Moog ladder filter, a four-pole design, became synonymous with a particular dense, punchy character: it removes high-frequency content aggressively enough to transform a thin oscillator waveform into something viscerally physical.

Digital implementations derive from the same mathematics but operate in the discrete-time domain. Infinite impulse response (IIR) filters — including the bilinear-transform versions of Butterworth, Chebyshev, and state-variable topologies — process each sample as a weighted sum of current and past input and output samples. Finite impulse response (FIR) filters instead convolve the signal with a fixed kernel, offering perfectly linear phase at the cost of higher CPU overhead and latency. The choice between IIR and FIR matters in mastering and post-production contexts where phase coherence between filtered and unfiltered parallel paths can cause comb-filtering artifacts. In synthesis and creative mixing, IIR filters are preferred because their gentle phase rotation contributes to the "analogness" that makes classic soft-synth emulations sound warm rather than clinical.

The resonance mechanism is implemented differently across filter topologies. In an analog state-variable or transistor-ladder filter, resonance is produced by feeding a portion of the output back to the input with a phase relationship that reinforces energy near the cutoff frequency. The feedback amount is controlled by the resonance knob. In digital models, the same feedback coefficient is implemented mathematically. At high resonance values, the feedback loop approaches unity gain at the cutoff frequency, and if the coefficient is pushed past this point the filter self-oscillates. Analog designs clip or soft-saturate at self-oscillation, giving the tone warmth; digital designs can produce a clean, pure sine tone or, if poorly coded, numerical overflow artifacts. This is why analog-modeled plugins incorporate deliberate saturation stages within their filter feedback paths — it is not a cosmetic addition but a functional necessity for accurate circuit behavior.

Filter slope and cutoff interact in ways that affect perceived loudness as well as tone. Because a steep LPF removes significant energy from the signal, high-order filtering can cause an apparent drop in level even when the cutoff is set well above the fundamental frequency content. When automating a filter sweep in a mix — dropping the cutoff on a synth lead through a breakdown, for example — producers often compensate by riding the output gain up slightly as the cutoff descends, maintaining perceived loudness consistency across the arrangement. Most modern plugin implementations include a makeup gain parameter or allow the cutoff-to-gain compensation to be calibrated internally. Understanding this interaction prevents the common mistake of interpreting a cutoff sweep as a dynamic problem and reaching for compression when a simple gain trim is the correct tool.

The practical consequence of all this theory is straightforward: choose your filter topology based on the character you want, set the cutoff by ear while watching a spectrum analyzer for confirmation, use resonance as a tonal color rather than a default setting, and account for level changes caused by steep slopes. These principles hold whether you are working inside a software synthesizer's filter page, an analog modular rack, or the EQ section of a DAW channel strip.

03 The Parameters

Every low-pass filter — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these and you can work with any implementation.

CUTOFF FREQUENCY (Fc)
Sets the frequency above which attenuation begins

Expressed in Hz, the cutoff marks the −3 dB point of the filter's response. In synthesis contexts it typically ranges from 20 Hz to 20 kHz with exponential scaling so that the knob feels musically even across octaves. In mixing, useful ranges are 8–18 kHz for air removal on buses, 2–6 kHz for telephone and lo-fi effects, and 200–800 Hz for heavy darkening effects on pads or room mics.

RESONANCE (Q / EMPHASIS)
Controls the amplitude of the peak at the cutoff frequency

At Q values below 0.5, the filter sounds smooth and slightly shelving. Between Q 0.7 and 2.0, the cutoff peak adds presence and tonal color without overwhelming the source. Above Q 4–6 on most analog-modeled filters, the resonance peak dominates the sound and can self-oscillate. In a mix context, resonance above 1.0 on an LPF is generally avoided unless the filtered artifact is intentional; in synthesis, high resonance is a core expressive parameter.

FILTER SLOPE (ORDER / POLES)
Determines how aggressively the filter attenuates above the cutoff

Expressed as dB per octave: 6 dB/oct (1-pole), 12 dB/oct (2-pole), 18 dB/oct (3-pole), 24 dB/oct (4-pole). The Moog ladder is a 24 dB/oct design; many Roland and Oberheim filters are 12 dB/oct. Steeper slopes remove more high-frequency content with a given cutoff position but also affect more of the transient information and phase response. In mastering, a 12 dB/oct slope at 20 kHz is more transparent than 24 dB/oct at the same frequency.

FILTER TOPOLOGY / TYPE
Selects the circuit model and its associated sonic character

Common types include Butterworth (maximally flat passband), Chebyshev (steeper initial rolloff with ripple), Bessel (linear phase, gentle slope), and ladder/transistor ladder (non-linear saturation, musically warm). In plugin terms, topology is often labeled as "Vintage," "Clean," or named after a hardware model. The choice shapes harmonic content, phase behavior, and whether the filter introduces pleasant saturation or remains transparent.

ENVELOPE MODULATION DEPTH
Controls how much an envelope generator sweeps the cutoff

In synthesizers, an ADSR or similar envelope is routinely routed to the filter cutoff with a depth parameter that sets the maximum sweep range in semitones or Hz. A depth of 100% with a short attack and medium decay creates the classic "pluck" of a bass synthesizer. Negative depth inverts the envelope, opening the filter as the envelope decays — useful for pads and ambient textures. In modular synthesis, this is a patched CV connection; in software it is a routable modulation matrix slot.

KEY TRACKING
Scales the cutoff frequency proportionally to the MIDI note played

At 100% key tracking, the cutoff rises by the same interval as the pitch, preserving tonal consistency across the keyboard range. At 0%, the same cutoff applies to every note, which can cause the filter to sound progressively more closed on higher-pitched notes. Intermediate values (50–75%) are common on lead synths to add slight brightness to upper registers without fully tracking pitch. On polyphonic pads, disabling key tracking and setting a fixed cutoff creates a more cohesive, ensemble-like timbre.

05 History & Origin

The low-pass filter predates recorded music as a formal concept. Oliver Heaviside's telegraph line equations of the 1880s described how electrical cables act as distributed low-pass filters — attenuating high-frequency components of the transmitted signal faster than low-frequency ones. By the early 1900s, electrical engineers George Campbell and Karl Willy Wagner independently developed the theoretical framework for LC (inductor-capacitor) ladder filter networks, publishing their designs around 1915. These networks were immediately applied in telephone repeater circuits to limit bandwidth and reduce noise, establishing the low-pass filter as a practical tool in telecommunications a full generation before the magnetic tape era.

In audio equipment, the low-pass filter entered the studio in the form of high-frequency rolloff switches on console channels and tape machines. The Neve 1073 preamp-EQ module, designed by Rupert Neve and introduced in 1970, included an 18 kHz high-pass filter and a high-frequency shelf, but it was the SSL 4000 series console of the late 1970s — used on countless hit records mixed by engineers including Bob Clearmountain and Alan Parsons — that standardized the high-cut filter as a routine mixing tool accessible to every channel. The API 550 equalizer, designed by John Starret in 1967, offered fixed high-frequency cut points that engineers at studios like Sunset Sound used to tame harshness on tape-distorted electric guitars long before the term "lo-fi" existed as an aesthetic category.

The musical use of the low-pass filter as a dynamic, performable parameter emerged from the synthesizer revolution of the late 1960s. Robert Moog's transistor ladder filter, patented in 1969 (US Patent 3,475,623), was the architectural breakthrough that made the LPF central to modern music. The four-pole, 24 dB/octave design with voltage-controlled cutoff and resonance gave musicians the ability to sculpt timbre in real time. Wendy Carlos's 1968 album Switched-On Bach demonstrated to a mass audience that filter sweeps could be as musically meaningful as pitch or rhythm. The ARP 2600 (1971) and later the Sequential Circuits Prophet-5 (1978), designed by Dave Smith, brought polyphonic voltage-controlled filters to keyboard players and established the filter sweep as a defining gesture of the era.

In the late 1980s and through the 1990s, the low-pass filter became the defining sound of electronic dance music. Roland's TB-303 Bass Line synthesizer, released in 1981 but adopted by Chicago acid house producers around 1986–87, featured a resonant 18 dB/oct LPF whose cutoff and resonance knobs, when turned in real time, produced the "squelching" acid bassline sound heard on records by DJ Pierre, Spanky, and Herb J under the name Phuture — most notably on "Acid Tracks" (1987). The filter sweep became not just a sound-design technique but a compositional device: the drop and subsequent reopening of a low-pass filter during a club track's breakdown replaced the conventional verse-chorus architecture with a tension-and-release arc native to the dancefloor.

06 How Producers Use It

On synthesizer bass lines and leads, the low-pass filter is the primary expressive control. A producer designing a growling reese bass in a drum and bass track will typically start with two or more detuned sawtooth oscillators, set the filter to a 24 dB/oct ladder model, dial the cutoff to around 300–600 Hz, add moderate resonance (Q 1.5–2.5), and then modulate the cutoff with an LFO synchronized to the tempo — typically a sine or triangle wave at half-note or quarter-note rate. This produces the rhythmic "talking" quality of classic Dillinja or Noisia basslines. On leads, the filter cutoff is envelope-modulated with a fast attack and medium decay so that each note begins bright and immediately darkens, giving the impression of acoustic plucking or bowing even on a purely synthetic waveform.

In drum mixing, the low-pass filter is most often applied to room microphones and overhead channels rather than individual close-mic signals. A room mic capturing kit ambience in a live-sounding rock or hip-hop session often contains an abundance of high-frequency splash from cymbals that competes with the overhead pair. Applying a 12–18 dB/oct LPF at 6–10 kHz on the room mic removes this competition, allowing the room mic to contribute warmth and body to the kit without adding cymbal clutter. Similarly, a high-cut at 14–16 kHz on the overheads bus — subtle, transparent, just catching the extreme air — can reduce harshness from high-hat bleed in a dense mix where multiple cymbal tracks are summed.

For vocal processing, producers use the low-pass filter most frequently in three scenarios: creating telephone or radio effects (cutoff 3–3.5 kHz, steep slope, light resonance), reducing sibilance as an alternative to a de-esser (gentle 6 dB/oct slope at 10–14 kHz), and processing doubled or layered vocal stacks where the additional layers are high-cut to sit behind the lead. The latter technique is particularly effective in pop and R&B production: the lead vocal receives full-bandwidth treatment while harmonies or doubles are LPF'd at 8–10 kHz, creating natural depth without artificial reverb. Engineers including Andrew Scheps and Young Guru have described versions of this approach in interviews as a way to add space without mud.

On the mix bus and in mastering, low-pass filters are used conservatively. A gentle 6 dB/oct slope beginning at 18–20 kHz can remove ultrasonic content that causes inter-sample peaks and streaming codec artifacts without any audible effect on the mix's perceived brightness. Some mastering engineers — notably Bob Ludwig and Greg Calbi — apply a very gentle high-cut shelf (not a true LPF) at the top of the audible range to reduce the harshness that modern brick-wall limiting imparts to high-frequency content. The threshold between a transparent mastering LPF and an audible one is razor-thin, which is why the slopes used in mastering contexts are almost never steeper than 12 dB/oct and are almost always set at or above 16 kHz.

07 In the Wild

Abstract knowledge becomes practical when you can hear it in music you know. These tracks demonstrate low-pass filter used intentionally, at specific moments, for specific purposes.

Daft Punk — "One More Time" (2000)
0:00–0:32 intro · Produced by Daft Punk (Thomas Bangalter & Guy-Manuel de Homem-Christo)

The track opens with the full-bandwidth instrumental mix heard through a low-pass filter at approximately 3–4 kHz, creating a muffled, AM-radio character before the filter opens to full spectrum at 0:32. This technique — starting filtered and gradually opening — became a blueprint for euphoric dancefloor builds. Listen for the resonance peak that adds a slight nasal coloration as the filter sweeps upward; it is subtle but identifiable on reference headphones and gave sound designers a specific target to replicate.

Phuture — "Acid Tracks" (1987)
Full track, most pronounced 1:10–2:30 · Produced by DJ Pierre, Spanky, Herb J

The defining document of the resonant LPF as a musical voice. A Roland TB-303 bass line is processed through the unit's internal 18 dB/oct filter with resonance at approximately 70–80% of maximum and the cutoff automated manually in real time. The result is a living, morphing tone that alternates between a deep sub-bass thud and a sharp, biting squeal depending on cutoff position. No pitch or rhythm changes are required; the filter alone creates the entire expressive arc of the piece.

Portishead — "Sour Times" (1994)
0:45–1:15 verse · Produced by Geoff Barrow & Adrian Utley

The sampled James Bond-style orchestral loop underpinning the verse is processed through a low-pass filter at approximately 4–5 kHz with a 12 dB/oct slope, giving it a worn, vinyl-degraded quality without removing the rhythmic punch of the brass stabs. This is a masterclass in using the LPF to impart lo-fi texture while preserving musical information. The contrast when Beth Gibbons's unfiltered vocal enters over the top is a deliberate frequency-space decision that creates intimacy and foreground depth.

Burial — "Archangel" (2007)
0:00–throughout · Produced by Burial (William Bevan)

Nearly every element in this track — the chopped vocal, the percussion, the synthesizer pads — passes through low-pass filtering at varying cutoff frequencies to simulate the sonic degradation of a cassette tape or a low-bitrate digital file. The result is a mix that exists almost entirely below 10 kHz, with the highest-energy content concentrated in the 200 Hz–4 kHz range. The absence of high-frequency air is not a deficiency but a compositional statement about urban sonic environments. Study this track on a flat reference system to understand how a filtered mix can still feel spatially complete.

Kendrick Lamar — "HUMBLE." (2017)
0:00–0:15 intro drum pattern · Produced by Mike WiLL Made-It

The kick drum in the opening four bars is processed with a low-pass filter at approximately 200 Hz, removing nearly all transient click and leaving only the fundamental thud of the shell. When the full kit enters at 0:15, the contrast between the filtered intro kick and the full-spectrum mix kick is startling and physically felt. This is a surgical use of the LPF as a dynamic contrast tool rather than a tonal correction, demonstrating that filter cutoff can function as a compositional parameter in hip-hop production as much as in electronic music.

08 Types & Variants

Butterworth Low-Pass Filter
SSL 4000 high-cut · dbx 2231 graphic EQ high end

The Butterworth design prioritizes a maximally flat frequency response in the passband — no ripple, no resonance peak — at the cost of a relatively slow initial rolloff. In mixing it sounds the most transparent of all filter topologies: the listener cannot easily identify where the filter is operating because there is no coloration artifact to cue the ear. Preferred in mastering and bus processing where transparency is paramount.

Transistor Ladder (Moog-Style) Filter
Moog Minimoog · Moog Model 15 · Moog Subsequent 37

The transistor ladder topology, as refined by Robert Moog, produces a 24 dB/oct rolloff with a musically-voiced resonance peak and inherent non-linear saturation in the feedback path. Even at moderate resonance settings, the filter imparts warmth to the passband because the transistor stages gently clip on peaks. This coloration is the defining sound of analog synthesis from the 1970s through the present day and is the reference point against which most plugin filter emulations are benchmarked.

State-Variable Filter
Oberheim SEM · ARP 2600 · Roland SH-101

The state-variable design simultaneously outputs low-pass, band-pass, and high-pass responses, often with a blend control that morphs between them. Its resonance character is more nasal and vocal than the ladder topology, and it can achieve self-oscillation more smoothly. The Oberheim SEM (1974), designed by Tom Oberheim and Bob Moog collaborator Dave Rossum, used a 12 dB/oct state-variable filter that became the signature of Oberheim polyphonics and later influenced the Prophet-5 voice architecture.

Chebyshev Low-Pass Filter
Rane EQ30S graphic EQ · Various crossover networks in studio monitors

The Chebyshev type I filter accepts a small, controlled amount of ripple in the passband in exchange for a steeper initial rolloff than the Butterworth. This makes it useful in crossover networks where rapid transition from pass to stop is needed within a narrow frequency range. In audio electronics it appears most often inside active speaker crossovers and in studio-grade graphic equalizers used for room correction.

FIR (Finite Impulse Response) Low-Pass Filter
Weiss DS1-MK3 (digital) · Lavry Engineering converters (anti-aliasing stage)

FIR filters are defined by linear phase response — every frequency passes through the filter with identical time delay, preserving transient waveshape perfectly. This makes them the preferred choice for anti-aliasing filters in analog-to-digital converters and for transparent high-cut processing in mastering contexts. The tradeoff is significant computational overhead and latency (pre-ringing), which is why FIR filters are not typically used in real-time synthesis contexts where low latency is critical.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    Setting the cutoff too low and calling it warmth
    Dropping the LPF cutoff below 6–8 kHz on a mix element that occupies the mid-high range — a snare, an acoustic guitar, a vocal — removes the definition transients and "air" that make the sound feel present and three-dimensional. The result is not warmth but mud: a dense, indistinct mass that sits behind the mix rather than in it. True warmth comes from saturation and careful low-mid shaping, not from high-frequency amputation. If the goal is warmth, compare the filtered result against the unfiltered source at matched levels before committing.
  • ✕
    Ignoring level compensation after steep filtering
    A 24 dB/oct LPF removes significant spectral energy from a signal, and the apparent loudness will drop even if the plugin's output meter shows no change in peak level. This level reduction is often misread as the filter "working correctly," leading to a mix where filtered elements are pushed up in fader level to compensate — which then makes them too loud when the filter is bypassed for comparison. Always apply makeup gain after a steep LPF, and always compare at matched perceived loudness levels to evaluate the filter's tonal impact alone.
  • ✕
    Using resonance on mix-bus low-pass filters
    A resonance peak at the cutoff frequency of a bus or master LPF creates a narrow boost that adds coloration precisely where the filter is supposed to create a clean transition. Even a Q of 0.7 on a master bus LPF at 18 kHz adds a subtle tilt to the high-frequency response that can accumulate with other processing and cause the master to feel harsh on certain playback systems. On buses and masters, resonance should be set to the minimum available value — typically 0 or 0.5 on most plugin EQs.
  • ✕
    Automating filter cutoff without automating output gain
    A common movement in electronic music is a long, slow LPF sweep from open (18–20 kHz) to closed (500 Hz – 1 kHz) over 16 or 32 bars to build tension before a drop. Without compensating gain automation, the track loses perceived energy as the cutoff descends, and the drop — intended to feel explosive — arrives after a section that has already deflated dynamically. The fix is a parallel gain automation lane that gradually increases the level of the filtered channel as the cutoff descends, maintaining density so the drop's energy contrast is preserved.
  • ✕
    Using a low-pass filter to fix high-frequency noise rather than addressing the source
    A microphone with a noisy preamp, a ground-loop hum with high-frequency harmonics, or a digital clocking artifact can produce broadband high-frequency noise that tempts producers to apply a steep LPF at 12–15 kHz to bury it. This removes the noise but also removes the air and definition of the signal above that point. The correct approach is to identify and eliminate the noise source — check ground connections, improve preamp gain staging, or re-record. If the recording cannot be redone, use a spectral repair tool like iZotope RX rather than a broadband LPF that discards useful signal along with the noise.
  • ✕
    Applying identical LPF settings to every element in the mix
    A producer who discovers that an LPF at 16 kHz improves the overhead bus sometimes applies the same setting to every track as a workflow shortcut. The result is a mix where every element has been treated identically, removing the frequency differentiation that gives each instrument its distinct space in the stereo field. Low-pass filtering decisions should be made per-track based on what each track contributes above the cutoff point. A bass guitar may benefit from an LPF at 5 kHz; a vocal may not need any LPF at all; a synth pad may need one at 10 kHz. Uniform treatment is rarely musically correct.

12 Frequently Asked Questions

A low-pass filter removes high-frequency content from a sound while leaving the lower frequencies intact. Think of it as a tone control that makes sounds darker as you turn it down. Every equalizer, synthesizer, and most DAW channel strips include at least one low-pass filter, sometimes labeled as a 'high-cut' filter.
They are the same thing described from two different perspectives. 'Low-pass' describes what the filter allows through (low frequencies). 'High-cut' describes what the filter removes (high frequencies). Synthesizer manufacturers tend to use 'low-pass'; console and EQ manufacturers tend to use 'high-cut.' Knowing that these terms are synonymous prevents confusion when working across different equipment and software.
Resonance adds a boost in amplitude at the cutoff frequency before the attenuation slope begins. At low settings it adds a subtle tonal presence; at high settings it creates a nasal, vocal coloration; and at maximum settings on many analog-style filters it causes self-oscillation, turning the filter into a pitch-stable sine wave oscillator. In mixing contexts, resonance above 0.7 Q on an LPF is generally avoided; in synthesis it is a primary expressive tool.
For transparent high-end cleanup on a mix bus without audible effect, start with a 6–12 dB/oct slope and a cutoff between 18 and 20 kHz. This removes ultrasonic content that can cause inter-sample clipping and streaming codec artifacts without touching the audible air of the mix. Only move the cutoff below 16 kHz if you have a specific sonic reason — below that point, the filter will be audible on a good playback system.
The acid sound comes from a resonant low-pass filter on a Roland TB-303 (or emulation) with the resonance set high — typically 60–85% of maximum — and the cutoff frequency automated or performed manually in real time. The high resonance creates a prominent peak at the cutoff, and as the cutoff moves, that peak sweeps through the frequency spectrum, creating a singing, morphing quality. The TB-303's specific 18 dB/oct diode ladder filter topology gives the resonance peak a particular character that has been widely replicated but rarely exactly matched.
A de-esser is almost always the better choice for sibilance control because it responds dynamically — only attenuating the high-frequency content when a sibilant sound occurs. A static low-pass filter set low enough to reduce sibilance will also reduce the presence and air of the vocal at all times, making it sound dull in non-sibilant passages. Use the LPF only if the vocal has a consistent high-frequency harshness throughout — not just during sibilant consonants — and even then, prefer a gentle 6 dB/oct slope above 12 kHz.
IIR (Infinite Impulse Response) filters are recursive — they feed past output samples back into the calculation, which creates phase shift (non-linear phase) but uses very little CPU. FIR (Finite Impulse Response) filters convolve the signal with a fixed coefficient array, guaranteeing linear phase (zero phase distortion) at the cost of significant computational load and latency. For synthesis and real-time mixing, IIR filters are standard because their phase rotation actually contributes to the warm, analog character producers seek. For mastering or sample-accurate alignment between filtered and unfiltered parallel paths, FIR or minimum-phase FIR designs are preferred.
As the LPF cutoff descends during a sweep, the signal loses high-frequency energy and its perceived loudness drops. To compensate, create a gain automation lane on the same track (or on a parallel utility gain plugin) and draw in a gradual level increase that mirrors the cutoff descent — typically 1–3 dB of makeup gain over the sweep range, depending on how steeply filtered the signal becomes. Some advanced plugins such as FabFilter Pro-Q 3 allow you to automate both cutoff and output gain simultaneously in a single automation lane by using the plugin's built-in gain compensation, but manual gain riding gives the most precise control over the curve shape.
