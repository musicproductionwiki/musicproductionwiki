EQ

/ˌiːˈkjuː/

Definition How It Works Parameters Quick Reference History How To Use It In The Wild Types Mistakes Related FAQ

EQ (equalisation) is the process of adjusting the amplitude of specific frequency bands within an audio signal. Producers use it to correct tonal imbalances, remove problematic frequencies, and carve out space so that individual elements sit cleanly in a mix. Every DAW and hardware signal chain includes some form of EQ, making it the most fundamental tone-shaping tool in music production.

01 Definition

Equalisation — universally abbreviated to EQ — is the selective amplification or attenuation of individual frequency bands within an audio signal. Unlike a simple volume fader, which raises or lowers the entire signal uniformly, an equaliser operates with surgical or broad-strokes precision across the frequency spectrum, allowing a producer or engineer to change the character of a sound rather than merely its loudness. EQ is present at virtually every stage of modern music production: during tracking to optimise a source before it hits a recorder, during mixing to balance instruments against one another, and during mastering to shape the final two-bus for playback consistency across systems.

The frequency spectrum audible to humans spans roughly 20 Hz to 20 kHz, and every instrument, voice, and synthesised element occupies a portion of that range — often overlapping with several others. When two elements share the same frequency region at comparable amplitude, the result is masking: neither is clearly defined, the mix sounds congested, and listener fatigue increases. EQ resolves this by allowing the producer to reduce the overlapping region in the less important element (subtractive EQ) while preserving or enhancing it in the primary element. This technique, sometimes called frequency carving or creating space, is the cornerstone of professional-sounding mixes.

EQ operates on both corrective and creative levels. Corrective EQ addresses problems at the source — the proximity effect in a close-miked vocal, the boxy 400 Hz buildup in a room-miked snare, the subsonic rumble from a poorly isolated studio — with the goal of making a recording sound as neutral or natural as possible before any further processing. Creative EQ, by contrast, is an intentional deviation from neutrality: the exaggerated high-frequency air on a lead vocal, the telephone or lo-fi band-pass effect on a sample, the hyped low-mid warmth on a bass guitar. Both approaches are valid and often coexist on the same channel strip.

It is worth distinguishing EQ from filtering in a strict sense, though the terms frequently overlap in production contexts. A filter is a single-pole or multi-pole circuit or algorithm that operates at a defined boundary frequency with a fixed slope, removing content above or below that point. An equaliser is a more complex assembly of filters — each with adjustable frequency, gain, and bandwidth parameters — arranged so the user can shape multiple regions simultaneously. In modern DAWs such as Ableton Live, Logic Pro, FL Studio, and Pro Tools, parametric EQs with eight or more bands are standard issue, and the distinction between filter and EQ is largely academic. What matters in practice is understanding which type of curve is appropriate for the task at hand.

The importance of EQ cannot be overstated relative to other mix tools. Compression controls dynamics; reverb and delay add spatial dimension; saturation adds harmonic content. EQ alone determines the tonal identity of every element in a mix. A snare drum that sounds thin and papery on its own can be transformed into a full, authoritative crack with three or four well-placed EQ moves. A muddy bass can become tight and defined. A harsh synthesiser pad can become smooth and enveloping. This power — and the subtlety required to wield it without damaging the natural character of a source — is why EQ mastery is considered the defining skill that separates competent engineers from exceptional ones.

02 How It Works

At its most fundamental level, an equaliser is a network of filters, each designed to alter the gain of a specific frequency range. In the analogue domain, filters are built from passive components (resistors, capacitors, inductors) or active components (operational amplifiers combined with RC networks) that create frequency-dependent impedance. When the impedance at a given frequency is high relative to adjacent frequencies, that frequency is attenuated; when it is low, that frequency passes with less loss or is boosted. The precise shape of the gain curve — how sharply it rises and falls, how wide the affected region is — is determined by the filter topology and component values. In the digital domain, these same behaviours are modelled using difference equations derived from the analogue prototypes, typically implemented as IIR (Infinite Impulse Response) or FIR (Finite Impulse Response) filters, each with distinct phase and latency characteristics.

The most critical concept for understanding EQ behaviour is the filter transfer function: a mathematical description of how a filter modifies amplitude (and phase) at every frequency. For a parametric bell filter — the most common EQ band type — the transfer function produces a smooth, symmetrical peak or dip centred at the chosen frequency, with a width determined by the Q (quality factor). Raising the gain parameter shifts the peak above 0 dB; lowering it creates a dip. The Q setting determines how many octaves wide the affected region is: a low Q of 0.5–1.0 creates a broad, musical curve that affects two or more octaves; a high Q of 8–20 creates a narrow notch that surgically targets a specific problem frequency with minimal impact on surrounding content. The relationship between Q and bandwidth in octaves is expressed as: BW (octaves) = log₂(1 + 1/Q²)^0.5 × 2 — in practice, producers rely on their ears rather than this formula, but understanding that higher Q means narrower bandwidth is essential.

Phase shift is an inescapable consequence of analogue-style EQ processing. Every filter introduces a phase relationship between input and output that varies with frequency — particularly pronounced around the filter's centre or cutoff frequency. For minimum-phase filters (the vast majority of analogue and IIR digital EQs), this phase shift is mathematically linked to the amplitude response: you cannot change the amplitude at a frequency without also changing the phase at nearby frequencies. In most mixing contexts this is sonically benign or even desirable — the phase shift from an analogue console EQ contributes to the warm, cohesive quality many engineers associate with classic hardware. However, when processing a stereo signal with mismatched EQ settings per channel, or when summing multiple EQ'd signals with overlapping frequencies, phase interactions can cause comb filtering or image narrowing. Linear-phase EQ — available in plugins such as the FabFilter Pro-Q series and iZotope Ozone — eliminates this by using FIR filters with a symmetric impulse response, adding a fixed latency but preserving the phase relationship between all frequencies. The trade-off is pre-ringing artefacts on transients at steep slopes, which is why linear-phase EQ is most appropriate on bus and mastering contexts rather than individual drum hits.

In the digital signal chain, an EQ plugin receives the audio stream as a sequence of numerical samples at the project's sample rate (typically 44.1 kHz, 48 kHz, 88.2 kHz, or 96 kHz). Each filter band processes the sample stream through its difference equation, computing an output sample from a weighted combination of current and previous input samples and previous output samples. Because digital filters operate at the Nyquist limit — the highest representable frequency is exactly half the sample rate — some digital EQ implementations can exhibit non-ideal behaviour near those limits, particularly for bell filters with high centre frequencies and high gain settings. High-quality EQ plugins address this with oversampling: internally processing at 2×, 4×, or 8× the project sample rate before downsampling back, eliminating Nyquist-related cramping and more faithfully replicating the smooth analogue curves they emulate.

03 Parameters

Frequency (Hz / kHz)
The centre frequency of a bell or notch band, or the cutoff frequency of a shelf or filter. This determines where in the spectrum the EQ acts. Frequencies are generally grouped into regions: sub-bass (20–60 Hz), bass (60–250 Hz), low-mids (250–500 Hz), mids (500 Hz–2 kHz), upper-mids (2–6 kHz), presence (6–10 kHz), and air (10–20 kHz). Setting frequency correctly is the primary skill of EQ use — a boost at 3 kHz lifts attack and definition; the same boost at 1 kHz adds honk and nasality.
Gain (dB)
The amount of amplitude increase (boost) or decrease (cut) applied at the centre frequency. Expressed in decibels, with 0 dB representing no change. Most parametric EQs allow a range of ±12 to ±24 dB per band. As a general principle, cuts can be applied freely and aggressively; boosts should be used with restraint, since boosting adds energy that can accumulate across multiple channels and stress downstream processing. Subtle boosts of 1–3 dB are often more musical than dramatic cuts; cuts of 6–12 dB or more are sometimes required to remove genuine problems.
Q (Quality Factor / Bandwidth)
Determines the width of the affected frequency range. Numerically, Q is the ratio of the centre frequency to the bandwidth: Q = fc / BW. A Q of 0.7 produces a broad, one-octave-plus curve suitable for tonal shaping; a Q of 1.4 is roughly one octave; a Q of 10 or higher creates a narrow surgical notch for removing specific resonances, room modes, or hum. Some EQ interfaces express this as bandwidth in octaves rather than Q — the two are inversely related.
Filter Type
The shape of the EQ curve applied at a given band. The primary types are: Bell (Peak/Dip) — symmetrical boost or cut centred at a frequency; Low Shelf — boosts or cuts all content below a set frequency, transitioning smoothly; High Shelf — same, operating above the set frequency; High-Pass Filter (HPF) — rolls off content below the cutoff, passing high frequencies; Low-Pass Filter (LPF) — rolls off content above the cutoff; Notch (Band-Reject) — extremely narrow cut targeting a single frequency; Band-Pass — passes only a narrow range, cutting everything above and below. Each serves distinct purposes: shelves for broad tonal shaping, bells for targeted adjustment, HPF/LPF for cleaning up extremes.
Slope (dB/octave)
Applies specifically to high-pass and low-pass filters, and to shelving filters on some EQs. Determines how steeply the filter attenuates beyond the cutoff frequency. Common slopes are 6 dB/octave (first-order, single-pole — very gentle), 12 dB/octave (second-order), 18 dB/octave (third-order), 24 dB/octave (fourth-order, Butterworth — the standard for most mix HPF work), and 48 dB/octave (eighth-order — very steep, surgical). Steeper slopes remove more content quickly but introduce more phase shift and potential ringing; gentler slopes are more transparent but less decisive.
Resonance / Q at Cutoff
On high-pass and low-pass filters on some parametric EQs (and on most synthesiser filters), a resonance or Q parameter controls whether the filter exhibits a peak just before the cutoff frequency. A resonant HPF on a bass instrument can add a characteristic sub-punch; excessive resonance at a filter cutoff produces the self-oscillating squeal familiar from synthesiser filter sweeps. In a mix EQ context, this is rarely used but can be useful for creative spectral sculpting.

05 History

The concept of frequency-selective amplification predates the term 'equaliser' by several decades. The first practical equalisers emerged in telephony during the 1920s, where engineers at Bell Laboratories — most notably Harry Nyquist and his colleagues — needed to compensate for the frequency-dependent signal loss (attenuation) that occurred over long telephone lines. These passive networks of inductors and capacitors were designed to equalise the frequency response of the transmission line back to flatness, restoring intelligibility to voice signals that would otherwise arrive muffled and thin. The very name 'equaliser' derives from this corrective purpose: the goal was not to shape sound creatively, but to make the output equal the input across all frequencies.

By the late 1940s and early 1950s, broadcast and recording engineers had begun to apply the concept artistically. Early recording consoles incorporated fixed high- and low-frequency shelving circuits, and by the mid-1950s, the first commercial parametric-style equalisers were appearing in broadcast facilities. The Pultec EQP-1, introduced by Pulse Techniques in 1951, became one of the most celebrated analogue EQs in history. Its passive inductor-capacitor design, combined with a subsequent active makeup gain stage, produced a characteristic low-frequency boost-and-cut interaction — boosting and cutting the same frequency simultaneously — that created a rich, musical curve impossible to precisely replicate with simpler designs. Pultec units defined the sound of countless recordings through the 1950s and 1960s and remain in daily use in world-class studios today, with faithful software emulations available from Universal Audio, Waves, and others. The Neve 1073 module, designed by Rupert Neve and introduced in 1970, established the three-band semi-parametric console EQ as the standard format for recording consoles, with its transformer-coupled topology contributing a harmonic character that sits at the core of the 'British console' sound.

The 1970s and 1980s saw the rise of the graphic equaliser — a bank of fixed-frequency band-pass filters with individual faders, arranged in octave or one-third-octave steps. Graphic EQs became standard in live sound reinforcement for feedback control and room correction, and proliferated in consumer hi-fi systems. Simultaneously, the fully parametric EQ — with continuously variable frequency, gain, and Q on every band — matured in professional studio hardware, exemplified by the API 550 series and the SSL 4000 series channel EQ. The digital era brought software parametric EQs of extraordinary precision and flexibility. The introduction of the FabFilter Pro-Q (first released 2008) redefined expectations for digital EQ, offering dynamic EQ modes, linear-phase operation, and per-band spectrum analysis in a single interface. Today, AI-assisted EQ — such as iZotope Neutron's spectral analysis and matching tools — continues to push the boundaries of what frequency shaping can accomplish.

06 How Producers Use It

In practical workflow, EQ appears at three distinct stages: during tracking, to optimise the signal entering the recording chain; during mixing, to balance and carve space between elements; and during mastering, to shape the final stereo mix for playback consistency. In modern in-the-box production, all three stages typically occur in a DAW, often with different EQ plugins selected for different purposes. A producer might use a vintage console emulation (Neve 1073, SSL G-Channel) for its harmonic character on individual channels, a clean transparent parametric (FabFilter Pro-Q, DMG Equilibrium) for surgical correction, and a gentle mastering EQ (Maselec MEA-2 emulation, Dangerous Music Bax EQ emulation) on the two-bus.

The most universally applied EQ technique in mixing is the high-pass filter (HPF) sweep: applying a 24 dB/octave HPF to every channel that does not need sub-bass content, rolling off from around 80 Hz to as high as 200 Hz depending on the instrument. This single move dramatically cleans up the low end of a mix, reducing masking on the kick drum and bass, and creating headroom that translates to perceived loudness. In Ableton Live, the EQ Eight offers dedicated HPF and LPF modes with adjustable slope, and producers typically insert it first in the effect chain before any dynamic processing. In Logic Pro, the Channel EQ with its frequency spectrum analyser makes the HPF placement visually intuitive. In FL Studio, the Parametric EQ 2 provides similar functionality, and many producers combine it with the Fruity Peak Controller for dynamic modulation of EQ bands — a creative technique that turns a static EQ into a quasi-dynamic processor.

The subtractive-first workflow is the standard professional approach: identify and remove problematic frequencies before adding any boosts. This is done by temporarily boosting a narrow-Q bell filter (Q of 8–12) and sweeping it across the spectrum while listening for the frequency where the problem — boxiness, harshness, muddiness — becomes most pronounced. Once identified, the boost is converted to a cut of appropriate depth. The advantage of this technique over visual spectrum-analyser-driven EQ is that it trains frequency identification by ear, developing the perceptual skill that ultimately determines mix quality. In Pro Tools, the seven-band EQ3 bundled with the DAW is sufficient for this diagnostic work, though most professional sessions use third-party plugins such as the UAD Neve 1073 or Sonnox Oxford EQ for their superior analogue modelling.

On the mix bus and during mastering, EQ moves become vastly more conservative — typically ±1 to ±3 dB per band rather than the ±6 to ±15 dB moves that may appear on individual channels. A common mastering approach is a gentle low shelf boost at 50–80 Hz (+1 to +2 dB) to add weight, combined with a high shelf boost at 12–16 kHz (+0.5 to +2 dB) to add air and openness — a curve informally known as a 'smile EQ.' Mid-side (M/S) EQ — processing the mono (mid) and stereo (side) components of a mix independently — is standard mastering practice, allowing engineers to tighten the low end (which is kept in the mid channel for mono compatibility) while adding width and air to the stereo image through high-frequency boosts in the side channel. The FabFilter Pro-Q 3 and the iZotope Ozone EQ both offer full M/S operation with per-band switching, making this technique accessible in any DAW.

07 In the Wild

On Michael Jackson's 'Thriller' (1982, produced by Quincy Jones, engineered by Bruce Swedien), the drum sound exemplifies corrective and creative EQ working in tandem. Swedien's famous 'Acusonic Recording Process' involved multi-miking with tight EQ decisions on each source. The kick drum features a narrow cut in the 250–400 Hz region to eliminate box-like room resonance, paired with a gentle boost around 60–80 Hz for weight and a click boost at 3–4 kHz for attack definition — a three-move framework that has become the template for processed kick sounds in pop and R&B for over four decades. The clarity of each element in that mix, despite the density of the arrangement, is a direct consequence of disciplined EQ carving throughout the session.

Daft Punk's 'Get Lucky' (2013, produced by Daft Punk and Nile Rodgers, mixed by Mick Guzauski) demonstrates masterful EQ in a funk-influenced pop context. Nile Rodgers' rhythm guitar — recorded through a clean amplifier with a Stratocaster — occupies the classic 'guitar pocket' between the bass and the kick, with a high-pass filter at approximately 100 Hz clearing the low end for Chic bassist Nathan East's playing, and a presence boost around 3–4 kHz giving the strumming pattern a crisp, cutting quality that defines the groove. The vocal arrangement by Pharrell Williams features a broad, gentle high-shelf boost beginning around 10 kHz — sometimes described as 'air EQ' — that gives the voice a luminous, effortless quality consistent throughout the mix. The song is a study in restraint: every EQ move serves the ensemble rather than the individual part.

Billie Eilish's 'bad guy' (2019, produced by Finneas O'Connell) is a modern case study in deliberate lo-fi EQ as creative identity. The bass line, one of the most recognisable in contemporary pop, is characterised by a severe high-frequency roll-off — a low-pass filter placed somewhere around 200–400 Hz — creating a blunted, almost subwoofer-exclusive presence. The vocal, conversely, is EQ'd with a strong presence boost in the 2–4 kHz range while sitting unusually close to the listener (a result of minimal reverb and a very dry, intimate frequency balance). The contrast between the ultra-dark bass and the bright, intimate vocal is entirely an EQ-constructed effect, demonstrating that creative EQ decisions can define the entire sonic identity of a production and become inseparable from its artistic character.

08 Types & Variants

Parametric EQ

The dominant format in professional studio work. A parametric EQ provides fully adjustable frequency, gain, and Q (bandwidth) on each band, along with selectable filter types (bell, shelf, high-pass, low-pass). With three to twelve or more bands, it is capable of both broad tonal shaping and surgical correction in the same instance. Modern software parametric EQs — the FabFilter Pro-Q 3, DMG Equilibrium, Sonnox Oxford EQ, and the stock EQ plugins in Logic Pro, Ableton Live, and Pro Tools — additionally offer real-time spectrum analysis, allowing visual confirmation of frequency content alongside critical listening. The parametric EQ is the default choice for channel-strip processing in any genre or context.

Graphic EQ

A fixed-frequency design in which a bank of band-pass filters — centred at standardised ISO frequencies in octave (10-band) or one-third-octave (31-band) increments — is controlled by individual vertical faders. The fader positions form a visual graph of the EQ curve, hence the name. Graphic EQs excel at room correction in live sound (identifying and cutting feedback frequencies rapidly) and in mastering contexts where a rough spectral matching is needed. Their limitation is fixed Q: because the bandwidth of each band is set, you cannot apply a surgical narrow cut without affecting adjacent bands. In mixing, graphic EQs have largely been supplanted by parametric EQs, though they remain in use as mix-bus tonal shapers in some workflows.

Semi-Parametric EQ

A format common in analogue console channel strips, exemplified by the Neve 1073 and SSL 4000 series. Semi-parametric EQs offer sweepable frequency on each band but a fixed or switchable Q rather than a continuously variable one. The SSL channel EQ's mid bands, for example, offer a choice between 'broadband' and 'narrowband' Q modes. This approach trades flexibility for speed — in a large recording session with thirty or more channels, semi-parametric controls can be set quickly by ear without the additional cognitive load of Q adjustment. The fixed Q values are typically chosen to be musically useful, and the resulting curves have a characteristic sound associated with the hardware.

Dynamic EQ

A hybrid of EQ and compression in which the gain of a specific EQ band changes in response to the signal level at that frequency. When the content in the target frequency range exceeds a set threshold, the band cuts (or boosts) by a gain determined by a ratio and time constants (attack and release). Unlike a static EQ cut, a dynamic cut is transparent when the problem frequency is not present and only acts when needed — making it ideal for intermittent resonances, vocal harshness that appears only on certain consonants, and sibilance control. The FabFilter Pro-Q 3 allows any band to be converted to dynamic operation; dedicated dynamic EQ plugins include the Waves F6 and the built-in dynamic mode in iZotope Neutron.

Linear-Phase EQ

A digital EQ implementation using FIR (Finite Impulse Response) filters that produce the same amplitude response as their minimum-phase counterparts but with zero phase shift at all frequencies. The trade-off is added latency (the FIR filter's delay, which grows with the length of the impulse response) and pre-ringing artefacts at steep slopes near transients. Linear-phase EQ is the preferred choice on stereo bus and mastering contexts — where preserving the phase relationship between all mix elements is critical — and when using M/S processing. It is generally avoided on individual tracks with sharp transients, where pre-ringing becomes audible.

Analogue Hardware EQ

Despite the proliferation of software emulations, analogue hardware EQs remain in active use in high-end studios. The Pultec EQP-1A (passive inductor design), Neve 1073 (transformer-coupled, Class A), API 550B (proportional Q, stepped frequencies), and SSL G-Series channel EQ each have distinct sonic signatures driven by their component tolerances, transformer characteristics, and circuit topologies. Hardware EQs introduce subtle harmonic distortion, phase behaviour, and noise floors that software emulations replicate with varying degrees of accuracy. For many engineers, the decision to use hardware versus software is as much about workflow and tactile control as it is about sonic difference, though on critical sources — lead vocals, main instruments — the character of well-maintained hardware EQ remains a point of distinction in elite-level recording.

09 Common Mistakes

1. Boosting to fix problems that require cutting. A common beginner error is reaching for a boost when a sound feels like it is missing something, without first identifying whether the perceived absence is caused by excess energy at a competing frequency masking the desired content. A bass guitar that 'lacks warmth' may not need a boost at 100 Hz; it may need a cut at 300–400 Hz to remove the muddiness that is obscuring its fundamental. Always attempt a corrective cut before adding energy. The cumulative effect of boosting multiple channels simultaneously raises the overall level of the mix, reduces headroom, and can push downstream processors into undesirable behaviour.

2. Ignoring phase interactions between EQ'd signals. When two signals that share frequency content are both processed with minimum-phase EQs, their combined phase relationships at shared frequencies may create constructive or destructive interference. This is particularly relevant when EQ'ing layered sounds (multiple microphones on a single drum, doubled guitars, parallel compression returns). The practical consequence can be a thinning or hollowing of the combined sound that is puzzling when each element sounds correct in isolation. A/B the layered sum with and without EQ to check for phase-induced thinning; mid-side processing or linear-phase EQ may resolve the issue.

3. Over-reliance on spectrum analyser visuals. While a spectrum analyser is a valuable diagnostic tool, mixing by eye rather than by ear produces EQ decisions calibrated to a visual target rather than a perceptual one. The equal-loudness contours of human hearing (Fletcher-Munson curves) mean that frequencies in the 2–4 kHz range are perceived as louder relative to their measured amplitude than frequencies in the sub-bass or extreme high end. A mix that looks spectrally 'balanced' on an analyser may sound mid-heavy and harsh. Develop the habit of closing your eyes, engaging the sweep-and-cut technique, and making decisions based on what you hear rather than what the display shows.

4. Applying the same HPF cutoff frequency indiscriminately. The common advice to 'high-pass everything' is valid in principle but frequently misapplied. Rolling off a piano at 200 Hz when it is playing the lowest notes of a bass-register arrangement removes fundamental content and makes the piano sound thin. Rolling off an acoustic guitar at 50 Hz rather than 100 Hz leaves unnecessary low-end content that accumulates with other instrument tracks and muddies the mix. The HPF cutoff should be set individually for each instrument, informed by the instrument's lowest useful fundamental frequency and the arrangement context — how much low-end contribution that instrument should have relative to the bass and kick.

5. EQ'ing in solo rather than in context. This is arguably the most consequential mistake in mixing. A sound that has been EQ'd in isolation often sounds impressive — full, detailed, and well-balanced — but fails completely when played back in the full mix. Frequencies that sound absent in solo may be adequately represented by other instruments in the arrangement; frequencies that sound natural in solo may be duplicated across several channels, causing a buildup that is only perceptible in context. Always make final EQ decisions with the full mix playing. Solo is a diagnostic tool for identifying specific problems, not a context for decision-making.

Frequently Asked Questions

A parametric EQ gives you continuous control over three parameters per band — frequency, gain, and Q (bandwidth) — making it the most flexible and precise EQ format available. A graphic EQ uses a fixed bank of filters at standardised ISO frequencies, each with a dedicated fader; you can boost or cut each band but cannot adjust its centre frequency or bandwidth. Parametric EQs are standard in mixing and mastering because they allow surgical precision and broad tonal shaping in the same instance. Graphic EQs are more common in live sound reinforcement, where rapid visual feedback and speed of operation outweigh the need for complete flexibility.
Both orders are valid and produce meaningfully different results, which is why many professional engineers use EQ both before and after compression on the same channel. EQ before compression means the compressor responds to the EQ'd frequency content — cutting a build-up at 300 Hz before compression prevents that frequency from triggering excessive gain reduction. EQ after compression means you are shaping a dynamically controlled signal, which can be more predictable and musical. A common professional workflow is to use a corrective EQ (cuts only) before the compressor to remove problems, then a creative or tonal EQ after compression to shape the final character. Neither approach is universally correct — experiment with both and trust your ears.
Q (quality factor) determines the bandwidth of an EQ band — specifically, how many frequencies on either side of the centre frequency are affected. A low Q (0.5–1.0) creates a broad, gentle curve affecting two or more octaves, appropriate for broad tonal shaping like adding warmth or air. A high Q (4–10+) creates a narrow peak or dip affecting a very small frequency range, appropriate for removing specific resonances, hum, or ring. In practice, start with a moderate Q around 1.0–2.0 for most tonal moves. If you are hunting for a specific problem frequency using the sweep-and-cut technique, boost the Q to 8–12 temporarily to isolate the problem, then reduce the Q slightly when you apply the cut to avoid sounding unnatural. Wide cuts tend to sound more musical than narrow ones at equivalent depth.
Mid-side EQ processes the mono component (the Mid — content that is identical in both the left and right channels) and the stereo component (the Side — content that differs between left and right) independently. By decoding a stereo signal into M and S components, applying separate EQ to each, and re-encoding back to stereo, you can control the frequency content of the centre and edges of the stereo image separately. Common mastering applications include cutting low frequencies in the Side channel to tighten the low end and improve mono compatibility (bass energy is kept in the Mid), and boosting high frequencies in the Side channel to add stereo width and air without affecting the mono balance. M/S EQ is available in the FabFilter Pro-Q 3, iZotope Ozone, and several other professional mastering tools. It is most useful at the mix bus and mastering stage; on individual mono tracks it has no effect on stereo image width.
Dynamic EQ applies compression or expansion to a specific EQ band — the gain of the band changes only when the signal level at that frequency exceeds a set threshold. A de-esser is a specialised dynamic processor that targets a narrow sibilance frequency range (typically 5–10 kHz on vocals) with a fast attack and release, effectively functioning as a highly focused dynamic EQ. A multiband compressor divides the entire spectrum into several broad bands and applies independent compression to each, which can alter the tonal balance significantly as levels change — a form of dynamic EQ applied at a broader scale. Dynamic EQ is generally more transparent and precise than multiband compression for correcting specific frequency problems, while multiband compression is better suited to heavy parallel processing and mastering scenarios where overall spectral balance control is the goal.
There is no absolute limit — a 20 dB notch cut to remove an 60 Hz hum tone is entirely valid, as is a 12 dB shelf boost on a kick drum sample being reshaped for a specific production aesthetic. However, as a practical guideline: cuts beyond 8–10 dB on a broad Q suggest the source has a fundamental problem that would be better addressed at the recording stage or with a different sample. Boosts beyond 6 dB on a parametric band introduce increasing amounts of phase shift and can start to sound unnatural, particularly on acoustically recorded instruments. On the mix bus and during mastering, moves beyond 3–4 dB per band are unusual and typically indicate a mix problem that should be solved by returning to individual channels. The goal is always the minimum EQ necessary to achieve the desired result.
Linear-phase EQ uses FIR (Finite Impulse Response) filters that alter amplitude without introducing any frequency-dependent phase shift — every frequency in the signal is delayed by exactly the same amount (the plugin's latency), preserving the phase relationships between all spectral components. This matters most in contexts where phase coherence across the spectrum is critical: stereo bus and mastering processing (where minimum-phase EQ can alter the perceived stereo image), M/S EQ (where phase relationships define the stereo/mono balance), and when making steep corrective cuts on a mix bus. The trade-offs are added latency (which most DAWs compensate for automatically) and pre-ringing — a faint echo-like artefact appearing just before sharp transients when steep filters are used. For this reason, linear-phase EQ is generally inappropriate on individual tracks with prominent transients such as drums or percussion, where the pre-ringing becomes audible. On melodic instruments and mix buses with gentle filter slopes, it is often the better choice.
Mastering EQ operates on a stereo mix rather than individual elements, which means every adjustment simultaneously affects all instruments in the mix. This requires a fundamentally different calibration of subtlety: where a mixing engineer might cut 6–8 dB at 300 Hz on a snare drum, a mastering engineer cutting 1 dB at 300 Hz on the mix bus is making a move of equivalent perceptual significance. Mastering EQ moves are typically very broad (low Q, often shelf-based) to shape the overall tonal balance without disturbing the relative levels of individual instruments. Mastering engineers also make extensive use of M/S EQ for low-end tightening and stereo width enhancement, and often apply EQ in two stages — a corrective EQ to address balance problems inherited from the mix, and a creative or 'enhancement' EQ to add air, weight, or presence appropriate to the target format and playback system. The reference tool in professional mastering is the calibrated monitoring system — not a spectrum analyser — and EQ decisions are made by comparing the processed output against commercial references in the same genre.
