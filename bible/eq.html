EQ

EQ is a signal processor that adjusts the amplitude of specific frequency ranges within an audio signal. It is the foundational mixing tool for shaping tone, removing problems, and carving space between instruments.

01 Definition

Every mix decision you make is an EQ decision — even the ones you think have nothing to do with frequency.

Equalization — universally abbreviated as EQ — is the process of altering the frequency balance of an audio signal by independently boosting or attenuating specific bands of the frequency spectrum. The term derives from the original telephony application: correcting, or "equalizing," the frequency response of transmission lines that rolled off high frequencies over long distances. In modern music production, the goal is rarely correction alone. EQ is simultaneously a surgical tool, a tonal sculptor, and a competitive weapon — the difference between a mix that translates on every system and one that sounds acceptable only in the room where it was made.

Every sound source occupies a region of the audible spectrum (roughly 20 Hz to 20 kHz), and every instrument radiates energy across a surprisingly wide range of that spectrum, not just at its fundamental pitch. A kick drum at 60 Hz also produces beater transient content at 3–5 kHz. A vocal centered around 200–3,000 Hz still contains sibilance at 6–10 kHz and room reflections down to 80 Hz. EQ allows the engineer to treat each frequency region independently, either emphasizing what makes a sound recognizable and pleasing, or suppressing what makes it muddy, harsh, or collision-prone in the context of a full arrangement. Without EQ, every element of a mix would fight for the same spectral real estate simultaneously.

The mechanism of equalization is rooted in filter design. A filter is a circuit — or its digital equivalent — that passes energy in a defined frequency region while reducing energy outside it. Bell curves (peaking filters), shelving filters, high-pass and low-pass filters, and notch filters each have distinct shapes in the frequency domain, and their combination on a single channel strip constitutes a parametric or semi-parametric equalizer. The Q parameter (quality factor) controls the bandwidth of a bell filter, determining whether the effect is broad and musical or narrow and surgical. These are not aesthetic abstractions — they are engineering specifications with measurable consequences on the phase response of the signal.

Misuse of EQ is the single most common cause of amateur-sounding mixes. Excessive boosting at muddy frequencies (200–400 Hz) produces a boxiness that no amount of reverb or compression can hide. Over-EQing in the high-mids (3–6 kHz) generates listener fatigue within minutes. Failing to high-pass tracks that have no useful low-frequency content — room noise, guitar bleed, vocal mic rumble — cumulatively raises the noise floor and turns the low end into an undifferentiated wall of energy. The great engineers — Bob Ludwig, Chris Lord-Alge, Tom Elmhirst — use EQ as a subtractive tool first and an additive tool second, a discipline that newer producers who learned on digital workstations with unlimited headroom tend to invert.

EQ also has a phase cost. Every filter introduces phase shift at or near its cutoff/center frequency — minimum-phase designs shift phase in a frequency-dependent way, while linear-phase designs add uniform latency to maintain phase coherence at the expense of pre-ringing artifacts. This trade-off is not academic: on a snare drum, a minimum-phase boost at 200 Hz will slightly smear the transient in a way that linear-phase EQ avoids, but linear-phase EQ on a heavily processed signal chain can introduce audible pre-ringing on sharp transients. Knowing which type to use — and when — is a marker of technical maturity. On mix buses and mastering chains, linear-phase EQ is often preferred; on individual tracks with sharp transient content, minimum-phase tends to sound more natural and punchy.

02 How It Works

At its core, an EQ is a bank of filters operating in parallel on the same audio signal. Each filter is defined by three parameters: center frequency (the Hz value at which the filter operates), gain (how many dB of boost or cut to apply), and Q or bandwidth (how wide or narrow the affected region is). In a parametric EQ, all three parameters are fully adjustable. In a graphic EQ, the center frequencies and bandwidths are fixed, and only gain is variable per band. The output of all bands is summed, producing a composite frequency response curve that represents the combined effect of all active filters on the signal.

The physics underlying EQ filters are rooted in analog circuit design — specifically, resonant RLC (resistor-inductor-capacitor) circuits whose impedance varies with frequency. A series RLC circuit will attenuate frequencies away from its resonant peak, forming a bandpass characteristic. Early hardware equalizers like the Pultec EQP-1A exploited the interaction of overlapping boost and cut circuits to produce a characteristic low-frequency "lift" that no single filter topology could achieve independently. Digital EQ implementations use mathematical equivalents (biquad IIR filters for minimum-phase, FFT-based convolution for linear-phase) that can precisely model analog behavior or extend beyond the limitations of physical components — allowing, for example, a Q value of 0.1 that would be impractical to build in hardware.

Phase shift is inseparable from filtering. The Hilbert transform relationship between a filter's magnitude response and its phase response means that any minimum-phase EQ producing a gain change at a given frequency also rotates the phase of that frequency relative to adjacent ones. For most musical material, this is inaudible in isolation but cumulative across a signal chain with many EQ stages. This is why experienced engineers are conservative with the number of active EQ bands, prefer cuts over boosts (cuts require less gain change to achieve the same perceptual result, and therefore impose less phase rotation), and why the concept of "EQ before or after compression" matters — the order of operations changes the phase and dynamic interaction between the two processors.

Understanding phase interaction also informs the classic debate of EQ placement in a signal chain. Placing a high-pass filter before a compressor ensures that low-frequency energy — which would otherwise trigger the compressor's gain reduction on every kick or bass transient — is removed before the detector circuit sees it. This is not a rule but a principle: knowing what the filter does to the signal that feeds the next processor is what separates intentional mixing from accidental mixing.

03 The Parameters

Every eq — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these completely and you can work with any implementation.

FREQUENCY
Center or cutoff frequency — the Hz target of the filter

Frequency sets where in the spectrum the filter operates. For a bell filter, it is the center of the boost or cut. For a high-pass or low-pass filter, it is the -3 dB cutoff point. Precision matters enormously: a mud cut at 280 Hz affects a different problem than one at 400 Hz, even if both feel like 'the boxy range.' Start broad, sweep to identify the offending frequency by ear, then set precisely.

GAIN
Amount of boost or cut in decibels

Gain determines how many dB the filter adds or removes at the target frequency. On most parametric EQs the range is ±18 to ±24 dB. Professional practice favors cuts over boosts — a -4 dB cut at a problematic frequency is audibly equivalent to a +4 dB boost with far less phase and noise penalty. Boosts beyond +6 dB are generally a signal that the source recording itself needs attention.

Q (BANDWIDTH)
Filter width — how wide or narrow the affected frequency range is

Q is the ratio of center frequency to bandwidth (Q = f₀ / BW). High Q values (6–20) produce narrow, surgical cuts useful for notching resonances or removing feedback. Low Q values (0.5–1.5) produce broad, musical curves suitable for tonal shaping. Critically, narrow boosts (high Q) tend to sound artificial and ringy because they reinforce a single harmonic; narrow cuts, by contrast, are transparent and effective for problem-solving.

FILTER TYPE
The shape and behavior of the filter curve

Common types include: Bell (peaking) — symmetrical boost or cut centered on the frequency; Low Shelf / High Shelf — boost or cut all frequencies below or above a defined point; High-Pass Filter (HPF) — attenuates frequencies below the cutoff, typically at 6, 12, or 24 dB/octave slopes; Low-Pass Filter (LPF) — attenuates above the cutoff; Notch — an extreme-Q bell cut approaching infinite attenuation. Choosing the correct filter type is as important as setting the frequency correctly.

SLOPE / ORDER
Rate of attenuation beyond the cutoff point

Slope applies primarily to high-pass and low-pass filters and is expressed in dB per octave: 6 dB/oct (1st order), 12 dB/oct (2nd order), 24 dB/oct (4th order), 48 dB/oct (8th order). Gentle slopes (6–12 dB/oct) sound more natural and are common on program material. Steep slopes (24–48 dB/oct) are used for aggressive filtering — removing kick drum low-end from a snare channel, for example. Steeper slopes introduce more phase shift.

PHASE MODE
Minimum-phase vs. linear-phase processing

Minimum-phase mode (the default in most EQs) processes in real time with no latency, introduces frequency-dependent phase shift, and tends to sound more analog and punchy — preferred on transient-heavy individual tracks. Linear-phase mode preserves phase relationships at the cost of added latency and potential pre-ringing on sharp transients — preferred on stereo buses, parallel processing, and mastering. FabFilter Pro-Q 3 allows per-band selection of phase mode, which is unusually powerful.

05 History & Origin

The history of equalization in audio begins with telephony, not music. In the 1920s, Bell Labs engineers were developing compensation networks to counteract the frequency-dependent signal loss across long-distance copper telephone lines. The goal was literal equalization: making the output spectrum equal to the input spectrum after degradation. H.A. Wheeler and other Bell Labs researchers formalized the filter topologies that would remain foundational to all subsequent EQ design. The transition from telephony to broadcasting brought these tools into radio transmission chains, and by the late 1930s, broadcast engineers were using passive equalization networks to shape program material for the first time in an aesthetic rather than purely corrective context.

The first equalizer purpose-built for music recording is generally attributed to the Pultec EQP-1A, introduced by Pulse Techniques in 1951 and designed by Eugene Shenk and Ollie Summerland. The EQP-1A was a passive program equalizer with a tube makeup gain stage, operating at fixed frequency points (20, 30, 60, and 100 Hz in the low band; 3, 4, 5, 8, 10, 12, and 16 kHz in the high band). Its defining characteristic was the simultaneous boost-and-cut behavior at low frequencies: boosting and cutting the same low-frequency band simultaneously produced a resonant lift below the cut point and attenuation above it — a curve impossible to replicate with a single filter and one that gave bass instruments a weight and definition that engineers found musically compelling. The EQP-1A was used on countless recordings of the 1950s and 1960s, including sessions at Capitol Studios in Hollywood and Columbia's 30th Street Studio in New York.

The parametric equalizer — where center frequency, gain, and bandwidth are all continuously variable — was developed by George Massenburg, who presented the concept in a landmark 1972 AES paper and implemented it in his own console designs. Prior to this, most studio equalizers were semi-parametric (fixed Q) or purely graphic (fixed frequency and Q). Massenburg's parametric design gave engineers unprecedented surgical control and became the template for every subsequent high-end mixing console EQ, from the SSL 4000 series (introduced 1976) to the Neve 1073 and 1084 modules of the early 1970s, which used transformer-coupled inductor-based designs to produce their distinctly rich, slightly colored frequency curves. The SSL G-Series bus compressor and EQ combination became the defining sound of 1980s pop and rock production — present on Michael Jackson's Thriller, countless Def Leppard and Phil Collins records, and virtually every major commercial release of the decade.

The arrival of digital audio workstations in the 1990s democratized EQ access. Early digital EQs — including Digidesign's built-in EQ III for Pro Tools — were criticized for sounding cold and phasey compared to their analog counterparts, a perception grounded in real differences in filter implementation and coefficient precision. The plugin era changed everything. Waves' Q10 (1992) and later the Renaissance EQ introduced analog-modeled curves to the desktop. The definitive modern reference, FabFilter Pro-Q (first released 2009, with Pro-Q 3 arriving in 2018), introduced per-band phase mode selection, dynamic EQ bands, mid/side processing, and real-time spectrum analysis that fundamentally changed how producers interact with frequency. Today, dynamic EQ — which applies gain changes only when a signal exceeds a threshold at a specific frequency — has blurred the line between equalization and multiband compression, creating new categories of processor that the original Bell Labs engineers could not have anticipated.

06 How Producers Use It

In practice, professional mixing engineers apply EQ in two fundamentally different modes: corrective and creative. Corrective EQ addresses problems specific to the recording — the 180 Hz resonance in an acoustic guitar body, the 4.5 kHz harshness of a particular large-diaphragm condenser on a bright vocalist, the 60 Hz mains hum on a DI bass track. These are treated with high-Q cuts (notches), often identified by sweeping a narrow boosted bell filter slowly across the spectrum while the track plays in context, listening for the frequency where the problem is most prominent, then converting the boost to a cut. The key discipline is to always evaluate corrective cuts in the context of the full mix, not in solo — a frequency that sounds problematic in isolation may be masked by other elements and not require treatment at all.

Creative EQ shapes the tonal character of a sound independent of any recording problem. High-shelf boosts above 12 kHz add "air" and openness to vocals and acoustic instruments. Low-shelf boosts at 80–100 Hz add weight and foundation to piano, synth pads, and guitar. A broad bell boost at 200 Hz adds warmth and body to thin recordings. Mid-range scoops (typically 500 Hz to 1 kHz, Q of 0.7–1.5) create the hollowed-out, aggressive character common in rock guitar tones. These decisions are informed by genre convention, reference tracks, and the relationship between elements in the arrangement — a vocal EQ that sounds thin in isolation may be exactly right once the full instrumentation occupies the mid-range frequencies the vocal was previously filling.

Mid/side (M/S) EQ is an advanced technique that processes the center (mono) and side (stereo) components of a signal independently. On a stereo bus or master, cutting the low end of the side channel below 200 Hz tightens the low end and improves mono compatibility — low-frequency stereo information is generally incoherent and translates poorly to mono playback systems. Boosting the high-frequency shelf in the side channel widens the perceived stereo image without affecting mono integrity. On individual stereo instruments like pianos or drum overheads, mid/side EQ allows the engineer to control the tonal balance of the centered image (often the main attack and fundamental of the instrument) separately from the spatial, ambient information in the sides. FabFilter Pro-Q 3 and Fabfilter's dynamic EQ mode make M/S processing per-band straightforward; in Pro Tools, M/S processing requires either a dedicated M/S plugin or manual encoding/decoding using aux routing.

On the mix bus and in mastering, EQ moves are typically smaller in magnitude (rarely exceeding ±2–3 dB) but broader in Q, affecting larger spectral regions and the overall tonal balance of the program material. Bob Ludwig, Bernie Grundman, and Greg Calbi — among the most respected mastering engineers working today — frequently describe their mastering EQ decisions in terms of tenths of a decibel rather than whole numbers, a precision made possible by years of reference listening and the leverage that small adjustments have on a fully assembled mix. The standard mastering chain EQ placement is: corrective EQ first, then compression, then creative or corrective EQ again if needed — though this order is broken deliberately for specific tonal goals.

AbletonEQ Eight is the primary parametric EQ — eight fully parametric bands with HPF/LPF, bell, and shelf types. Enable the spectrum analyzer in the background for visual feedback. For surgical work, switch to Spectrum (separate device) alongside EQ Eight. The Adaptive Q option normalizes gain based on bandwidth — disable it if you need predictable narrow boosts.

The built-in spectrum display updates in real time — use peak hold to identify transient frequency content. For vintage-style coloration, Maximus (multiband) can approximate shelving behavior, but for surgical work, Parametric EQ 2 is the go-to.
Logic ProChannel EQ is Logic's primary workhorse — eight bands with an integrated spectrum analyzer and a clean linear-phase-adjacent design. For vintage color, the Vintage EQs collection (introduced Logic Pro X 10.5) includes faithful models of the Neve 1073, API 550, and SSL 4000 channel EQs. Use Channel EQ for corrective work and Vintage EQs for tonal character.

For professional work inside Pro Tools, most engineers use third-party plugins (FabFilter Pro-Q 3, Waves SSL E-Channel, or McDSP AE600) — the stock EQ is a functional starting point, not a destination.

Enable the frequency response display in the ReaEQ window and the real-time spectrum via the track's VU meter context menu. For Reaper users, the free Tokyo Dawn TDR Nova (dynamic EQ) is a highly recommended supplement.

07 In the Wild

Abstract knowledge becomes practical when you can hear it in music you know. These tracks demonstrate eq used intentionally, at specific moments, for specific purposes.

Dr. Dre feat. Snoop Dogg — "Nuthin' But a G Thang" (1992)
Intro and verse bass elements throughout · Produced by Dr. Dre

The bass on this track is a masterclass in low-frequency EQ carving. Dre and engineer Mel-Man used high-Q cuts in the 300–500 Hz range on the bass synth to remove boxiness, leaving the fundamental energy at 50–80 Hz intact while allowing the kick drum — tuned to approximately 55 Hz — to punch through without frequency collision. Listen on a subwoofer: the kick and bass occupy complementary frequency slots rather than competing for the same space. The vocal chain also shows deliberate HPF placement — the low end is completely absent, focusing all vocal energy from 100 Hz upward and preventing it from cluttering the bass-heavy instrumental.

Radiohead — "How to Disappear Completely" (2000)
String arrangement, beginning at 1:05 · Produced by Nigel Godrich

Nigel Godrich's EQ work on this track demonstrates the use of low-shelf cutting and high-pass filtering to create spectral space for the voice within a dense string arrangement. The strings have been high-passed aggressively — estimated at 200 Hz or higher on several sections — preventing them from accumulating low-mid energy that would bury Thom Yorke's vocal. The result is strings that feel present and lush in the mid and upper registers but transparent in the critical 150–300 Hz range where the vocal fundamental lives. This is subtractive EQ used architecturally, shaping entire sections of the arrangement rather than individual tracks.

Kendrick Lamar — "HUMBLE." (2017)
Snare and vocal relationship throughout · Produced by Mike WiLL Made-It

Mike WiLL Made-It's production on this track uses extremely deliberate frequency separation between the snare (dominant energy at 200 Hz and 4–5 kHz) and Kendrick's vocal (fundamentals at 150–300 Hz, presence at 2–4 kHz). A narrow cut around 250 Hz on the snare prevents it from occupying the same range as the vocal fundamental, while the snare's crack at 4–5 kHz sits just above the vocal's primary presence peak. The kick drum sits at approximately 55–65 Hz with almost no information above 150 Hz — leaving the entire mid-range to the vocal and snare. This level of EQ architecture across a production is what creates translation: the mix sounds coherent on phone speakers, earbuds, and club systems simultaneously.

Fleetwood Mac — "The Chain" (1977)
Bass guitar outro from 3:15 · Produced by Fleetwood Mac / Ken Caillat / Richard Dashut

John McVie's bass in the famous outro section is a textbook example of analog EQ shaping for definition in the low-mid range. The bass has been boosted at approximately 800 Hz to 1 kHz with a fairly broad Q — unusual for a bass instrument — which places the string attack and note definition in a frequency range where it cuts through the guitars and drums without simply being turned up in level. The low fundamental (60–100 Hz) is present but not exaggerated, giving the bass groove and punch rather than just weight. This approach — boosting definition frequencies rather than fundamental frequencies on bass — is one of the foundational techniques in rock mixing.

08 Types & Variants

Parametric EQ
Neve 1073 · API 550A · SSL 4000 G Channel

The most flexible and widely used EQ type — each band offers independent control of frequency, gain, and Q. The Neve 1073's transformer-coupled inductor design imparts a characteristic warmth and harmonic density that is emulated by dozens of plugins; the API 550A uses a proportional Q design where bandwidth automatically narrows as gain increases. Parametric EQs are the standard tool for both corrective and creative mixing tasks.

Graphic EQ
Klark Teknik DN360 · dbx 2231 · API 560

Fixed center frequencies with fixed Q values — only gain is variable per band. Bands are typically spaced one octave or one-third octave apart, with individual slider controls. Graphic EQs are less flexible than parametric designs but offer an instant visual representation of the frequency curve shape. They remain standard in live sound and monitor calibration contexts where speed of adjustment matters more than surgical precision.

Program (Passive) EQ
Pultec EQP-1A · Pultec MEQ-5 · Manley Massive Passive

Passive designs using inductor-capacitor networks with a tube or solid-state makeup gain stage. The passive circuit's interaction with the makeup amplifier produces frequency-dependent harmonic saturation and gentle phase curves that are considered uniquely musical. The Pultec EQP-1A's simultaneous boost-and-cut technique for low frequencies — boosting and cutting the same band to produce a resonant hump below the cut frequency — remains one of the most widely copied EQ moves in professional audio.

Dynamic EQ
Neve 8803 (semi-dynamic) · SPL PQ · (primarily a plugin-era concept)

A dynamic EQ applies gain changes at a specific frequency only when the signal at that frequency exceeds a defined threshold — functioning as a frequency-specific compressor. This makes it ideal for controlling resonances that appear only on certain notes (e.g., a 500 Hz honk that emerges only on loud vocal phrases) or for controlling sibilance transparently without a dedicated de-esser. FabFilter Pro-Q 3's dynamic band feature and Waves F6 are the primary software implementations.

Linear-Phase EQ
(Primarily a digital concept) · Weiss EQ1-LP · TC Electronic System 6000

Achieves the same magnitude response as a minimum-phase EQ but with uniform phase delay across all frequencies, preserving phase coherence between frequency bands. The trade-off is added latency (unsuitable for real-time monitoring) and potential pre-ringing on sharp transients — a subtle smearing before a transient attack that can reduce perceived punch. Best suited for mastering, bus processing, and any context where phase coherence across a stereo field is more important than absolute transient fidelity.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    Boosting without a corresponding cut elsewhere
    Every boost in a dense mix raises the overall spectral energy in that region — including from other instruments that were not the target of the boost. If you boost 3 kHz on a vocal, you also raise 3 kHz on any bleed present in the mic. The professional reflex is to ask: before I boost this frequency on this track, is there another track that could be cut in the same region to achieve the same perceptual result with less total energy added to the mix? Subtractive EQ first, additive EQ only when subtractive approaches are exhausted.
  • ✕
    EQing in solo instead of in context
    A track EQ'd to sound balanced in solo will almost always sound wrong in the mix — because the mix context changes the perceived frequency balance entirely through masking, summation, and psychoacoustic interaction. High-passing a guitar at 120 Hz sounds thin and hollow in solo; in a mix with a kick, bass, and other instruments, that same guitar sits clearly without cluttering the low end. Always make EQ decisions with the full mix playing, using solo only to identify a specific problem frequency.
  • ✕
    Over-high-passing everything
    High-pass filters are one of the most useful tools in mixing, but indiscriminate aggressive high-passing — setting 200 Hz HPFs on guitars, keyboards, and background vocals simultaneously — removes the harmonic foundation of the mix and produces a thin, cold, radio-unfriendly sound. Instrument fundamentals and low harmonics create warmth and body. Set HPF cutoffs as low as the track's lowest useful content — not as a default habit regardless of source material.
  • ✕
    Narrow high-Q boosts for tonal shaping
    Narrow bell boosts (Q above 4–5) sound artificial and ringy when used for general tone shaping because they reinforce a single harmonic in an unnaturally focused way that the ear perceives as coloration rather than presence. Narrow boosts are appropriate for dramatic effect in electronic music or for compensating a very specific recording deficiency, but for natural-sounding tonal enhancement, keep Q values below 2. The only common exception is a narrow boost used to find a frequency (sweeping technique) — which is immediately converted to a cut once the target is identified.
  • ✕
    Ignoring the low end on non-bass instruments
    The most common cause of muddy, unfocused low ends in amateur mixes is the accumulated low-frequency content of non-bass instruments — guitars, pianos, synth pads, room mics, and overheads — that individually seem minor but sum to a significant low-frequency wall. High-pass filtering these sources at conservative but appropriate frequencies (guitars at 80–100 Hz, piano at 60–80 Hz, room mics at 100–150 Hz) cleans the low end dramatically without audibly thinning the individual sounds. Use a spectrum analyzer on the mix bus to visualize the contribution of each track to the cumulative low-frequency energy.
  • ✕
    Treating EQ as a substitute for gain staging
    An EQ that is overdriving its input — receiving a signal that is too hot — will produce intermodulation distortion in the filtered bands that cannot be removed with further EQ. Similarly, using large boosts (+10 dB or more) to compensate for a thin-sounding source is almost always less effective than addressing the source: repositioning a microphone, changing the acoustic environment, or choosing a different sample or synth patch. EQ can adjust the balance of what is already there; it cannot add frequency content that was never captured.

11 Frequently Asked Questions

A parametric EQ allows independent control of frequency, gain, and bandwidth (Q) for each band — giving precise, surgical control over the exact shape of the frequency curve. A graphic EQ uses fixed center frequencies and fixed bandwidths, with only gain adjustable per band via individual faders. Parametric EQs are standard in recording and mixing; graphic EQs are common in live sound and room tuning where visual feedback from the fader positions matters.
The order depends on the intent. EQ before compression means the compressor responds to the EQ'd signal — useful when you want to remove a bass frequency before the compressor so it doesn't trigger excessive gain reduction on low-end transients. EQ after compression means you shape the tonal balance of the already-compressed signal — useful for corrective tone shaping after compression has altered the frequency balance. Most engineers use both: a corrective EQ before the compressor to clean up the input, and a creative or corrective EQ after to refine the result.
As a general rule, cuts beyond -8 to -10 dB at a narrow frequency suggest a recording problem that should be addressed at source rather than post-processing. Boosts beyond +6 dB almost always introduce audible coloration and phase issues — if you need that much boost, a different microphone position, instrument, or sample is a more efficient solution. On a mastering chain, moves beyond ±3 dB are considered significant. These are guidelines, not absolute rules — extreme EQ as an effect is valid in creative contexts.
Q is the ratio of center frequency to bandwidth, numerically equal to f₀ / BW. Higher Q = narrower, more focused effect; lower Q = wider, more gradual curve. For finding and notching a resonance or problem frequency, use Q values of 4–10. For musical tonal shaping — warmth, presence, air — use Q values of 0.5–2. For corrective work on specific recordings, Q of 1.5–4 is common. Most mixing engineers default to Q values between 0.7 and 1.4 for broad, musical shaping moves.
Linear-phase EQ applies the same magnitude response as a standard (minimum-phase) EQ but delays all frequencies equally rather than introducing frequency-dependent phase shift. This preserves phase coherence at the cost of latency and potential pre-ringing on sharp transients. Use linear-phase EQ on stereo bus and mastering processing where phase integrity across the stereo field matters, and on parallel EQ processing where phase alignment with the dry signal is critical. Avoid it on individual tracks with sharp transients — the pre-ringing artifact reduces punch.
Mid/side (M/S) EQ encodes the stereo signal into its summed mono center (Mid) and stereo difference (Side) components, applies independent EQ to each, then decodes back to stereo. This allows frequency-specific width control: cutting the side channel's low end below 200 Hz tightens the bass in mono, while boosting the side channel's high shelf widens the stereo image. M/S EQ is standard practice in mastering and increasingly common on stereo drum buses and mix buses. FabFilter Pro-Q 3, Fabfilter's standard, supports per-band M/S switching.
Both tools apply gain reduction at specific frequency ranges in response to signal level, but their architectures differ. A dynamic EQ places a compressor on a specific EQ band — the band's gain moves only when the signal at that frequency exceeds a threshold. A multiband compressor splits the signal into frequency bands using crossover filters and compresses each band independently, affecting everything above a threshold. Dynamic EQ is more transparent and surgical for targeted frequency control (e.g., controlling a 500 Hz vocal honk); multiband compression is more appropriate for broad dynamic control across spectral regions. The distinction matters most in mastering.
Filter topology, coefficient precision, oversampling, and analog modeling determine how an EQ sounds beyond its magnitude response. Minimum-phase IIR filters introduce frequency-dependent phase shift that differs between implementations even at identical gain/frequency/Q settings. Analog-modeled EQs introduce transformer saturation, inductor non-linearity, and harmonic distortion that fundamentally alter the character of the signal. An SSL E-Channel EQ plugin set to +4 dB at 3 kHz sounds different from a Neve 1073 emulation at the same setting because the original hardware circuits were architecturally different — and competent plugin modeling captures that difference.
