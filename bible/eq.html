EQ

/ˌiːˈkjuː/

Quick Answer Definition How It Works Parameters Quick Reference History How To Use It In The Wild Types Mistakes Calculator Related Further Reading FAQ

EQ is a signal processor that boosts or cuts specific frequency ranges within an audio signal. It is used in mixing and mastering to shape tone, remove problem frequencies, and create space between elements in a mix.

01 Definition

Every mix decision you make is an EQ decision — even the ones you think have nothing to do with frequency.

Equalization — universally abbreviated as EQ — is the process of adjusting the amplitude of specific frequency bands within an audio signal. At its core, EQ is a filter network: a set of circuits or algorithms that selectively amplify or attenuate portions of the frequency spectrum, measured in hertz (Hz) and kilohertz (kHz), while leaving other portions relatively unchanged. The result is a reshaped frequency response — a deliberate redistribution of energy across the audible spectrum roughly spanning 20 Hz to 20 kHz. EQ is applied in virtually every stage of modern audio production, from tracking and mixing through to mastering and broadcast delivery, making it arguably the most fundamental and consequential tool in a producer's arsenal.

What distinguishes EQ from mere volume adjustment is its surgical specificity. Rather than making something louder or quieter across the board, EQ allows an engineer to remove the 200 Hz buildup muddying a snare, lift the 3 kHz presence range where a vocal cuts through dense instrumentation, roll off sub-bass below 30 Hz on a synth pad that would otherwise consume headroom without contributing audible energy, or gently shelve air frequencies above 10 kHz to add apparent sparkle to a dull room recording. The creative and corrective possibilities are essentially boundless. A well-executed EQ move can resolve a frequency conflict that no amount of automation, compression, or reverb adjustment could fix. A poorly executed one can hollow out an instrument's body, create piercing resonances, or collapse a mix's sense of depth and dimension.

Producers often conflate EQ with tone-shaping for individual instruments, but the discipline extends far beyond that. In the context of a full mix, EQ is the primary tool for spectral balance — the distribution of energy across the frequency spectrum so that no range is overcrowded and no range is starved. When a kick and a bass guitar compete in the 60–100 Hz range, the solution is almost always EQ: complementary cuts and boosts that carve distinct spectral spaces for each instrument. This concept of frequency masking — where one sound obscures another because they overlap in both frequency and time — is the central problem that EQ exists to solve. Understanding masking is what separates producers who use EQ reactively from those who use it architecturally.

The distinction between corrective and creative EQ is essential. Corrective EQ addresses problems: resonant frequencies from room acoustics, microphone proximity effect, the boxy buildup in the 300–500 Hz range common to untreated rooms, or the harsh transient crack of a poorly placed snare mic. Creative EQ, by contrast, is additive and intentional — boosting the 60 Hz weight of a kick for a particular genre's demands, shelving the highs of a guitar to push it backward in the stereo field, or scooping the midrange of a synth pad to let vocals sit forward without gain automation. The best engineers do both simultaneously, treating EQ not as a corrective afterthought but as a compositional tool applied with the same intentionality as arrangement or sound design.

Misuse of EQ is one of the most common technical failures in amateur productions. Broad, deep cuts create phase artifacts that smear transients and introduce comb filtering when combined with adjacent elements. Excessive high-frequency boosts reveal noise floors, amplify digital harshness, and cause ear fatigue. Boosting multiple instruments in the same frequency range without corresponding cuts elsewhere raises the overall spectral energy in that band, leading to a frequency buildup that demands more aggressive limiting at the mastering stage — a chain reaction that compresses dynamic range and flattens a mix. The governing principle most professional engineers operate by is this: cut to correct, boost to enhance — and even then, cut first.

02 How It Works

At the electronic level, an EQ is a network of filters — circuits designed to have a frequency-dependent gain response. In analog hardware, this is achieved using combinations of resistors, capacitors, and inductors (or, in later designs, operational amplifiers) that form resonant circuits tuned to specific frequencies. When a signal passes through a filter network tuned to, say, 1 kHz, the circuit attenuates or amplifies energy at that frequency while applying a mathematically predictable slope to the surrounding frequencies — the shape of which is described by the filter's Q (quality factor) and its order (slope steepness, expressed in dB per octave). In digital EQ plugins, these analog behaviors are modeled using digital filter algorithms — most commonly biquad IIR (Infinite Impulse Response) filters — that replicate the gain, phase, and frequency response of their analog counterparts with varying degrees of accuracy depending on the algorithm and the plugin's oversampling implementation.

Every EQ band operates on the same fundamental principle: it applies a gain offset (positive or negative, measured in dB) to a specific region of the frequency spectrum, defined by a center frequency (in Hz or kHz) and a bandwidth (described by Q). A high Q value produces a narrow, surgical notch or boost — useful for targeting a single resonant frequency without affecting neighbors. A low Q value produces a broad, gentle curve that affects a wide range of frequencies simultaneously — more musical sounding, appropriate for tonal shaping. Filter types further define behavior: a bell (or peaking) filter is symmetrical around its center frequency; a shelf filter boosts or cuts all frequencies above (high shelf) or below (low shelf) a set point; a high-pass filter (HPF) rolls off content below a cutoff frequency at a defined slope; and a low-pass filter (LPF) does the inverse above it. Parametric EQs give the engineer full control over frequency, gain, and Q for each band independently — the standard in professional mixing and mastering contexts.

Phase behavior is one of the most consequential and least-discussed aspects of EQ operation. Every minimum-phase EQ filter introduces phase shift as a byproduct of its amplitude response — frequencies near the filter's center frequency are delayed relative to those further away. In isolation this is generally inaudible, but on parallel tracks, across stereo pairs, or when multiple EQs are stacked on a single channel, accumulated phase shift can introduce comb filtering, smear transients, and reduce the perceived clarity and punch of a mix. Linear-phase EQs solve this by applying a uniform time delay to all frequencies — preserving phase relationships at the cost of pre-ringing artifacts and higher processing latency. Zero-latency minimum-phase designs remain preferred on individual tracks for their transient integrity; linear-phase designs are often reserved for mastering chains and mid/side processing where phase coherence across a stereo bus is paramount.

03 The Parameters

Every eq — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these completely and you can work with any implementation.

FREQUENCY
Center or cutoff frequency of the filter band

Measured in Hz or kHz, this sets the focal point of a filter's gain action. For bell filters, it defines the peak or trough of the curve. For high-pass and low-pass filters, it defines the -3 dB cutoff point where attenuation begins. Setting this parameter correctly requires both a trained ear and a spectrum analyzer — sweeping a boosted band slowly through a region while listening for the problem frequency is the standard technique for corrective work. Sub-bass sits 20–80 Hz; bass fundamentals 80–250 Hz; low midrange 250–500 Hz; midrange 500 Hz–2 kHz; upper mids 2–6 kHz; presence 3–8 kHz; brilliance 8–12 kHz; air 12–20 kHz.

GAIN
Amplitude boost or cut applied at the center frequency

Expressed in decibels (dB), gain determines how much the selected frequency range is amplified (positive values) or attenuated (negative values). In professional mixing practice, corrective cuts are frequently as deep as -6 to -12 dB to tame resonances, while musical boosts rarely exceed +4 to +6 dB on individual elements — beyond that, the boost tends to sound artificial and draws attention to itself. On mastering EQs, even 0.5–1 dB adjustments can have audible and significant impact across an entire mix. At extreme gain settings (+/- 15 dB or more), minimum-phase EQs introduce substantial phase rotation that accumulates across a mix chain and can cause audible smearing.

Q (BANDWIDTH)
Sharpness or width of the filter's frequency response curve

Q (quality factor) is the ratio of center frequency to bandwidth — a Q of 1.0 means the filter affects approximately one octave on either side of the center frequency. Higher Q values (2.0, 4.0, 8.0+) produce increasingly narrow, surgical curves suitable for notching out a single resonant frequency or targeting a specific instrument harmonic. Lower Q values (0.5–0.8) produce broad, gentle shelving-like curves that affect wide frequency ranges, which is preferable for tonal shaping and musical enhancement work. Abusing high-Q boosts — sharp peaks above +6 dB — creates whistle-like resonances that are immediately identifiable as artificial and are particularly fatiguing to listen to over time.

FILTER TYPE
The shape of the gain response curve — bell, shelf, HPF, LPF, notch

Filter type determines the fundamental topology of how gain is applied across frequency. Bell (peaking) filters are symmetrical around the center frequency and are the most versatile for mixing. High-pass filters (HPF) roll off all content below a cutoff at a specified slope (6, 12, 18, or 24 dB/octave) and are used on virtually every non-bass element in a mix. Low-shelf and high-shelf filters provide gentle, frequency-wide adjustments below or above a set point — the Pultec-style simultaneous boost-and-cut technique exploits shelf filter topology to achieve a resonant bass enhancement impossible with a standard bell filter. Notch filters are extreme-Q cuts used to surgically eliminate specific interference frequencies such as mains hum (50 Hz / 60 Hz) or feedback resonances.

SLOPE
Rate of attenuation for high-pass and low-pass filters

Slope is expressed in dB per octave (or equivalently, by filter order — each order = 6 dB/octave). A 6 dB/octave (1st order) HPF is very gentle, creating a gradual roll-off that leaves significant energy below the cutoff. A 24 dB/octave (4th order) HPF is aggressive, cutting sharply below the cutoff with minimal musical energy leaking through. In practice, 12 dB/octave (2nd order) is the most common general-purpose slope for mixing HPFs on non-bass elements, while 6 dB/octave is sometimes preferred on bass elements where preserving sub-harmonic energy is desirable. Steeper slopes on resonant filters can introduce audible ringing — particularly on oversampled digital EQs — which is why Butterworth filter designs (maximally flat response) are preferred for transparent high-pass work.

BAND ENABLE / BYPASS
Active monitoring of the effect each individual band contributes

The ability to solo or bypass individual EQ bands is among the most underused features in parametric EQs. Professional engineers frequently bypass the entire EQ after dialing in settings to confirm that every band they have engaged is improving the sound rather than simply changing it — a distinction that becomes difficult to hear when working inside the effect rather than comparing A/B. Gain compensation during bypass is critical: because boosts increase perceived loudness, an EQ with active boosts will always sound better than the bypassed signal at equal monitoring volume, which can lead engineers to mistake level increase for genuine sonic improvement. FabFilter Pro-Q 3 and iZotope Neutron include gain-matched bypass for this reason.

05 History & Origin

The history of equalization begins in telecommunications rather than music. The term itself originated in telephone engineering — the goal was to equalize the frequency response of long telephone lines that attenuated high frequencies more aggressively than low ones due to the capacitance of copper cable. Early passive equalizer networks, developed through the 1920s and 1930s at Bell Telephone Laboratories and by engineers including H.W. Bode and Harry Nyquist, were designed to flatten this response so that voice intelligibility was preserved over long-distance transmission. These passive networks were the direct ancestors of the passive equalizers that would later define the character of studio recording.

The first dedicated audio equalizer designed explicitly for music recording was the Western Electric 9A filter set, used in early broadcast and recording contexts through the 1930s and 1940s. The pivotal commercial breakthrough came in 1951 when Eugene Shenk and Ollie Summerland introduced the Pultec EQP-1A through Pulse Techniques, Inc. The EQP-1A used passive inductor-capacitor filter networks and a subsequent active makeup gain stage — the interaction of which produced the famous Pultec low-end boost technique, where simultaneously boosting and cutting the same low frequency created a resonant bass lift with a corresponding tightening effect that passive circuit topology made uniquely possible. The EQP-1A became a standard fixture on every major recording console through the 1960s and remains one of the most emulated EQ circuits in history, with accurate hardware reissues and digital models (UAD Pultec EQP-1A, Waves PuigTec EQP-1A) still in regular professional use.

The parametric equalizer — offering independent control of frequency, gain, and bandwidth — was developed by George Massenburg, who introduced the concept in a landmark 1972 AES paper. Massenburg's design freed engineers from the fixed-frequency limitations of graphic and semi-parametric equalizers, enabling the precise, interactive frequency control that modern mixing practice depends upon. Through the 1970s and 1980s, API, SSL, Neve, and other console manufacturers integrated increasingly sophisticated EQ sections into their channel strips. The Neve 1073 module (1970), with its three-band transformer-coupled EQ, became the defining reference for musical-sounding analog equalization — its particular harmonic character, rooted in the Marinair transformer and discrete transistor circuitry, is widely considered the benchmark against which all subsequent EQ designs are measured for musicality and depth.

The SSL 4000 series G-Bus equalizer, introduced in the early 1980s and heard on the majority of major label recordings from that decade through the mid-1990s, represented a different design philosophy — faster, more transient-accurate, with a distinctive high-frequency boost that contributed to the polished, aggressive sound characteristic of 1980s pop and rock production. With the transition to digital audio workstations in the 1990s, software EQ initially lagged behind analog hardware in sound quality due to limitations in early DSP algorithms. The introduction of UAD hardware accelerators and plugins like the Waves Q10 and eventually the FabFilter Pro-Q series (2008 onward) established that digital EQ could match and in many ways surpass analog hardware in precision and flexibility, while plugin models of classic hardware such as the UAD Neve 1073 and Pultec EQP-1A brought the character of vintage hardware to any production rig. Today, dynamic EQ — which applies gain changes proportionally to input level, functioning as a hybrid between parametric EQ and multiband compression — represents the current frontier of the tool's development, with implementations in iZotope Neutron, FabFilter Pro-Q 3, and TDR Nova among the most widely used in professional contexts.

06 How Producers Use It

On drums, EQ is used at both the individual element and bus levels to control the spectral relationships between closely recorded or sampled elements. A kick drum typically receives an HPF at 30–50 Hz to remove inaudible sub-bass energy and a boost at 60–80 Hz for weight, combined with a cut at 200–350 Hz to reduce boxiness and a boost at 3–5 kHz to bring out the beater attack. Snare EQ often involves a cut at 400–600 Hz to remove the wooden ring that builds up in dense arrangements, and a boost at 200 Hz for body or 5–8 kHz for crack. Hi-hats frequently receive a steep HPF at 300–500 Hz in dense mixes to prevent them from muddying the midrange. Overhead microphones — particularly in rock and live tracking contexts — are often high-passed at 100–150 Hz and have the buildup between 300–600 Hz reduced to prevent them washing out the snare and kick that are captured on dedicated close microphones.

On vocals, the EQ chain typically begins with an HPF at 80–120 Hz (higher for female vocals, lower for male, with the specific cutoff informed by the singer's fundamental range) to eliminate microphone handling noise, HVAC rumble, and proximity effect buildup. A broad cut at 300–500 Hz addresses the boxy quality common to cardioid condensers recorded in untreated rooms. The most critical boost on a lead vocal is usually in the 2.5–4 kHz presence range — the frequency band most responsible for vocal intelligibility and forward placement in a dense mix — applied with a moderate Q of 1.0–2.0 to avoid sounding nasal or brittle. Air frequencies above 10–12 kHz are frequently shelved up by 1–3 dB to add sparkle and openness. De-essing (a specialized narrow-band dynamic cut at 5–10 kHz) is often applied either as a dedicated plugin or using a dynamic EQ band targeting the sibilance range specific to the singer and microphone combination.

On bass instruments — electric bass guitar, 808 sub-bass, synthesizer bass — EQ decisions are governed by the relationship between the sub-bass (40–80 Hz), the fundamental note range (80–200 Hz), and the upper harmonic content (200–800 Hz) that determines how much the bass is felt versus heard on smaller speakers. In electronic music production, the sub-bass is often filtered sharply below 35–40 Hz and boosted around 55–70 Hz for maximum impact on subwoofer systems while preserving headroom. In rock and pop contexts, the upper harmonic range of a bass guitar at 700 Hz–1.5 kHz is often lifted to ensure the bass remains audible on laptop speakers and earbuds that cannot reproduce the fundamental frequencies. Sidechain-informed EQ — particularly the technique of applying a narrow dynamic cut to the bass in the exact frequency range where the kick drum's fundamental sits — is widely used in electronic music to ensure kick-bass clarity without static frequency conflicts.

On mix buses and master chains, EQ operates at a far more conservative scale than on individual elements. A 0.5–1 dB boost at 40–60 Hz using a warm analog EQ model can add perceived weight to an entire mix. A gentle high shelf of +1–2 dB above 12 kHz on a mastering EQ adds air and translates well across consumer playback systems. Mid/Side (M/S) EQ — processing the mid and side channels of a stereo bus independently — allows engineers to tighten the low end by cutting sub-bass from the side channel only (concentrating bass energy in the center where it belongs), widen the stereo field by boosting high frequencies in the side channel, or remove a resonance that exists only in the center of the mix without affecting the stereo spread. This technique, standard in mastering, is increasingly used by mixing engineers on stereo instrument buses such as drum overheads and room microphones.

AbletonEQ Eight is the stock parametric — eight bands, A/B comparison, and per-band cut/shelf/bell mode switching. Enable oversampling (2x or 4x) in the context menu for mastering work to reduce aliasing on high-frequency boosts. The frequency display is logarithmic and accurate; use Spectrum (also stock) feeding into EQ Eight for a real-time analysis view.
FL StudioParametric EQ 2 (fruity) is the native workhorse — seven bands, frequency spectrum overlay, and a per-band Q lock feature. For surgical notch work, enable the high-precision mode from the band context menu. Fruity PEQ 2 runs efficiently even at high band counts; stack two instances for more bands on dense vocal chains without significant CPU penalty.
Logic ProChannel EQ is a clean, transparent eight-band linear/minimum-phase selectable EQ — activate linear phase mode in the settings for mastering bus use. The Vintage EQ Collection (Vintage Console EQ, Vintage Graphic EQ, Vintage Tube EQ) models the Neve 1073, SSL G-Bus, and a tube EQ respectively and are genuinely usable on individual tracks where analog character is desirable. Smart EQ is AI-assisted but serves as a starting point only.
Pro ToolsEQ3 7-Band is the native stock option — functional but transparent to a fault, lacking the harmonic complexity of third-party options. 07 In the Wild

Abstract knowledge becomes practical when you can hear it in music you know. These tracks demonstrate eq used intentionally, at specific moments, for specific purposes.

Dr. Dre ft. Snoop Dogg — "Nuthin' But a G Thang" (1992)
0:00–0:15 intro, throughout · Produced by Dr. Dre

The low-end balance on this track is a masterclass in sub-bass EQ. The kick drum and bass synthesizer occupy almost entirely separate frequency spaces — the kick carries the 60–80 Hz punch while the synth bass is carved to sit above it at 90–150 Hz, creating a layered low end that hits on both subwoofers and consumer speakers. The midrange is notably scooped across the mix — a broad cut around 300–500 Hz gives the arrangement an open, airy quality that became a defining characteristic of West Coast G-funk productions. Listen on a subwoofer and then on earbuds to hear how deliberately the low-end spectral separation was engineered for translation across playback systems.

Radiohead — "How to Disappear Completely" (2000)
Full track · Produced by Nigel Godrich

Nigel Godrich's EQ approach on this track is defined by radical subtraction rather than addition. The string arrangement and Thom Yorke's vocal coexist without frequency conflict because Godrich applied significant mid-frequency cuts to the string recordings in the 1–3 kHz range — the exact range where vocal intelligibility lives — allowing the vocal to sit forward in the mix with no boosting required. The overall spectral balance is weighted heavily toward the low midrange and sub-200 Hz register, creating the track's characteristic sense of weight and foreboding. The absence of high-frequency content above 10 kHz is deliberate and gives the track a muted, analog-recorded quality that distinguishes it from contemporary digital productions of the same period.

Beyoncé — "Crazy in Love" (2003)
0:00–0:08 horn intro, verse vocal · Produced by Rich Harrison

The horn sample (sourced from The Chi-Lites' 'Are You My Woman') was aggressively EQ'd to sit within a modern production context — the original recording's low-mid weight was cut substantially to prevent it from competing with the kick and bass, and the upper harmonic range was boosted to give it presence without requiring level changes. Beyoncé's lead vocal receives a characteristic presence boost in the 3–4 kHz range that gives it projection and intelligibility even during the densest sections of the track — a technique Rich Harrison's production is consistently reliant on. The sub-bass on the kick is unusually extended for a pop record of this era, suggesting minimal HPF filtering at the mix stage to maximize impact on club systems.

Burial — "Archangel" (2007)
Full track · Produced by Burial (William Bevan)

Burial's EQ aesthetic on this track operates in direct opposition to the clarity-maximizing approach of mainstream electronic music mixing. The vocal samples are deliberately low-passed, removing high-frequency content and reducing them to warm, blurred textures that feel like memories rather than present-tense recordings. The sub-bass is narrow-banded and specific — sitting in the 50–70 Hz range with a sharp high-pass above it to prevent it from building up into the midrange and interfering with the vinyl-crackle texture layer that occupies the upper-mid space. The result is a mix with almost no energy above 8 kHz that nonetheless does not feel muffled, because the few high-frequency elements that exist — the hi-hat and cymbal transients — are allowed to breathe in the absence of competing brightness.

08 Types & Variants

Parametric EQ
Neve 1073 · API 550A · SSL G-Series Channel EQ

The parametric EQ offers independent control of frequency, gain, and Q (bandwidth) for each band, making it the most versatile and precise EQ type in production use. It is the standard tool for both corrective and creative mixing work. Vintage implementations like the Neve 1073 impart harmonic character through transformer saturation; modern digital parametrics like FabFilter Pro-Q 3 prioritize phase accuracy and transparency. The parametric is appropriate for virtually every mixing and mastering task — its depth of control is the reason it supplanted graphic EQs in professional studio contexts by the early 1980s.

Graphic EQ
Klark Teknik DN27 · dbx 2231 · MXR 10-Band

A graphic EQ presents a fixed set of frequency bands — typically at octave or one-third-octave intervals — each controlled by a fader whose position visually represents the boost or cut applied. Frequency and Q are fixed per band; only gain is adjustable. Graphic EQs are most common in live sound reinforcement for room correction and feedback control, and were standard in consumer hi-fi systems. In studio recording, graphic EQs are occasionally used as tone-shaping tools on bus processing where their fixed-bandwidth curves produce a specific, predictable character, but they have largely been superseded by parametric designs for critical mixing applications.

Passive / Program EQ
Pultec EQP-1A · Pultec MEQ-5 · Manley Massive Passive

Passive equalizers use inductor-capacitor networks without active amplification in the filter section itself — a subsequent gain stage compensates for the passive circuit's insertion loss. The resulting filter shapes are asymmetrical, frequency-interaction-rich, and possess a musical quality that is extremely difficult to replicate in active or digital designs. The Pultec EQP-1A's ability to simultaneously boost and cut at the same low frequency — producing a resonant bass enhancement that tightens as it lifts — remains the canonical example of a technical quirk that became an aesthetic signature. Passive EQs are used primarily for color and character rather than precision correction.

Dynamic EQ
SPL Transient Designer (early concept) · TDR Nova · FabFilter Pro-Q 3 (dynamic bands)

Dynamic EQ applies gain changes proportionally to the input signal level at a specific frequency band — functioning like a frequency-specific compressor or expander. A dynamic low-mid cut, for example, will only engage when the 300–500 Hz region of a vocal exceeds a threshold, leaving the EQ transparent on lighter passages. This preserves the natural tone of an instrument during quieter moments while automatically correcting frequency buildup during louder phrases. Dynamic EQ has become the preferred tool for managing the sibilance of vocalists without the artifacts of static de-essing, and for controlling the low-end buildup of bass instruments whose fundamentals vary by register.

Linear Phase EQ
Weiss EQ1-LP · Dangerous Music BAX EQ

Linear phase EQs apply an identical time delay to all frequencies, eliminating the phase shift that standard minimum-phase EQ filters introduce. This preserves transient integrity and phase relationships between stereo channels, making linear phase designs the preferred choice for mastering bus processing and mid/side EQ where phase accuracy across a stereo field is paramount. The trade-off is pre-ringing — a subtle artifact that appears before transients at frequencies near the filter's point of action — and increased processing latency. Linear phase mode should generally be avoided on individual tracks where its pre-ringing artifacts can smear transients, particularly on percussive elements.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    High-passing too aggressively on instruments with low fundamentals
    Applying an HPF at 100 Hz or higher to a bass guitar, piano, or cello removes fundamental notes and destroys the instrument's body. The result is a thin, floaty sound that cannot be recovered downstream. For bass instruments, set HPFs at 30–50 Hz maximum to remove only inaudible sub-sonic content. For midrange instruments, HPF cutoffs between 80–120 Hz are appropriate — always sweep down from an aggressive setting until you hear body returning rather than sweeping up from zero.
  • ✕
    Boosting mud frequencies to add warmth
    The 200–400 Hz region contains the buildup frequencies that make a mix sound muddy and congested — not the warmth frequencies producers are typically seeking. True warmth lives in the 80–150 Hz range. Boosting 300 Hz on a vocal or snare to add body more often adds the boxy, room-reflection quality of an untreated space. Cut the 250–400 Hz range first to reveal whether the muddiness clears; if warmth is genuinely missing, add it with a gentle boost at 80–120 Hz.
  • ✕
    Using identical EQ curves across different performances of the same instrument
    Vocal takes, guitar performances, and drum sessions all exhibit frequency variation between takes — different mic positions, different proximity effect, different room conditions. Saving and recalling an EQ preset from a previous session and applying it without auditioning on the new material ignores these differences. The corrective EQ curve for a vocal recorded 15 cm from a Neumann U87 in a live room will be wrong for the same vocalist recorded 30 cm from the same microphone in a booth. Always build EQ settings fresh by ear for each new recording, using previous sessions only as reference starting points.
  • ✕
    Excessive high-frequency boosting to compensate for a dull source
    Boosting above 10 kHz to add air to a dull recording amplifies noise floor, tape hiss, digital artifacts, and room reflections along with the desired brightness. Beyond 3–4 dB of shelf boost, the high-frequency content begins to sound artificial and fatiguing. If a source is fundamentally dull due to microphone placement, acoustic environment, or recording chain, EQ can partially compensate but cannot restore detail that was never captured. A better approach is gentle saturation (which generates upper harmonics) combined with conservative air-band boosting — see Saturation for the harmonic generation approach.
  • ✕
    Evaluating EQ decisions in solo rather than in mix context
    An EQ curve that sounds correct on a soloed vocal will almost certainly be wrong in a full mix. Soloing removes the masking relationships between instruments, making a mix element sound starved of frequencies that are actually occupied by other instruments in context. The 300 Hz cut that sounds extreme on a soloed guitar is the exact cut needed when the bass guitar, piano, and room mics are all simultaneously adding energy in that range. Make all significant EQ decisions with the full mix playing — solo only to identify specific problem frequencies using the sweep-and-cut technique, then immediately return to the full mix to confirm the result.
  • ✕
    Stacking multiple EQ plugins without monitoring cumulative phase shift
    Each minimum-phase EQ band introduces phase rotation at its center frequency. Stacking three or four EQ plugins on a single channel — a common workflow when using a corrective EQ before a character EQ before a dynamic EQ — can accumulate significant phase shift that manifests as smeared transients, reduced punch, and a loss of mono compatibility. Audit stacked EQ chains periodically by summing the channel to mono and checking for comb filtering or cancellation artifacts. Consolidating moves into a single EQ instance reduces phase accumulation and often reveals that multiple plugins were duplicating rather than complementing each other.

11 Frequently Asked Questions

The conventional answer is EQ before compression for corrective work, EQ after for tonal shaping — but the real answer is that both positions have distinct and useful effects. EQ before compression means the compressor responds to the frequency-corrected signal, which is appropriate when you are removing a resonance that would otherwise trigger the compressor disproportionately. EQ after compression means you are shaping the tone of the compressed signal — any boosts you add will not cause additional compression. In practice, professional engineers often use both: a corrective EQ before the compressor to control the frequency response the compressor 'hears,' and a tonal EQ after to shape the final character of the processed sound.
A parametric EQ gives you independent control over three parameters per band: the center frequency (where the filter acts), the gain (how much boost or cut is applied), and the Q or bandwidth (how wide or narrow the frequency region affected). A graphic EQ has fixed frequencies at octave or third-octave intervals, fixed bandwidth, and only allows gain adjustment via faders. Parametric EQs are preferred in studio production because their adjustability allows precise targeting of any frequency, while graphic EQs are most common in live sound for room correction where their visual layout and fixed-frequency bands match the task.
In professional mixing practice, boosts above +6 dB on individual tracks begin to sound artificial and are usually a sign that a corrective cut elsewhere in the frequency spectrum would solve the underlying problem more transparently. Mastering engineers rarely exceed +2 dB. The general principle is that if you need more than +6 dB to achieve the tonal result you are looking for, the source recording likely has a fundamental problem — microphone choice, placement, or room acoustics — that EQ cannot fully compensate for. Cuts can be as deep as necessary to solve a problem; boosts should be as conservative as possible.
Q (quality factor) determines the bandwidth of the filter — how many adjacent frequencies are affected on either side of the center frequency. A Q of 0.7 affects approximately 2 octaves; a Q of 1.4 affects about 1 octave; a Q of 4.0 affects roughly a quarter octave. For broad tonal shaping (adding warmth, brightness, or air), use low Q values between 0.5 and 1.0. For targeted corrective cuts (removing a resonant frequency, notching out a specific harmonic), use high Q values between 2.0 and 8.0. The sweet spot for most musical boosting — presence lifts, air-band enhancements — is typically Q 1.0–2.0, which is wide enough to sound natural but narrow enough to be precise.
Mid/Side EQ processes the center (mid) and sides (the stereo difference signal) of a stereo signal independently rather than treating left and right channels equally. This allows you to, for example, cut sub-bass from the side channel only — concentrating low-end energy in the center of the mix where it belongs for proper mono compatibility — while boosting high frequencies in the side channel to widen the stereo image. M/S EQ is primarily a mastering technique but is increasingly applied by mixing engineers on stereo buses: drum overheads, room mics, and stereo synthesizer pads benefit from mid/side processing that tightens low-end while preserving or widening high-frequency stereo content.
Dynamic EQ applies frequency-specific gain changes that are triggered by the input signal level at that frequency band — it only cuts or boosts when the signal in that band exceeds (or falls below) a threshold. Multiband compression splits the signal into frequency bands using crossover filters and applies compression independently to each band. The key difference is in behavior: dynamic EQ operates like a frequency-specific compressor with the gain change expressed as an EQ curve, while multiband compression applies more aggressive gain reduction with ratio, attack, and release controls that can significantly alter the spectral character of a sound. Dynamic EQ is generally more transparent and musical for tonal correction; multiband compression is more appropriate for controlling the overall spectral balance of a bus or master.
Mix bus EQ should be applied conservatively — typically no more than +/-1.5 dB per band — and should enhance the overall spectral balance rather than correct individual element problems that should have been addressed at the track level. Listen for the mix bus EQ's effect on the entire frequency spectrum: a low shelf boost should add weight without making the kick and bass mask each other; a high shelf boost should add air without making cymbal and vocal consonants harsh. Apply mix bus EQ early in the mixing process if possible, so your individual track decisions are made with the bus EQ in place — adjusting mix bus EQ at the end of a mix often requires re-evaluating track-level decisions that were made without it.
Standard parametric EQ filters are minimum-phase designs: they introduce frequency-dependent phase shift as a byproduct of their amplitude response, meaning different frequencies are delayed by different amounts as they pass through the filter. This phase smear is generally inaudible on individual tracks but becomes significant on stereo buses and during mastering, where phase coherence between the left and right channels determines the stability and focus of the stereo image. Linear phase EQs apply a uniform delay to all frequencies — preserving phase relationships completely — at the cost of introducing pre-ringing artifacts near filter transition bands and adding processing latency. For mastering EQ and M/S processing on a mix bus, linear phase mode is preferred; for individual track work, minimum-phase designs are better because their superior transient response outweighs the phase accuracy benefits of linear phase processing.
