Multiband Compression

/ˈmʌltiˌbænd kəmˈprɛʃən/

Quick Answer Definition How It Works Parameters Quick Reference History How To Use It In The Wild Types Mistakes Calculator Related Further Reading FAQ

Multiband Compression is a dynamic processing technique that divides audio into two or more frequency bands and applies independent compression to each. Unlike broadband compression, it lets you tame a boomy low end without affecting high-frequency transients.

01 Definition

When a single compressor threatens to crush your mix into submission, multiband compression is the scalpel that lets you operate on exactly the problem — nothing more, nothing less.

Multiband compression is a signal-processing technique in which an audio signal is divided into discrete frequency bands by a crossover network, and each band is then passed through its own independent compressor with individually adjustable threshold, ratio, attack, release, and makeup gain controls. The resulting bands are recombined at the output, producing a signal whose dynamic behavior has been sculpted frequency-by-frequency rather than globally. Most implementations offer between two and six bands, with four-band configurations — typically covering sub/bass, low-mid, high-mid, and high-frequency ranges — representing the industry standard for mastering and bus-processing workflows.

The fundamental motivation for multiband processing arises from a core limitation of broadband, or full-band, compression: because a single compressor responds to the total RMS or peak energy of a signal, a loud low-frequency event such as a kick drum transient or bass note will trigger gain reduction that simultaneously affects every other frequency region. On a dense mix, this pumping artifact — often perceived as the high frequencies ducking each time the bass hits — is the audible fingerprint of a mix that is fighting against its own compression. Multiband compression solves this by isolating the energy that is causing the problem and compressing only that band, leaving adjacent frequency regions dynamically untouched.

It is important to distinguish multiband compression from dynamic EQ, with which it shares considerable conceptual territory. Both tools apply frequency-selective dynamic processing, but their operational philosophies differ in a meaningful way. A dynamic EQ applies a parametric EQ curve that becomes deeper or shallower in response to a level-detection circuit — it is an EQ with a dynamic dimension. A multiband compressor, by contrast, splits the full signal into separate paths, processes each path as a whole waveform, and re-sums at the output. The crossover filters themselves introduce phase shift and coloration that become part of the character of the tool. Understanding this distinction helps producers choose the right tool: dynamic EQ for targeted corrective work on a specific resonance or tonal buildup, multiband compression for broader dynamic shaping of an entire frequency region.

Multiband compression exists on a spectrum of transparency. At one extreme sit linear-phase multiband processors — used extensively in mastering — whose crossover filters introduce no phase shift and preserve the time-domain integrity of transients at the cost of pre-ringing artifacts and higher latency. At the other extreme are analog-modeled designs that use minimum-phase crossovers, introducing gentle phase rotation that adds warmth and cohesion at the cost of slight transient smear. Between these poles sit a range of hybrid designs. The choice of which to use is not merely academic: on a mastering chain, linear-phase operation protects the stereo image and transient punch that a mix engineer labored to achieve; on a drum bus in a creative session, the coloration of a minimum-phase unit may be precisely the character a producer is seeking.

Used judiciously, multiband compression is one of the most powerful tools in a serious producer's arsenal. Used carelessly, it is one of the most destructive — capable of hollowing out a mix, creating unnatural tonal shifts, and introducing inter-band artifacts that are difficult to diagnose and worse to hear at volume. The entry that follows covers the technical architecture, historical development, parameter-by-parameter guidance, common mistakes, and genre-specific applications in the depth required to deploy this tool with confidence.

02 How It Works

At its core, a multiband compressor is a signal-routing architecture built around a crossover network. The incoming audio is fed simultaneously into a bank of bandpass filters — or, more precisely, a cascade of low-pass and high-pass filters whose cutoff frequencies define the crossover points. In a four-band unit, three crossover points (commonly set around 80–120 Hz, 500–800 Hz, and 3–5 kHz) divide the spectrum into four contiguous bands. Each filter output feeds its own full-featured compressor circuit with independent threshold, ratio, attack, release, knee, and makeup gain controls. Critically, in most designs a sidechain detector monitors each band's own level; some advanced units allow cross-band or external sidechain signals, enabling one band's dynamics to modulate another band's gain reduction.

The compressor in each band operates identically to a standard broadband compressor at the electro-mechanical or DSP level. When the signal in a given band exceeds its threshold, the gain reduction circuit attenuates the output of that band according to the set ratio. The attack time determines how quickly gain reduction is applied after the threshold is crossed — fast attack times (1–5 ms) can suppress transients effectively but may dull the punch of a snare or pluck, while slow attack times (20–80 ms) allow the initial transient to pass through uncompressed before the compressor clamps down. Release time governs how quickly the gain reduction recovers toward unity gain once the signal drops back below threshold; too-fast release causes audible pumping, while too-slow release creates a compressed, lifeless sustain tail. These time constants interact with musical tempo and the spectral content of each band, which is why attack and release settings appropriate for a low-frequency band are almost never appropriate for the same unit's high-frequency band.

After each band has been independently gain-reduced, the processed band signals are summed back together by an output mixer stage. This re-summation is the most technically sensitive stage in the architecture. In minimum-phase designs, the crossover filters introduce all-pass phase shifts that partially cancel at the crossover frequencies — a phenomenon that can cause comb-filtering artifacts if the crossover points are set in musically sensitive regions. Linear-phase designs use FIR filters that are computationally expensive and introduce latency (typically 15–100 ms depending on the resolution and sample rate) but guarantee a flat summed phase response. Many modern DAW-native multiband compressors offer a toggle between these two modes, and understanding which mode is active is essential to diagnosing subtle artifacts in the output.

The threshold parameter in a multiband compressor deserves special attention because its effective behavior differs from a broadband compressor in a non-obvious way. Because each band contains only a fraction of the total signal's energy, the absolute signal levels seen by each band's detector are lower than those seen by a full-band compressor on the same material. Low-frequency bands tend to carry the most energy in most program material; high-frequency bands carry the least. A producer setting a −18 dBFS threshold on a high-frequency band will find the compressor triggering on every sibilant and hi-hat hit, while the same threshold on the low-frequency band may barely engage on all but the loudest bass transients. Calibrating thresholds by watching each band's gain-reduction meter during playback of representative program material — rather than by setting thresholds numerically — is the correct technique for all but the most corrective applications.

Finally, the output gain or makeup gain of each band functions not merely as a compensation tool but as a quasi-EQ. Because gain reduction in a band lowers that band's average level, the makeup gain restores it — but the amount of makeup gain applied determines the processed band's contribution to the final summed output. Producers routinely use the per-band output gain controls as a post-compression spectral balance adjustment, effectively performing a broadband EQ move at the same time as dynamic processing. While this dual function is powerful, it also means that seemingly small changes in threshold or ratio will alter the spectral balance of the output, requiring compensating adjustments to makeup gain that can quickly create an opaque chain of interdependent settings.

03 The Parameters

Every multiband compression — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these and you can work with any implementation.

CROSSOVER FREQUENCY
Defines where one band ends and the next begins

Crossover points determine which frequencies each compressor responds to. Poorly placed crossovers — for instance, splitting at 200 Hz and catching the fundamental of a kick drum in the low-mid band rather than the bass band — will cause audible tonal shifts under compression. Standard starting points for a four-band mastering chain are 80–120 Hz, 500–700 Hz, and 3–5 kHz, but these should always be adjusted to align with the specific spectral content of the program material.

THRESHOLD
Level at which gain reduction begins in a given band

Because each band contains only a slice of the total signal energy, thresholds must be set by watching the band's own gain-reduction meter during playback rather than by applying absolute dBFS values from experience with broadband compressors. Typical mastering thresholds for gentle bus glue range from −18 to −24 dBFS depending on the band's average energy. More aggressive corrective settings on a problem band may push thresholds as low as −30 dBFS to catch only peaks.

RATIO
Amount of gain reduction applied above the threshold

Ratios from 1.5:1 to 3:1 are standard for transparent bus and mastering work; anything above 4:1 begins to introduce audible compression artifacts. The low-frequency band often benefits from slightly higher ratios (2.5:1 to 4:1) to control sub-bass energy, while the high-frequency band should generally be kept below 2:1 to avoid dulling transients. Limiting ratios of 10:1 and above are occasionally used on the low-frequency band to function as a sub-limiter.

ATTACK TIME
Speed at which gain reduction engages after threshold crossing

Attack time interacts strongly with the spectral content of each band: low-frequency bands have longer wavelengths, meaning a 5 ms attack will begin to round off bass transients that a 30 ms attack would allow through cleanly. High-frequency bands respond to much shorter transients — a 1–3 ms attack on a high-mid or air band can effectively suppress sibilance and cymbal hash. A common mastering approach sets slower attacks on low bands (20–50 ms) and faster attacks on high bands (3–10 ms).

RELEASE TIME
Speed at which gain reduction recovers once signal drops below threshold

Release time determines the rhythmic character of the compression. On the low-frequency band, release times that are too short (below 50 ms) on bass-heavy material cause pumping artifacts that are felt as a wobble in the low end. Program-dependent auto-release modes, available in most modern multiband processors, adapt to the tempo and dynamics of the material and are recommended as a starting point. For creative pumping effects on a drum bus, a manual release of 50–150 ms set to tempo is effective.

KNEE
Controls the curvature of the gain reduction onset around the threshold

A hard knee applies gain reduction immediately and fully the moment the signal crosses threshold, which is audible and punchy but can sound aggressive on sustained material. A soft knee — typically 3–6 dB wide — gradually increases the ratio as the signal approaches and passes the threshold, producing a smoother and more transparent result. Mastering applications almost universally favor a soft knee of at least 3 dB to preserve musicality across the compression range.

MAKEUP GAIN (OUTPUT GAIN PER BAND)
Compensates for level reduction and shapes inter-band tonal balance

Per-band makeup gain is a dual-function control: it compensates for the gain reduction introduced by compression and simultaneously alters the processed band's contribution to the summed output, effectively functioning as a post-compression EQ. A 1 dB increase in the makeup gain of the low-frequency band will boost the perceived bass in the final output, regardless of the compression settings. For this reason, producers should A/B the processed output at level-matched gain before committing to any tonal changes introduced through makeup gain.

SOLO / BYPASS PER BAND
Auditions a single band in isolation or bypasses its compressor

Soloing individual bands for diagnostic purposes is one of the most productive habits when setting up a multiband processor. Listening to the low-frequency band in isolation while cycling through representative program material quickly reveals whether the crossover points are capturing the intended frequency range and whether the compressor is responding musically. Band bypass allows direct before/after comparison within a single band, isolating its contribution to the audible effect of the overall processor.

05 History & Origin

The conceptual foundation of multiband compression predates digital audio by several decades. In the 1930s and 1940s, broadcast engineers at RCA and AT&T Bell Laboratories were investigating frequency-selective gain control as a means of improving speech intelligibility and managing the dynamic range of AM radio transmission. The VOGAD (Voice Operated Gain-Adjusting Device) and related automatic gain control systems of the era applied broadband attenuation triggered by voice energy, but engineers quickly recognized that low-frequency noise and hum were causing unnecessary gain reduction that degraded speech clarity. By the early 1950s, experimental multi-band AGC systems that divided the audio spectrum into two or three bands were in documented use within telephone transmission research, establishing the architectural principle that would define multiband compression for the next seventy years.

The first commercially significant multiband compressor designed explicitly for music production was the Urei/Universal Audio 1176 family's conceptual contemporaries in broadcast — but the hardware unit most credited with establishing the multiband compressor as a mastering tool is the Orban Optimod series, introduced in 1975. The Optimod 8000 was designed by Robert Orban for FM broadcast processing and used a five-band compression architecture to control the spectral dynamics of program material for transmission. The unit became ubiquitous in radio stations through the late 1970s and 1980s and introduced an entire generation of broadcast engineers to the concept of frequency-selective dynamic control. Its characteristic sound — dense, forward, with a tightly controlled low end — influenced the aesthetic of FM rock radio and made frequency-band compression a recognized production value in popular music.

The transition from broadcast to recording studio mastering began in earnest in the late 1980s and early 1990s with the introduction of hardware units aimed specifically at the mastering engineer. The Aphex Dominator II and the Drawmer DL441 Quad Noise Gate (which included band-selectable compression) were early entrants, but the unit that genuinely defined the mastering-grade multiband compressor was the TC Electronic TripleC/Finalizer, released in 1997. The Finalizer combined three-band compression with limiting and dithering in a single rack unit and became one of the most widely used mastering processors of its era, appearing on a significant percentage of commercially mastered recordings through the late 1990s and early 2000s. Its distinctive ability to tighten the low end while preserving high-frequency detail was widely imitated and signaled the arrival of multiband compression as a mainstream mastering technique rather than a broadcast-specific tool.

The software era transformed both the accessibility and the sophistication of multiband compression. Waves Audio's C4 Multiband Compressor, released in 1997, was among the first high-quality plug-in implementations and rapidly became an industry standard. iZotope's Ozone series, first released in 2001 and now in its eleventh major iteration, integrated multiband compression into a comprehensive mastering suite that became the reference-point tool for a generation of project-studio mastering engineers. FabFilter's Pro-MB, released in 2012, introduced a new paradigm with its dynamic-range mode (allowing the compressor to act as an expander or compressor depending on direction), its per-band mid-side processing, and its extremely low-latency linear-phase option. The Pro-MB's interface design — with interactive, draggable band handles on a frequency display — became the visual template for virtually every major multiband plug-in released afterward and remains the industry benchmark for transparent mastering-grade multiband compression as of 2026.

06 How Producers Use It

On a mix bus or stereo master, multiband compression is most productively deployed as a finishing tool rather than a problem-solver — the distinction matters because it changes the approach entirely. When used as a finishing tool, the goal is to add density and cohesion to a mix that is already well-balanced: ratios stay low (1.5:1 to 2.5:1), gain reduction in any band rarely exceeds 3 dB, and the primary purpose of the processor is to allow each frequency region to achieve its full dynamic potential independently. Mastering engineers such as Bob Ludwig and Vlado Meller have described this approach as giving the mix "frequency-specific room to breathe" — the low end can be compressed more aggressively to control bass transients without the high-frequency compression that would dull the mix's air and presence.

On individual instrument buses, multiband compression takes on a more corrective role. The most common application is the bass guitar or bass synth bus, where a multiband compressor with two or three bands can even out the tonal inconsistency between the lower fundamental frequencies and the upper harmonics. A bass guitar played across multiple positions on the neck will naturally shift tonal balance — open E sounds fundamentally different from a fretted note at the fifth position — and a low-band compressor set to catch these energy spikes without affecting the upper harmonic content produces a bass track that translates more consistently across playback systems. A common starting point is a two-band split at 120–160 Hz with a 3:1 ratio and 20–30 ms attack on the low band, leaving the upper band either uncompressed or lightly compressed at 1.5:1.

Vocal chains benefit from multiband compression primarily for two issues: low-frequency buildup (proximity effect from close-miked recording, or LF muddiness from room modes) and high-frequency sibilance and harshness. A three-band setup with the low band compressed to control proximity buildup below 200 Hz, the mid band lightly glued for density, and a slow-attack high-band compressor to smooth sibilant peaks above 5–6 kHz is a practical starting configuration. Many engineers prefer a dedicated de-esser for sibilance control ahead of a two-band multiband compressor for body control, rather than relying on a single multiband unit to do both jobs — this separation gives more independent control and avoids inter-band artifacts in the vocal's most sensitive frequency range.

On drum buses, multiband compression is used to control the relationship between the sub-kick energy and the body and crack of the snare and toms. A three-band split at approximately 100 Hz and 4–5 kHz applies sub/kick compression in the low band without affecting snare crack in the high band — a particularly useful technique when the kick and snare are tuned to conflict spectrally and a broadband compressor would kill the snare's attack every time the kick hits. High-ratio settings on the low band (3:1 to 5:1) with a 30–50 ms attack allow the kick transient through before clamping the sustain, creating a tighter, more controlled low end without sacrificing punch. Parallel processing of the compressed drum bus against an uncompressed version (New York compression) is commonly combined with multiband processing for maximum impact.

AbletonAbleton's Multiband Dynamics device (found in Audio Effects → Dynamics) offers three bands with independent Above and Below threshold compressor/expander controls per band. For mastering work, enable the Soft Knee option and reduce the Above ratio to 2:1 per band; watch the GR meters in performance view. Pair with Ableton's Spectrum device on a separate track for real-time crossover calibration.
FL StudioParametric Equalizer 2 does not provide dynamic processing; FL's native multiband option is the Maximus plug-in (included in all editions). Maximus offers three bands with highly configurable compressor, limiter, and stereo parameters. Use the Low band's Tension parameter to shape the response curve of gain reduction for a more musical character on bass-heavy material. The preset library is a practical starting point for genre-specific configurations.
Logic ProLogic Pro X's native Multipressor provides four bands and is capable of transparent mastering-grade processing when configured carefully. Enable Linear Phase mode under the settings wheel for mastering applications; accept the increased latency (Logic compensates automatically). The Ratio and Knee controls are well-calibrated for musical response — start with a 2:1 ratio and 4 dB soft knee across all bands, then differentiate per-band settings from there.

For mastering chains on a Pro Tools HDX system, the iZotope Ozone Dynamics module is commonly preferred for its low-latency linear-phase option and metering integration. 07 In the Wild

Abstract knowledge becomes practical when you can hear it in music you know. These tracks demonstrate multiband compression used intentionally, at specific moments, for specific purposes.

Daft Punk — "Get Lucky" (2013)
0:00–0:45 intro · Produced by Daft Punk, Co-produced by Pharrell Williams · Mastered by Bob Ludwig

Listen on a full-range playback system to the relationship between the bass guitar's sub-fundamental and its upper harmonic content throughout the intro groove. The low end sits exceptionally tight without losing the warmth of the analog bass tone — characteristic of Ludwig's multiband mastering approach where the sub band is compressed at a moderate ratio while the mid-band bass harmonics are allowed relatively free dynamic movement. The kick drum punches through without causing any audible broadband pumping, indicating independent low-band control. Compare on earbuds versus studio monitors to hear how the frequency-band separation translates across playback systems.

Kendrick Lamar — "HUMBLE." (2017)
0:00–0:32 · Produced by Mike Will Made-It · Mastered by Colin Leonard

The 808 kick in the opening bars demonstrates textbook multiband low-end management: the sub-fundamental is dense and controlled without bloat, while the upper-harmonic crack of the 808 body sits in the mid range with full dynamic presence. Colin Leonard's mastering on this track is widely cited for its loudness and punch without the broadband compression artifacts that plague similarly loud masters. The hi-hats and vocal transients in the chorus retain their attack and air — evidence that the high-frequency band is lightly compressed or nearly bypassed while the low band does the heavy dynamic work. A/B the first verse to the chorus to hear how consistent the tonal balance remains across dramatic changes in arrangement density.

Billie Eilish — "bad guy" (2019)
0:00–0:14 bass intro · Produced by Finneas O'Connell · Mastered by John Greenham

The bass synth line that opens this track is a study in controlled low-frequency dynamics: the fundamental sits at approximately 55–65 Hz and is remarkably even in level note-to-note, while the upper harmonic buzz of the synth sits distinctly above it in the mid range. This frequency separation in the source recording makes multiband processing straightforward — Greenham's mastering maintains the sub-bass density without the mud that often plagues low-register synth basses on commercial releases. At the drop into the chorus, the entire mix's dynamic range is compressed but the tonal balance shifts very little, indicating that per-band makeup gain was carefully calibrated to match the spectral balance of the mix before and after compression.

Massive Attack — "Teardrop" (1998)
0:20–1:00 · Produced by Massive Attack, Mixed by Craig Armstrong · Mastered at Metropolis Studios

An older example that illustrates multiband processing at a transitional moment in mastering history — the TC Electronic Finalizer was in widespread use among European mastering engineers by 1998 and its signature three-band architecture is audible in the tightly controlled relationship between the kick and bass frequencies and the open, present quality of the harpsichord and vocal in the mid and high bands. Notice that the kick drum's sub-bass content does not cause the mid-range elements to duck or pump — the frequency-selective compression keeps each region dynamically independent. This track remains a standard reference for testing low-frequency translation on consumer speakers.

08 Types & Variants

Linear-Phase Multiband Compressor
FabFilter Pro-MB (linear-phase mode) · Weiss DS1-MK3

Uses FIR (Finite Impulse Response) crossover filters that introduce no phase shift between bands, preserving the stereo image and transient integrity of the source material. The trade-off is increased latency (typically 15–100 ms) and computational cost. The industry standard for mastering applications where any phase coloration is unacceptable.

Minimum-Phase (Analog-Modeled) Multiband Compressor
Waves C4 · SSL G-Bus Compressor with HP sidechain

Uses IIR (Infinite Impulse Response) crossover filters derived from analog circuit topology, introducing phase shift that adds warmth and cohesion reminiscent of classic hardware processing. Lower latency makes these suitable for tracking and mixing sessions. The coloration introduced by minimum-phase crossovers is often considered musically desirable on drum buses and mix buses in rock, hip-hop, and electronic music.

Dynamic EQ / Hybrid Multiband
FabFilter Pro-MB (dynamic EQ mode) · Waves F6 Floating-Band Dynamic EQ

Blurs the boundary between multiband compression and dynamic EQ by applying parametric EQ curves that deepen in response to level detection rather than splitting the signal into fully separate waveform paths. Offers more surgical control with less inter-band artifact risk, making it the preferred tool for corrective work on a single resonant frequency or narrow-band sibilance rather than broad frequency-region dynamic shaping.

Broadcast / Loudness Multiband Processor
Orban Optimod 8600 · Omnia.9 · Wheatstone AirAura X3

Architectures with five or more bands designed for FM, DAB, and streaming loudness normalization. Attack and release times are optimized for rapid gain control over dense, varied program material rather than musical transparency. Used in broadcast signal chains rather than music production, though their influence on the sonic aesthetic of commercial radio from the 1970s onward profoundly shaped how mastered music has been consumed by the public.

Mid-Side Multiband Compressor
FabFilter Pro-MB (M/S mode) · iZotope Ozone Dynamics (M/S)

Processes the mid and side channels of a stereo signal independently within each frequency band, enabling frequency-selective stereo-width control. A common mastering application is compressing the low-frequency side channel more aggressively than the mid to mono the sub-bass below 100 Hz (improving low-end translation on mono systems) while leaving the high-frequency stereo field open. Requires careful gain calibration to avoid altering the perceived width of the stereo image unintentionally.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    Setting identical attack and release times across all bands
    Low-frequency wavelengths are physically much longer than high-frequency wavelengths, meaning the same 5 ms attack time will clip transient content very differently in each band. On a bass band, 5 ms is fast enough to begin attenuating the kick drum's fundamental attack; on a high-frequency band, 5 ms may be too slow to catch sibilant bursts. Always set attack and release independently per band, starting slower on low bands and faster on high bands, then adjust by ear.
  • ✕
    Using too many bands on material that doesn't require it
    More bands means more crossover filters, more phase interaction, and more controls to miscalibrate. A four-band processor applied to a vocal track where only the sibilance and proximity effect need addressing is almost always inferior to a de-esser plus a single-band high-pass-filtered broadband compressor. Start with the minimum number of bands required to solve the specific problem, and add bands only when a simpler approach is demonstrably insufficient.
  • ✕
    Over-compressing one band and using makeup gain to re-balance tonally
    When a single band is compressed by 8–10 dB and makeup gain is used to restore it, the result is a frequency region with dramatically reduced dynamic range that sounds tonally similar to the original but feels lifeless and flat under prolonged listening. Maximum gain reduction per band for transparent mastering work should stay below 3–4 dB. If a band requires more than 6 dB of gain reduction to control, the problem likely originates in the mix and should be addressed there rather than on the master.
  • ✕
    Misplacing crossover frequencies so they bisect instrument fundamentals
    A crossover at 200 Hz placed in the middle of a bass guitar's fundamental range — which spans roughly 41 Hz (low E) to 300 Hz (D at the fifth fret, second position) — will cause the compressor to respond to only part of the instrument's energy, creating tonal inconsistency as different notes trigger different amounts of gain reduction in different bands. Crossovers should be placed in spectral gaps between instruments, not through the fundamental range of any single source.
  • ✕
    Bypassing the processor rather than using band bypass for diagnostic A/B comparison
    Bypassing the entire multiband processor for a before/after comparison is misleading if the processor is applying makeup gain, because the bypassed signal will be quieter than the processed signal. The brain reliably perceives louder audio as better-sounding, so uncompensated level differences will make the processed version appear superior even when it sounds worse at matched levels. Always level-match the bypassed and processed signals — typically by reducing the processor's output gain until both states measure the same LUFS — before making any quality judgment.
  • ✕
    Applying multiband compression to a poorly mixed track expecting a mix fix
    Multiband compression cannot correct a mix that has fundamental balance problems. If the bass is 6 dB too loud, a low-band compressor can tame transients but will leave the sustained bass energy largely unaffected and will introduce pumping artifacts from the over-gain situation. A multiband mastering processor works best on a mix that is already 90% correct; it is a finishing and optimization tool, not a corrective surgery suite. Stems or remix access is required to fix fundamental mix imbalances.

12 Frequently Asked Questions

A standard compressor applies the same gain reduction to the entire frequency spectrum whenever the signal crosses its threshold. Multiband compression divides the audio into frequency bands and applies independent compression to each band, so a loud bass transient can be tamed without affecting the dynamics of the midrange or high frequencies. This frequency-selective control is the defining feature that makes multiband compression particularly valuable for mastering and bus processing, where preserving the tonal balance of an already-balanced mix is essential.
Four bands is the most practical configuration for the majority of mastering applications, covering sub/bass, low-mid, high-mid, and high-frequency regions. Two or three bands are sufficient for simpler program material or when only one or two frequency regions require dynamic control. More than four bands introduces significantly more crossover filter interaction and parameter complexity without proportional benefit in most cases — six or more bands are most useful in broadcast loudness processing, not musical mastering.
For mastering and any application where preserving the stereo image and transient integrity of the mix is the priority, linear-phase mode is the correct choice despite its latency overhead. For tracking, mixing, and creative bus processing where the coloration of minimum-phase filters is acceptable or desirable, minimum-phase mode offers lower latency and a warmer, more analog character. Many engineers use minimum-phase in the mixing stage and switch to linear-phase only on the mastering chain.
Start by identifying the spectral content of the material: listen for where the bass instruments' fundamentals sit, where the midrange instruments live, and where the high-frequency content — cymbals, air, presence — begins. Place crossover points in spectral gaps between these regions rather than through the fundamental range of any single instrument. Common starting points are 80–120 Hz, 500–800 Hz, and 3–5 kHz for a four-band mastering chain, but these should always be confirmed by soloing each band and verifying that the intended instruments are captured within each band's range.
Multiband compression and EQ are complementary rather than interchangeable tools. EQ applies static tonal changes that are independent of signal level; multiband compression applies dynamic changes that respond to level variations. Using the per-band makeup gain controls of a multiband compressor to make tonal adjustments is a common technique, but doing so couples your tonal decisions to your dynamic decisions in ways that can be difficult to untangle. For static tonal correction, EQ before and after the multiband compressor remains the cleaner workflow.
Hollowness after multiband processing most commonly results from over-compression in the low-mid band (approximately 200–600 Hz), which contains the body and warmth of most musical instruments. Excessive gain reduction here — more than 3–4 dB — removes the sense of weight and presence from the mix. Check the crossover points to ensure the low-mid band is not encroaching on the bass fundamentals, reduce the ratio in the low-mid band, and raise its threshold to catch only the most prominent peaks. A/B at matched loudness to confirm the hollow character is from the processor and not a level illusion.
Use a dynamic EQ when the problem is a specific, narrow resonance or peak — for example, a 3 kHz harshness in a vocal, a 200 Hz boominess in an acoustic guitar, or a 8 kHz sibilance that a de-esser is not handling musically. Dynamic EQ is more transparent and surgical for these narrow-band corrective tasks because it does not split the full signal into separate waveform paths. Use a multiband compressor when an entire frequency region requires independent dynamic shaping — for example, controlling the sub-bass while leaving the mid and high bands unaffected on a full mix or bus.
No. Many mastering engineers regard multiband compression as a specialized tool rather than a default insert on every master. Simple, well-mixed acoustic recordings often benefit more from a transparent broadband compressor and careful EQ than from multiband processing. Multiband compression is most valuable on dense, spectrally complex material — electronic music, hip-hop, pop, and rock mixes where specific frequency regions have dynamic inconsistencies that a broadband compressor cannot address without compromising the entire mix. When in doubt, master without multiband compression first and determine whether a specific frequency-selective dynamic problem actually exists before introducing the complexity of multiband processing.
