Vibrato

/vɪˈbrɑːtoʊ/

Quick Answer Definition How It Works Parameters Quick Reference History How To Use It In The Wild Types Mistakes Related Further Reading FAQ

Vibrato is a periodic oscillation of pitch around a center frequency, creating a wavering expressive quality in sustained notes. It is produced mechanically by performers, or electronically via LFO-driven pitch modulation in synthesizers and audio processors.

01 Definition

The difference between a note that sits still and a note that breathes — that's vibrato. Master it and every sustained tone in your mix gains a pulse.

Vibrato is the periodic, cyclic oscillation of a sound's fundamental pitch around a stable center frequency. Unlike tremolo, which modulates amplitude, vibrato modulates pitch — the frequency itself rises and falls rhythmically, creating the characteristic wavering quality associated with expressive vocal performance, bowed strings, and analog synthesizers. The modulation is typically sinusoidal and symmetrical: the pitch deviates equally above and below the nominal pitch, producing no net upward or downward drift when averaged over a full cycle. In practice, vibrato rates used in musical performance typically fall between 4 Hz and 8 Hz, with classical violin and operatic voice settling near 5–6 Hz as a perceptual sweet spot where the ear fuses individual pitch excursions into a unified, enriched tone.

In electronic music production, vibrato is almost universally implemented via a low-frequency oscillator (LFO) routed to the pitch of an oscillator, a sample playback engine, or an audio-rate pitch shifter. The LFO's rate controls how fast the pitch cycles, its depth controls how wide the pitch swing is (measured in cents or semitones), and its waveform shape determines the character of the modulation — a sine wave yields smooth, organic vibrato, while a triangle wave is marginally edgier, and a sample-and-hold shape produces robotic, stepped pitch variation more accurately called pitch-quantized flutter. The onset of vibrato can be shaped by a delay parameter (sometimes labeled "vibrato delay" or "mod delay"), which holds the pitch steady for a set time before the LFO engages — a critical feature for mimicking natural performance, where singers and violinists typically begin a note straight before adding vibrato.

Vibrato occupies a precise perceptual niche. Psychoacoustic research, including foundational work by Johan Sundberg at KTH Stockholm and Ingo Titze at the National Center for Voice and Speech, demonstrates that vibrato at 5–6 Hz with a depth of roughly 50–100 cents (±25–50 cents around the center pitch) is perceived not as out-of-tune singing but as a richer, more sustained tone. The auditory system effectively integrates the pitch excursions and locks onto the geometric mean as the "heard" pitch. This is why a wide vibrato at slow rate can feel sloppy and out-of-tune while the same total pitch excursion at 6 Hz sounds expressive and controlled — timing and symmetry are everything. Producers working with sampled instruments must account for this: vibrato baked into a sample interacts with any additional LFO vibrato added in the sampler, and the combined modulation can quickly exceed the perceptual coherence zone.

Vibrato's relationship to timbre is underappreciated in production contexts. Because oscillating the pitch of a harmonically rich sound also oscillates the spacing of its harmonics relative to any static spectral features — room resonances, filter cutoffs, formants — vibrato introduces an indirect, correlated amplitude and timbral modulation alongside the pitch modulation. This is part of why analog polysynths with chorus and slight detuning sound so wide and complex: the individual voices each carry slight vibrato from their LFOs, and those pitch excursions continuously modulate their positions within the filter's frequency response, creating movement in the harmonic spectrum that no static sound can achieve. Producers who rely exclusively on chorus and reverb for "width" often overlook gentle, voice-specific vibrato as a superior tool for organic movement.

From a mix engineering perspective, vibrato affects how a sustained note occupies spectral space. A pitch-stable sustained pad locks into fixed frequency relationships with other elements; a pad with 20-cent vibrato at 5 Hz traces a narrow band of frequencies rhythmically, softening any masking or beating relationship it has with adjacent tonal content. This can be a powerful mixing tool — a slight vibrato on a sustained organ or string layer can alleviate frequency clashes with a vocal without EQ surgery. Conversely, unwanted vibrato on a bass instrument below 200 Hz will produce audible pitch instability and should generally be removed or minimized. Context and frequency range are decisive factors in whether vibrato enhances or destabilizes a mix.

02 How It Works

At its core, vibrato is pitch modulation: the instantaneous frequency of a sound source deviates cyclically from its target pitch. Electronically, this is achieved by routing a low-frequency oscillator output to the frequency control input (often the 1V/oct or FM input) of an oscillator, or to the pitch parameter of a sampler voice or pitch-shifting algorithm. The LFO generates a continuous, periodic signal — typically sinusoidal — that the synthesizer or processor interprets as a time-varying frequency offset. If the center pitch is A4 (440 Hz) and the LFO drives a ±50 cent deviation at 5 Hz, the instantaneous frequency sweeps from approximately 427 Hz to 453 Hz and back five times per second. Because the deviation is symmetrical, the perceived pitch remains 440 Hz; only the quality of the tone changes.

The relationship between cents depth and audible pitch width is logarithmic, not linear. One semitone equals 100 cents, and a 100-cent symmetric vibrato means the pitch spans from 50 cents below to 50 cents above — approximately a whole semitone of total excursion. At 440 Hz, 50 cents downward is about 427 Hz and 50 cents upward is about 453 Hz, an asymmetric frequency range in Hz terms even though it is symmetric in cents. This matters in FM synthesis and audio-rate modulation contexts: excessive vibrato depth in FM can introduce audible sidebands that alter timbre, not just pitch. Keeping vibrato depth below 100 cents (±50 cents) keeps the modulation within the pitch-expressive range without distorting the harmonic structure of the source sound.

Modern DAW implementations typically offer several LFO waveform options for vibrato. A sine wave is the standard choice: it produces smooth, gradual acceleration and deceleration through the pitch cycle, matching natural vocal and string vibrato closely. A triangle wave has a more mechanical, linear pitch sweep that can sound slightly clinical. Sawtooth and reverse-sawtooth waves produce asymmetric pitch rises or falls followed by abrupt resets — useful for stylized or synthetic effects. A random or sample-and-hold waveform creates stepped, unpredictable pitch jumps sometimes called pitch flutter or glitch vibrato, common in experimental electronic music. The choice of waveform directly determines whether vibrato reads as organic or mechanical, and most production scenarios require sine or triangle for anything meant to sound natural.

Onset behavior is the parameter most producers neglect. Natural performers virtually never begin vibrato from the attack of a note — there is always a brief straight-tone period, typically 100–400 ms, before the oscillation engages. This straight-to-vibrato transition is what separates expressive vibrato from the queasy, seasick quality of vibrato that begins on the attack. In hardware synthesizers this is addressed with a dedicated "vibrato delay" control; in DAWs it is typically achieved by automating the LFO depth from zero, using envelope-controlled LFO modulation depth, or using an LFO with a ramp-up (attack) shape that gradually increases amplitude after note onset. When layering multiple instruments with vibrato, staggering onset times and slightly offsetting rates between voices prevents phase locking — synchronized vibrato across layers collapses to simple pitch shifting rather than the complex beating and shimmer of independent oscillators.

Under the hood, audio-rate vibrato in digital systems is implemented as a variable-delay line whose delay time is modulated, or more directly as real-time pitch shifting using phase vocoder, granular, or resampling algorithms. At very short, audio-rate modulation depths and speeds, the effect transitions from vibrato into frequency modulation, generating audible sidebands — this is the basis of FM synthesis. Understanding this continuum helps producers use vibrato parameters deliberately: sub-5 Hz, sub-100-cent modulation is vibrato; above roughly 20 Hz it enters FM territory, producing timbral rather than expressive changes. The boundary between the two is a powerful creative space, used deliberately on instruments like the Yamaha DX7 and its descendants to create evolving, inharmonic timbres from simple sine waves.

03 The Parameters

Every vibrato — hardware or plugin — operates on the same core parameters. Know these and you can work with any implementation.

RATE
Speed of the pitch oscillation cycle

Controls how many times per second the pitch completes a full up-down cycle, measured in Hz. Musical vibrato typically operates between 4–8 Hz; values below 3 Hz produce a slow, sea-sick wavering while values above 10 Hz enter an unnatural flutter territory. Rate should generally sync to the emotional context — faster rates for intensity, slower for warmth.

DEPTH
Width of pitch deviation from center

Determines how far the pitch swings above and below the center frequency, measured in cents or semitones. Subtle vibrato for backing vocals sits around ±15–25 cents; expressive leads use ±30–60 cents. Depths exceeding ±100 cents (one semitone each direction) tend to sound out-of-tune rather than expressive, especially on bass-register instruments.

WAVEFORM
Shape of the LFO modulation signal

Determines the character of the pitch movement. Sine wave is standard for organic vibrato, closely matching natural voice and string behavior. Triangle gives a more mechanical linear sweep. Random/S&H creates glitchy, stepped pitch variation. Saw waves produce directional asymmetry — useful for dive effects but rarely for traditional vibrato.

DELAY / ONSET
Time before vibrato engages after note start

Sets the latency between note-on and the moment the LFO modulation begins, typically ranging from 0 ms to 600 ms. This parameter is critical for realism — natural performers always begin straight before adding vibrato. Setting onset delay to 150–300 ms dramatically increases the naturalness of sampled instruments and synth lead lines.

LFO ATTACK / RAMP
Fade-in time of the vibrato depth

Controls how quickly the LFO modulation rises from zero depth to the set depth value, distinct from onset delay. A 200–400 ms ramp produces a smooth, gradual vibrato entrance rather than an abrupt full-depth engagement at the delay point. Combining onset delay with a gentle ramp closely models classical vibrato technique and is essential for convincing virtual instruments.

PHASE / STEREO SPREAD
Phase offset between left and right LFO channels

In stereo vibrato implementations, this parameter shifts the LFO phase between L and R channels, creating a mild pitch-beating effect that widens stereo image. At 180° offset, the left channel is descending in pitch as the right ascends. Subtle values (30–90°) add organic stereo movement; extreme values create an artificial, disorienting spin effect.

05 History & Origin

Vibrato as a deliberate expressive technique predates electronic music by centuries. Baroque string players documented the use of left-hand finger oscillation to ornament sustained notes as early as the 17th century, with Christopher Simpson's The Division Viol (1659) describing the technique explicitly. By the late 19th century, operatic singing pedagogy formalized vibrato as a core expressive tool, and the distinction between wobble (too slow, too wide), tremolo (amplitude-only), and true vibrato (pitch-centered, controlled) became a fixture of conservatory instruction. The physiological mechanism — oscillating subglottal pressure and the cricothyroid muscle's fine tension adjustments — was characterized in detail by William Vennard in Singing: The Mechanism and the Technic (1967), establishing the scientific foundation that later informed electronic vibrato modeling.

Electronic vibrato emerged alongside early voltage-controlled synthesis. The Hammond Organ, introduced in 1935, implemented a mechanical vibrato scanner — a rotating capacitive element that scanned across tone wheel outputs and introduced periodic pitch deviation. The Hammond's vibrato control was not an LFO in the modern sense but a physical scanning mechanism with a fixed rate of approximately 7 Hz, giving the instrument its characteristic warm shimmer. Leo Fender's guitar amplifiers of the early 1950s included circuits labeled "vibrato" that were technically tremolo (amplitude modulation), a mislabeling that persists in guitar amplifier nomenclature to this day. True pitch vibrato in guitar amplifiers arrived with the Magnatone Amplifier Company's models from 1956 onward, which used a frequency-modulating varistor circuit that genuinely varied pitch — an approach admired by Lonnie Mack and Buddy Holly.

Synthesizer-based vibrato in modern form crystallized with the Minimoog (1970), which provided a dedicated LFO section (referred to as the "oscillator modulation" circuit) capable of routing a low-frequency sine or triangle wave to oscillator pitch. Bob Moog's 1V/oct pitch standard made LFO modulation depth predictable and calibratable across instruments. The Roland Juno-106 (1984) and Yamaha DX7 (1983) brought vibrato to mass-market polyphonic synthesis — the DX7's operator vibrato, driven by the global LFO section, defined the characteristic shimmering leads of 1980s pop production. Engineers like David Bowie collaborator Tony Visconti and Michael Jackson producer Quincy Jones shaped records where synth vibrato was a primary textural element, audible on tracks like "Billie Jean" (1982) and "Let's Dance" (1983).

In the digital audio workstation era, vibrato shifted from dedicated hardware to plug-in and native instrument parameters. The introduction of Melodyne by Celemony in 2001 — and its later DNA (Direct Note Access) version in 2008 — gave producers the ability to edit, reduce, or add vibrato to existing recorded audio with precision previously impossible outside of re-recording. This transformed vibrato from a performance attribute into a post-production variable: a vocal with excessive natural vibrato could be tightened, while a straight-toned sample could receive tastefully shaped modulation after the fact. Auto-Tune, introduced by Antares in 1997, also interacted with natural vibrato in complex ways, and its retune speed parameter became a de facto vibrato-shaping tool when set at intermediate speeds — slower retune speeds preserve natural vibrato while faster settings clamp it, a distinction central to the difference between corrective and creative pitch processing.

06 How Producers Use It

For synthesizer leads and pads, vibrato is most effectively applied at the voice level rather than post-processing, preserving the interaction between pitch modulation and any filter or waveshaper in the signal chain. On a subtractive synth lead, routing an LFO to oscillator pitch at 5 Hz with ±30 cent depth and a 200 ms onset delay transforms a clinical, static tone into something that breathes and sings. The key is using the LFO ramp or envelope-controlled depth so the vibrato grows in naturally — starting a sustained lead note with full vibrato depth sounds mechanical and draws unflattering attention. On pads, keeping depth below ±20 cents and rate between 4–5 Hz produces slow harmonic movement that adds width and life without sounding deliberately manipulated.

Vocal production workflows divide into two camps: shaping natural vibrato in recorded performances versus adding synthetic vibrato to straight-toned or pitch-corrected vocals. For recorded vocals, Melodyne's pitch modulation envelope is the industry-standard tool — it allows precise reduction of vibrato in verses for intimacy and preservation or enhancement in choruses for power. Auto-Tune's retune speed interacts with natural vibrato: a speed of 20–25 is slow enough to preserve natural modulation while correcting drift, while speeds of 5–10 begin to clamp the vibrato, creating the characteristic Auto-Tune effect. For adding vibrato to straight vocals or heavily pitch-corrected tracks, a subtle pitch LFO via a pitch shifter plug-in (Waves SoundShifter, Eventide H3000, or Antares Choir) with onset delay automation is preferable to applying vibrato at the channel level, as it allows per-phrase control.

Guitar and bass guitar vibrato in production contexts typically come from the performance itself — a guitarist's finger vibrato, a whammy bar, or a Bigsby tailpiece — but producers working with DI re-amped tracks, modeling amplifiers, or virtual guitar instruments use plug-in vibrato to add expression to otherwise stiff MIDI guitar parts. IK Multimedia AmpliTube and Neural DSP Archetype plugins include vibrato circuits modeled on Magnatone-style pitch deviation. On bass guitar, vibrato should be used sparingly and only on higher-register fills or solos; applying pitch modulation to the fundamental bass notes below 200 Hz creates mix instability and can cause phase issues with kick drum fundamentals.

String and orchestral library production relies heavily on vibrato type and intensity switching. Most professional libraries (Spitfire Audio, EastWest Hollywood Strings, Vienna Symphonic Library) include multiple vibrato presets — non-vibrato (sul tasto), light vibrato, and full vibrato — either as separate articulations or as cross-faded layers controlled by CC11 (expression) or CC21. The producer's skill lies in automating vibrato intensity across a phrase rather than selecting a static layer: building from non-vibrato at the note attack to full vibrato at the sustain, then releasing back to non-vibrato at the phrase tail, produces a performance that closely mimics human string playing. Adding a small amount of additional LFO vibrato on top of a lightly vibratoed library string often produces more realism than using the full-vibrato articulation alone, because the library's baked-in vibrato rate is fixed while an added LFO can be tempo-varied.

AbletonUse Operator's LFO section routed to oscillator pitch for synth vibrato; set LFO type to Sine, activate Retrigger for consistent onset, and use Envelope > attack on the LFO amount to ramp depth in. For audio-rate pitch vibrato on samples, Ableton's Pitch plug-in in combination with an LFO MIDI device works cleanly — automate the Pitch Semitones parameter via LFO MIDI at audio rate.
FL StudioIn FL's native synthesizers (Harmor, Sytrus), route the internal LFO to pitch with a sine waveform and use the LFO attack knob for ramp-in. For live audio processing, PitchShifter on an insert with automation of the coarse/fine pitch parameter via a Pattern clip works well. The Vocodex plugin can also create formant-stable vibrato on vocals using its pitch modulation section.
Logic ProLogic's Modulator plug-in (LFO mode) can be MIDI-controlled and routed as a MIDI plugin ahead of any instrument for pitch vibrato. Within ES2, assign LFO1 to pitch with Sine waveform and use the ModPad to dial in depth dynamically. For audio vibrato on vocals or instruments, the built-in Pitch Shifter II with subtle automation (±15–30 cents at 5 Hz) is effective; automate the Semitones fine parameter with a drawn automation curve.
Pro ToolsPro Tools lacks a stock dedicated vibrato effect, so the standard workflow is Antares Auto-Tune Pro (set to graphical mode with manual pitch lane drawing) or the Eventide Pitch Factor plugin for expressive vibrato on audio. For MIDI instruments, route an LFO from Xpand!2 or Structure to pitch modulation. The free Tal-Vibrato VST3 is widely used in Pro Tools sessions for a dedicated, low-latency vibrato insert.
ReaperReaper's ReaPitch plug-in supports pitch modulation via parameter modulation — right-click any parameter, select "Parameter Modulation," choose LFO, set shape to Sine, frequency to 5 Hz, and depth in semitones. This routes Reaper's built-in modulation engine directly to ReaPitch without any additional plugins, making it the most streamlined native vibrato implementation of any major DAW.

07 In the Wild

Abstract knowledge becomes practical when you can hear it in music you know. These tracks demonstrate vibrato used intentionally, at specific moments, for specific purposes.

Jeff Buckley — "Hallelujah" (1994)
0:48 onwards · Produced by Gary Lucas and Jeff Buckley (Columbia Records)

Buckley's vocal vibrato on the sustained notes of the verse is a masterclass in rate and depth control. Listen at 0:48 where he holds 'cold' — the vibrato begins approximately 200 ms after attack, rises gradually to roughly ±40 cents of depth, and runs at approximately 6 Hz. The onset and ramp are entirely natural and physiological, but the values map precisely onto ideal LFO parameters. The vibrato interacts with the sparse reverberant room, creating a shimmering halo around the pitch that no static tone could produce.

Pink Floyd — "Shine On You Crazy Diamond" (1975)
0:12–0:45 · Produced by Pink Floyd (Harvest Records)

David Gilmour's opening guitar solo uses finger vibrato at approximately 4–5 Hz with asymmetric depth — wider on the downward push than the upward release, a characteristic of finger vibrato versus mechanical vibrato. The tone from the Hiwatt DR103 amplifier and the Cornish pedalboard allows the vibrato's pitch excursions to modulate the amplifier's natural compression in real time, producing a secondary amplitude envelope that would not exist with a mechanically symmetric vibrato. Production assistant Brian Humphries captured this through close-mic'ing the cabinet with a Neumann U87, preserving the microtonal complexity of the effect.

Daft Punk — "Something About Us" (2001)
0:30 · Produced by Daft Punk (Virgin Records)

The lead vocoder/synthesizer melody uses subtle pitch vibrato, approximately ±20 cents at 5 Hz, added to what appears to be a Roland VP-330 vocoderized signal. The vibrato depth is conservative enough to sit inside the vocoder's carrier pitch uncertainty, making it feel like an organic performance quality rather than a deliberate effect. Compare the modulated synth lead to the unmodulated pads underneath — the contrast in aliveness is the textbook argument for light vibrato on melodic content over static harmonic backgrounds.

Aretha Franklin — "(You Make Me Feel Like) A Natural Woman" (1967)
1:10–1:45 chorus · Produced by Jerry Wexler (Atlantic Records)

Franklin's vibrato during the chorus demonstrates wide-depth, controlled-rate classical technique applied to gospel-rooted R&B. The rate holds near 5.5–6 Hz throughout the held notes while depth increases across the phrase — a performance dimension that Melodyne analysis shows as a progressive LFO depth automation from roughly ±20 cents at note onset to ±55 cents at the phrase peak. Jerry Wexler and engineer Tom Dowd used minimal compression on the lead vocal to preserve the dynamic envelope of the vibrato rather than flattening its natural amplitude variation.

Hans Zimmer — "Time" from Inception OST (2010)
2:30–3:00 · Produced by Hans Zimmer (Remote Control Productions)

The string ostinato section demonstrates vibrato layering at scale: sections of real strings from the London Symphony Orchestra performing with natural vibrato are layered with synthetic string samples whose vibrato rates are slightly offset (approximately 5 Hz vs. 5.3 Hz). The beat frequency between the two vibrato rates produces a slow, approximately 0.3 Hz amplitude modulation — an unplanned but characteristic beating that became central to Zimmer's orchestral-hybrid sound palette.

08 Types & Variants

Natural / Physiological Vibrato
Human voice · Bowed string instruments

Produced organically by muscular oscillation — in singers via cricothyroid muscle tension variation, in string players via left-hand finger oscillation. Rate and depth vary continuously with emotion, dynamics, and technique, making natural vibrato the benchmark against which all electronic implementations are measured. Natural vibrato is inherently imperfect: rate and depth fluctuate slightly within phrases, which is precisely what makes it sound alive.

Mechanical Vibrato
Fender Jazzmaster tremolo arm · Bigsby vibrato tailpiece · Magnatone amplifier varistor circuit

Physically actuated pitch deviation using springs, rotating mechanisms, or reactive electronic components. The Bigsby operates at approximately 3–6 Hz depending on spring tension and wrist pressure, with a characteristic asymmetry — pitch drops more easily than it rises due to string tension physics. Magnatone-style varistor circuits produce a smooth, sinusoidal pitch modulation distinct from tremolo, making them the most accurate mechanical analog to LFO vibrato.

LFO-Driven Synthesizer Vibrato
Minimoog Model D · Roland Juno-106 · Yamaha DX7

The standard form in electronic music production: a dedicated low-frequency oscillator modulates the pitch of one or more oscillators. Rate and depth are set by panel controls or MIDI CC, and the interaction between the vibrato LFO and any filter or FM operator in the signal path produces timbral coloration beyond simple pitch modulation. The Juno-106's global LFO-to-DCO routing means all voices share a single vibrato rate, giving the instrument its locked, chorus-like shimmer distinct from polysynths with independent per-voice LFOs.

Digital / Algorithmic Vibrato
Antares Auto-Tune · Celemony Melodyne · Eventide H3000

Implemented via real-time pitch shifting algorithms, these systems can apply vibrato to any audio source regardless of origin, and in the case of Melodyne, can add, edit, or remove vibrato from already-recorded material. Algorithmic vibrato quality varies significantly with implementation — phase vocoder-based systems can introduce spectral smearing at high depths, while high-quality resampling or granular-based shifters (Eventide, Serato Pitch 'n' Time) maintain transparency up to ±100 cents. Antares' AVOX Choir uses algorithmic vibrato with per-voice phase randomization to simulate ensemble spreading.

Tape / Wow-and-Flutter Vibrato
Ampex 440 tape machine · Studer A80 · cassette Portastudio at slow speeds

Unintentional but aesthetically significant pitch modulation from tape transport irregularities. Wow refers to low-rate, large-deviation flutter (below 4 Hz) while flutter refers to higher-rate, smaller modulation (above 6 Hz). In lo-fi and vintage-emulation contexts, tape wow and flutter are deliberately introduced using plug-ins like Waves REEL ADT, Softube Tape, or iZotope Vinyl, and the irregular, non-sinusoidal modulation profile produces a warmth that regular LFO vibrato does not replicate. The non-periodic variation is the key: it reads as mechanical imperfection rather than deliberate effect.

09 Common Mistakes

  • ✕
    Setting vibrato depth before onset delay — the queasy attack
    Applying full LFO depth from note-on produces a pitch-sick quality that sounds cheap and artificial, particularly on lead synths and sampled instruments. The fix is always to set onset delay (100–300 ms) and LFO ramp (100–200 ms) before dialing in depth. Every millisecond of straight tone at the start of a note increases perceived naturalness and pitch clarity.
  • ✕
    Using vibrato on low bass frequencies — mix instability
    Pitch modulation below 200 Hz creates audible pitch instability that conflicts with the harmonic anchor function of bass instruments. A bass line with vibrato pushes and pulls against chord tones, creating dissonance that reads as sloppy intonation rather than expression. Keep vibrato off fundamental bass notes; if a bassline has fills or solos above the 5th fret range, vibrato becomes acceptable above approximately 250–300 Hz.
  • ✕
    Stacking vibrato from multiple sources without checking interaction
    Sampled instruments often have vibrato baked into their recordings; adding additional LFO vibrato at the sampler level doubles the modulation, producing excessive depth and an unnatural, wobbling effect. Always audition a sample in solo before adding vibrato, checking with a pitch analyzer whether the source already carries its own modulation. If it does, either use the raw sample's natural vibrato or switch to a non-vibrato articulation before adding your own.
  • ✕
    Identical vibrato rate across all instruments in a mix — phase locking
    When multiple instruments share the exact same vibrato rate and LFO phase, their pitch deviations lock in unison, collapsing to a unison-detuned effect rather than the independent shimmer of natural ensemble performance. Offset vibrato rates by small amounts — 0.1–0.3 Hz between instruments — and use different LFO phase starts. The resulting beating between independent modulation cycles is the source of the warmth producers associate with large string or choir sections.
  • ✕
    Vibrato depth measured in semitones instead of cents — scale mismatch
    Some plug-ins default to semitone-unit depth displays, where a setting of '1' means ±1 semitone (100 cents) — far wider than musical vibrato in most contexts. Setting depth to '1 semitone' thinking it means '1 cent' produces a wildly out-of-tune wobble. Always confirm the unit system: cents are the correct unit for expressive vibrato, and anything above ±100 cents total excursion (±1 semitone) is outside conventional musical vibrato range.
  • ✕
    Applying vibrato post-reverb instead of pre-reverb — unnatural spatial cue
    When vibrato is inserted after reverb in the signal chain, the reverb tail is static while the dry signal modulates, creating an unnatural disconnect — as if the performer is wobbling in a frozen, non-responsive room. Vibrato should sit before any time-based processing (reverb, delay) so that the pitch modulation is reflected in the reverb tail, matching the acoustic behavior of a real vibrating instrument in a space.

In production, confusing the two leads to choosing the wrong processing tool for the desired result.

Rates below 4 Hz sound labored or unwell; rates above 8 Hz sound mechanical or anxious.

Celemony Melodyne is the industry standard for adding, editing, or removing vibrato from recorded audio. In Melodyne's pitch modulation envelope, you can draw in LFO-shaped pitch curves on individual notes with full control over rate, depth, and onset. Alternatively, a pitch shifter plug-in with automatable pitch parameters (Waves SoundShifter, Antares Pitch) can apply vibrato to a region by drawing a sinusoidal automation curve — less precise than Melodyne but faster for simple applications.
Check whether the vibrato is asymmetric — some pitch shifters and modulators bias pitch upward slightly, causing the apparent center pitch to drift sharp. Also verify that onset delay is set: without it, the pitch begins oscillating immediately from note-on, before the listener's auditory system has locked onto the center pitch, making even moderate depth sound uncentered. Finally, ensure the depth unit is cents, not semitones — a depth of '0.5' in semitone mode equals 50 cents, which is likely wider than intended.
Vibrato should be placed before reverb and delay in the signal chain so that the pitch-modulated source feeds the time-based processors. This creates reverb tails and delay repeats that reflect the pitch movement of the source, matching natural room acoustics. Vibrato placed after reverb modulates only the dry signal, leaving a static reverb tail that sounds unnatural — as if the performer is in a soundproof booth while the room is frozen. The exception is creative use: post-reverb vibrato can produce warped, hallucinatory effects deliberately.
A sine wave most closely matches the smooth, symmetrical pitch oscillation of natural vocal vibrato. Research on trained singers shows the instantaneous frequency curve is close to sinusoidal, with natural deviations in regularity that prevent it from being perfectly periodic. For maximum realism, use a sine wave LFO with a small amount of random modulation applied to the rate (±0.3 Hz variation) to introduce the natural irregularity of physiological vibrato. Triangle wave is acceptable but slightly mechanical; all other waveforms are specialized rather than naturalistic.
Yes, when a vibrato-processed signal is summed with a dry copy of itself — a common scenario with parallel processing, wet/dry knobs, or accidental double-tracking — the oscillating phase relationship between the modulated and unmodulated signals creates a continuously sweeping comb filter effect, similar to a flanger. This can sound like an unintended flanger rather than clean vibrato. Always monitor whether a vibrato effect is fully wet when applied in a send/return configuration, and ensure no unintended dry signal is leaking into the vibrato return bus.
