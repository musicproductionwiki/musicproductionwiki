The Complete Guide to Sidechain Compression

First published May 2026 · Updated May 2026 — full DAW setup section, genre applications, advanced techniques added
MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 14 min read Beginner → Advanced

Sidechain compression is one of those techniques that sounds like a niche trick until you understand it — and then you hear it everywhere. In dance music it's the pumping heartbeat that makes a room move. In pop mixing it's the invisible hand that keeps the kick drum punching through a dense arrangement. In broadcast it's how your voice ducks the background music automatically. The same underlying mechanism produces all of these results, from the most obvious effect in a Daft Punk record to the most transparent low-end management in a professional mix. Understanding sidechain compression is understanding one of the most versatile tools in the entire production toolkit.

Quick Answer

Sidechain compression uses an external signal — called the sidechain or key input — to trigger a compressor on a different signal. The most common example: a compressor on the bass guitar uses the kick drum as its trigger signal, so every time the kick hits, the compressor ducks the bass slightly, creating space in the low end for the kick to punch through. In dance music, the same technique applied more aggressively creates the iconic "pumping" effect where the entire mix breathes in rhythm with the kick drum. The sidechain signal triggers the compression; the sidechain signal itself is not affected.

What you'll learn: How sidechain compression works at the signal level, every major use case from transparent mixing to dramatic effect, step-by-step setup in Ableton, Logic, FL Studio, and Pro Tools, settings reference for every application, the records that defined the technique, genre-specific approaches, common mistakes, and three practical exercises from beginner to advanced.

How Sidechain Compression Works Beginner

A standard compressor has one input: the audio signal you want to compress. When that signal exceeds the threshold you've set, the compressor reduces its gain. The signal being compressed and the signal being analyzed are the same thing.

A compressor with sidechain capability has two inputs: the audio signal you want to compress (which runs through the compressor and gets processed), and a separate sidechain input that the compressor listens to but does not process. The compressor's gain reduction is now triggered by the sidechain signal rather than by the signal being compressed. When the sidechain signal exceeds the threshold, the compressor reduces the gain of the main signal — regardless of what the main signal is doing at that moment.

This decoupling of "what gets compressed" from "what triggers the compression" is the key insight. The trigger and the target are independent. This unlocks a creative and functional range that standard compression cannot provide.

Signal flow: the kick drum triggers the compressor's gain reduction, but only the bass guitar's level is affected. The kick passes through completely unprocessed.

Every Major Use Case Beginner

Kick and bass low-end management is the most practically useful sidechain application in mixing. The kick drum and bass guitar share the same frequency range (roughly 60–120Hz), and when both hit simultaneously at similar levels, they compete and cloud the low end. Sidechain the compressor on the bass to the kick drum signal: every time the kick hits, the bass ducks slightly (3–6dB of gain reduction), creating space for the kick's punch, then recovers between hits when the bass has the low end to itself. The result is a low end that feels both punchy (the kick lands clearly) and full (the bass is present in the spaces). This is standard professional practice in virtually every genre with prominent bass and kick.

The pumping dance music effect is the same mechanism applied aggressively. In house, techno, and EDM, a compressor on the full mix (or a pad, synth, or reverb bus) is sidechained to the kick drum with fast-to-medium attack, slow release (matching the tempo), high ratio, and deep threshold. The result is the entire mix visibly breathing in rhythm with the kick — elements duck on the downbeat and swell back up before the next hit. Daft Punk's "One More Time," Eric Prydz's "Call on Me," virtually every major house or techno release since the mid-1990s uses this technique as a compositional and rhythmic element, not just a mixing tool.

Vocal ducking in broadcast and podcasting automatically reduces background music whenever someone speaks. A compressor on the music bus is sidechained to the vocal or microphone signal. When the presenter speaks, the music ducks. When they stop, the music returns. The threshold is set just above the noise floor so that only actual speech triggers the ducking, not room noise. This used to require expensive hardware; modern DAWs make it a 30-second setup.

Reverb ducking keeps reverb tails from cluttering a mix in busy arrangements. A compressor on a reverb return bus is sidechained to the dry vocal or instrument signal. Every time the dry signal hits, the reverb ducks, preventing the reverb tail from smearing into new notes. Between notes, the reverb opens up and breathes. This creates mixes that sound spacious but remain intelligible — the reverb is present when you need the dimension but absent when it would cause problems.

Frequency-specific sidechaining (multiband/dynamic EQ) is a more advanced application where only specific frequency content triggers compression. Sidechain an EQ into the key input of a compressor, filtering it to pass only the bass frequencies: now your compressor responds only to low-frequency energy, not to the full spectrum. This lets you create a compressor that ducks only the bass content of a pad when the kick hits, while leaving the pad's midrange and highs untouched.

GR = Gain Reduction. Release time should generally align with the beat subdivision — at 120 BPM, one quarter note = 500ms, one eighth note = 250ms. Set release to recover cleanly between trigger hits.

Step-by-Step Setup in Every Major DAW Intermediate

Ableton Live: The most streamlined sidechain routing of any major DAW. Insert a compressor on your target track (bass, pad, reverb return). In the compressor, expand the Sidechain section by clicking the triangle. Enable "Sidechain" toggle. In the "Audio From" dropdown, select the kick drum track. Set "Audio From" post-FX or pre-FX as preferred (pre-FX is standard — you want the raw kick signal triggering compression, not a processed version). The compressor now reacts to the kick. Adjust threshold until you see 3–6dB of gain reduction on kick hits in the GR meter.

Logic Pro: Insert Logic's Compressor on the target track. Click the "Side Chain" menu in the plugin header and select the kick drum track from the dropdown. Logic handles the routing automatically — no send or return routing required. Enable the compressor and set your threshold, ratio, attack, and release as needed. The key input monitor button (headphone icon in Logic's compressor) lets you audition what the sidechain signal sounds like for diagnostic purposes.

FL Studio: Sidechain routing in FL Studio uses the Mixer. Route the kick drum channel to a mixer track. On the compressor plugin on the bass channel (Fruity Peak Controller or a third-party compressor with sidechain input), set the sidechain source to the kick's mixer track. Alternatively, use the Fruity Peak Controller connected to the volume automation of the bass channel — a more FL Studio-native approach that achieves the same gain reduction effect with slightly different character. Third-party compressors with dedicated sidechain inputs (FabFilter Pro-C 2, Waves Renaissance Compressor) simplify the process significantly.

Pro Tools: Create a bus send from the kick drum track to a bus (Bus 1, for example). Set the send to pre-fader so the kick's level doesn't affect the sidechain signal's strength. On the bass track, insert a compressor that has a key input (the stock Pro Tools Compressor II has this; most third-party compressors do too). Set the key input of the compressor to receive from Bus 1. Enable the key input. The compressor now listens to the kick on that bus while processing the bass.

💡 The Ghost Sidechain Technique

You don't need an audible kick drum in your mix to use kick-triggered sidechain compression. Create a silent kick drum track — a kick drum sample that you mute completely in the mix. Route this muted kick to the sidechain input of your compressor. The muted kick triggers compression rhythmically without being heard in the final mix. This is how producers achieve pumping or rhythmic breathing in tracks where the kick drum is removed, or where they want the effect on elements in sections where no kick is playing.

What the Pros Actually Do

📀 Record Reference

Daft Punk — "One More Time" (2000) — The pumping compression on this record is probably the most recognizable sidechain effect in music history. Thomas Bangalter applied aggressive sidechain compression to the mix bus — triggered by the kick drum — creating the rhythmic volume undulation that's become synonymous with French house music. The ratio is extreme, the release is tuned to the 128 BPM tempo, and the effect is so pronounced that it functions as a musical element rather than a mixing decision. Every pad, synth, and reverb tail in the mix breathes with the kick. The technique was not invented by Daft Punk — it traces back to early Roland TR-808 and TR-909 based house and techno productions from Chicago and Detroit in the 1980s — but this record brought it to mainstream global consciousness.

📀 Record Reference

Calvin Harris — "Summer" (2014) — A study in using sidechain compression to create rhythmic energy in a major pop production. The bass and pad elements are sidechained to the kick with moderate pumping — noticeable on headphones but not as dramatic as a pure house record. The sidechain release is carefully tuned so the pads swell back up just before the next kick hit, creating a sense of anticipation and forward momentum. This is sidechain compression as groove engineering: the effect is felt physically before it's consciously noticed. Harris and his production team use this technique on virtually every record — it's a signature of the commercial dance-pop sound of the 2010s.

📀 Record Reference

Dr. Dre — "The Next Episode" (1999) — A master class in transparent sidechain compression for low-end management. The bass line and kick drum coexist with remarkable clarity in a densely arranged track — neither dominates, yet both are fully present. This clarity in the low end is a signature of Dre's production style and is achieved through careful sidechain ducking of the bass against the kick, combined with precise EQ carving. The sidechain here is not an effect — it's infrastructure. Most listeners have no idea it's happening. The tell is to listen closely and notice that the bass seems to briefly step aside every time the kick hits, creating a rhythmic pulse in the low end that reinforces the groove without cluttering it.

Training Your Ear to Hear Sidechain

Listening Exercise 1 — Hearing Obvious Pumping

Put on Daft Punk — "Around the World" (1997) and listen specifically to the synth bass and the pads rather than the melody. Focus on what happens rhythmically to the volume of those elements in relation to the kick drum. You should be able to clearly hear the swell-and-duck pattern: elements drop in volume precisely when the kick hits and recover in the space between hits.

Once you can clearly hear the pumping, put on any track that doesn't use obvious sidechain compression — a jazz recording, a classical piece, an acoustic folk track — and notice the complete absence of that rhythmic volume modulation. The contrast makes the sidechain effect unmistakable. Now go back to the Daft Punk and try to identify which elements are being compressed and which aren't — the kick itself isn't pumping; it's triggering the compression in everything around it.

Listening Exercise 2 — Hearing Transparent Sidechain

Put on Kendrick Lamar — "HUMBLE." (2017), produced by Mike Will Made-It and focus entirely on the low end throughout the track. Listen for whether the 808 bass and the kick drum seem to share the frequency space or fight over it. Notice that despite both being very present and loud, neither obscures the other — the kick punches through clearly every time, and the bass remains full and present in between hits.

This is transparent sidechain compression doing its job invisibly. If you want to confirm it, try mixing a similar track without sidechaining the bass to the kick and listen to how quickly the low end becomes congested compared to a professional record. The muddy, indistinct low end that characterizes many bedroom productions is often simply the absence of this technique.

How Sidechain Differs Across Genres

House / Techno

Extreme pumping is compositional, not corrective. High ratio (10:1+), deep threshold (15–20dB GR), release tuned to tempo. The effect is the point — every element in the mix breathes with the kick in a way that's felt physically on a dance floor. Often applied to the entire mix bus rather than individual elements.

Hip-Hop / Trap

Transparent low-end management between 808 bass and kick. Moderate settings (4:1–8:1, 3–6dB GR), fast attack, medium release. The goal is clarity and punch, not audible pumping. Ghost sidechain technique (muted kick trigger) is common in trap for rhythmic bounce on pads without an obvious kick dominating the arrangement.

Pop / Commercial

Moderate pumping on pads and synths adds energy and rhythmic movement without the full house effect. Often blended subtly — audible on headphones, felt rather than consciously heard on speakers. Reverb ducking is almost universal in polished pop mixing to maintain intelligibility in dense arrangements.

EDM / Big Room

Aggressive sidechain on everything during build sections, with the effect ramping up in intensity toward the drop. Many producers automate the compressor threshold or ratio through a build section so the pumping becomes increasingly dramatic — a production technique as much as a mixing one.

Rock / Live Instruments

Sidechain used primarily for transparency rather than effect. Bass sidechained to kick for low-end clarity. Reverb ducking on guitars and vocals to maintain mix separation in busy sections. The technique is present but invisible — designed to preserve the feeling of live performance without the frequency congestion of un-processed recordings.

Podcast / Broadcast

Vocal-triggered ducking of background music is the universal application. Attack set slow enough to not cut off the beginning of words (10–30ms), release set slow enough to avoid jarring volume jumps (200–500ms). Threshold set to trigger only on actual speech, not on room noise or breathing.

What Most Producers Get Wrong with Sidechain

❌ Mistake 1 — Release Time Mismatched to Tempo

The most common sidechain compression mistake is setting the release time by ear without reference to the tempo. If your release is too short, the compressed element recovers before the next kick hit — creating a choppy, pumping effect even when you wanted smooth ducking. If your release is too long, the element hasn't recovered by the time the next kick hits — causing it to stay compressed throughout the track and lose energy. Calculate your release time mathematically: at 128 BPM, one beat = 469ms. Set release to approximately 400–450ms for clean recovery on every beat. At 90 BPM (one beat = 667ms), set release to 600ms. Your ears will confirm the right value, but starting from the math prevents obvious errors.

❌ Mistake 2 — Sidechain on the Kick Itself

A surprisingly common confusion: routing the compressor so that the kick drum compresses itself using its own signal as the sidechain input. This doesn't achieve sidechain compression — it's just regular compression with an extra step. The sidechain source must be a different signal from the one being compressed. The kick triggers compression on the bass, pad, or mix bus; the kick itself is not the target of the compression (unless you're doing a very specific effect where you want the kick to trigger its own ducking, which is unusual).

❌ Mistake 3 — Using Sidechain Instead of Fixing the Mix

Sidechain compression between kick and bass is a tool for refining an already well-mixed low end, not a solution to fundamental frequency conflicts that should be solved with EQ. If your bass and kick are fighting badly, aggressive sidechain compression will create audible pumping while the problem remains. The correct order: use EQ to carve distinct frequency spaces for the kick and bass first (boost the kick's click at 3–5kHz, cut the bass slightly at the kick's fundamental frequency), then use gentle sidechain compression (3–5dB GR) to add the final clarity. Sidechain as the first and only tool creates mixes that sound processed rather than professional.

❌ Common Myth — "Sidechain compression is only for electronic music"

Transparent sidechain compression between bass and kick is used in virtually every professionally mixed genre — hip-hop, pop, rock, R&B, country, film scores. The dramatic pumping sound is associated with electronic music because it's used as an obvious effect there, but the transparent version of the same technique is everywhere. If you're making music with a kick drum and a bass element sharing the low end, sidechain compression is relevant regardless of genre.

Advanced Techniques Advanced

Frequency-filtered sidechain: Insert an EQ before the key input of your compressor and filter it to pass only the frequency range you want to trigger compression. A high-pass filter at 200Hz on the sidechain input means only bass frequencies in the trigger signal cause compression — the snare's midrange content won't accidentally trigger the compression when it hits. This is essential for precise kick-triggered sidechain when the trigger track (a drum bus rather than an isolated kick) contains multiple frequency components.

Parallel sidechain compression: Rather than routing sidechain compression directly into your signal chain, use it in parallel — blend the sidechained (compressed) signal with the dry (uncompressed) signal using New York or parallel compression technique. This preserves the transients of the uncompressed signal while still achieving the rhythmic volume modulation of sidechain. The result is a pumping effect with more punch and less obvious artifacts than fully wet sidechain.

Multiband sidechain: Apply sidechain compression only to specific frequency bands of your target signal using a multiband compressor with sidechain input, or by routing your target signal through a crossover and applying sidechain compression only to the low band. The mid and high frequencies of your bass or pad remain unaffected while only the low end ducks on kick hits. This creates a subtler, more transparent effect where the kick punches through without affecting the character of the higher frequencies of the compressed element.

Sidechain on reverb tails specifically: Separate your reverb return into two channels — a dry reverb return and a sidechained copy. Apply sidechain compression only to the sidechained copy, blending it back in at a lower level. This creates a reverb that partially ducks on hits (clearing the attack) while maintaining some constant ambient presence (from the unsidechained channel). More nuanced than fully sidechaining all reverb, producing a more natural-sounding space that's still controlled.

Practical Exercises

Beginner Your First Kick/Bass Sidechain
  1. Open a session with a kick drum and a bass synth or bass guitar. If you don't have one, create a simple four-on-the-floor kick pattern (kick on every beat) and a sustained bass note playing throughout.
  2. Insert a compressor on the bass channel. Enable the sidechain input and route it to the kick drum track (exact steps vary by DAW — see the DAW setup section above).
  3. Set: ratio 4:1, attack 5ms, release 100ms. Lower the threshold until you see approximately 4–6dB of gain reduction on kick hits in the compressor's GR meter. You should hear the bass very slightly duck on each kick hit.
  4. Solo the bass and listen carefully while the pattern loops. You should hear a subtle rhythmic undulation — a slight volume dip on the kick hits. If you can't hear it at all, increase the ratio or lower the threshold. If it sounds extreme and pumping, reduce the ratio or raise the threshold.
  5. Un-solo and listen with the full mix. The kick should feel cleaner and punchier in the low end while the bass remains present. Compare with sidechain bypassed — the difference is often most obvious on headphones.
Intermediate Tempo-Synced Pumping Effect
  1. On a pad or synth track (sustained chords work best), insert a compressor with sidechain input routed to the kick drum. A pad track is ideal because the sustained nature makes the pumping effect clearly audible.
  2. Set aggressive settings: ratio 10:1, attack 1–3ms, threshold low enough for 12–15dB of gain reduction on kick hits.
  3. Now set the release time based on your track's BPM. Calculate: 60,000 ÷ BPM = milliseconds per beat. For 128 BPM: 60,000 ÷ 128 = 469ms per beat. Set release to approximately 420ms so the pad recovers just before the next kick hit.
  4. Listen to the result. The pad should dramatically duck on every kick hit and swell back up rhythmically. If it sounds choppy or stuttering, the release is too short — increase it. If the pad never fully recovers, the release is too long — decrease it.
  5. Experiment with different release times: try 1/8 note (234ms at 128 BPM) for faster pumping, or 1/2 note (938ms at 128 BPM) for slower, more dramatic swells. Each creates a different rhythmic character.
Advanced Reverb Ducking for Mix Clarity
  1. Set up a reverb send on a vocal track — a plate reverb with 1–2 second decay. Notice how the reverb tail from one word or phrase bleeds into the beginning of the next, reducing intelligibility in busy sections.
  2. On the reverb return channel, insert a compressor with sidechain input. Route the sidechain to the dry vocal channel (not the reverb send).
  3. Set: ratio 6:1, attack 1–3ms (fast — you want the reverb to duck as soon as the vocal hits), release 150–250ms (enough time for the reverb to open up between words/phrases but not so long it stays compressed throughout a phrase).
  4. Set the threshold so you see 8–12dB of gain reduction whenever the vocal is active. The reverb should dramatically duck when the vocal is singing and open up in the pauses between phrases.
  5. Listen to how the reverb still adds dimension to the vocal in the spaces (pauses, held notes, phrase endings) while staying out of the way during dense lyrical sections. Compare with the reverb uncompressed — the ducked version should feel simultaneously more spacious and more intelligible.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is sidechain compression?

Sidechain compression is a technique where a compressor is triggered by an external audio signal (the sidechain input) rather than the signal it's compressing. Most commonly used in electronic music to create the pumping effect where the bass or pads duck in volume every time the kick drum hits — the kick feeds the sidechain input of the compressor on the bass track, causing it to compress in response to every kick hit. The result is rhythmic breathing that glues the kick and bass together.

How do I set up sidechain compression in my DAW?

In Ableton Live: Insert a compressor on the bass track. Enable the sidechain section. Set the Audio From source to the kick drum track. In Logic Pro: Insert the Compressor plugin on the bass. Click the Side Chain dropdown and select the kick drum track. In FL Studio: Right-click the volume knob of the bass mixer channel and select 'Sidechain to this track', then select the kick. In Pro Tools: Use a compressor with an external key input (like the Avid Dynamics III), route the kick to a bus, and assign that bus as the key input.

What compressor settings should I use for sidechain compression?

For the classic pumping EDM effect: Ratio 4:1-8:1, Attack as fast as possible (0.1-1ms), Release matching the tempo (set so the compressor releases just before the next kick hit — typically 100-300ms at 128 BPM), Threshold set so the kick triggers 6-10 dB of gain reduction on the bass. For transparent kick-bass glue (not obvious pumping): Ratio 2:1-4:1, Attack 5-20ms, Release 80-150ms, Gain reduction 2-4 dB. The release time is the most critical setting — too slow and the bass never fully recovers; too fast and the pumping sounds erratic.

What is the difference between sidechain compression and volume automation?

Sidechain compression responds automatically and in real time to the kick drum's dynamics — it pumps harder when the kick is louder and less when it's quieter. Volume automation is a static reduction applied regardless of the kick's level. Sidechain compression is more musical and responsive; volume automation is more predictable and precise. Many producers use both: sidechain compression for the dynamic interaction, volume automation to fine-tune the overall balance between kick and bass in specific sections.

Can sidechain compression be used for things other than kick and bass?

Yes — sidechain compression has many applications. Ducking dialogue under music in video production (music compresses when speech is present). Ducking reverb under a dry signal (the reverb return ducks when the dry signal plays, keeping it clear). Ducking a synth pad under a lead vocal. Creating rhythmic pumping effects on any sustained element in response to a rhythmic trigger. Ghost sidechaining (using a silent MIDI-triggered click as the sidechain signal to create pumping without an actual kick drum in the mix). The principle — one signal controlling another's compression — applies wherever dynamic interaction between elements is desired.

What is ghost sidechain compression?

Ghost sidechain compression (also called 'fake sidechain' or 'MIDI sidechain') creates pumping compression without requiring an actual kick drum as the trigger. A MIDI instrument is set up to trigger brief, silent audio clips (often a very short sine wave burst) on every beat. This silent signal is routed to the sidechain input of a compressor on the bass or pad track. The compressor responds to this silent trigger and creates the pumping effect rhythmically — useful when the track has no kick drum, or when you want pumping that's independent of the actual kick's timing and dynamics.

Why does my sidechain compression sound unnatural?

The most common causes: Release time too slow — the bass doesn't recover before the next kick, so it stays permanently compressed rather than pumping rhythmically. Attack too slow — the compressor misses the kick transient and doesn't respond tightly. Threshold too high — the kick isn't triggering enough compression to create audible pumping. Gain reduction insufficient — try 6-10 dB for obvious pumping. Monitoring at low volume — pumping effects are most audible at higher monitoring levels. Check the release time first — it's the parameter that most determines whether sidechain sounds musical or broken.
