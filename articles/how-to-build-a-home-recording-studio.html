Gear Recording Studio Setup

How To Build A Home Recording Studio

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 · 21 min read · Verified May 7, 2026
Quick Answer — Updated May 2026

A functional home recording studio requires five things: a computer, a DAW, an audio interface, a microphone, and headphones or monitors. You can build a setup capable of radio-ready recordings for around $300. The biggest quality improvements come not from expensive gear but from room setup and basic acoustic treatment — both of which cost almost nothing.

Updated May 2026

Building a home recording studio is one of the most consequential decisions a musician or producer makes — not because it is expensive or complicated, but because getting it right from the start saves you from the expensive mistake cycle of buying the wrong gear, realizing the limitation, replacing it, and then finally buying what you should have started with. The same fundamental setup that captures radio-ready recordings can be assembled for $300. The gap between a $300 starter studio and a $3,000 professional setup is real, but it is smaller than most people assume — and far smaller than the gap between a $0 "use the built-in microphone" setup and a properly equipped $300 one.

This guide structures the home studio build around budget tiers, so you can see exactly what you get at each level, what the upgrade path looks like, and which pieces of gear deliver the most improvement per dollar spent. It also covers the things that matter most and get the least attention — room setup, acoustic treatment, and monitoring position — because getting those right costs almost nothing and improves your recordings and mixes more than any equipment upgrade.

The Core Components: What Every Studio Needs

A home recording studio, at its most essential, requires five things. Every additional piece of gear beyond these five is an upgrade to improve quality, capability, or workflow — but these five are the non-negotiables that define a functional studio.

1. A Computer

The central processing unit for your entire studio. Everything else connects to it. The specifications that matter for music production are CPU performance (particularly single-core speed for synthesizer plugins), RAM (16 GB minimum in 2026), and storage (512 GB SSD minimum). A laptop or desktop from the last four years with these specifications handles most home studio work without issues. For Apple Silicon Macs, even base-tier M-series chips handle large plugin counts that would tax mid-range Intel or AMD machines. See our guide to the best laptops for music production for specific recommendations across every price range.

2. A DAW (Digital Audio Workstation)

The software where you record, arrange, mix, and produce music. Logic Pro ($199, Mac only) is the best value professional DAW — it includes a world-class plugin suite, Dolby Atmos mixing tools, and a massive sample library. Reaper ($60, Mac and Windows) is the best value overall and runs efficiently on modest hardware. Ableton Live is the standard for electronic music and live performance. FL Studio dominates hip-hop and beat-making. GarageBand (free, Mac) is surprisingly capable for beginners and is a genuine starting point, not a toy. Your DAW choice is partly workflow preference and partly platform — Logic Pro requires a Mac.

If you are choosing your first DAW, our best DAW for beginners guide covers the full comparison of these options with specific workflow recommendations based on genre and experience level.

3. An Audio Interface

The hardware that connects professional microphones and instruments to your computer. It provides the preamp gain needed to bring a microphone signal to a recordable level, phantom power (+48V) for condenser microphones, and analog-to-digital conversion that your computer's built-in soundcard cannot provide at acceptable quality. For most home studios, a two-input interface is sufficient — you can record one or two sources simultaneously. The Focusrite Scarlett range is the dominant entry-level choice and with good reason: the preamps are clean, latency performance is reliable, and driver support is excellent across Mac and Windows. See the full best audio interfaces under $200 guide for specific recommendations at every budget in this range.

4. A Microphone

Your primary capture device. The type of microphone matters significantly for how it sounds and what it can capture well. Dynamic microphones are robust, handle loud sources well, and do not require phantom power — the Shure SM58 ($99) and Shure SM7B ($399) are industry standards. Condenser microphones are more sensitive, capture more detail, and are the standard for studio vocal recording — the Audio-Technica AT2020 ($99), Rode NT1 5th Gen ($249), and AKG C214 ($299) are common beginner-to-intermediate choices. Our guide to best microphones for home studio covers the full range of options with audio comparisons.

5. Headphones or Studio Monitors

How you hear what you are making. Closed-back studio headphones (Sony MDR-7506, Beyerdynamic DT 770 Pro) are the safest starting choice in an untreated room because they are not affected by room acoustics. Studio monitors sound more natural and are better for long mixing sessions, but they require acoustic treatment to be used accurately — an untreated room colors the sound in ways that lead to bad mixing decisions. Start with headphones if your room is untreated, and add monitors as you treat the space.

The most important thing nobody tells you: The biggest single improvement you can make to your recordings and mixes costs almost nothing. Proper room setup — monitor placement, listening position, and basic acoustic treatment — has more impact on the quality of your work than upgrading from a $99 microphone to a $500 one. Address the room before you upgrade the gear.

Budget Tiers: What You Get at Every Level

The following gear recommendations are organized by total budget. Each tier is a complete, functional studio — not a compromise waiting to be upgraded. The upgrades between tiers are real and worthwhile, but every tier can produce professional-sounding recordings when used correctly.

Tier 1: The $300 Starter Studio

This setup covers every essential component and is capable of recording vocals, acoustic guitar, podcasts, and basic instrument tracking at a quality level that is genuinely usable for release.

The Sony MDR-7506 headphones have been a reference standard in radio and studio environments for over 30 years.

What you don't get: The ability to record two microphones simultaneously, acoustic treatment, studio monitors, or the finer preamp quality and conversion accuracy of higher-end interfaces.

Tier 2: The $700 Intermediate Setup

At this level, you add a second input for stereo recording or simultaneous tracking of two sources, step up to better headphones or a first pair of monitors, and optionally add basic acoustic treatment.

The Scarlett 2i2 adds a second input, which matters if you ever record acoustic guitar while singing, or want to record a conversation. The DT 770 Pro provides a more accurate low-frequency representation that helps with bass and kick drum decisions during mixing.

Tier 3: The $1,500 Serious Home Studio

This is the level at which a home studio starts to genuinely compete with lower-end commercial studios for most recording tasks. The difference at this tier is primarily monitors and proper acoustic treatment — the two things that together allow accurate mixing in a home environment.

  • Audio Interface: Focusrite Scarlett 4i4 (4th Gen) — $219. Four inputs, balanced line outputs, MIDI I/O, and better headphone output than the 2i2.
  • Microphone: Rode NT1 5th Gen ($249) or AKG C214 ($299). At this budget level, a second dynamic microphone — the Shure SM7B ($399) — becomes a worthwhile addition for podcasting, broadcast-style vocals, and louder sources.
  • Studio Monitors: Yamaha HS5 ($399/pair) or KRK Rokit 5 G5 ($299/pair). These are the two most commonly recommended entry-to-mid monitors in this price range. The HS5 has a flatter, more clinical response. The KRK has a slightly scooped low-mid that some producers prefer for electronic music.
  • Headphones: Beyerdynamic DT 770 Pro — kept from Tier 2 or added here if not already owned.
  • Acoustic Treatment: At minimum, 4–6 broadband absorption panels ($200–$300 DIY or purchased), two bass traps for room corners ($100–$150). This is where the real money in Tier 3 should go.
  • DAW: Logic Pro or Ableton Live Standard ($449).

Total estimated cost: $1,400–$1,700 depending on DAW and monitor choice.

What changes at this level: You now have monitors for the first time, which — in a treated room — gives you a more natural and fatiguing-free listening environment for long mixing sessions. The additional inputs on the 4i4 allow simultaneous multi-mic recording. The acoustic treatment investment is the most impactful upgrade in this entire tier.

Tier 4: The $3,000+ Professional Home Studio

At this level, diminishing returns set in for most home producers. The improvements are real but incremental — better converters, lower noise floors, more accurate monitoring, and greater acoustic control. This tier makes sense if you are recording clients, releasing music at a competitive professional level, or working in audio for picture or broadcast.

  • Audio Interface: Universal Audio Apollo Twin X ($899) or Apogee Duet 3 ($595). At this level, the preamp quality and conversion accuracy are genuinely superior to Focusrite entry-level, and UA's Unison preamp technology provides hardware-modeled preamp character.
  • Microphone: Rode NT1 5th Gen plus Shure SM7B, or step up to the AKG C414 XLS ($699) — one of the most versatile large-diaphragm condensers available at any price.
  • Studio Monitors: Yamaha HS8 ($699/pair), Adam Audio T7V ($399/pair), or step up to Adam Audio A7V ($899/pair). The Adam Audio ribbon tweeter provides notably better high-frequency resolution than dome tweeters at this price.
  • Acoustic Treatment: Full broadband panel coverage of the mix position's reflection points, proper bass trapping in all four corners, and ideally a diffusion panel behind the listening position. Budget $500–$1,000 for proper treatment at this level, or significantly more for custom-built panels.
  • MIDI Controller: A 49 or 61-key weighted or semi-weighted MIDI keyboard (Arturia KeyLab 49, $249) dramatically improves the workflow for melodic and harmonic composition.

Total estimated cost: $3,000–$5,000 depending on the monitor and interface choices.

Acoustic Treatment: The Most Underrated Investment

Most home studio guides spend 80% of their words on gear and a paragraph on acoustics. This is backwards. Room acoustics have a larger impact on the quality of your recordings and mixes than any equipment upgrade below $2,000. A great microphone in an acoustically poor room produces worse recordings than a modest microphone in a treated room.

There are two distinct problems acoustic treatment solves, and they are often confused with each other:

Soundproofing vs. Acoustic Treatment

Soundproofing prevents sound from traveling between rooms. It requires mass — thick walls, decoupled floors, double-pane windows. It is expensive, typically requires construction, and is usually impractical in a rented space. Most home studios do not achieve true soundproofing, and most do not need it.

Acoustic treatment controls the behavior of sound within a room. It addresses reflections, standing waves, and reverberation time. It is achievable with relatively inexpensive materials and has a dramatic impact on recording and mixing quality. This is what you actually need.

What Acoustic Treatment Does

In an untreated room, sound from your monitors reaches your ears twice: directly from the speakers, and again from reflections off every surface — walls, ceiling, floor, desk, and screen. These reflections arrive milliseconds after the direct sound and interfere with it, causing certain frequencies to be amplified (peaks) and others to be canceled (nulls). The result is that what you hear at your mix position does not accurately represent what is on the track. You compensate for what you hear — making the mix sound right in your room — but it sounds wrong everywhere else.

Acoustic treatment reduces these reflections, making the sound at your listening position more accurate and more like what is actually in the recording. For more detail on implementation, see our dedicated home studio acoustic treatment guide.

The Priority Order for Treatment

  1. First reflection points on the side walls. Place absorption panels where a mirror on the wall would reflect your monitors to your ears. These panels have the single largest impact on monitoring accuracy.
  2. Bass traps in the corners. Low frequencies build up in room corners. Bass traps — thick, dense absorption material placed in corners — reduce this buildup and improve the accuracy of bass and low-mid reproduction.
  3. Ceiling reflection point. The ceiling reflection at the mix position is often overlooked. A panel on the ceiling directly between the monitors and your head makes a significant difference.
  4. Rear wall. Diffusion or absorption on the rear wall behind the listening position reduces the buildup of late reflections.
  5. General room liveliness. Bookshelves with irregular book depths, sofas, rugs, and curtains all add diffuse, natural absorption that reduces the overall reverberation time of the room.

DIY Treatment

The most cost-effective acoustic treatment you can build yourself uses rigid fiberglass or rockwool (stone wool) insulation panels, roughly 2 inches thick, in a simple wooden frame with fabric stretched over the front. A 2-foot by 4-foot panel of this construction costs approximately $30–$50 in materials and provides genuine broadband absorption. Acoustic foam tiles (the pyramid or wedge variety sold cheaply online) absorb only high frequencies and are largely ineffective for the low-mid problems that matter most in mixing — avoid them as your primary treatment.

Home studio layout: Monitors form an equilateral triangle with the mix position. Absorption panels sit at the first reflection points on side walls. Bass traps go in all four corners. The listening position is placed at approximately one-third of the room length from the front wall.

Room Setup and Monitor Placement

Monitor placement and listening position are the two most impactful free improvements available to any home studio. Most people set up their monitors wherever their desk fits and accept whatever they hear. This is a significant mistake. A well-positioned pair of $299 monitors will give you more accurate mixing information than a poorly-positioned pair of $1,000 monitors.

The Equilateral Triangle

Your two monitors and your head should form an equilateral triangle — all three sides equal in length. A common starting point is a 1-meter triangle (monitors 1 meter apart, your head 1 meter from each monitor). This places you in the sweet spot of both monitors simultaneously, giving you the most accurate stereo image and frequency response. Angle the monitors slightly inward ("toed in") so the tweeters point directly toward your ears.

Monitor Height

The tweeters should be at ear height when you are seated in your normal mix position. Most studio monitors are designed with this in mind, but monitor stands or isolation pads may be needed to achieve the correct height on a desk. Monitor isolation pads (foam or rubber platforms) also decouple the monitors from the desk surface, which otherwise acts as a resonant surface and adds coloration to the low frequencies.

Avoid Wall Proximity

Placing monitors close to walls — particularly the front wall behind them — causes bass buildup through boundary reinforcement. Every halving of the distance to a wall doubles the low-frequency buildup. Ideally, monitors should have at least 12–18 inches of clearance from the front wall. If this is not possible in your room, some monitors have a low-frequency shelf switch that compensates for boundary proximity — check your monitor's documentation.

Listening Position

Avoid placing your listening position at the exact center of the room length. The room's modal frequencies — standing waves — are strongest at the midpoint of each dimension. A position approximately one-third of the room length from the front wall (or two-thirds from the rear wall) tends to have more even bass response. This is a starting point — precise positioning benefits from measurement with a calibration microphone and software like Room EQ Wizard (free).

Headphone Mixing

If your room is untreated, headphone mixing is frequently more reliable than monitor mixing. Closed-back headphones eliminate room acoustics entirely and provide a consistent reference regardless of environment. The limitation of headphones is the unnatural stereo imaging — sounds appear inside your head rather than in front of you — and the tendency for mixes made entirely on headphones to sound too wide or too narrow on speakers. The best practice is to check your mix on both. For a complete guide to this workflow, see our article on how to mix in headphones.

The Software Stack: DAW, Plugins, and Utilities

Beyond the DAW itself, a functional home studio software stack includes a small set of essential plugin categories and utility software. You do not need an extensive plugin library to make professional recordings and mixes — a handful of well-chosen tools covers every essential task.

Essential Plugin Categories

  • EQ: Every DAW includes a built-in EQ that is more than adequate for learning and for professional work. Logic Pro's Channel EQ, Ableton's EQ Eight, and Reaper's ReaEQ are all capable tools. When you are ready for a dedicated plugin, FabFilter Pro-Q 4 ($179) is the industry standard for surgical precision and visual clarity.
  • Compression: Again, built-in compressors in any current DAW are sufficient to start. Logic's Vintage VCA, Ableton's Glue Compressor, and FL Studio's Fruity Peak Controller all work. For a dedicated plugin, FabFilter Pro-C 2 ($179) or the free Analog Obsession LALA are excellent options.
  • Reverb: Logic Pro includes the outstanding Space Designer convolution reverb. Ableton includes Hybrid Reverb. For third-party options, Valhalla Room ($50) is the best value professional reverb plugin available and is used on major-label releases.
  • Metering: A loudness meter is essential for mastering to streaming platform standards (typically -14 LUFS integrated for Spotify, Apple Music). Youlean Loudness Meter (free) is the standard tool for this.

Free Plugin Resources

Before spending money on plugins, exhaust the free options. Logic Pro's included plugin suite (Space Designer, Vintage series, Alchemy synth) is so comprehensive that many professional Logic users rarely reach for third-party tools. Our guide to the best free VST plugins covers the top free options across every essential category for Windows and Mac users.

Utility Software

  • Room EQ Wizard (REW): Free acoustic measurement software. Used with a calibrated measurement microphone (MiniDSP UMIK-1, $75), it maps your room's frequency response at the mix position and identifies problem frequencies. Invaluable for acoustic treatment placement and room correction decisions.
  • Splice or Looperman: Sample and loop libraries for building productions and finding reference material.
  • LANDR or iZotope Ozone: AI-assisted mastering tools for preparing releases. iZotope Ozone 11 ($199) is the most comprehensive option; LANDR is subscription-based and convenient for quick masters.

Recording Vocals and Instruments: Practical Setup

Gear and room setup are preconditions, but the actual recording session decisions — microphone placement, gain staging, headphone monitoring — determine the quality of the captured audio. These practices cost nothing and make a significant difference.

Gain Staging

Set your input gain so that a loud singing performance peaks at around -12 to -6 dBFS in your DAW. This leaves headroom for transients without clipping, and avoids the noise floor problems that come with setting gain too low. A common mistake is setting gain so conservatively that you record at -24 dBFS or lower, which brings up the noise floor significantly when you boost the track in the mix. The interface's gain knob controls the preamp — set it before you press record, not during.

Microphone Placement for Vocals

For condenser microphones (AT2020, NT1), place the microphone 6–12 inches from the vocalist's mouth, slightly above the mouth and angled downward. This position reduces proximity effect (low-frequency boost from close distance) and plosive energy (the "p" and "b" sounds that overload microphone capsules). A pop filter placed 2–4 inches in front of the microphone intercepts plosives before they reach the capsule. Recording away from room boundaries — ideally in the center of the room or in a treated corner — reduces room reflections in the recording. For a complete guide to vocal recording technique and microphone choice, see our guide on how to record vocals in a home studio.

Monitoring While Recording

Use closed-back headphones for monitoring while recording vocals. Open-back headphones bleed significantly into the microphone — even at moderate volumes — and will appear in your vocal recording. The interface's direct monitoring function (available on most audio interfaces) routes the input signal directly to your headphones with near-zero latency, which is critical for a performer to sing or play in time. DAW software monitoring introduces buffer-dependent latency that is often distracting or unusable for live performance.

Recording Electric Guitar

For electric guitar, a direct injection (DI) box or the instrument input on your audio interface allows direct recording. Combine this with an amp simulator plugin (Neural DSP, Line 6 Helix Native, or the free Guitar Rig 7 Player from Native Instruments) for a complete guitar recording solution without a physical amplifier. For those who prefer recording through an amplifier, microphone placement on the speaker cabinet — typically a dynamic microphone placed at the edge of the cone, 1–3 inches from the grille — determines the tone more than almost any other variable. See our full guide to recording electric guitar for detailed placement techniques.

Recording Acoustic Guitar

Acoustic guitar benefits most from condenser microphone placement at the 12th fret (where the neck meets the body), 6–12 inches away, angled slightly toward the sound hole. This position captures the balanced combination of string detail and body resonance without the excessive boom that comes from pointing directly at the sound hole. A second microphone on the body side can be blended for a more open sound. For a stereo acoustic guitar recording, an XY pair of condensers (two microphones crossed at 90 degrees, capsules as close as possible) provides a coherent stereo image that collapses cleanly to mono.

The Right Order of Upgrades

One of the most common home studio mistakes is spending money in the wrong order. The following priority list represents the order in which upgrades deliver the most improvement per dollar spent, based on the actual limiting factors at each stage of a typical home studio build.

Priority 1: Fix the Monitoring Chain First

Before upgrading any recording gear, ensure you can accurately hear what you are recording. If your headphones are low-quality consumer models (not studio reference headphones), replace them first. Accurate monitoring is the precondition for every other quality judgment — you cannot make good mixing decisions with inaccurate playback.

Priority 2: Acoustic Treatment

After headphones, acoustic treatment of your recording and mixing space delivers the next largest improvement per dollar. Even minimal treatment — four broadband panels at the first reflection points — transforms the reliability of mixing decisions. Build or buy these before upgrading any signal chain components.

Priority 3: Microphone

The microphone is your primary capture device, and the quality of the signal it captures propagates through every stage of your production. Upgrading from a built-in laptop microphone or a cheap USB microphone to even an entry-level XLR condenser (AT2020) is a dramatic quality step. Beyond that, the improvement from the AT2020 to the Rode NT1 is meaningful, and from the NT1 to the AKG C414 or Neumann TLM 103 ($999) is again real but less dramatic than the earlier jumps.

Priority 4: Audio Interface

The difference between a $119 Focusrite Scarlett Solo and a $899 Universal Audio Apollo is real in blind tests — the UA has better preamps, lower noise floor, and higher-quality conversion — but it is a smaller difference than between a good and poor microphone, and far smaller than between a treated and untreated room. Upgrade the interface after the room, headphones, and microphone are addressed.

Priority 5: Studio Monitors

Studio monitors are only as useful as the room they are in. Buying excellent monitors and placing them in an untreated room is a poor investment. Monitors belong later in the upgrade sequence, after at least basic acoustic treatment is in place. The combination of even modest monitors (Yamaha HS5, KRK Rokit 5 G5) in a properly treated room outperforms expensive monitors in an untreated space every time.

Priority 6: DAW and Plugins

The DAW and plugin stack are the last priority because a free DAW (GarageBand, Reaper trial) and free plugins are sufficient to make professional recordings and mixes. Upgrading to Logic Pro or Ableton Live Suite adds workflow efficiency, a broader included plugin library, and instrument and effect quality — but it does not fix problems caused by poor acoustics, poor monitoring, or poor recording technique. Invest in the physical studio first.

For producers focused on electronic music, beat-making, or AI-assisted workflows, the software stack becomes more central — see our AI music production tools complete guide for the current state of AI tools that integrate with this setup.

Practical Exercises

Beginner Exercise

Build Your Starter Signal Chain on Paper

Before spending any money, write down the complete signal chain from your voice or instrument to your DAW: microphone → XLR cable → audio interface → USB → computer → DAW. Research the price of each component at the Tier 1 level ($300 budget) and confirm the total fits your available budget. Identifying every component — including cables — before purchasing prevents the common mistake of ordering an interface and microphone and then discovering you have no cable to connect them.

Intermediate Exercise

Find Your First Reflection Points

Sit in your normal mix position with a mirror flat against the left side wall. Slide the mirror along the wall until you can see the face of your left monitor reflected in it — that point is the first reflection point and is where an absorption panel should be placed. Repeat for the right wall and the ceiling. Take photos of the three positions and use them to plan your acoustic treatment layout before purchasing materials.

Advanced Exercise

Measure and Correct Your Room Response

Download Room EQ Wizard (free), acquire a calibrated measurement microphone (MiniDSP UMIK-1 or similar), and run a full-range frequency response measurement at your mix position. Identify the worst peaks and nulls in the 60–250 Hz range — these are the modal problems your bass traps and panel placement need to address. Compare the measurement before and after adding or repositioning treatment panels, and document the improvement in dB at the most affected frequencies.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ What do I need to build a home recording studio?
The five essential components are a computer, a DAW, an audio interface, a microphone, and headphones or studio monitors. You can build a fully functional home studio for around $300 using an entry-level interface like the Focusrite Scarlett Solo, a condenser microphone like the AT2020, and studio headphones like the Sony MDR-7506.
FAQ How much does it cost to build a home recording studio?
A functional starter home studio costs $300–500 including a basic audio interface, a condenser microphone, studio headphones, and a free or low-cost DAW. A solid intermediate setup with better gear and basic acoustic treatment costs $700–1,500. A professional home studio with quality monitors, proper treatment, and high-end recording gear costs $2,000–5,000 or more.
FAQ Do I need acoustic treatment for a home studio?
Acoustic treatment is not required to get started, but it has a larger impact on recording and mixing quality than almost any equipment upgrade. First reflection point panels and corner bass traps are the highest-priority treatment, and even four broadband absorption panels dramatically improve the reliability of mixing decisions in a typical room.
FAQ What is the best DAW for home recording?
For beginners on Mac, GarageBand is free and capable enough for professional work. Logic Pro at $199 is the best value professional DAW on any platform. Reaper at $60 is the best cross-platform value and runs on modest hardware. Ableton Live is the standard for electronic music and live performance, and FL Studio is the most popular choice for hip-hop and beat-making.
FAQ What microphone should I buy for a home studio?
For a starter budget, the Audio-Technica AT2020 (around $99) is an excellent condenser microphone for vocals and acoustic instruments. At the $200–300 range, the Rode NT1 5th Gen offers a notably lower noise floor and more polished response. The Shure SM7B ($399) is the professional standard for broadcast-style vocals and sources that require a dynamic microphone.
FAQ Do I need studio monitors or headphones for mixing?
Both are valuable. Headphones are consistent regardless of room acoustics and are the better starting choice in an untreated space. Studio monitors sound more natural and are better for long sessions but require acoustic treatment to be used accurately. The best practice is to check mixes on both — start with quality closed-back headphones if your room is untreated.
FAQ What is an audio interface and do I need one?
An audio interface connects professional XLR microphones and instruments to your computer, providing the preamp gain, phantom power, and analog-to-digital conversion that built-in computer soundcards cannot provide. Yes, you need one — without an interface you cannot properly connect a studio microphone, will have unacceptable recording quality, and will experience latency problems when monitoring.
FAQ How should I set up my home studio room?
Place your monitors at ear height, equidistant from your listening position, with your seat forming an equilateral triangle with the speakers. Keep monitors at least 12–18 inches from the front wall to avoid bass buildup. Place your listening position at roughly one-third of the room length from the front wall, and add absorption panels at the first reflection points on the side walls as a first priority.
