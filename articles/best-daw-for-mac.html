Best DAW for Mac

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 · 20 min read · Verified May 7, 2026
Quick Answer — Updated May 2026

For most Mac users, Logic Pro is the best DAW — it offers unmatched value at a one-time cost of $199.99, deep Apple Silicon optimization, and a professional feature set that rivals software costing three times as much. If you produce electronic music or perform live, Ableton Live Suite is the stronger choice. Beginners can start free with GarageBand and graduate to Logic Pro without changing ecosystems.

Updated May 2026. Choosing a DAW on Mac is one of the most consequential decisions you will make as a music producer or audio engineer. Unlike Windows-only shops, Mac users have access to the most tightly integrated, natively optimized software on the market — including the industry-standard Logic Pro, Ableton Live, and a genuinely viable free tier in GarageBand. But the landscape is more competitive than ever in 2026, with FL Studio's Mac performance improving dramatically, Pro Tools completing its subscription transition, and Bitwig Studio carving out a serious niche among modular-minded producers. This guide cuts through the noise with accurate, up-to-date assessments of every major option so you can spend less time researching and more time making records.

What Makes a DAW Great on Mac Specifically?

Not all DAWs are created equal on macOS. A DAW that runs brilliantly on Windows may introduce headaches on an M-series Mac — latency quirks, Rosetta 2 translation overhead, plugin compatibility gaps, or iCloud conflicts. Here is what actually matters when evaluating DAW performance on Apple hardware:

  • Apple Silicon native builds: Software compiled for ARM (M1/M2/M3/M4 chips) runs dramatically faster and consumes less battery than apps running under Rosetta 2 translation. By May 2026, Logic Pro, Ableton Live 12, Bitwig Studio, and GarageBand are fully native. FL Studio 21 added full Apple Silicon support in a 2024 update. Pro Tools achieved native M-series support in Pro Tools 2023.3. Cubase 13 and Studio One 7 are also fully native.
  • Core Audio latency: macOS Core Audio is extremely stable. Well-optimized DAWs can achieve round-trip latencies under 3ms at 64-sample buffer sizes on M-series hardware — critical for tracking live instruments and real-time synthesis.
  • Plugin compatibility: The shift from Intel to Apple Silicon created a period of VST/AU plugin fragmentation. In 2026 virtually all major plugin vendors ship Universal Binary or ARM-native versions, but edge cases remain — particularly with older iLok-protected titles.
  • macOS integration: Features like AirDrop project sharing, iCloud Drive syncing, Handoff, and Logic Pro for iPad (which shares projects natively with Logic Pro for Mac) add genuine workflow advantages that cross-platform DAWs cannot match.
  • Memory architecture: M-series unified memory means an M3 Max MacBook Pro with 128GB RAM can comfortably handle orchestral sample libraries that would require a dedicated Windows workstation with discrete RAM channels. DAWs that stream efficiently from disk (Logic, Cubase) benefit most.

With those criteria established, let us examine each major DAW in detail.

Logic Pro — The Mac Native Standard

Logic Pro remains the default recommendation for the vast majority of Mac users in 2026, and for good reason. At a one-time purchase price of $199.99 from the Mac App Store, it provides a feature set that competes with Pro Tools Ultimate and Cubase Pro at a fraction of the recurring cost. Logic Pro 11, released in 2024 and Updated through 2026, introduced Session Players (AI-powered virtual band members), Stem Splitter (on-device stem separation), ChromaGlow saturation plugin, and Mastering Assistant — features that in any other DAW would be paid add-ons or third-party plugins.

Core workflow strengths: Logic's linear arrangement workflow is deeply mature. The Smart Tempo feature automatically detects and conforms audio to project tempo without destructive time-stretching artifacts — a massive time-saver when working with live band recordings. Flex Time and Flex Pitch handle rhythmic and pitch correction natively, with Flex Pitch offering a visual interface comparable to Melodyne's Note Editor for basic tuning tasks. The Step Sequencer, introduced in Logic 10.5, brings pattern-based programming directly into the arrange window without the context-switching that plagues some DAWs.

Sound library and instruments: Logic ships with one of the most extensive sound libraries of any DAW — over 100GB of professionally recorded content including the Steinway concert grand (sampled with multiple microphone positions and sympathetic resonance modeling), the full Alchemy synthesizer (a genuinely world-class instrument with additive, spectral, and granular synthesis), Retro Synth, ES2, Vintage B3 organ, and the incredibly realistic vintage drum machines in Drum Machine Designer. Third-party producers routinely note that Logic's stock instruments outperform the bundled content in DAWs costing significantly more.

Apple Silicon performance: On an M3 or M4 MacBook Pro, Logic Pro is a performance showcase. Opening a 200-track orchestral session with heavy use of Alchemy and Space Designer convolution reverb runs at buffer sizes that would stall competing DAWs. The unified memory architecture allows Logic to keep enormous sample libraries loaded without disk streaming artifacts. Logic Pro for iPad shares .logicx project files natively with Logic Pro for Mac, enabling a genuine mobile-to-desktop workflow for capturing ideas.

Limitations to know: Logic's MIDI implementation, while functional, lags behind Cubase and Ableton when it comes to complex MIDI transforms and articulation management. The mixer channel strip design, while logical, can feel limiting when routing complex parallel processing chains — experienced engineers often reach for a third-party channel strip plugin. Logic also lacks a dedicated clip launcher (Session View equivalent), making it a weaker choice for live performance scenarios. The 64-bit summing engine is excellent, but Logic does not support AAF import/export, which creates friction when collaborating with Pro Tools users at the post-production stage.

Pro Tip — Logic Pro Free Trial: Apple offers a 90-day free trial of Logic Pro directly from the Mac App Store. This is one of the most generous trials in the DAW market — enough time to complete several full productions before committing. Download it alongside GarageBand and use the "Open in Logic Pro" feature to migrate your existing GarageBand projects instantly.

Ableton Live 12 — Built for Electronic Music and Live Performance

Ableton Live's dual-view paradigm — Session View for launching clips and experimenting with arrangements, Arrangement View for linear composition — remains the most elegant solution in the DAW market for electronic music production and live performance. Ableton Live 12 introduced MIDI Transformations (humanize, recombine, connect), Scale Awareness across the entire project, and deeper Max for Live integration. It runs natively on Apple Silicon and performs exceptionally well on M-series hardware.

Session View workflow: If you make beats, house music, techno, ambient, or any genre rooted in loops and patterns, Ableton's Session View is transformative. You can sketch out an entire song structure by triggering clips in real time, record the arrangement by performing it live into Arrangement View, and then refine the transitions. This top-down creative approach is fundamentally different from Logic's or Pro Tools' blank-canvas arrangement view, and many producers find it removes the paralysis of the blank arrangement.

Max for Live and modular thinking: Max for Live, included with Live Suite, allows producers to build custom instruments, audio effects, and MIDI processors using Max/MSP visual programming. In 2026, the Max for Live library includes thousands of community-created devices — generative sequencers, custom LFO shapes, spectral processors, and complete synthesizer architectures. This makes Ableton Live Suite arguably the most extensible DAW on the market. For producers interested in modular synthesis concepts without purchasing hardware, Max for Live is a compelling sandbox.

Pricing structure: Ableton offers three tiers. Live Intro is $99 but limits you to 8 audio tracks, 8 MIDI tracks, and 5 scenes — insufficient for serious production. Live Standard is $449 and removes those limits with 32 automation lanes and a reasonable plugin and instrument set. Live Suite is $749 and includes Max for Live, all instruments (including Meld, Drift, Mango, and the full Wavetable synthesizer), and the complete effects rack. Most professional users need Suite. Educational pricing reduces Suite to approximately $299. Importantly, Ableton offers free lifetime upgrades within a version but charges for major version upgrades — though historically these upgrades are offered at significant discounts to existing users.

Where Ableton falls short on Mac: Ableton Live's audio engine, while excellent, requires slightly more CPU than Logic Pro for equivalent track counts on the same hardware. The plugin delay compensation (PDC) implementation is solid but can occasionally cause issues with complex sidechaining setups — something that the sidechain workflow in Ableton guides address specifically. Ableton also lacks a score editor, built-in notation view, or audio-to-MIDI conversion as sophisticated as Logic's Flex Pitch. If you are recording live bands or doing post-production work alongside electronic music, these gaps will surface quickly.

Push 3 integration: If you are a Mac-based electronic producer, the combination of Ableton Live 12 Suite and the Ableton Push 3 hardware controller creates one of the tightest hardware-software integrations in modern music production. Push 3 can operate as a standalone instrument or as a deeply integrated DAW controller — and on Mac, the USB-C connection is plug-and-play with zero driver installation required.

Pro Tools — Industry Standard for Recording and Post-Production

Pro Tools maintains its position as the lingua franca of commercial recording studios, film post-production facilities, and broadcast environments. In 2026, Avid has completed the transition to a tiered subscription model, which has been controversial but also made Pro Tools more accessible at entry points that were previously unavailable.

Pricing in 2026: Pro Tools Intro is free with limitations (16 audio tracks, 16 MIDI tracks, no plug-in delay compensation on auxiliary tracks). Pro Tools Artist runs $9.99 per month or $99 annually — a reasonable entry point for serious hobbyists. Pro Tools Studio is $39.99 per month or $399 annually, and this is the tier most professional engineers will need for unlimited audio tracks, advanced automation, and full plugin compatibility. Pro Tools Ultimate (previously called HD) is $99.99 per month or $999 annually and adds HDX hardware support, surround and immersive audio, and advanced collaboration features.

Why Pro Tools on Mac matters: If you are engineering records at professional studios, doing ADR and Foley work for film and television, or mixing broadcast content, Pro Tools is essentially non-negotiable. AAF and MXF interchange, the Edit and Mix windows' proven layouts, clip grouping, the Tab to Transient feature, and the industry-wide familiarity with the session format mean that Pro Tools sessions travel between studios without translation issues. Learning to work fluently in Pro Tools is a genuine career asset — studio clients and music supervisors expect it.

Apple Silicon and macOS performance: Pro Tools 2023.3 and later run natively on Apple Silicon without Rosetta translation. Performance on M-series Macs has improved significantly, though Pro Tools' HDX hardware acceleration via Thunderbolt requires specific hardware configurations. The software-only mode (without HDX cards) performs well on M3 and M4 Macs at standard buffer sizes. iLok license management now supports iLok License Manager 6, which has improved stability on macOS Ventura and Sonoma.

Limitations for music producers: Pro Tools is engineered primarily for recording and mixing, not electronic music production. The MIDI sequencing environment, while functional, is significantly less inspiring than Logic's, Ableton's, or even FL Studio's piano roll. There are no built-in pattern-based instruments or clip launching paradigms. The bundled plugin set (Pro Tools comes with AIR instruments and effects) is adequate but not as deep as Logic's or Ableton Suite's included content. For hybrid producers who record live instruments but also program beats, Pro Tools works best as part of a two-DAW workflow rather than an all-in-one solution.

For a detailed direct comparison, see our Ableton vs Pro Tools breakdown, which covers workflow differences for hybrid producers in depth.

FL Studio 21 — Pattern-Based Production Now Fully Mac Native

FL Studio's relationship with Mac has historically been rocky. For years it ran via Wine-based translation with frustrating performance issues. Image-Line's full native Mac port, completed across FL Studio 20.9 and refined in FL Studio 21, has changed this dramatically. In 2026, FL Studio 21 on an Apple Silicon Mac performs comparably to its Windows counterpart — a remarkable engineering achievement.

The step-sequencer and pattern workflow: FL Studio's core paradigm — building patterns in the Step Sequencer and Channel Rack, then arranging them in the Playlist — remains one of the fastest workflows for beat-making in any DAW. The piano roll in FL Studio is consistently rated by producers as the most feature-rich of any DAW: per-note panning, per-note velocity, note grouping, articulation slots, ghost notes from other patterns, and a robust set of MIDI tools including the Strum, Flam, Chord, and Arp tools. For hip-hop and trap production, this workflow is hard to beat.

Pricing and the lifetime free updates promise: FL Studio's most compelling commercial proposition is lifetime free updates. You pay once and receive every future version at no additional cost. FL Studio Fruity (MIDI output only, no audio recording) is $99. FL Studio Producer Edition — the tier most producers need — is $199 and includes audio recording, basic plugin set, and full Playlist features. FL Studio All Plugins Edition is $499 and includes every Image-Line instrument and effect, most notably Harmor, Sytrus, Parametric EQ 2, Gross Beat, and Maximus.

Plugins and instruments: Image-Line's bundled instruments have always been a highlight. Harmor is a genuinely advanced additive/subtractive synthesizer. Sytrus implements FM synthesis with six operators plus a morphing filter. Gross Beat is the definitive time and volume manipulation tool for glitch, stutters, and rhythmic gating effects — essentially irreplaceable in certain production styles. Parametric EQ 2 is a clean, accurate surgical EQ that competes favorably with many paid third-party options.

Mac-specific considerations in 2026: Audio Unit (AU) plugin support in FL Studio on Mac is functional, and VST3 support is solid. However, some producers report that FL Studio's audio engine efficiency on Mac, while much improved, still trails Logic Pro on identical hardware — specifically when running many instances of CPU-intensive plugins simultaneously. FL Studio also lacks a dedicated score editor and its audio comping workflow is less refined than Logic's or Pro Tools'. That said, for pure beat-making and electronic production, the Mac version of FL Studio in 2026 is fully competitive.

Other Strong Contenders: Bitwig, Studio One, Cubase, and GarageBand

Bitwig Studio 6

Bitwig Studio is the DAW for producers who want Ableton-style clip launching with a more modular, technically adventurous approach. Bitwig's The Grid — a modular synthesis environment embedded directly in the DAW — is genuinely unlike anything else in the market. Every device in Bitwig can be opened and modified at the modular level, meaning you can rewire the internals of a synthesizer or effect in ways no other DAW permits.

Bitwig Studio 6 (2025) introduced Nested FX Chains, improved Arranger clip behavior, and expanded Note Expression support, allowing per-note automation of any parameter without leaving the piano roll. On Apple Silicon, Bitwig runs natively and performs excellently. Pricing: Bitwig Studio is $399 with a 12-month upgrade plan ($169/year after the first year). For sound designers, modular synthesis enthusiasts, and experimental electronic producers, Bitwig may be the most technically capable DAW on this list.

PreSonus Studio One 7

Studio One 7 is the surprise dark horse of this comparison. PreSonus has spent years refining a DAW that combines Logic-style single-window workflow with genuinely forward-thinking features: the Show Page for live performance, the Project Page for mastering (complete with DDP export and audio analysis), and native Stem Separation powered by Spleeter. Studio One 7 on Mac runs natively on Apple Silicon. Studio One Professional is $399.95 as a perpetual license or $14.99 per month. For recording engineers who want a professional workflow without the Pro Tools subscription commitment, Studio One is a serious option.

Steinberg Cubase Pro 13

Cubase Pro 13 at $579.99 (perpetual license) is the MIDI power-user's choice. Expression Maps allow incredibly granular articulation switching for orchestral sample libraries. The VariAudio pitch correction is competitive with dedicated tools. HALion Sonic SE 3, included with Cubase, provides a capable playback engine for sample-based instruments. Cubase runs natively on Apple Silicon and is the preferred DAW of many composers working in both film scoring and pop/rock production. The learning curve is steep, but the ceiling is extremely high.

GarageBand — The Free Starting Point

GarageBand deserves mention not as a consolation prize but as a genuinely capable free DAW. Available at no cost from the Mac App Store, GarageBand in 2025-2026 includes the Live Loops grid (a simplified Ableton-style clip launcher), Drummer (AI-based session drummer with 32 styles), a full Alchemy synthesizer subset, and Smart Controls. Tracks created in GarageBand open natively in Logic Pro — making the GarageBand-to-Logic upgrade path the smoothest beginner-to-professional transition in any DAW ecosystem.

For anyone completely new to music production on Mac, starting with GarageBand is the correct decision. For structured guidance on building your first productions, the best DAW for beginners guide covers GarageBand's workflow alongside other entry-level options in detail.

Choosing the Right DAW for Your Specific Workflow

The best DAW for Mac is ultimately the one that maps most closely to how you think about making music. Here is how to match your production style to the right tool:

If you record live bands and singer-songwriters: Logic Pro is the clear choice. Its Smart Tempo, Flex Time, Flex Pitch, and comping workflow handle all the realities of tracking live performances. The built-in drummer tracks and bassist allow you to sketch arrangements before the session, and the Space Designer convolution reverb with its built-in impulse response library includes genuine spaces from Abbey Road and other historic studios. The cost-per-feature ratio is unmatched.

If you make electronic music primarily: Ableton Live 12 Suite or Bitwig Studio 6 are the top contenders. For most electronic producers, Ableton wins on the strength of its ecosystem — Push hardware, the enormous Max for Live library, and the clip launching paradigm that matches how electronic tracks actually evolve. For producers with a modular mindset or serious interest in sound design at the synthesis level, Bitwig's The Grid is worth the slightly steeper learning investment. For guidance on specific electronic genres, see our best DAW for electronic music deep-dive.

If you make hip-hop and trap beats: FL Studio 21 on Mac is now a fully viable choice, with its unmatched piano roll and step sequencer workflow. Logic Pro is also strong here — many of the biggest hip-hop and trap producers use Logic's built-in drum machines and Alchemy for instrument patches. The choice often comes down to whether you prefer pattern-based composition (FL Studio) or a more freeform arrangement approach (Logic).

If you work in professional studios or post-production: Pro Tools Studio or Ultimate is the only answer. The career and collaboration benefits of Pro Tools fluency far outweigh the subscription cost for working engineers. Many professionals also keep a second DAW — Logic or Ableton — for personal creative work, and use Pro Tools exclusively for client sessions.

If you are a complete beginner on Mac: Start with GarageBand (free), use it to learn basic recording, MIDI programming, and arrangement concepts, and upgrade to Logic Pro when the limitations start to bite. This is genuinely the best onboarding ramp in music production. Alternatively, read our Ableton vs Logic Pro for beginners comparison if you are trying to decide which ecosystem to commit to from the start.

On laptop battery life and thermal performance: For producers who work on MacBook Pro or MacBook Air, battery and thermal management matters. Logic Pro is consistently the most power-efficient DAW on Apple Silicon — sessions that run for 3+ hours on battery are realistic. Ableton Live is slightly more power-hungry. Pro Tools and FL Studio, being more recently ported, can occasionally cause more thermal activity on complex sessions. If you frequently produce on battery, Logic's efficiency advantage is meaningful.

Plugin considerations across DAWs: All DAWs on this list support AU (Audio Units) — Apple's plugin format — and most now support VST3 as well. Logic Pro dropped VST support entirely but its AU ecosystem is enormous. If you have an existing VST collection from Windows, ensure the developers offer AU or VST3 Mac versions before committing. Most major plugin houses (Fabfilter, iZotope, Waves, Native Instruments, Arturia) ship Universal Binary versions of all their titles. Budget producers building their first plugin chains should check our best free VST plugins guide — many of these work across all Mac DAWs.

Computer hardware considerations: The DAW choice interacts with your Mac hardware. An M4 MacBook Air with 16GB of unified memory handles Logic Pro or Ableton Live comfortably for most productions. If you are running large orchestral template sessions with sample libraries exceeding 64GB loaded into RAM, an M3 Max or M4 Max Mac Studio or MacBook Pro with 64-128GB of unified memory is advisable regardless of which DAW you choose. For a complete hardware perspective, the best laptops for music production guide covers current Mac and PC options.

Two-DAW strategies: Many professional Mac-based producers run two DAWs — a primary creative tool and a secondary mixing or recording environment. Common combinations in 2026 include: Logic Pro (primary composition) + Pro Tools (mixing and delivery); Ableton Live (beat-making and live performance) + Logic Pro (recording and finishing); FL Studio (beat programming) + Logic Pro (mixing and arrangement refinement). This is not inefficiency — it is using each tool for what it does best.

Collaboration and file sharing: Consider how often you need to share sessions with collaborators. Logic Pro's .logicx format is widely understood by other Logic users and exports cleanly to AAF for post-production handoff. Ableton's .als files transfer seamlessly between Mac and Windows Ableton users. Pro Tools sessions are the universal commercial standard. If you frequently collaborate with Windows-based producers, Ableton Live or Pro Tools are the lowest-friction options — their session formats are cross-platform without conversion steps.

Mastering and delivery tools: Most Mac DAWs now include some mastering capability. Logic Pro's Mastering Assistant uses AI analysis to apply loudness-matched EQ, compression, and limiting. Studio One 7's Project Page provides a dedicated mastering environment with DDP export and ISRC code embedding. For producers who handle their own masters, these built-in tools reduce or eliminate the need for dedicated mastering software. If you need more advanced mastering capabilities, our guide to mastering a song at home covers both DAW-native and third-party approaches.

The subscription vs. perpetual license debate: In 2026, the music production software market has split into three commercial models. Perpetual license DAWs (Logic Pro, FL Studio, Bitwig, Cubase, Studio One) charge once — though major version upgrades typically carry a fee. Subscription DAWs (Pro Tools) charge monthly or annually and include all updates. Hybrid DAWs (Ableton) charge a higher one-time fee but charge again for major version upgrades (though existing users receive discounts). For budget-conscious producers, Logic Pro's one-time cost and FL Studio's lifetime update promise represent the best long-term value. For professionals whose software cost is a business expense, Pro Tools' subscription can be written off annually and ensures access to the latest versions without a capital expenditure decision.

Audio interface compatibility: On Mac, audio interfaces are generally plug-and-play via Core Audio — no driver installation required for most class-compliant USB and Thunderbolt interfaces. This is a significant advantage over Windows, where driver conflicts can create hours of troubleshooting. Focusrite Scarlett, Audient, Universal Audio, and MOTU interfaces all work seamlessly with every DAW on this list on Apple Silicon Macs running macOS Sonoma and Sequoia. For interface selection guidance, our best audio interface for home studio guide covers current options across all budget ranges.

Practical Exercises

Beginner Exercise

30-Minute GarageBand Beat Challenge

Open GarageBand on your Mac and create a complete 8-bar loop using only the built-in Drummer track, one software instrument, and one audio loop from the Sound Library. Export to AAC and play it back on headphones. This exercise proves you can create something finished without spending a dollar on software and teaches you the core recording-to-export workflow that transfers directly to Logic Pro.

Intermediate Exercise

Recreate the Same Beat in Two DAWs

Take a simple 8-bar beat you have already made in your primary DAW and attempt to recreate it from scratch in a trial version of a different DAW — if you use Logic, try Ableton, or vice versa. Pay attention to where the workflow slows you down and where it feels natural. This comparative exercise reveals which DAW genuinely matches your creative instincts rather than which one you simply learned first.

Advanced Exercise

Stress-Test Your DAW for Professional Sessions

Load your DAW with 60+ audio tracks, at least 4 instances of a reverb plugin, 4 instances of a compressor plugin, and 2 instances of a convolution reverb, then reduce the buffer size to 128 samples and attempt to record a new audio track without dropouts. Document the CPU and memory usage, then repeat the test at 64 samples. This benchmarks your current Mac-and-DAW combination against the demands of professional recording sessions and reveals exactly where your headroom ends.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ Is Logic Pro worth it for Mac users in 2026?
Absolutely. At a one-time cost of $199.99, Logic Pro delivers a professional feature set — including Alchemy, Flex Pitch, Smart Tempo, and AI-powered Session Players — that rivals DAWs costing three to five times as much. It is natively optimized for Apple Silicon and remains the best value in Mac music production software.
FAQ Can you run Ableton Live on an M-series Mac?
Yes. Ableton Live 12 runs natively on Apple Silicon (M1 through M4) without Rosetta translation. Performance is excellent, with low-latency operation achievable at 64-sample buffer sizes on M3 and M4 Macs.
FAQ Is GarageBand good enough for professional music production?
GarageBand is powerful enough to produce commercially released music — numerous chart records have been made with it — but its track and plugin limitations make it a stepping stone rather than a long-term professional tool. The seamless upgrade path to Logic Pro makes starting with GarageBand a smart strategy for beginners.
FAQ Does Pro Tools work well on Apple Silicon Macs?
Yes, since Pro Tools 2023.3, Avid's DAW runs natively on Apple Silicon without Rosetta translation. Performance has improved significantly on M-series hardware, though HDX hardware acceleration requires specific Thunderbolt-connected Avid interfaces.
FAQ Which Mac DAW is best for hip-hop and trap production?
FL Studio 21 and Logic Pro are the top choices for hip-hop and trap on Mac. FL Studio's piano roll and step sequencer workflow is unmatched for beat programming, while Logic Pro's stock drum machines and Alchemy synthesizer make it a powerful all-in-one option for producers who also record vocals and live instruments.
FAQ What is the best free DAW for Mac?
GarageBand is the best free DAW for Mac by a significant margin. It includes Drummer, Alchemy instruments, Live Loops, and a full recording environment at no cost. No other free DAW on Mac matches its depth, Apple Silicon optimization, or upgrade path to Logic Pro.
FAQ Can I use VST plugins in Logic Pro on Mac?
Logic Pro does not support VST or VST3 — it exclusively uses Audio Units (AU). However, virtually all major plugin developers release AU versions of their plugins, so the practical limitation is minimal. If you have VST-only plugins, a wrapper tool like Soundflower or dedicated AU wrappers may allow use in some cases, but native AU versions are strongly preferred.
FAQ Is Bitwig Studio worth considering over Ableton Live for Mac users?
For producers interested in modular synthesis, deep sound design, and a more open architecture, Bitwig Studio 6 is absolutely worth considering. The Grid modular environment is more powerful than Max for Live for pure synthesis, though Ableton's ecosystem — including Push hardware and community devices — is significantly larger and more mature.
