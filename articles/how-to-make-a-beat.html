How to Make a Beat: Complete Beginner's Guide

A step-by-step walkthrough of everything you need to know to make your first beat — from drum programming to export.

By MusicProductionWiki • Updated May 2026 • ~4,000 words
Quick Answer: To make a beat, you need a DAW (FL Studio, Logic Pro, or Ableton Live), drum samples or a drum plugin, and a basic understanding of rhythm and melody. Start by programming a kick-snare pattern, add hi-hats, build a bass line, layer a melodic element, then arrange the parts into a full song structure. Export as WAV when done.

What You Need to Make a Beat

You do not need a recording studio, expensive hardware, or years of music school training to make a beat. The barrier to entry has never been lower. Here is the minimum you need:

  • A computer: Mac or PC, ideally with 8 GB RAM or more. A laptop works fine.
  • A DAW: Your primary production software. FL Studio, Ableton Live, Logic Pro, or GarageBand (free on Mac) all work well for beat-making.
  • Headphones or studio monitors: Do not make beats through laptop speakers. You need accurate audio reproduction to make decisions about sound. Budget studio headphones like the Audio-Technica ATH-M50x or Sony MDR-7506 are around $100–150 and dramatically improve your ability to hear what you're doing.
  • Drum samples or a drum plugin: Your DAW will include these. You do not need to buy anything extra at the start.

Optional but helpful: a MIDI keyboard or pad controller lets you play in notes more naturally. An audio interface allows you to connect better headphones or studio monitors. But for your first beats, a laptop and headphones are enough.

Choosing Your DAW

The DAW is the software you use to make beats — it is your instrument. All professional DAWs are capable of producing chart-topping music. The best DAW for making beats is the one you will actually use, but here is a quick guide to the most popular options.

If you're on a Mac with no budget, GarageBand is a fully capable tool to start with.

Step 1 — Set Your Tempo and Key

Before programming a single note, set the two fundamental parameters that every element of your beat will relate to:

  • Tempo (BPM): How fast the beat moves. Hip-hop: 80–100 BPM. Trap: 130–160 BPM (though half-time drumming makes it feel half that speed). Lo-fi: 70–90 BPM. House: 120–130 BPM. Drum & Bass: 160–180 BPM. Start with a tempo in the range of the genre you're making. You can always change it — but having a BPM set from the start means every loop and sample you add will be in sync.
  • Key: The musical key your beat is in. If you're sampling, the sample dictates the key. If you're programming from scratch, choose a key that matches the mood you want. Minor keys tend to feel darker or more emotional; major keys tend to feel brighter and more uplifting. Common choices in hip-hop: A minor, C minor, G minor. Knowing your key means every note you add will be in scale and sound musical.

Step 2 — Program Your Drums

Drums are the engine of a beat. They establish the rhythm, the feel, and the genre. Start here — the rest of the beat should be built around the groove your drums create.

The Basic Pattern: Kick and Snare

In hip-hop and most popular music, the kick drum typically falls on beats 1 and 3, and the snare (or clap) falls on beats 2 and 4 in a 4/4 time signature. This is sometimes called the "backbeat" — and it is the foundation of nearly every genre of popular music from rock to hip-hop to house.

In your DAW's step sequencer (FL Studio's channel rack, Ableton's drum rack, Logic's Drum Machine Designer), activate the steps that correspond to these positions:

  • Kick: steps 1 and 9 in a 16-step sequencer (beats 1 and 3)
  • Snare: steps 5 and 13 (beats 2 and 4)

This is the most basic pattern. Loop it and listen. Even with just kick and snare, it should feel like something is moving forward in time. If it doesn't feel right at the basic level, changing the tempo or swapping samples is the fix — not yet adding more elements.

Adding Hi-Hats: The Groove Driver

Hi-hats fill in the space between kick and snare and establish the rhythmic density of a beat. Common patterns:

  • Straight 8ths: Every other step in a 16-step grid (steps 1, 3, 5, 7... etc). Basic, driving. Used in many hip-hop beats.
  • Straight 16ths: Every step. Energetic, machine-gun feel. Common in trap.
  • Offbeat: Steps 3, 7, 11, 15. Creates a shuffle feel with a lot of space.

The most interesting hi-hat patterns mix closed and open hi-hats. An open hi-hat on the 16th note before the snare (the "and" of beat 2) creates tension that resolves when the snare hits. This is one of the most used tricks in hip-hop drum programming.

Velocity and Swing: Making Drums Feel Human

Perfectly quantised, equal-velocity drums sound mechanical. Two adjustments make them feel alive:

  • Velocity variation: In your step sequencer or piano roll, vary the volume of individual hits. Hi-hats that land on strong beats (1 and 3) can be louder than the ones in between. Snares with slightly different velocities feel like a human drummer's natural accent variation.
  • Swing: Swing delays the offbeat 16th notes very slightly, creating a shuffle feel that makes the drums breathe. Most DAWs have a swing/shuffle percentage. 50–60% swing is subtle and musical; higher percentages get into jazz shuffle territory.

Add a percussion layer — shakers, rimshots, 808 hi-hats, or finger snaps — to fill in rhythmic gaps. Layering multiple percussion elements creates complexity and character. This is the difference between a programmed beat and a groove.

Step 3 — Build the Bass Line

The bass line is the melodic and rhythmic anchor of the beat. It occupies the low-frequency range (40–250 Hz) and works in tandem with the kick drum to create the physical feel of the music.

808s vs. Bass Plugins

In hip-hop and trap, the most common bass approach is the 808 — a synthesised bass/kick hybrid from the Roland TR-808 drum machine that has long, sustained, pitch-bending notes. 808s are controlled with pitch in the piano roll, which means you are simultaneously programming the bass line and the pitch of a long, resonant tone.

For other genres, a dedicated bass plugin or a sampled bass guitar works better. Popular bass synth plugins include Serum, Vital (free), and Arturia Bass Station. Splice and Loopmasters have extensive royalty-free bass sample libraries.

How to Write a Bass Line

Follow these principles and your bass will work almost every time:

  • Start on the root note: If your beat is in A minor, start the bass line on an A. The root is the most stable note and gives the bass line a strong foundation.
  • Follow the kick drum rhythm: Place bass notes where the kick hits. When both the kick and bass arrive together, the low end feels punchy and unified.
  • Keep it simple at first: The most iconic bass lines in hip-hop are often just 2–4 notes per bar, repeated with small variations. Complexity is the enemy of groove at this stage.
  • Use the minor pentatonic scale: In A minor, the minor pentatonic scale is A, C, D, E, G. Notes from this scale will always sound good together and always fit in your key.

Step 4 — Add Melody and Harmony

The melodic element is what gives a beat its emotional identity. In hip-hop, this is often a sampled loop, a piano riff, a guitar loop, or a synthesiser melody. In electronic music, it might be a chord progression on a pad synth or a lead sequence.

Using Samples

Sampling — taking a section of an existing recording and using it as the musical foundation of a new beat — is one of the defining techniques of hip-hop production. To use samples legally for release, you need to clear the sample with the original rights holders (which can be expensive) or use royalty-free sample packs from platforms like Splice or Loopmasters.

When you load a sample into your DAW, you need to match its tempo to yours. Most DAWs can warp or time-stretch samples automatically. In Ableton, turn on Warp mode. In FL Studio, enable Stretch in the sample settings. In Logic Pro, Flex Time handles this.

Programming a Melody from Scratch

If you're writing your own melody using a plugin or keyboard, open the piano roll in your DAW. Stay in key (only use notes that belong to your chosen scale — most DAWs let you highlight scale notes). Some principles that work:

  • Melodies that go up create excitement and momentum. Melodies that go down feel reflective or melancholic. Use this intentionally.
  • Repeat rhythmic motifs — a 2-note or 3-note rhythmic pattern that recurs throughout the melody creates memorability.
  • Leave space. Silence in a melody is as important as the notes. A melody that rests gives the listener's ear somewhere to breathe.
  • Loop the melody over your drum pattern and see whether it locks into the groove. If it feels like it's fighting the rhythm, adjust the note positions to fall more naturally on the beat or on the syncopated offbeats.

Chord Progressions

For a fuller, richer sound, add a chord pad or chord stabs beneath the melody. A simple two-chord loop (for example: Am – F, or Cm – Bb) gives the beat harmonic movement without complexity. Four-chord progressions (Am – G – F – E, for example) are common in pop-influenced hip-hop and drill. Use sustained pads for emotional depth, shorter chord hits for rhythmic energy.

Step 5 — Arrange the Beat

A loop is not a beat. A beat has an arrangement — an intro, verses, a hook, bridges, and an outro. When you send a beat to a vocalist or sell it online, it needs this structure. Here is a standard hip-hop beat arrangement:

  • Intro (8–16 bars): Usually just drums and bass, or a stripped version of the full beat. Gives the rapper room to set up the first verse.
  • Verse (16–32 bars): Full beat. All elements present.
  • Hook/Chorus (8–16 bars): Often a variation — elements added or removed to distinguish the hook from the verse. A melodic hook that builds can signal the emotional peak of the track.
  • Bridge (8 bars, optional): A break from the main pattern. Can be drums-only, melody-only, or a new chord progression.
  • Outro (8–16 bars): Strip elements back down. Let the beat breathe out.

In your DAW's arrangement or song view, drag your loops into this structure. Use automation to add variation — open up the filter on a synth for the hook, drop out the melody for the intro, add a reverb swell going into the chorus.

Step 6 — Basic Mix and Sound Selection

Before exporting, spend time on a basic mix so the beat is balanced and ready for a vocalist to rap over.

  • Volume levels: The kick and bass should anchor the low end. The snare should cut through without overpowering. Melodic elements should sit below the kick/snare in loudness.
  • EQ: High-pass filter everything that isn't kick, bass, or low percussion — cut below 80–100 Hz on guitars, synths, and pads to make room for the bass.
  • Sidechain: In trap and EDM, the bass is often sidechained to the kick so it ducks every time the kick hits. This creates the classic pumping sound and prevents kick and bass from clashing in the low end.
  • Reverb: A small room or plate reverb on snare and hi-hats adds space. Keep it subtle — over-reverbed drums sound amateur.

Step 7 — Export Your Beat

When the beat is finished and arranged, export it. The standard formats:

  • WAV, 24-bit, 44.1 kHz: The standard format for professional exchange. This is what you send to a vocalist, a label, or upload to a beat store.
  • MP3, 320 kbps: For preview files or social media. Not for distribution or professional use.
  • Stems/trackouts: Exporting each element as a separate file (kick WAV, snare WAV, melody WAV, etc.). This is what you sell as a trackout license — it allows the mixing engineer to use the individual elements.

In FL Studio: File → Export → WAV. In Ableton: File → Export Audio/Video. In Logic Pro: File → Bounce → Project or Section.

Practical Exercises

Beginner: The 60-Second Beat Challenge

Open your DAW. Set the tempo to 90 BPM. In the drum machine or step sequencer, program: kick on steps 1 and 9, snare on steps 5 and 13, hi-hat on every other step. Load a bass synth or plugin and play a single root note on beat 1 of every bar. Loop for 4 bars. That's your first beat. Add one more element — a piano chord on beat 1, a vocal chop, a lead synth note — and call it done. The goal is to complete something, not to make something perfect. Finishing trains a different set of skills than starting.

Intermediate: Flip a Sample

Go to Splice or another royalty-free sample library and download a soul or jazz loop. Import it into your DAW and set the tempo to match (use Ableton's Warp or FL Studio's stretch function). Chop the sample into pieces using the chop tool — isolate a piano chord, a horn hit, or a vocal. Rearrange those pieces into a new pattern. Build drums around the groove of the sample. The exercise teaches you to hear rhythmic possibilities inside existing recordings — one of the core skills of hip-hop production.

Advanced: Full Track Arrangement

Take one of your existing 4-bar loops and build a full 3-minute track around it. Use automation to create variation — filter sweeps, reverb throws, volume rides. Create an intro that's stripped back, a verse with the full loop, a hook with an added melodic element or a pitch shift, and an outro that fades or breaks down. Export as a full-length WAV. Listen back on three different playback systems — headphones, phone speaker, car speakers — and note what sounds good and what doesn't. That listening session will teach you more than any tutorial.

FAQ — How to Make a Beat

What do I need to make a beat?

At a minimum you need a computer, a DAW (digital audio workstation), and headphones. A MIDI keyboard and audio interface improve the workflow but are not essential for beginners. Free DAWs like GarageBand (Mac) or LMMS let you start with zero cost.

What DAW is best for making beats?

FL Studio is the most popular DAW for beat production, widely used in hip-hop and trap. Ableton Live is preferred for electronic and experimental styles. Logic Pro is popular for hip-hop and pop on Mac. GarageBand is a great free option for Mac beginners.

How long does it take to learn how to make beats?

Most people can produce a basic beat within 1–2 weeks of consistent practice. Getting good enough to produce professional-quality beats typically takes 1–3 years of regular production. The gap between a basic beat and a great one is mostly ear training and taste.

Do I need a MIDI keyboard to make beats?

No. Beats can be made entirely with a mouse using the piano roll in your DAW. A MIDI keyboard or pad controller makes entering notes faster and more expressive, but it is not required, especially at the beginning.

What BPM should a hip-hop beat be?

Traditional hip-hop is typically 80–100 BPM. Trap beats usually run 130–160 BPM, though the drums are often felt at half-time, making them feel slower. Lo-fi hip-hop typically sits at 70–90 BPM. Match the BPM to the feel you want — not to a rule.

What are the main elements of a beat?

The core elements of a beat are: drums (kick, snare, hi-hats), bass line, melodic loop or chord progression, and often a top-line lead melody. Additional layers like counter-melodies, pads, FX, and vocal chops add depth and character.

Where can I get drum samples for beats?

Splice, Loopmasters, and Cymatics all offer large libraries of royalty-free drum samples. Your DAW also includes built-in sample libraries. Native Instruments' Battery and other drum VSTs include extensive factory content that can be used immediately.

How do I sell beats online?

The most common beat-selling platforms are BeatStars and Airbit. You upload your beats, set prices for different license types (non-exclusive lease, exclusive, trackout), and collect payments automatically. Building a social media following and YouTube presence significantly increases sales.
