The Apogee Element 24 occupies a unique position in the professional audio interface market, combining Apogee's legendary converter technology with hands-on hardware control in a compact 2U rack format. Since its introduction, the Element series has attracted producers and engineers seeking studio-grade sound quality without the complexity of larger interfaces. This review examines whether the Element 24 delivers on its promise of professional performance in a streamlined package. Updated May 2026.

After extensive testing in various production scenarios—from tracking live instruments to mixing dense electronic arrangements—the Element 24 proves to be a compelling option for Mac-based studios prioritizing audio fidelity and workflow efficiency. However, its Thunderbolt-only connectivity and limited expansion options mean it's not the right choice for every producer.

Design and Build Quality

The Element 24 features a distinctive industrial design that balances form and function effectively. The 2U rackmountable chassis is constructed from robust aluminum with a matte black finish that resists fingerprints and scratches. At approximately 12 inches deep, it fits comfortably in standard equipment racks without protruding excessively.

The front panel is dominated by a large, multi-function controller knob that serves as the primary interface for adjusting levels, selecting inputs, and navigating menus. This knob provides excellent tactile feedback with a smooth, dampened rotation that feels precision-engineered. Surrounding the controller are dedicated buttons for monitoring functions, talkback, and input selection, all illuminated with clear LED indicators.

Two combo XLR/TRS inputs on the front panel provide convenient access for quickly connecting microphones or instruments without reaching around to the rear. These front-panel inputs include dedicated 48V phantom power switches and high-impedance instrument inputs, making them genuinely versatile for various recording scenarios.

Key Consideration: The Element 24 requires a Thunderbolt 2 or Thunderbolt 3 connection (with adapter) to function. There is no USB connectivity option, which limits compatibility to Mac computers and recent Windows machines with Thunderbolt support. Before purchasing, verify your computer's Thunderbolt capabilities and consider whether this connectivity restriction aligns with your current and future studio plans.

The rear panel offers a comprehensive but straightforward I/O configuration. You'll find six additional line-level inputs on balanced TRS connectors, eight line outputs (also TRS), stereo S/PDIF digital I/O, and MIDI in/out ports. The layout is logical and well-spaced, making cable management straightforward even in dense equipment racks.

One notable omission is ADAT connectivity, which means you cannot expand the channel count using external preamps with ADAT output. This design decision keeps the Element 24 focused and simple but may frustrate users who anticipate needing more than eight simultaneous inputs for larger tracking sessions.

Audio Performance and Technical Specifications

Apogee built its reputation on converter quality, and the Element 24 upholds that legacy admirably. The interface supports sample rates up to 192 kHz with 24-bit resolution, though most producers will find 48 kHz or 96 kHz perfectly adequate for professional work. The A/D and D/A converters deliver a dynamic range exceeding 120 dB, resulting in exceptionally clean recordings with minimal noise floor.

During critical listening tests comparing the Element 24 to interfaces in similar and higher price brackets, the Apogee consistently impressed with its transparent character. High-frequency detail remains pristine without harshness, and low-end reproduction is tight and accurate. The converters don't impose a particular sonic signature, which is exactly what you want from professional conversion—faithful reproduction of the source material.

The microphone preamps are genuine Apogee designs, not rebranded third-party circuits. They offer 75 dB of gain, sufficient for most dynamic and condenser microphones, including lower-output ribbon mics. The preamp character is clean and neutral with excellent headroom, though they lack the colorful saturation of vintage-style preamps. If you're seeking transformer warmth or tube harmonics, you'll need to add those characteristics through external preamps or software processing.

Latency performance is excellent, particularly when using the DSP-based monitoring features. With DSP monitoring engaged, you can achieve near-zero latency regardless of your DAW buffer settings, which is crucial for tracking performances that require real-time effects. Even when monitoring through your DAW at 128 samples, latency remains imperceptible for most applications.

The Element 24 demonstrates exceptional clock stability, an often-overlooked specification that significantly impacts audio quality, particularly in the digital domain. Jitter measurements are impressively low, contributing to the interface's clear, focused stereo imaging and precise transient reproduction. When connecting external digital gear via S/PDIF, the Element 24 can serve as master clock or sync to external sources with reliable lock times.

Software Control and DSP Effects

The Element 24 is controlled through Apogee's Element Control software, which runs on both Mac and Windows (though Windows support came later and requires specific Thunderbolt implementations). This software provides comprehensive control over all interface parameters, including routing, monitoring, DSP effects, and input configuration.

The interface design is clean and intuitive, organized into clear sections for inputs, outputs, and monitoring. The large virtual representation of the hardware controller provides visual feedback that matches the physical unit, creating a cohesive user experience whether you're adjusting settings via software or hardware.

One of the Element 24's standout features is its library of DSP-powered effects, which run on dedicated processing hardware within the interface. This means you can apply EQ, compression, and reverb to input signals during tracking without taxing your computer's CPU and without introducing additional latency. The DSP effects can be monitored in real-time while recording the dry signal, or you can commit effects to the recorded audio if desired.

The DSP effects include channel strip processing (EQ, compression, gate), guitar amp simulation, and reverb. The EQ is a parametric design with four bands plus high-pass and low-pass filters, providing musical shaping capabilities suitable for tracking. The compressor offers standard threshold, ratio, attack, and release controls with transparent to moderately aggressive character depending on settings.

For guitarists and bassists, the included amp simulations provide a range of classic tones from clean to high-gain. While these won't replace sophisticated amp modeling software like those found in dedicated guitar amp simulators, they're perfectly adequate for monitoring during tracking or sketching out arrangement ideas. The ability to monitor through these effects with zero latency while recording a clean DI signal is invaluable for maintaining creative flow.

The reverb processor offers multiple algorithms including rooms, halls, plates, and chambers. Quality is respectable, though not exceptional—these reverbs serve tracking and monitoring purposes well but lack the depth and richness of high-end dedicated reverb plugins. Still, having hardware reverb available for zero-latency monitoring is a significant workflow advantage.

Element Control includes comprehensive routing capabilities, allowing you to create custom monitor mixes with different levels and effects for each output pair. This flexibility makes the Element 24 suitable for small tracking sessions where musicians need individualized headphone mixes. The software can store and recall complete routing and effects configurations, which is useful when working on multiple projects with different setup requirements.

Workflow and Integration

The Element 24's workflow centers on the large controller knob and its integration with both the hardware and software. This controller can adjust virtually any parameter—input gain, monitor level, headphone output, DSP effect parameters—simply by clicking the relevant item on-screen or selecting it via the hardware buttons. This approach provides faster, more intuitive control than clicking and dragging with a mouse.

The hardware's talkback functionality is particularly well-implemented. A dedicated button activates talkback, routing your computer's built-in microphone (or an external source) to the output buses while dimming the studio monitors. This feature is essential when tracking musicians in separate rooms or isolation booths, and having it readily accessible on the front panel eliminates the need for separate talkback systems.

Integration with popular DAWs is seamless. The Element 24 appears as a standard Core Audio or ASIO device, so it works immediately with Logic Pro, Pro Tools, Ableton Live, Studio One, and other professional applications. Apogee has worked closely with Apple over the years, and this shows in the particularly tight integration with Logic Pro, where the Element 24's hardware controls can directly manipulate Logic's transport and track parameters.

For producers working with hardware synthesizers and outboard effects, the Element 24 provides sufficient connectivity for modest setups. The eight line inputs accommodate multiple stereo sources, and the eight outputs support multiple monitor systems or can route to external processors. When integrating external gear into a mix, understanding proper gain staging techniques becomes crucial for maintaining optimal signal levels throughout the chain.

The MIDI I/O is a welcome inclusion that many modern interfaces omit. Having dedicated MIDI ports means you can connect vintage synthesizers and hardware controllers without occupying USB ports or dealing with MIDI-to-USB adapters. The implementation is straightforward and reliable, with standard 5-pin DIN connectors that work with all MIDI-equipped hardware.

One workflow consideration is the lack of extensive metering within the hardware itself. While the Element Control software provides detailed metering for all inputs and outputs, there are no hardware meters beyond basic signal presence indicators. If you prefer monitoring levels without looking at your computer screen, you may find this limiting. Some engineers keep the Element Control window visible on a secondary display to maintain visual reference for levels during tracking sessions.

Real-World Performance Testing

Testing the Element 24 across diverse production scenarios reveals both its strengths and limitations. For singer-songwriter sessions involving vocals, acoustic guitar, and perhaps a keyboard, the interface performs flawlessly. The two front-panel preamps provide plenty of clean gain for condenser microphones, and the DSP effects allow performers to hear themselves with appropriate reverb and compression, enhancing comfort and performance quality.

Recording a full drum kit highlights the Element 24's channel count limitations. With only eight inputs total (two mic preamps plus six line inputs), you're limited to eight microphone positions unless you add external preamps connected to the line inputs. For many producers, eight channels suffice for basic drum tracking: kick, snare, two toms, overhead pair, and room mics. However, engineers accustomed to more extensive miking approaches will find this restrictive.

When tracking drums or any source requiring multiple microphones, the quality of your microphone placement techniques becomes even more critical with limited channel counts—you need to capture balanced, complete sounds with fewer microphones than you might use with expanded I/O options.

For electronic music production, where most sounds originate from software instruments or hardware synths connected via line inputs, the Element 24 excels. The pristine converters ensure that your carefully crafted synth patches translate accurately from the analog to digital domain. When mixing in-the-box, the transparent D/A conversion reveals every detail of your mix without coloration, which is exactly what you need for making accurate mixing decisions.

The low-latency DSP monitoring proves invaluable when recording virtual instruments via MIDI controllers. Even with large sample libraries that introduce latency within your DAW, you can monitor through the Element 24's direct monitoring path with no delay. This makes playing expressive passages on controllers feel natural and responsive, removing a common frustration when working with complex sample libraries.

In mixing scenarios, the Element 24 provides reliable monitoring with accurate translation to other playback systems. Mixes created while monitoring through the Element 24 consistently translated well to consumer playback systems, car stereos, and club sound systems. The interface's transparent character means you're hearing your mix decisions without significant coloration from the monitoring chain.

Stability has been excellent throughout extended testing. The Thunderbolt connection provides reliable, low-latency performance even during demanding sessions with large track counts and numerous plugins. Unlike some USB interfaces that occasionally disconnect or experience audio dropouts, the Element 24 maintained rock-solid connectivity through weeks of continuous use.

Comparison and Value Proposition

At $895, the Element 24 occupies a competitive price segment populated by interfaces from Universal Audio, Antelope Audio, and RME. Each manufacturer brings different strengths to this price range, making the choice dependent on your specific priorities.

Compared to Universal Audio's Apollo Twin series, the Element 24 offers more physical I/O and simpler operation, while the Apollo provides access to UAD's extensive plugin library and superior DSP effects quality. If you value tactile control and straightforward operation over expansive plugin options, the Element 24 is the better choice. However, producers who rely heavily on UAD plugins for tracking and mixing may find the Apollo's ecosystem more valuable.

RME's Babyface Pro FS offers comparable audio quality in a more portable format with excellent driver support across platforms, but lacks the Element 24's hardware controller and front-panel I/O convenience. RME's TotalMix software is more complex but also more powerful for routing, making it better suited to complex monitoring scenarios. The Element 24 counters with more intuitive operation and superior industrial design.

When compared to more affordable interfaces in the $400-$600 range from Focusrite, PreSonus, or MOTU, the Element 24 justifies its premium pricing through superior converter quality, more refined preamps, and better construction. The difference in audio quality is audible in critical listening, particularly in the clarity of high frequencies and the tightness of low-end reproduction.

The Element 24's value proposition ultimately depends on your platform and priorities. For Mac-based producers who value audio quality, tactile control, and workflow efficiency over extensive I/O expansion, it represents excellent value. The legendary Apogee sound quality, combined with genuinely useful DSP effects and intuitive operation, creates a package that enables creative work without technical obstacles.

However, Windows users should carefully consider the Thunderbolt requirements and ensure compatibility before purchasing. The lack of USB connectivity means the Element 24 won't work with many Windows computers, and even on compatible systems, Thunderbolt implementation can be less reliable than on Mac. If you work cross-platform or anticipate switching to Windows in the future, interfaces with USB connectivity offer greater flexibility.

One consideration that affects perceived value is the lack of upgrade path for expanded I/O. With no ADAT connectivity, you cannot add additional channels through external preamp units. If your production needs evolve to require more simultaneous inputs, you'll need to replace the entire interface rather than expanding it incrementally. For some users, this limitation makes interfaces with expansion capabilities more future-proof investments.

The Element 24's resale value has historically been strong, as Apogee products maintain their reputation and desirability in the used market. This factor partially offsets the higher initial investment compared to budget alternatives, as you'll recoup a larger percentage of your investment if you eventually upgrade or change systems.

When selecting any audio interface, it's worth considering how it fits within your complete monitoring chain, including studio monitor placement and acoustic treatment, as even the finest converters cannot overcome problems in the listening environment.

Practical Exercises

Beginner Exercise

Optimize Your Recording Chain

Connect a microphone to input 1, enable 48V phantom power if needed, and adjust the gain while monitoring the input meter in Element Control. Practice setting levels so peaks reach -12 to -6 dBFS, leaving adequate headroom while maintaining strong signal-to-noise ratio. Experiment with the DSP channel strip, adding subtle EQ and compression to hear how these processors affect the monitoring experience during tracking.

Intermediate Exercise

Create Custom Monitor Mixes

Set up a multi-source session with at least three different inputs (microphone, line instrument, and playback from your DAW). Using Element Control's routing matrix, create two different monitor mixes on separate output pairs—one for the performer with added reverb and compression, and one for yourself with dry signals. Practice quickly adjusting these mixes using the hardware controller while a track plays, developing muscle memory for the interface's workflow.

Advanced Exercise

Hybrid Processing Integration

Design a recording chain that uses the Element 24's DSP effects for tracking while routing selected channels through external analog processors before returning to line inputs. Create a configuration where you monitor through the DSP effects but record both the dry signal and the post-DSP signal on separate tracks, allowing you to blend or choose between them during mixing. Document the routing scheme and save it as a template for future sessions requiring this hybrid approach.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ Does the Apogee Element 24 work with Windows computers?
Yes, but with limitations. The Element 24 requires a Thunderbolt connection and works on Windows computers with Thunderbolt 2 or 3 ports. However, Apogee's Windows driver support came later than Mac support and requires specific Thunderbolt implementations. Compatibility is not guaranteed on all Windows systems, so verify your specific computer model's compatibility before purchasing. Mac compatibility is more straightforward and reliable.
FAQ Can I expand the input count on the Element 24 with external preamps?
You can connect external preamps to the six line inputs on the rear panel, effectively expanding your preamp options, but you cannot expand the total channel count beyond eight inputs. The Element 24 lacks ADAT connectivity, which means you cannot add digital expansion units. If you regularly need more than eight simultaneous inputs, consider a different interface with ADAT expansion capabilities.
FAQ How does the Element 24's sound quality compare to more expensive Apogee interfaces?
The Element 24 uses the same fundamental converter technology as higher-end Apogee products, delivering professional sound quality with exceptional transparency and detail. While flagship Apogee interfaces like the Symphony I/O offer additional features, expanded I/O, and marginally superior specifications, the Element 24's core audio quality is comparable. Most producers will find the Element 24's converters more than adequate for professional work, with differences from higher-end models being subtle rather than transformative.
FAQ Can I record with DSP effects printed to the track?
Yes, you can configure the Element 24 to record the processed signal with DSP effects applied, or you can record the dry input signal while monitoring through effects. The Element Control software allows you to choose whether effects are printed during recording or used only for monitoring. Many engineers prefer recording dry signals to maintain flexibility during mixing, but the option to commit effects during tracking is available when desired.
FAQ What latency can I expect with the Element 24?
When using DSP monitoring, latency is essentially zero (under 2ms round-trip), making it imperceptible for recording and performance. When monitoring through your DAW, latency depends on your buffer size settings—at 128 samples and 48 kHz, expect approximately 5-6ms round-trip latency, which is acceptable for most applications. The Thunderbolt connection provides consistently lower latency than comparable USB interfaces, particularly at smaller buffer sizes.
FAQ Is the Element 24 suitable for location recording?
The Element 24 can work for location recording if you have a laptop with Thunderbolt connectivity and don't require extensive I/O. However, it's designed primarily as a desktop/rack-mounted interface rather than a portable solution. The 2U chassis, while robust, is larger than truly portable interfaces. If location recording is a primary use case, consider more compact interfaces designed specifically for mobile applications.
FAQ Do I need the Element Control software running at all times?
The Element Control software does not need to remain open during normal operation—your routing, DSP effects, and settings are stored in the hardware and persist when the software is closed. However, you'll need to launch Element Control to adjust DSP parameters, modify routing, or change input configurations. The hardware controller provides quick access to essential functions like gain and monitor level without opening the software.
FAQ Can the Element 24 provide enough gain for ribbon microphones?
The Element 24's preamps offer 75 dB of maximum gain, which is sufficient for most ribbon microphones including lower-output models, though some exceptionally quiet ribbons might benefit from an inline preamp or cloud lifter for optimal signal-to-noise ratio. The preamps are exceptionally clean with low noise floors, so even when pushed to higher gain settings, they maintain excellent audio quality without objectionable hiss or artifacts.
