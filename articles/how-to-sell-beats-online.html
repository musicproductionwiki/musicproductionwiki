How to Sell Beats Online in 2026: The Complete Guide

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 13 min read Intermediate

Legal disclaimer: This article provides general information about beat licensing and online sales. It is not legal advice. Consult a music attorney for guidance on specific licensing agreements, copyright matters, and contract terms.

Selling beats online is a legitimate and scalable income stream for music producers. The beat marketplace has matured significantly — platforms like BeatStars and Airbit handle transactions, licensing, and file delivery automatically, and YouTube has created a massive discovery channel through type beats. Producers at every level, from bedroom producers to platinum-credited engineers, participate in the beat market.

This guide covers the complete process: understanding license types, setting prices, choosing platforms, marketing through YouTube and social media, and building a beat business that generates consistent income.

Beat License Types: What You're Actually Selling

Before setting up a store, you need to understand what you're selling. A beat license is not a sale of the beat itself — it is permission to use the beat under specific conditions. The type of license determines the price, the buyer's rights, and your future income from that beat.

Non-Exclusive Lease (Most Common)

The buyer pays for the right to use the beat commercially, but you retain ownership and can sell the same beat to unlimited other artists. Typically includes tagged mp3 (with your producer tag audible in the audio), untagged wav, and often track stems. Usage is limited by stream counts, sales numbers, or time period — common limits are 10,000–100,000 streams, 2,500 sales copies, or 1–2 years. The artist must credit you as the producer. You can keep selling non-exclusive leases indefinitely unless an exclusive is purchased.

Non-Exclusive Premium Lease

Same structure as standard non-exclusive but with higher usage limits (unlimited streams, more sales copies) and often includes stems in addition to wav and mp3. Priced higher to reflect the expanded rights. Suited for artists with growing audiences who expect to exceed standard lease limits.

Exclusive License

The buyer purchases full rights to the beat. You may not sell this beat to anyone else after the exclusive is purchased. The buyer gets all files, full stems, and typically unlimited commercial rights with no stream or sales caps. Priced significantly higher because you are surrendering all future income from that beat. Once an exclusive is sold, any existing non-exclusive lease holders typically retain their lease rights until they expire — clarify this in your licensing terms.

Unlimited / Buyout License

A non-exclusive license with no usage restrictions — unlimited streams, unlimited sales, unlimited time. The buyer has full commercial use rights but you can still sell the beat to others. Priced between premium lease and exclusive. Increasingly popular because it removes the anxiety around lease limits for working artists.

Pricing Strategy

Pricing beats is about perceived value as much as market rate. Starting too low trains your audience to expect low prices and devalues your work. Starting too high with no track record prevents sales entirely. The goal is pricing that converts buyers while reflecting the quality of your production.

Basic Non-Exclusive Lease (MP3)$20–$50
Non-Exclusive Lease (WAV + MP3)$35–$75
Premium Lease (WAV + Stems)$75–$150
Unlimited / Buyout$150–$400
Exclusive (new producer)$150–$500
Exclusive (established producer)$500–$5,000+
Pricing psychology: Create three tiers with meaningful value differences between them. A buyer who opens your store should see a clear progression — Basic for casual use, Premium for serious artists who need stems, Exclusive for artists who need full ownership. If the tiers are too similar in what they offer, buyers default to the cheapest option. Make each tier meaningfully better.

Your own website keeps buyers in your brand ecosystem, captures email addresses, and eliminates marketplace competition from appearing on the same page as your beats.

YouTube: The Most Important Marketing Channel

YouTube is where most beat buyers find producers. The search term format "Artist type beat + year" — "Lil Baby type beat 2026," "Drake type beat," "SZA R&B type beat" — generates millions of searches per month from artists specifically looking for beats to buy or record over.

Uploading type beats to YouTube with a visualizer (a simple image or animated artwork) serves two purposes: SEO-driven discovery (artists searching for type beats find your video), and social proof (view count and subscriber count build credibility).

1
Choose your type beat keywords carefully. Research which artist type beats have search volume without overwhelming competition. Newer artists (rising acts rather than superstars) often have less competition in search results. Target artists whose sound matches your production style — credible association builds brand trust.
2
Create a simple visual. A static image with the beat title, your producer name, and your store link is sufficient. Animated visualizers (waveforms, particles) perform better but are not required. Include your store link in the first line of the video description.
3
Include a producer tag at the beginning. A 1–2 second audio tag with your producer name ("This is a [Your Name] beat") serves as branding and as a reminder to buy before using. Tags should be subtle and brief — overly long or annoying tags frustrate buyers.
4
Upload consistently. YouTube's algorithm rewards channels that upload regularly. Two to four type beats per week is the sustainable target for most producers. Quality is more important than quantity — one great beat outperforms ten mediocre ones.
5
Use chapters and timestamps. If your beat has sections, add timestamps in the description (Intro, Verse, Chorus) so viewers can skip to the parts they want. This improves viewer engagement metrics that YouTube's algorithm uses to promote the video.

Social Media and Other Channels

TikTok and Instagram Reels: Short clips of your beats — 15–30 seconds of the most compelling section — perform well as short-form content. Behind-the-scenes production footage (making a beat in real time) builds audience connection and surfaces your work to non-searching discovery. The algorithm reaches people who don't know they're looking for beats.

Twitter/X: The music industry uses Twitter actively. Networking with artists, commenting on producers' work, and sharing your beats in music community spaces builds relationships that lead to collaborations and direct sales outside the marketplace. Reply to artists asking for producers in their tweets.

Email list: Every sale generates a buyer whose email you should capture. A list of 500 previous buyers who you email when you drop new beats converts at significantly higher rates than cold social media traffic. BeatStars Pro and your own store both allow email collection and automation — send a release announcement email every time you drop new beats.

Building a Catalog That Sells

A beat store with 5 beats signals a hobbyist. A store with 100+ beats signals a professional producer. Target 20–50 beats before actively marketing, then continue adding 2–4 beats per week. Catalog depth matters because:

  • Buyers browse — they want choice, not a single option
  • Different beats serve different artist needs, genres, and tempos
  • More catalog means more YouTube videos means more SEO discovery
  • Non-exclusive leases on older beats generate ongoing passive income without new work

Organize your catalog by genre, mood, and tempo. BeatStars and Airbit both support tagging and categorization — use them. An artist searching for "dark trap beats" who can filter your catalog to those specifically converts faster than one who has to scroll through 100 beats of mixed styles.

The Sample Problem

Selling beats that contain uncleared samples is legally problematic. If a buyer uses your sample-containing beat commercially and the original rights holder identifies it, both you and the buyer face copyright infringement liability. Most beat marketplaces prohibit sample-based beats in their terms of service and will remove them if identified.

Solutions: use royalty-free sample packs (Splice and Loopmasters both provide commercially clearable samples), create original elements from scratch using synthesizers and drum machines, or if you sample, clear the sample before selling beats containing it. The cost of clearing a sample typically makes it economically unviable for beat selling unless the beat commands a very high exclusive price.

Honest Income Reality

The top 1% of beat producers on BeatStars earn $10,000–$50,000+ per month. The bottom 80% earn under $500 per month. Most new producers earn nothing for the first 3–6 months while building catalog and YouTube presence. This is normal and expected — beat selling is not passive income from day one. It becomes increasingly passive after a catalog and audience are established.

Realistic timeline for a dedicated new producer: Month 1–3 (catalog building, first YouTube uploads, minimal income), Month 4–6 (first meaningful sales as YouTube views grow, $100–500/month possible), Month 7–12 ($500–2,000/month for producers who market consistently), Year 2+ ($2,000–10,000/month for producers who have built a catalog and audience).

The producers who succeed treat it as a business with consistent production, consistent marketing, and consistent improvement — not a lottery where a viral video changes everything overnight.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much do beats sell for online?

Non-exclusive leases typically sell for $25–75. Premium leases with stems sell for $75–150. Exclusive licenses range from $150–500 for new producers to $500–5,000+ for established producers. Pricing depends heavily on your reputation and track record.

What is the best platform to sell beats?

BeatStars for marketplace discovery — it has the largest buyer base. Use BeatStars Pro ($20/month) to avoid the 30% free tier fee. Supplement with your own website for direct sales at full margin.

What is a non-exclusive beat license?

Permission to use the beat commercially while the producer retains ownership and can sell the same beat to other artists. Usage is limited by stream counts, sales, or time period. The producer keeps the master rights.

How do I market beats online?

YouTube type beats (Artist name + type beat + year) is the primary channel. TikTok and Instagram Reels for short clips. Twitter/X for industry networking. Email list of previous buyers for new release announcements.

Can I sell beats made with samples?

Selling beats with uncleared samples is legally risky and against most platform terms. Use royalty-free sample packs from Splice or Loopmasters, or create original elements to avoid copyright issues.

Is selling beats profitable?

It can be, but requires 6–12 months of consistent production and marketing before meaningful income develops. Top producers earn $10,000–$50,000+/month. Most new producers earn under $500/month in the first year. Success requires both quality production and sustained YouTube and social marketing.
