How to Sell Beats Online — Complete Guide 2026

Updated May 2026  ·  14 min read  ·  Music Business

Quick answer: To sell beats online, list your beats on BeatStars or Airbit (the two dominant platforms), set up three license tiers (MP3 lease, WAV lease, exclusive), price your MP3 lease between $25–$50 to start, and drive traffic via YouTube type beat videos and TikTok. Producers with 50+ quality beats on a platform and consistent social media presence commonly reach $500–$3,000/month within 12–18 months.

Selling beats online is one of the most accessible ways for a music producer to generate independent income. The infrastructure exists, the market is global, and the barrier to entry is low. But most producers who try it make the same foundational mistakes: they upload a handful of beats, set confusing prices, do no marketing, and wonder why nothing sells.

This guide covers the entire beat-selling system — from choosing the right platform and setting up licenses to driving traffic, pricing correctly, and scaling toward a sustainable income.

Choosing Your Platform

Three viable paths exist for selling beats online. Most producers use a combination.

BeatStars

BeatStars is the largest beat marketplace in the world. It has the most built-in buyer traffic — artists actively searching for beats land on BeatStars first. The platform handles payment processing, license delivery, and storefront hosting. The free plan takes 30% of each sale. The Pro plan ($19.99/month) reduces the cut to zero on most transactions and adds features including a custom domain, more upload slots, and advanced analytics.

For a new producer, BeatStars is the right starting point. The built-in traffic means you can make sales before you've built any social media presence. The trade-off is the fee structure and the fact that you're competing with hundreds of thousands of other producers on the same platform.

Airbit

Airbit is the second-largest dedicated beat marketplace. It has a slightly smaller buyer pool than BeatStars but a comparable feature set and a similar pricing structure. Some producers run stores on both platforms simultaneously to maximise exposure. Airbit's interface is often considered cleaner and easier to navigate for buyers. The free plan takes 30%; paid plans ($7.99–$29.99/month) reduce fees significantly.

Your Own Website

A direct sales website (via Shopify, a custom WordPress store, or a beat-selling-specific platform like your own BeatStars Pro Page with a custom domain) eliminates marketplace fees entirely and gives you full brand control. The challenge: you must drive all your own traffic. Most successful producers build a direct store after they've established a social media following and email list. BeatStars and Airbit are the feeder systems — your own site is the long-term goal.

Licensing — What to Offer and How to Price It

Licensing is the product you're actually selling. Understanding license types is non-negotiable — getting this wrong creates legal problems and leaves money on the table.

The Three Core License Tiers

Basic / MP3 Lease ($25–$50): The buyer receives a stereo MP3 of the beat. They can record a song to it and release it commercially up to a specified stream/sales cap (commonly 10,000–100,000 streams depending on your terms). The beat remains in your store and can be leased to multiple artists. This is your highest-volume, lowest-margin tier — most of your sales will be here.

WAV Lease ($50–$100): The buyer receives a high-quality stereo WAV file. Same terms as the MP3 lease but with better audio quality. Some tiers at this level also include separate track stems (individual drum, melody, and bass files). The buyer can use the beat for commercial release up to a higher cap (commonly 100,000–500,000 streams).

Exclusive Rights ($200–$2,000+): The buyer receives full exclusive ownership of the beat — stems, WAV, all formats. The beat is retired from your store. No other artist can license it. For a new producer, start exclusive pricing at $200–$500. As your brand grows, exclusives from established producers regularly sell for $1,000–$10,000.

Legal note: Beat lease agreements are contracts. BeatStars and Airbit provide standard license templates — use them. If you write your own license terms, have them reviewed by a music attorney. Vague licensing creates disputes and can end producer-artist relationships badly. Always use written contracts.

Unlimited Licenses

Some producers offer an Unlimited Lease tier ($150–$400) — a non-exclusive license with no stream or sales cap. This is an effective middle tier between the WAV lease and full exclusive: the buyer pays a premium for unlimited commercial use without the producer retiring the beat. Many artists find this tier ideal for major releases where they want security without paying exclusive prices.

Building a Catalogue That Sells

A single great beat doesn't build a business. Catalogue size is one of the strongest predictors of consistent beat-selling income. The more quality beats you have available, the more surfaces you have for potential buyers to find you.

The threshold effect: producers with fewer than 20 beats in their store rarely make consistent sales. Producers with 50+ quality beats typically see their first consistent monthly income. Producers with 100+ beats across a well-defined niche (drill, trap, R&B, lo-fi) start to build a recognisable brand that drives repeat buyers and referrals.

Quality always outweighs quantity. Fifty polished, mixed, and mastered beats will outperform 200 rough drafts. Before uploading, every beat should be mixed and mastered — use a limiter on the master to match commercial loudness standards (around -9 to -7 LUFS integrated). Your beat is competing with major-label instrumentals. It needs to sound like it belongs in that company.

Driving Traffic — The Marketing System

Traffic is the constraint. BeatStars provides some built-in search traffic, but the producers who build real income all invest heavily in content marketing. The most effective channels:

YouTube Type Beats

A "type beat" video on YouTube is the highest-ROI marketing activity for most beat producers. Title the video "[Artist Name] Type Beat [Year]" — for example, "Central Cee Type Beat 2026 — Dark UK Drill." Artists search YouTube for beats by the sound they want, and a well-titled video ranks in YouTube search for years. A single popular type beat video can drive consistent sales for 2–3 years after upload. Upload one type beat video per week minimum. The visual: a simple loop video (waveform animation, dark background with beat title) is sufficient — production quality of the visual doesn't significantly affect conversion.

TikTok and Instagram Reels

Short-form video content works exceptionally well for beat producers. Beat-making process videos (time-lapses of a beat being built in FL Studio or Ableton), beat flips (taking a well-known sample and building a beat from it), and challenge content all generate discovery. Your bio link goes to your BeatStars store or website. Even a single viral TikTok can spike store traffic significantly — the goal is consistent posting (3–5 reels per week) rather than occasional viral attempts.

Email List

An email list is the most underused asset in beat selling. Offer a free beat download in exchange for an email address. Send weekly emails with new beat releases, limited-time discount codes, and exclusive previews. An email list of 1,000 engaged subscribers converts significantly better than 10,000 passive social media followers, because the relationship is direct and intentional.

Practical Exercises

🟢 Beginner — Set Up Your First Beat Store

Create a free BeatStars account. Upload your three best beats. For each beat: write a descriptive title (include genre and mood — "Dark Trap Beat — Minor Piano"), add tags (genre, mood, BPM, key), and set pricing using the three-tier structure: MP3 lease $30, WAV lease $60, exclusive $250. Use BeatStars' standard license agreements (don't write your own). Share the store link on your Instagram bio. You now have a functioning beat store. The goal for week one is to get three beats live — not to make sales yet.

🟡 Intermediate — Build Your First Type Beat Video

Choose your most current, commercially relevant beat. Identify the artist it sounds closest to (be specific — "Central Cee" not "UK drill"). Open a video editor (CapCut, DaVinci Resolve, or even iMovie). Create a simple loop video: black background, animated waveform or a static image of your DAW session, the beat title in large white text. Title the YouTube video exactly: "[Artist] Type Beat [Year] — [Mood/Descriptor]". Write a description that includes your BeatStars link in the first two lines, plus "Free download" and your license pricing. Tags: include the artist name, type beat, the genre, the year, and mood descriptors. Upload and publish. This is your first marketing asset — it will compound over time.

🔴 Advanced — 90-Day Beat Business Sprint

Commit to a 90-day sprint: produce and upload 3 beats per week (39 beats total). Upload one YouTube type beat video per week (13 videos). Post 3 short-form videos per week on TikTok or Instagram Reels — mix of process content, beat previews, and reaction/commentary. Set up a free Mailchimp account and create a lead magnet (one free beat download) to start building an email list — add the signup link to every YouTube description and your BeatStars bio. At the end of 90 days, review: which beats got the most plays? Which videos got the most views? Which genre got the most engagement? Double down on what worked and cut what didn't. Most producers see their first consistent income in month 2 or 3 of this system.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much do beat producers make online?

Beginners typically earn $0–$200/month. Mid-level producers with a catalogue and social following earn $500–$3,000/month. Top BeatStars producers report $5,000–$30,000/month. Income is driven by catalogue size, content marketing, and beat quality.

What is the best platform to sell beats?

BeatStars is the largest marketplace with the most built-in traffic. Airbit is a strong alternative. Many established producers sell direct via their own website to eliminate fees. Starting on BeatStars and building toward a direct store is the standard path.

What licenses should I offer?

Three tiers: Basic/MP3 lease ($25–$50), WAV lease ($50–$100), and Exclusive rights ($200–$2,000+). Leases allow multiple artists to buy the same beat. Exclusives transfer full rights and retire the beat from your store.

Do I need a website to sell beats?

Not to start. BeatStars and Airbit provide a full storefront out of the box. Once you're generating consistent income, a direct website gives you more control and eliminates platform fees.

How do I get traffic to my beats?

YouTube type beat videos are the highest-value traffic source — a single video can drive consistent sales for years. TikTok and Instagram Reels are secondary. SEO on your BeatStars profile (tags, descriptions) provides baseline platform search traffic.

What is a type beat?

A type beat is produced in the style of a specific artist — for example, a "Drake type beat." Type beats are the dominant SEO strategy for beat producers on YouTube and BeatStars because artists search by the sound they want.

Should I offer free beats?

Free beats (in exchange for an email or social follow) are an effective list-building strategy. They introduce new artists to your sound without giving away commercially valuable content. Keep your best work behind a paywall.

How do I price my beats?

Starting: MP3 lease $25–$35, WAV lease $50–$75, exclusive $200–$500. As your brand grows, raise prices. Top producers charge $150+ for MP3 leases and $500–$5,000 for exclusives. Price too low and you devalue your work; price too high before establishing credibility and you won't convert.
