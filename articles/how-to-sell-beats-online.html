Music Business Beat Selling

How to Sell Beats Online in 2026: The Complete Producer's Guide

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 12 min read All Levels

This article is for educational purposes. We are not lawyers. Consult a music attorney for advice specific to your situation, particularly regarding licensing agreements and rights management.

Selling beats online is one of the most accessible paths to income as a music producer. Unlike traditional music industry pathways that require label deals, management, or industry connections, beat selling is a direct transaction between producer and artist. You make the beat, list it for sale, an artist finds it and buys a license, and you receive payment — often while you sleep. The infrastructure to do this exists at low or no cost, the audience of artists looking for beats is enormous and growing, and the barrier to entry is lower than it has ever been. What separates producers who generate meaningful income from beat selling from those who earn nothing is understanding how the business actually works. This guide covers all of it.

What you will learn: How beat licensing works, which platforms to use and why, how to price your beats, what files to deliver, how to market your beats effectively, and realistic income expectations at every stage of growth.

Understanding Beat Licensing: The Foundation

Before listing a single beat for sale, understand what you are actually selling. When a producer sells a beat, they are not usually selling ownership of the beat — they are licensing the right to use it. The distinction matters enormously and determines what the artist can do with the beat, what you can do with it after the sale, and what rights and royalties each party holds going forward.

Non-Exclusive Licenses

A non-exclusive license allows you to sell the same beat to multiple different artists simultaneously. Artist A buys a non-exclusive license and records a song using your beat. Artist B buys the same non-exclusive license and records a different song using the same beat. Both artists have paid for the right to use the beat, but neither owns it exclusively. You retain the copyright to the beat itself and can continue selling non-exclusive licenses to other artists indefinitely.

Non-exclusive licenses are the backbone of online beat selling. They allow producers to generate income from the same beat multiple times and build a catalog of assets that earn repeatedly over time. The trade-off is that multiple artists may release songs using the same beat simultaneously, which can create awkward situations for the artists involved — most professional artists prefer exclusive licenses for major releases for this reason.

Exclusive Licenses

An exclusive license transfers the right to use the beat exclusively to one artist. Once sold exclusively, you can no longer sell non-exclusive licenses to other artists for that beat. The artist who holds the exclusive license typically has broader usage rights and greater commercial freedom. Most producers continue earning from non-exclusive licenses on a beat until an exclusive sale is made, then remove it from non-exclusive availability after the exclusive sale.

Exclusive beats command significantly higher prices — typically 5 to 20 times the non-exclusive rate. An exclusive sale also typically transfers more rights to the buyer, potentially including the underlying composition. Exclusive licensing agreements are more complex and benefit from legal review, particularly for high-value sales.

Where to Sell Beats: Platform Breakdown

BeatStars

BeatStars is the dominant beat selling platform with the largest artist customer base and the most established discovery ecosystem. Producers upload beats, set prices for each license tier, and artists browse and purchase directly on the platform. BeatStars processes payments, delivers files, and generates license agreements automatically. The free plan takes a 30% commission on sales. The Pro plan at $20/month reduces the commission to 0% on all sales, which pays for itself after a small number of transactions. For any producer taking beat selling seriously, the Pro plan is worth the investment immediately.

BeatStars' key strength is discoverability — its large user base means artists actively browse the platform for beats, which generates organic sales even for new producers who have not yet built an audience. SEO-optimizing your beat titles and tags for genre and BPM searches on the platform is one of the highest-leverage actions a new producer can take.

Airbit

Airbit is BeatStars' primary competitor with a cleaner interface and a similarly large artist community. The commission structure and feature set are comparable to BeatStars Pro. Most serious beat producers maintain storefronts on both platforms simultaneously — the additional setup time is minimal and the combined reach meaningfully increases sales volume. Beats upload once and the same audio file can be listed on both platforms without any conflict.

Your Own Beat Store

Building your own beat store website using platforms like Shopify combined with a digital delivery plugin, or using a service like TrakTrain, gives you 100% of revenue from direct sales and full control over the customer experience. The trade-off is that you must drive all traffic yourself rather than benefiting from a platform's existing user base. Your own store becomes a high-priority destination as your audience grows — direct sales at 100% margin significantly outperform platform-based sales at 70–100% margin for producers with established followings.

YouTube as a Sales Channel

YouTube is not technically a beat selling platform but it is the highest-ROI marketing channel for beat selling. Upload beat videos — a static image or simple waveform visualization combined with the beat audio — with titles like "Dark Trap Beat 2026 (Free Download / Type Beat)" or "Melodic Drill Beat in the Style of X". These videos rank in YouTube search for producers actively looking for beats to use and drive direct traffic to your BeatStars or Airbit store. YouTube beat videos are one of the most consistent and scalable channels for generating beat sales at scale.

Pricing Strategy

Pricing beats is one of the most psychologically challenging aspects of beat selling for new producers. The instinct to price low to generate sales conflicts with the reality that underpricing communicates low quality and attracts artists who are not serious about their releases. Standard market rates exist for each license tier for a reason — they reflect what serious artists expect to pay and what the market has established as fair value.

Start at market-rate pricing, not below it. If your first beats do not sell immediately, the problem is not pricing — it is discoverability and catalog size. A producer with 20 beats at $30 per MP3 lease will generate fewer sales than a producer with 200 beats at $30, not because of the price but because of catalog volume and the increased probability of an artist finding exactly the sound they need.

Raise prices as your catalog grows and your reputation develops. When you begin generating consistent monthly sales, it is a signal that the market values your work and that raising prices will increase revenue without proportionally reducing volume. Most established beat producers with 500+ beat catalogs charge $40–$80 for MP3 leases, $80–$150 for WAV leases, and $150–$400 for trackout leases, with exclusive prices negotiated individually.

What Files to Deliver

For an MP3 lease: a high-quality MP3 (320 kbps minimum) of the full beat with a brief producer tag at the start identifying you as the producer. The tag is standard practice and protects your work — artists who buy leases understand and expect it.

For a WAV lease: a full 24-bit WAV stereo mix of the beat at 44.1 kHz or higher. No additional stems required for this tier. Include the license agreement as a PDF in the delivery.

For a trackout lease: all individual instrument stems as separate WAV files — drums (kick, snare, hi-hat, percussion individually or as a grouped drum stem depending on your arrangement), bass, melody, keys, sample layers — plus the full stereo mix. This allows the artist's engineer to mix and process each element of the beat independently. Trackout files are what enable professional mixing engineers to properly integrate the beat into the final song.

For exclusive sales: everything in the trackout package plus the original DAW project file, documentation of any third-party samples used and their clearance status, and a formal exclusive license agreement. Third-party sample clearance is the producer's responsibility — selling a beat built on an uncleared sample creates legal liability for both the producer and the purchasing artist.

Marketing Your Beats

YouTube Beat Video Strategy

Create beat videos for every beat you produce. The video itself can be minimal — a high-quality image, an animated cover, or a simple waveform visualizer. The title is everything. Include the genre, key descriptors, BPM, and the year. Use "type beat" language if relevant to your genre. Examples: "Dark Melodic Trap Beat 2026 (FREE) | 144 BPM" or "UK Drill Beat Hard | Dark Minor Drill Type Beat 2026". Upload consistently — the algorithm rewards regular upload cadence and a larger library of videos provides more search entry points.

Instagram and TikTok

Short-form video previews of beats on TikTok and Instagram Reels drive awareness to artists who may not be actively searching for beats but encounter them passively. Show the production process — building a beat from scratch, adding a sample flip, adjusting sounds — rather than just playing the finished product. Behind-the-scenes production content performs particularly well and builds the producer brand beyond just being a beat vendor.

Direct Artist Outreach

Proactive outreach to artists whose sound matches your beats converts at a higher rate than passive discovery, particularly in the early stages before your catalog has enough volume for organic discovery to generate consistent sales. Find artists on SoundCloud or YouTube whose sound fits your beats. Listen to their work. Send a short, specific message — not a generic pitch — that demonstrates you know their music and have made something that suits them specifically. Attach a free beat preview. This approach is time-intensive but effective for building early relationships that can produce long-term collaborative and commercial value.

PRO Registration and Royalties

Register with a performing rights organization — ASCAP, BMI, or SESAC in the US — before your beats are released commercially. When music containing your beat is performed publicly (played on streaming services, used in videos, broadcast on radio or TV), your PRO collects performance royalties on your behalf and distributes them to you. Without PRO registration, those royalties are uncollected and lost permanently.

Register each beat as a musical work with your PRO after uploading it to your store. When an artist releases a song using your beat, follow up to ensure the song is registered with their PRO with your co-writer/producer credit correctly attributed. Disputes over producer credits and royalty splits are a common issue in beat selling — clear agreements established at the point of sale prevent most of them.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Uploading beats with uncleared samples is the most legally dangerous mistake in beat selling. Using a sample from a commercially released song without clearing the sample creates liability for you and every artist who buys a license to that beat. Either clear the sample properly, use royalty-free sample packs, or produce original music. If you do use samples, disclose this clearly in the beat listing so artists are aware before purchasing.

Neglecting your license agreement is the most business-damaging mistake. The default license agreement templates on BeatStars and Airbit are functional starting points but should be reviewed and customized. Pay particular attention to the usage limit caps on each tier, the exclusivity terms, the royalty split on commercial releases, and the consequences for exceeding usage limits. A producer who does not understand their own license agreements cannot enforce them when disputes arise.

Not tagging beats properly on BeatStars and Airbit means fewer artists find them. Use all available tag fields — genre, mood, BPM, key, instruments. Write a description that includes naturally occurring search terms an artist might use to find your beat. The platforms use these tags for internal search and discovery — incomplete tagging is leaving organic discovery on the table.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the best platform to sell beats online?

BeatStars and Airbit are the two dominant platforms with the largest artist customer bases. Most serious producers use both simultaneously. BeatStars has more total users; Airbit has a cleaner interface and comparable features.

How much should I charge for beats?

Standard 2026 pricing: MP3 lease $20–$50, WAV lease $40–$100, trackout lease $75–$200, exclusive rights $200–$2,000+ depending on your profile and track quality. Start at market rates and raise prices as your catalog and reputation grow.

What is a non-exclusive beat license?

A non-exclusive license allows you to sell the same beat to multiple artists simultaneously. Each artist has rights to use it under the terms of their license, but you retain the copyright and can continue selling it to others indefinitely.

What is an exclusive beat license?

An exclusive license gives one artist the right to use the beat exclusively. Once sold exclusively, you can no longer sell non-exclusive licenses for that beat. Exclusive sales command significantly higher prices — typically 5 to 20 times the non-exclusive rate.

Do I need to register with a PRO to sell beats?

Yes. Registering with ASCAP, BMI, or SESAC in the US ensures you collect performance royalties when music containing your beats is streamed, broadcast, or performed publicly. Without registration, those royalties are lost permanently.

How do I market my beats to get sales?

YouTube beat videos with genre and BPM in the title drive the most consistent organic discovery. TikTok short-form previews build awareness. Direct outreach to matching artists builds early relationships. SEO-optimize your beat titles and tags on BeatStars and Airbit for internal platform discovery.

How much can you realistically make selling beats?

Beginners with 20 to 50 beats earn $0–$500 per month. Producers with 200 to 500 beats and consistent marketing earn $500–$5,000 per month. Top producers with large catalogs earn $10,000–$50,000+. It typically takes 12 to 24 months of consistent effort to reach meaningful passive income levels.

What files should I deliver when I sell a beat?

MP3 lease: high-quality MP3 stereo mix. WAV lease: 24-bit WAV stereo mix. Trackout lease: individual WAV stems plus the full mix. Exclusive: all files above plus the DAW project file, sample documentation, and a formal exclusive license agreement.
