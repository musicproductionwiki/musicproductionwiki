What Is A Music Manager

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 · 7 min read · Verified May 7, 2026
Quick Answer — Updated May 2026

A music manager is a professional who oversees the business and strategic side of an artist's career — negotiating deals, coordinating team members, booking shows, and guiding long-term direction. Unlike a booking agent or label A&R, the manager is the artist's closest business partner, typically earning 15–20% of gross income in exchange for day-to-day career oversight.

Updated May 2026

Behind almost every working artist is a manager making calls, reading contracts, and connecting dots the artist doesn't have time to connect. If you're a producer or musician trying to understand the music industry, knowing what a music manager actually does — and when you need one — is foundational knowledge.

What a Music Manager Does

A music manager (also called an artist manager or personal manager) is the primary business representative for a recording artist, band, or music producer. Their job is to coordinate every aspect of a client's career that doesn't fall under a specific specialist role.

In practice, that means:

  • Negotiating and reviewing record deals, publishing agreements, and licensing contracts
  • Building and coordinating the artist's professional team (booking agent, publicist, entertainment lawyer, business manager)
  • Setting short- and long-term career strategy
  • Approving or vetoing creative and business decisions in line with the artist's goals
  • Handling day-to-day communication with labels, promoters, and brands
  • Identifying sync licensing and brand partnership opportunities

Understanding how music royalties work is a major part of a manager's skill set — they need to know where money flows from streaming, publishing, performance, and sync in order to protect their client's income.

How Music Managers Get Paid

Music managers work on commission — they take a percentage of the artist's gross earnings rather than charging a flat fee or hourly rate. The standard rate in most markets is 15% to 20% of gross income. Some newer or developing managers charge as low as 10%; elite managers with proven track records sometimes negotiate 20–25%.

Commission Rate Typical Context What It Covers
10–15% Developing artist, new manager Recordings, touring, merch, brand deals
15–20% Standard industry rate All income streams (gross)
20–25% Top-tier or superstar management All income, sometimes net touring

Most management contracts also define a sunset clause — a provision that limits the manager's commission on deals they originated after the management relationship ends. This protects both parties during a transition.

Manager vs. Booking Agent vs. Label A&R

The manager sits between the artist and every other industry professional — acting as the central hub for career decisions.

A booking agent focuses exclusively on securing live performance opportunities — they work show-by-show and earn a separate commission (typically 10%) only on touring income. A label A&R represents the record label's interests, not the artist's. A business manager handles accounting, taxes, and financial planning — a completely different role often confused with artist management.

The personal manager is the only member of an artist's team whose job is to represent the artist's interests across every single area of their career.

When Do You Actually Need a Manager?

Industry Benchmark

Most industry professionals agree that artists should seek formal management when they're generating consistent income, have real leverage (streaming numbers, touring demand, or a bidding war), and when business tasks are taking time away from creating. Signing a manager before you have leverage usually means signing with someone who doesn't have leverage either.

Producers specifically tend to need management once they're placing records regularly, fielding multiple beat licensing inquiries, or receiving interest from publishing companies. At that stage, knowing how to read a music contract becomes critical — both for you and for anyone you're trusting to negotiate on your behalf.

Early in a career, most artists self-manage by necessity. This means learning to promote music independently, handling their own distribution through platforms like DistroKid, and building a fanbase before a manager has real incentive to invest their time.

How to Find and Vet a Music Manager

The best managers come through warm introductions — industry attorneys, other artists, or A&R contacts are the most reliable sources. Cold outreach rarely results in serious management offers.

When evaluating a potential manager, ask:

  • What artists have you managed, and what were the outcomes?
  • Who are your active industry relationships (labels, agents, publishers)?
  • What does your day-to-day involvement look like for a developing artist?
  • What are the exact commission terms and sunset clause provisions?

Always have an entertainment lawyer review any management agreement before signing. Management contracts typically run 1–3 years, and the terms around post-term commission on pre-term deals can have major financial implications.

Self-Management for Producers

Many independent producers successfully self-manage throughout their careers, especially in the beat-selling and sync licensing space. Building your own relationships with music supervisors, understanding how to get sync licensing deals, and learning the business side of music production gives you leverage and negotiating power with or without a manager.

If you're building toward a record deal, understanding how to get a record deal — including what labels actually look for — will sharpen your pitch whether you're approaching labels directly or through a manager. The more business-literate you are, the better partner you'll be to any manager you eventually work with.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ What percentage does a music manager take?
The standard music manager commission is 15–20% of an artist's gross income. Newer managers may work for 10–15%, while top-tier managers sometimes negotiate 20–25%.
FAQ Is a music manager the same as a booking agent?
No. A booking agent focuses only on securing live shows and earns commission solely on touring income. A manager oversees the entire career across all income streams.
FAQ Do music producers need a manager?
Not always. Many producers self-manage successfully, especially in beat selling and sync licensing. A manager becomes valuable when business demands consistently pull focus away from production work.
FAQ What is a sunset clause in a music management contract?
A sunset clause limits how long a former manager can collect commission on deals they originated after the management relationship ends — typically tapering from full rate to zero over 1–3 years.
FAQ How do I find a music manager?
The most reliable route is warm introductions through entertainment lawyers, established artists, or A&R contacts. Building genuine industry relationships and demonstrating leverage (streams, touring demand, bidding interest) makes managers far more likely to approach you.
FAQ What is the difference between a music manager and a business manager?
A personal manager handles career strategy, deal-making, and team coordination. A business manager is an accountant or financial advisor who handles taxes, budgeting, and financial planning — a completely separate role.
FAQ Can I manage myself as a musician?
Yes, and many independent artists do. Self-management works well early in a career and in niche markets like beat selling and sync. The tradeoff is time — business tasks can crowd out creative work as your career grows.
FAQ What should be in a music management contract?
A management contract should specify commission rate, term length (typically 1–3 years), the scope of services, expense reimbursement terms, and a clearly defined sunset clause for post-term commissions.
