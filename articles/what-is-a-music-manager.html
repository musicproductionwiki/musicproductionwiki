What Is a Music Manager? Complete Guide to Artist Management

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2025 12 min read

A music manager — or personal manager — is the business partner responsible for guiding an artist's overall career. They coordinate the team, negotiate deals, handle day-to-day business decisions, and serve as the strategic architect of the artist's long-term career direction. A great manager can be the difference between an artist with talent and an artist with a career. Understanding what managers do and how the relationship works is essential before signing with one.

What we'll cover: What a music manager actually does day to day, how managers are compensated, the difference between a manager and other industry roles, when you're ready for a manager, how to find one, what to look for in a management deal, and red flags to avoid.

What a Music Manager Actually Does

The manager's role is broader than any other member of the music industry team. While a booking agent focuses on live performance, a publicist on press, and a lawyer on contracts, the manager oversees all of these relationships and ensures they work toward a coherent career strategy.

Team building and coordination: The manager assembles and coordinates the artist's team — hiring and overseeing the booking agent, publicist, entertainment attorney, accountant, and any other specialists. They serve as the central point of communication between all team members, ensuring everyone is working toward the same goals and that no ball gets dropped between departments.

Deal negotiation: Managers negotiate on behalf of the artist in most business contexts — with labels, publishers, brands, promoters, and sponsors. While entertainment attorneys handle the legal documentation, managers typically lead the business negotiation and engage attorneys to review and close deals. A well-connected manager with existing label relationships opens doors that an unsigned artist approaching labels alone cannot access.

Career strategy: The long-term strategic decisions of an artist's career — which label to sign with or whether to stay independent, which touring markets to prioritise, which brand partnerships align with the artist's image, when to release albums, how to position the artist in a changing market — are the manager's primary responsibility. This requires both music industry knowledge and genuine understanding of the specific artist's goals and identity.

Day-to-day operations: On a practical level, managers handle the continuous stream of business that a working artist generates — approving or declining requests, answering industry emails, coordinating scheduling, managing relationships with promoters and venues, reviewing royalty statements, and handling any business problem that arises.

How Managers Are Paid

Managers work on commission — a percentage of the artist's gross income. The standard commission rate is 15–20% in most markets. This means the manager's financial incentive is aligned with the artist's success: the more the artist earns, the more the manager earns. There is typically no fixed salary or retainer — managers invest their time and connections in expectation of future commission income.

The commission base — what income sources the manager takes a percentage of — varies by deal and should be negotiated carefully. Standard management commissions typically cover: recording royalties, touring income, merchandise, endorsements, sync licensing fees, and publishing income. Some management deals exclude specific income streams: income from deals that existed before the manager came on board, income from the artist's pre-existing publishing catalog, or income from side projects outside the main artist project.

The sunset clause: One of the most financially significant provisions in a management contract. The sunset clause determines how long after the management relationship ends the manager continues to receive commission on deals made during the contract period. A standard sunset clause might provide: 100% commission for the first year after termination, 50% for the second year, 25% for the third year. This means an artist who signs with a manager, records an album, and then terminates the management relationship may still owe commission on that album's royalties for years. Negotiate sunset clauses carefully before signing.

Manager vs. Other Industry Roles

Manager vs. booking agent: A booking agent secures live performance engagements — finding venues, negotiating performance fees, and routing tours. The booking agent is a specialist focused entirely on live work and takes 10–15% of performance fees specifically. The manager oversees the booking agent and provides strategic direction for the live career. Both are needed for a working touring artist.

Manager vs. entertainment attorney: The entertainment attorney reviews and negotiates the legal language of contracts, provides legal advice, and handles any legal disputes. The manager identifies and negotiates deals at the business level; the attorney implements them legally. Never sign a contract without attorney review regardless of what your manager says.

Manager vs. publicist: A publicist secures press coverage — reviews, features, interviews, and media mentions — through relationships with music journalists and publications. The manager oversees the publicist and ensures press strategy aligns with the overall career plan. Publicists are typically engaged on a monthly retainer basis rather than commission.

Manager vs. label A&R: A&R works for the record label and represents the label's interests. The manager works for and represents the artist's interests. These interests sometimes align and sometimes don't — a manager's job is to protect the artist's interests in negotiations with the label, including against A&R pressure.

When You're Ready for a Manager

The common mistake is seeking a manager too early — before you have enough momentum to attract a good one, or before you have enough business activity to justify a management commission. A manager who signs you with no activity to manage and no income to commission is either a bad manager (no one better wanted you) or a predatory one (looking to lock you into a deal while you're vulnerable).

Signs you're ready for professional management: you have consistent and growing streaming numbers (50,000+ monthly listeners with upward momentum), you're receiving genuine industry interest (label A&R reaching out, sync placement opportunities, booking requests from venues), your live performance is strong enough to support a touring career, and you have more business opportunities than you can handle competently alone.

Before reaching that threshold, self-management with the support of an entertainment attorney for contract review is often the better approach. Self-managed artists retain 100% of their income, maintain full control, and develop business knowledge that serves them throughout their career regardless of whether they eventually sign with a manager.

How to Find a Manager

The best managers don't advertise — they find artists whose trajectory makes them worth managing. The most reliable paths to genuine manager relationships: industry showcases and conferences (SXSW, CMJ, Music Connection events) where managers are actively scouting; referrals from entertainment attorneys, producers, or other artists who can vouch for your work; social media momentum that makes your name appear in industry conversations; and direct outreach to management companies with a compelling pitch and strong streaming data.

A compelling management pitch includes: a concise artist bio, a streaming data summary showing growth trajectory, recent press or sync placements, upcoming performance dates, and a clear statement of what you're looking to accomplish and why you need management support now. The pitch should demonstrate that you've already built something worth managing, not that you need a manager to build it from scratch.

Red Flags in Management Deals

Upfront fees: legitimate managers do not charge fees to represent an artist. Anyone who wants money upfront for "administrative costs," "promotional packages," or any other reason is not a legitimate manager. Long terms without performance clauses: a management contract for three or more years with no performance benchmarks (if the manager hasn't secured X result by Y date, the artist can exit the contract) locks an artist into a relationship with no accountability. Uncommonly high commission rates: anything above 20% commission on all income streams is above market. Commission on gross income the artist didn't receive (payments withheld by a label during recoupment, for example) is another problematic structure. Always have an entertainment attorney review any management contract before signing.
