Best for: Project studio engineers and bedroom producers who need accurate, comfortable closed-back headphones for both mixing and tracking applications, particularly those working with vocals, rock, indie, and acoustic genres.

Not for: Producers specializing in bass-heavy electronic music or hip-hop who require extended sub-bass monitoring, or engineers who exclusively need open-back soundstage for mastering work.

The Shure SRH840A represents the evolution of a studio monitoring staple that has served engineers and producers for over a decade. As someone who has mixed countless tracks through various monitoring solutions, I've spent three months putting these headphones through rigorous testing to determine whether they deserve a place in the modern production workflow.

Shure's professional audio heritage is undeniable, but the studio headphone market has become intensely competitive. The SRH840A aims to bridge the gap between affordable consumer headphones and prohibitively expensive audiophile models, targeting the producer who needs accuracy without compromise.

Design and Build Quality

The SRH840A maintains the industrial aesthetic that characterizes Shure's professional line. The predominantly black matte finish with silver accents conveys functionality over fashion—a deliberate choice that aligns with studio tool philosophy rather than lifestyle product marketing.

The headband construction uses a dual-frame design with an adjustable steel slider mechanism that provides 10 discrete sizing positions. This differs from the infinitely adjustable bands found on some competitors, but the click-stop design ensures consistent positioning across sessions. The inner headband features generously padded leatherette that distributes weight effectively across the crown.

Each ear cup measures approximately 2.2 inches in depth with a circumaural design that fully encompasses the ear. The cups rotate 90 degrees for single-ear monitoring during tracking sessions—a feature that remains essential for engineers working with live performers. The closed-back design provides approximately 20-25 dB of ambient noise reduction, sufficient for most home studio environments without requiring active noise cancellation.

Build Material Analysis: The SRH840A utilizes reinforced polymer for the outer shell rather than metal, reducing weight while maintaining structural integrity. After three months of daily use including transport in a backpack, I've observed no stress cracks or finish deterioration. The detachable cable system with bayonet-style locking connectors eliminates the primary failure point that plagues permanently wired headphones.

The ear pads use velour-style fabric rather than synthetic leather, a decision that significantly impacts long-session comfort. Velour provides superior breathability and reduces heat buildup during extended mixing marathons. Replacement pads are available directly from Shure for $29, and the magnetic attachment system allows tool-free replacement in under 30 seconds.

At 12.3 ounces (349 grams) including cable, the SRH840A sits in the mid-weight category. This represents a reasonable compromise between substantial build quality and all-day wearability. For comparison, this is approximately 20% lighter than the Beyerdynamic DT 770 Pro but 15% heavier than the Audio-Technica ATH-M50x.

Frequency Response and Sound Signature

The core question for any studio headphone: does it tell the truth? I've conducted extensive frequency response analysis using both measurement equipment and practical mix translation tests across multiple playback systems.

Shure specifies a frequency response of 5 Hz to 25 kHz, but the meaningful analysis occurs in the critical 80 Hz to 12 kHz range where most mixing decisions are made. The low-end response extends smoothly down to approximately 40 Hz before beginning a gradual rolloff. This represents honest reproduction rather than the artificial bass boost found in consumer headphones.

The presence region elevation around 3-4 kHz is the most distinctive characteristic of the SRH840A sound signature. This 2-3 dB lift enhances vocal intelligibility and allows detailed assessment of problematic resonances in this critical range. However, engineers transitioning from perfectly flat headphones should expect a brief adaptation period—mixes initially tend toward slight darkness until you recalibrate your internal reference.

Bass response deserves particular attention. The closed-back design provides punch and impact without the bloat that characterizes poorly tuned consumer headphones. Kick drums present with clear definition of both the fundamental and beater attack. Bass guitar sits precisely in the mix rather than overwhelming other elements. However, the sub-40 Hz rolloff means that true sub-bass content—increasingly important in modern hip-hop and electronic production—requires verification on secondary monitoring systems or dedicated subwoofers.

Stereo imaging presents with good width and reasonable depth cues, though the closed-back design inherently limits soundstage compared to open-back alternatives. Panning decisions translate accurately to speakers, which ultimately matters more than creating an artificially expansive headphone experience that doesn't reflect real-world playback.

Comfort and Isolation

Comfort directly impacts mixing quality—fatigued ears make poor decisions. The SRH840A excels in this category through thoughtful ergonomic design rather than exotic materials.

The velour ear pads provide significantly better thermal performance than synthetic leather alternatives. During four-hour mixing sessions, my ears remained cool and dry, whereas leather-padded headphones typically require breaks after 90 minutes. The fabric also prevents the sticky sensation that develops with perspiration contact against synthetic materials.

Clamping force measures moderate on the spectrum—sufficient to maintain positioning without creating pressure points. Engineers with larger heads or those sensitive to pressure should expect a brief break-in period of approximately 20-30 hours before the headband reaches optimal flexibility. The adjustment mechanism provides enough range to accommodate heads from approximately 21 to 24 inches in circumference comfortably.

Weight distribution proves excellent due to the balanced design. The 12.3-ounce mass centers over the ear cups rather than extending forward, preventing the forward-tilt fatigue that plagues some studio headphones. After six-hour sessions, I experienced no neck strain or head soreness—a crucial factor for professional work.

Isolation performance suits project studio environments effectively. The closed-back design attenuates external noise by approximately 20-25 dB across the frequency spectrum, with slightly better rejection of high-frequency content. This proves sufficient for working in typical home environments with moderate ambient noise from HVAC systems, computer fans, or household activity. However, engineers working in truly noisy environments—adjacent to busy streets or in apartments with thin walls—may find the isolation insufficient for critical listening at reasonable monitoring levels.

The headphones provide minimal sound leakage, an important consideration for tracking sessions. Performers monitoring in the same room as open microphones will not bleed significant headphone audio into recordings, even at elevated monitoring levels. This makes the SRH840A suitable for home recording scenarios where isolation matters.

Connectivity and Accessories

The SRH840A ships with two detachable cables: a 7-foot straight cable for desktop monitoring and a 10-foot coiled cable that extends to approximately 25 feet for tracking applications. Both cables terminate in 3.5mm connectors with gold-plated contacts, and a screw-on 6.35mm (1/4-inch) adapter is included for professional audio interface connectivity.

The bayonet-style locking mechanism provides secure cable retention—I've yet to experience an accidental disconnection even with the cable snagged on desk edges or armrests. The connectors lock with a satisfying quarter-turn and release via a small button on the connector body. This represents a significant reliability improvement over friction-fit connectors that loosen over time.

Cable quality meets professional standards with adequate shielding and strain relief at both termination points. The wire gauge handles power delivery for the 40mm drivers without introducing measurable resistance-based frequency response alterations. However, the rubber jacket picks up cable noise from friction against clothing—a minor annoyance during tracking when movement is necessary.

Shure offers an optional wireless adapter (RMCE-BT2) that replaces the cable with a Bluetooth module featuring aptX, aptX HD, and AAC codec support. At $149, this accessory transforms the SRH840A into a hybrid solution for engineers who need wireless convenience for reference listening while maintaining wired connectivity for critical mixing work. The adapter includes its own USB-C charging port and provides approximately 10 hours of wireless operation per charge.

The included carrying case is disappointingly basic—a soft zippered pouch that provides scratch protection but minimal impact resistance. Engineers who travel frequently should consider investing in a hard-shell case for transport security. The folding mechanism allows relatively compact storage with the ear cups rotated flat against the headband.

Mixing Performance and Translation

The ultimate test: how do mixes created on the SRH840A translate to other playback systems? I've completed twelve full mixes over three months using these headphones as primary monitors, then evaluated translation across studio monitors, consumer speakers, car audio systems, and earbuds.

Translation quality ranks among the better closed-back headphones in this price category. The accurate midrange reproduction ensures that vocal levels, guitar tones, and mix density decisions transfer reliably to speakers. The slightly elevated presence region initially led me to reduce brightness in the 3-4 kHz range by approximately 1 dB more than necessary, but after adapting to the SRH840A signature over approximately 20 hours of mixing, this tendency resolved.

Bass translation requires the most attention. The honest low-frequency response helps prevent the excessive bass boost that plagues mixes created on consumer headphones, but the sub-40 Hz rolloff means that modern hip-hop and electronic productions with significant sub-bass content need verification on systems with extended low-frequency response. I recommend using reference tracks with known sub-bass content to calibrate your expectations.

The closed-back design provides sufficient isolation for accurate monitoring at reasonable levels without requiring excessive volume to overcome ambient noise. This proves crucial for hearing health during long sessions. I successfully mixed at levels between 75-85 dB SPL (measured at the ear) while maintaining clarity and detail resolution.

Transient response handles percussive elements accurately. Kick drum attacks present with sharp definition, allowing precise attack time adjustments on compression. Snare drum snap remains clearly defined without the smear that characterizes slower driver designs. Hi-hat and cymbal detail resolves cleanly, though the presence peak emphasizes this content slightly more than perfectly flat monitors.

The SRH840A reveals mix problems effectively—a critical quality for studio tools. Harsh sibilance, resonant frequencies, and masking issues present clearly enough to guide corrective EQ decisions. However, the headphones don't exaggerate problems to the point of encouraging over-correction, a balance that requires careful tuning during headphone design.

For specific production tasks, the SRH840A performs differently across genres. Electronic music producers benefit from the accurate bass response for balancing kick and bass relationships, though sub-bass verification remains necessary. Rock and indie engineers get excellent midrange clarity for guitar and vocal work. Jazz and acoustic music benefit from the natural tonal balance and good transient response for capturing percussion nuance. Hip-hop and modern pop producers should supplement with full-range monitors for sub-bass verification.

Comparison with Alternatives

At $249, the SRH840A competes in a crowded market segment. Direct competitors include the Audio-Technica ATH-M50x ($169), Beyerdynamic DT 770 Pro ($179), and Sony MDR-7506 ($99).

The Audio-Technica ATH-M50x offers a more consumer-friendly sound signature with enhanced bass response and slightly recessed midrange. This makes them immediately impressive for casual listening but less accurate for professional mixing work. The M50x provides better portability with a more compact folding design, but comfort during extended sessions falls short of the SRH840A due to firmer ear pads and higher clamping force. For producers prioritizing accuracy over excitement, the SRH840A represents the better choice despite the $80 price premium.

The Beyerdynamic DT 770 Pro (80-ohm version) delivers exceptional comfort and a slightly more extended frequency response at both extremes. The DT 770 Pro's bass extends lower with better sub-bass reproduction—a significant advantage for modern electronic and hip-hop production. However, the Beyerdynamic also exhibits a more pronounced treble peak around 8-10 kHz that can lead to overly dark mixes after adaptation. The DT 770 Pro's non-detachable cable represents a reliability concern, though Beyerdynamic's build quality is legendary. Choosing between these models depends on priority: extended frequency response (DT 770 Pro) versus cable serviceability and midrange accuracy (SRH840A).

The Sony MDR-7506 remains an industry standard despite its 1991 introduction. At $99, it offers remarkable value with honest frequency response and proven reliability. However, comfort lags significantly behind modern designs, and the coiled cable cannot be replaced without soldering skills. The SRH840A provides a substantial comfort upgrade and better low-frequency extension, justifying its higher price for engineers who spend multiple hours daily in headphones.

For engineers considering open-back alternatives, the Sennheiser HD 560S ($199) offers superior soundstage and more natural tonal balance. However, open-back designs provide zero isolation and leak sound extensively, making them unsuitable for tracking applications or noisy environments. The SRH840A serves studios where versatility across both mixing and tracking is required.

Value and Final Verdict

The SRH840A occupies an interesting position in the studio headphone market. It doesn't offer the lowest price, the widest frequency response, or the most luxurious materials. Instead, it delivers a carefully balanced combination of accuracy, comfort, and practical features that serve the working producer effectively.

At $249, the value proposition depends on your production environment and workflow. For engineers working primarily in treated studio spaces with quality monitors, the SRH840A serves best as a secondary reference tool for late-night sessions and tracking. In this context, the price is justified by the comfort and accuracy for extended use.

For bedroom producers without acoustic treatment or quality monitors, the SRH840A becomes more essential—potentially serving as the primary monitoring solution. Here, the investment delivers significant value by providing honest frequency response that translates reliably to other systems, preventing the mix translation disasters that plague productions created on consumer headphones.

The detachable cable system and available replacement parts extend the useful life significantly compared to permanently wired alternatives. Assuming five years of regular use, the effective cost drops to approximately $50 per year—reasonable for a professional tool that directly impacts work quality.

After three months of intensive use, the SRH840A has earned a permanent place in my studio workflow. While I don't rely on them exclusively—no single monitoring solution suffices for professional work—they've become my preferred headphones for late-night mixing sessions and tracking applications. The comfort allows genuine all-day use, and the accurate midrange reproduction has improved my vocal and guitar mixing decisions.

The SRH840A won't revolutionize your production workflow, but it also won't mislead you during the critical mixing decisions that separate amateur from professional-sounding productions. For many engineers, that honest reliability is exactly what a studio tool should provide. If you need a closed-back headphone that tells the truth, handles daily professional use, and remains comfortable for marathon sessions, the SRH840A deserves serious consideration despite more affordable alternatives.

Practical Exercises

Beginner Exercise

Headphone Reference Calibration

Load three professionally mixed reference tracks in genres you typically produce. Listen through the SRH840A at a comfortable level and take notes on bass weight, vocal brightness, and overall tonal balance. Compare these observations to your existing monitors or headphones to begin building your internal reference for how accurate mixes should sound on this system.

Intermediate Exercise

Mix Translation Test

Complete a full mix using only the SRH840A as your monitoring source. Export the mix, then evaluate it on at least four different playback systems: studio monitors, car audio, smartphone speakers, and earbuds. Document translation issues by frequency range and adjust your mixing approach to compensate for the SRH840A's slight presence peak. Repeat with three additional mixes to develop calibrated ears.

Advanced Exercise

Comparative Frequency Analysis

Use measurement microphones or calibrated analysis tools to compare the SRH840A frequency response against your primary studio monitors in your treated listening environment. Create custom EQ compensation curves if necessary, but more importantly, identify the specific frequency regions where the headphones deviate from your monitors. Use this knowledge to make informed mixing decisions, particularly in the 2-6 kHz presence region and below 40 Hz where the SRH840A shows its most significant characteristics.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ Are the Shure SRH840A good for mixing and mastering?
The SRH840A is well-suited for mixing with its accurate midrange and honest bass response, making it excellent for vocal work, instrument balance, and general mix decisions. However, for mastering work, the sub-40 Hz rolloff and closed-back design limitations suggest using them as a secondary reference alongside quality studio monitors or open-back headphones with more extended frequency response.
FAQ How does the SRH840A compare to the original SRH840?
The SRH840A features updated 40mm neodymium drivers with improved transient response and slightly extended frequency response at both extremes. The new model also includes detachable cables with bayonet-style locking connectors (the original had a fixed cable), compatibility with optional Bluetooth adapters, and redesigned ear pads with better breathability. The overall sound signature remains similar with enhanced clarity and detail resolution.
FAQ Can the SRH840A be used with audio interfaces and mobile devices?
Yes, the SRH840A works with both professional audio interfaces and consumer devices. With 42-ohm impedance and 99 dB/mW sensitivity, they're efficient enough to be driven adequately by smartphones, tablets, and laptop headphone outputs, though they benefit from dedicated headphone amplifiers for optimal dynamic range. The included 3.5mm to 6.35mm adapter provides compatibility with professional studio equipment.
FAQ How is the comfort during long mixing sessions?
The SRH840A excels in long-session comfort due to velour ear pads that provide excellent breathability, moderate clamping force that balances security with pressure distribution, and well-balanced weight distribution. Most users can wear them comfortably for 4-6 hour sessions without significant fatigue, though a brief 20-30 hour break-in period helps the headband reach optimal flexibility.
FAQ Do the SRH840A require a headphone amplifier?
The SRH840A doesn't require a dedicated headphone amplifier for basic functionality—most audio interfaces and consumer devices provide adequate power. However, a quality headphone amp can improve dynamic range, reduce distortion at higher volumes, and provide better low-frequency control. For professional mixing work, using the headphone output from a quality audio interface typically provides better results than consumer device outputs.
FAQ What replacement parts are available for the SRH840A?
Shure offers replacement ear pads, cables (both straight and coiled versions), and the carrying pouch as separate spare parts. The ear pads use a magnetic attachment system for tool-free replacement and cost approximately $29 per pair. Replacement cables cost around $29-39 depending on style. This parts availability significantly extends the headphone's useful life compared to models without serviceable components.
FAQ Are the SRH840A suitable for tracking and recording sessions?
Yes, the SRH840A works excellently for tracking applications. The closed-back design provides 20-25 dB of noise isolation, preventing external sounds from disrupting performer concentration. Minimal sound leakage means performers can monitor at reasonable levels without bleeding audio into sensitive microphones. The 90-degree rotating ear cups allow single-ear monitoring, and the detachable cable prevents damage from accidental pulls during performance.
FAQ How does the bass response affect electronic music production?
The SRH840A provides accurate bass response from approximately 40 Hz upward, making it excellent for balancing kick drums and bass guitars in most genres. However, the sub-40 Hz rolloff means that modern electronic music and hip-hop productions with significant sub-bass content require verification on full-range monitoring systems. The honest bass presentation helps prevent over-boosting but doesn't provide complete information about extreme low-frequency content.
