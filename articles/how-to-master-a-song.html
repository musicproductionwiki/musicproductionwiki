How to Master a Song: The Complete Professional Guide (2025)

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2025 55 min read

Learning how to master a song is the final step between a finished mix and a released record — and the step most producers either skip, rush, or approach with fundamental misunderstandings about what mastering can and cannot achieve. It is also the most misunderstood stage in music production — often described in mystical terms, reduced to "making it loud," or dismissed as unnecessary for independent artists. None of these characterisations are accurate. Mastering done well is invisible: the listener hears the music more clearly, feels it more intensely, and notices nothing technical. Mastering done poorly is immediately audible — over-limited, harsh, lifeless, or inconsistent across a body of work.

This guide is the most comprehensive mastering reference available for producers, engineers, and artists who want to understand mastering at a professional level — whether they plan to master their own music or work with a professional mastering engineer and understand what they're paying for.

What this guide covers: The philosophy and psychology of mastering, the complete signal chain with technical depth, every processor explained with settings ranges, the full LUFS and loudness ecosystem, format-specific mastering (streaming, vinyl, CD, sync), stem mastering, AI mastering honestly assessed, the pre-mastering checklist, the A/B referencing protocol, the diagnostic troubleshooting table, what mastering can and cannot fix, when to hire a professional, and the mastering philosophy that separates lasting engineers from those who don't last.

What Is Audio Mastering? What It Is — and What It Isn't

Mastering is the process of preparing a completed stereo mix (or stems) for distribution. It serves three distinct functions: technical preparation (ensuring the audio meets the technical requirements of every distribution format), quality control (listening with fresh ears and trained perception for problems the mix engineer missed), and tonal and dynamic optimisation (adjusting the mix's frequency balance, dynamic range, and overall loudness to be appropriate for its genre and distribution context).

What mastering is not: a fix for a bad mix. This phrase is repeated in every mastering guide because it's ignored with equal frequency. The logic seems obvious — if the mix has a problem, why not correct it at mastering rather than going back to the mix session? Because mastering processors affect the entire stereo image simultaneously. A low-mid EQ cut to address the bass guitar's muddiness also affects the kick drum, the piano, the reverb tails, and every other element in that frequency range. A compressor applied to control a vocal peak also compresses every other element at the moment the vocal peaks. The surgical precision that allows mix engineers to address individual elements simply does not exist in mastering's stereo bus context.

The honest boundary: mastering can address broad tonal balance issues, overall dynamic range, loudness for distribution, and subtle cohesion enhancement. It cannot fix frequency problems specific to individual instruments, timing or pitch issues in the performance, inappropriate reverb or delay settings, or fundamental arrangement or production problems. A mix that needs mastering is a good mix that needs polish and preparation. A mix that needs fixing needs to go back to the mix engineer.

The Mastering Philosophy: Restraint as Craft

The engineers who have been mastering records for 20, 30, 40 years share a philosophy that beginners consistently resist: the best mastering is the mastering you can't hear. Every processor applied at mastering is a risk — a risk of introducing artefacts, reducing dynamics, adding colour that changes the mix's character in ways the artist didn't intend. The philosophy of restraint says: apply only what is genuinely necessary, at the minimum effective amount, and stop.

This philosophy is counterintuitive because processing feels like progress. More EQ, more compression, more limiting — it feels like mastering is being done. But the goal is not to do mastering; it's to make the music ready. A completely unprocessed mix that happens to already have correct tonal balance, appropriate dynamics, and suitable loudness is a better master than a heavily processed mix with artefacts and over-limitation. Mastering is a means to an end, never an end in itself.

The second element of lasting mastering philosophy: serve the music, not the technical specifications. Every technical target — LUFS values, true peak ceilings, frequency balance targets — exists to serve the listener's experience of the music. When a technical specification conflicts with the music's emotional intention, the music wins. A classical piano recording that genuinely calls for wide dynamic range should not be crushed to meet a loudness target that suits a hip-hop release. Genre context and musical intention determine what "correct" sounds like, and technical targets are tools that serve that determination, not ends in themselves.

The Psychology of Mastering: How Your Brain Works Against You

Understanding the psychological phenomena that compromise mastering decisions is as important as understanding the technical tools. Your brain is a sophisticated but unreliable measuring instrument for audio quality, and mastering sessions expose every one of its weaknesses.

Ear fatigue: Extended listening causes the auditory system to reduce its sensitivity — particularly in the high frequencies — as a protective mechanism. After 60–90 minutes of focused listening, your perception of high-frequency content is measurably reduced. This has a predictable consequence: EQ and limiting decisions made late in a session will compensate for this reduced sensitivity by adding more high frequencies than the music actually needs, producing a bright, harsh master that sounds wrong outside the session. The professional response is to keep mastering sessions short (90 minutes maximum of focused critical work), take regular breaks, and make no irreversible decisions in the final hour of any session.

The loudness bias: Human perception consistently rates louder signals as better-sounding, all other things being equal. When you apply a limiter and gain and compare the processed signal to the unprocessed signal at different loudness levels, the louder version almost always sounds "better" — more present, more exciting, more finished. This is a perceptual artefact, not a quality difference. The professional mitigation: gain-match every comparison. When evaluating a processor (EQ, compressor, limiter) against bypass, adjust the gain so both versions play at the same perceived loudness before forming a judgment. Loudness-matched comparison reveals whether the processor is actually improving the sound or merely making it louder.

Expectation bias: You know this mix. You've heard it hundreds of times in production and mixing. This familiarity creates expectation — your brain fills in detail that may or may not be in the audio and compensates for problems it has learned to ignore. Fresh ears — someone who hasn't heard the mix before — will notice things you've become blind to after repeated exposure. The professional mitigation: leave at least 24 hours between completing the mix and beginning mastering. Play the mix once, all the way through, before touching any processor. Take notes on what you hear with those relatively fresh ears, then begin.

The "it's finished" bias: When a mix is labelled "final" or "ready for mastering," there's a psychological pressure to process it and deliver a master rather than identify problems and send it back. This bias leads to mastering of mixes that genuinely need revision — and to masters that compensate for mix problems in ways that make the problems harder to fix later. Resist this pressure. If the mix has a fundamental problem, flag it and return it before mastering begins.

The fresh ears protocol: A specific sequence of steps for approaching a mastering session with maximum perceptual accuracy. Step 1: complete the mix session and leave the building (or close the project). Do not listen to it again until the following morning. Step 2: on returning, do not listen to any other music for 20 minutes. Step 3: load the mix and listen once, all the way through, without a cursor near the playback controls. Take notes by hand (not on screen — the physical act of writing slows the note-taking and improves observation). Step 4: make a single list of every genuine concern before opening any processor. Step 5: begin mastering only after completing this initial assessment. This sequence is not ceremonial — each step addresses a specific psychological bias that compromises mastering quality.

The Complete Mastering Signal Chain (In the Right Order)

The mastering signal chain is the sequence of processors applied to the stereo mix in a specific order. Unlike mixing, where the order of individual track processors can vary by engineer preference, the mastering chain has a more standardised order that reflects the logical relationship between each stage's function. Understanding why each processor occupies its position in the chain is as important as understanding what each processor does.

Why this order: Corrective EQ comes first because removing frequency problems before compression ensures the compressor responds to a cleaner, more accurate signal. Saturation comes after corrective EQ but before compression so that the harmonic content added by saturation is included in what the compressor shapes. Compression comes before stereo width adjustment because compression affects the stereo image, and adjustments to stereo width should be made to the compressed signal rather than requiring re-evaluation after compression. The limiter is always last — it sets the final output ceiling after all other processing is complete.

Gain Staging in Mastering: The Exact Numbers

Gain staging in mastering is more critical than in any other production stage because every processor in the chain is sensitive to input level, and errors compound through the chain. The goal: maintain an appropriate signal level at every stage, ensuring no processor receives a signal that's too hot (causing internal clipping or over-driven processing) or too quiet (losing headroom or accuracy).

Input level for the mastering chain: The stereo mix should arrive with peaks no higher than -3 to -6 dBFS and an average (RMS) level around -18 to -14 dBFS. If the mix is louder than this — if it was mixed with a limiter on the bus or was already processed to streaming levels — the first step is to reduce the input gain to bring it into the correct range before processing. A mix that peaks at -0.5 dBFS provides almost no headroom for the mastering chain, and processors applied to such a hot signal will behave differently and less predictably than when applied to a properly gain-staged input.

Through the chain: Each processor should receive the signal at approximately the same level it would see in a professional context — typically with peaks around -6 to -10 dBFS through most of the chain. Use the input and output gain controls on each processor to maintain this level rather than allowing each stage to change the level dramatically. The compressor's makeup gain should restore the level reduced by gain reduction. The limiter's input gain (or the gain stage immediately before it) is where final loudness adjustment happens — not scattered through the chain.

Using a VU meter for gain staging: Many mastering engineers use a VU meter (calibrated to 0 VU = -18 dBFS) as a reference for gain staging. When the mix averages around 0 VU on the meter, it is in the correct range for most analogue-style mastering processors. Digital processors also perform best in this range. The VU meter's integration time (300 ms) averages out short transients and gives a more reliable picture of the music's average level than peak meters, which react to every transient spike.

How to EQ a Master: Corrective EQ First

The first EQ in the mastering chain is corrective — its purpose is to identify and address frequency balance problems in the mix before any other processing is applied. The approach is fundamentally subtractive: find and cut problems before considering any additive moves.

The reference track comparison is the most reliable way to identify corrective EQ needs. Load a commercial reference track in the same genre, match its loudness to the mix, and alternate between them on loop. Systematic differences — the mix has noticeably more low-mid content, the mix lacks the high-frequency air of the reference — indicate the corrective moves needed. The ear is better at identifying differences than identifying absolute problems, and the reference provides the contrast that makes differences obvious.

Dynamic EQ in mastering: Many mastering engineers now use dynamic EQ (FabFilter Pro-Q3's dynamic bands, TDR Nova) for frequency-specific issues that only appear on certain passages. A mix that sounds balanced on verses but accumulates low-mid mud when the full arrangement plays is the ideal dynamic EQ application — a dynamic cut that only engages when the problematic frequency content exceeds a threshold, leaving the sparser passages unaffected. Dynamic EQ produces more transparent mastering results than static EQ for intermittent problems, because the correction only applies when it's needed rather than permanently altering the frequency balance.

Linear phase vs minimum phase EQ: Standard parametric EQ (minimum phase) introduces phase shift alongside its frequency changes. At low frequencies and steep slopes, this phase shift is audible as a subtle colouration that some engineers find problematic on stereo bus material. Linear phase EQ applies the same frequency change with zero phase shift, producing cleaner results on complex stereo material at the cost of introducing latency. Most DAWs compensate for this latency automatically in offline rendering. In mastering contexts where transparency is paramount — particularly on acoustic and orchestral material — linear phase EQ is preferred by many engineers. FabFilter Pro-Q3 and Voxengo's linear phase processors handle this well. For most popular music mastering, the phase difference between minimum and linear phase EQ at the amounts used is inaudible.

Saturation and Analogue Colour

Saturation in mastering adds harmonic content — primarily 2nd and 3rd order harmonics — that creates a sense of warmth, cohesion, and density that purely digital processing doesn't produce. It is the closest software approximation of the colouration introduced by analogue tape, transformer-based hardware, and tube electronics in the traditional mastering chain.

The key principle: saturation in mastering is applied at amounts that are felt rather than heard. If you can clearly identify a saturation effect — warmth, rounding of transients, brightness — the amount is too high for transparent mastering use. The appropriate level is where the A/B comparison between processed and unprocessed reveals a subtle cohesion and density difference, not an obvious tonal change.

The Waves J37 (tape saturation emulation), the Softube Tape, UAD's Studer A800, and iZotope Ozone's Vintage Tape module all provide appropriate mastering-grade saturation at low settings. The settings: drive/input at the minimum amount that produces audible (barely) harmonic addition, typically producing no more than 1–2 dB of harmonic content above the fundamental. Saturation amount on a loudness meter shouldn't change the reading significantly — if it's raising the LUFS measurement noticeably, the drive is too high.

Mastering Compression: Glue, Not Control

Mastering compression serves a different purpose than mixing compression. In mixing, compression controls the dynamics of individual elements — keeping vocals consistent, making drums punchy, gluing a mix bus. In mastering, compression's primary role is cohesion — the subtle transformation of independently mixed elements into a unified whole that sounds like it was recorded in the same space on the same day.

The settings for mastering compression reflect this purpose: very low ratios (1.2:1 to 2:1), slow to moderate attack (30–100 ms, allowing transients through), programme-dependent or musical release (auto or 150–300 ms), and modest gain reduction (1–3 dB maximum). These settings are so gentle that many engineers question whether the compressor is doing anything — and that's exactly right. Mastering compression that is audible as compression is too much compression.

The compressor types most used in mastering reflect this philosophy. The Fairchild 670, the Manley Variable Mu, and the GML 8900 are all programme-dependent compressors — they respond to the musical content rather than rigidly applying fixed time constants. Their gentle, musical behaviour at low gain reduction levels is what produces the "glue" effect without the obvious dynamic shaping of a fast VCA compressor. Software equivalents include the Waves PuigChild, the Softube Tube-Tech CL 1B, and Ozone's Vintage compressor modules.

The SSL G-Bus compressor — a VCA design with fixed attack options — is used in mastering by engineers who want slightly more defined compression character, particularly on rock, pop, and hip-hop material where the pumping behaviour at faster attack settings is an aesthetic choice rather than a transparency failure. Ratio: 2:1 maximum. Attack: slowest available for transparent use (30 ms), faster (3–10 ms) for deliberate character. Release: auto or 0.1 second. Gain reduction: 1–3 dB.

Parallel mastering compression: Blending a heavily compressed version with the dry signal — parallel compression at the mastering stage — allows adding density and sustain without the dynamic shaping of direct compression. The parallel signal is compressed aggressively (4:1–8:1, fast attack, 6–10 dB gain reduction) and blended at low level with the direct signal. The result: added thickness and sustain in a way that doesn't reduce peaks, preserving dynamic impression while increasing perceived density. This technique is more commonly used in certain genres (rock, electronic, hip-hop) than in acoustic music mastering where dynamic integrity is paramount.

Stereo Width and Mid-Side Mastering

Mid-side (M-S) processing is one of mastering's most powerful and most misused tools. Understanding what M-S processing actually does — and what it can and cannot achieve — is essential before applying it.

A stereo signal can be decomposed into two components: the Mid (the sum of Left and Right, equivalent to a mono signal) and the Side (the difference of Left and Right, representing the stereo information). Processing these components independently allows adjustments that are impossible with standard stereo processing. Boosting the Side signal adds width. Cutting the Side signal narrows the stereo image. Applying EQ to the Mid only affects the centre-panned elements (lead vocal, bass, kick). Applying EQ to the Side only affects the stereo width elements (room ambience, panned instruments, chorus effects).

Practical M-S applications in mastering:

Bass management: Low frequencies below 100–200 Hz should be mono in almost all distribution contexts — vinyl requires mono bass below 200 Hz, and stereo sub-bass wastes headroom and causes phase problems on most playback systems. A high-pass filter applied only to the Side channel below 150–200 Hz makes the sub-bass mono without affecting the mid and high-frequency stereo image. This is one of the most consistently useful M-S moves in mastering.

Width control: A mix that feels too narrow can have its Side signal boosted slightly (0.5–1.5 dB) to increase perceived width. A mix that feels too wide, or whose stereo elements collapse unpredictably in mono, can have its Side signal reduced. Important: always check mono compatibility after any Side level change. Summing to mono in your DAW and verifying that the mix remains coherent and that no important elements disappear is a required step.

Independent frequency shaping: The mid channel often needs different EQ treatment than the side channel. The bass and lead vocal (mid channel) may need different correction than the room ambience and panned instruments (side channel). A high-frequency boost on the side channel adds air to the stereo elements without brightening the lead vocal further. A low-mid cut on the mid channel reduces vocal muddiness without affecting the warmth of the stereo reverb returns.

Mono compatibility testing: Every mastered track must be checked in mono. The test: sum both channels of the stereo master to mono using your DAW's mono sum button or a dedicated mono check plugin (such as Boz Digital Labs' +10db). Listen for: elements that disappear or become significantly quieter (phase cancellation from stereo processing), frequency response changes (mid-side EQ affects mono differently than stereo), and loss of clarity or definition. Elements that are absent from mono but present in stereo will be missed by listeners on mono playback systems — phone speakers, many smart speakers, some club systems.

How to Use a Limiter for Mastering: True Peak, LUFS, and Avoiding Artefacts

The limiter is the most consequential processor in the mastering chain and the one most directly responsible for the quality difference between professional and amateur mastering results. Understanding limiters requires understanding true peak, inter-sample peaks, LUFS, and the relationship between all three.

True peak vs sample peak: A sample peak measurement shows the level of individual audio samples — the discrete values in the digital audio file. A true peak measurement reconstructs the continuous analogue waveform that would be produced by a perfect digital-to-analogue converter and measures the peaks of that reconstructed waveform. Because the analogue waveform can reach higher levels between samples than the samples themselves indicate, true peak measurements can exceed sample peak measurements. This is called inter-sample peaking (ISP).

Why this matters: when streaming services encode your WAV file to AAC or MP3 for delivery, the codec reconstruction process can cause these inter-sample peaks to clip — producing distortion in the delivered audio even if your WAV file technically never exceeded 0 dBFS. A WAV master with sample peaks at -0.1 dBFS may have true peaks of +0.5 to +1.5 dBFS or higher, and when encoded to AAC, these true peaks can cause audible clipping.

The solution: set your limiter's output ceiling in true peak mode, not sample peak mode. The standard ceiling for streaming delivery is -1 dBTP (true peak). Some engineers use -0.3 dBTP for extra safety. Never set a true peak ceiling above -0.5 dBTP for streaming delivery if you want to guarantee your master arrives at the listener without codec-induced distortion.

Limiter algorithm types: Modern limiters use different algorithms that produce meaningfully different sonic results at the same gain reduction amount.

IRC (Intelligent Release Control) — iZotope Ozone: Programme-dependent limiting that adapts attack and release to the musical content. IRC IV is the current generation; it handles transients with exceptional transparency at moderate gain reduction (3–5 dB) and produces more audible artefacts at heavy reduction (6+ dB). Best for: pop, electronic, hip-hop at competitive loudness targets.

Transparent limiting — FabFilter Pro-L2: Multiple algorithms including Transparent (smoothest, lowest distortion), Aggressive (heavier dynamic shaping for competitive mastering), and Bus (optimised for stereo bus use). The Transparent algorithm at 3–4 dB gain reduction is the reference standard for clean modern mastering. Best for: acoustic, jazz, classical, and any material where the limitation artefacts are most audible.

Clip-based limiting — Tokyo Dawn Labs Limiter 6: Multi-stage limiting with true peak control, high-frequency limiting, and transient management. The multi-stage approach (high-frequency limiter → clipper → limiter → output) handles loud transients more cleanly than single-stage limiting by addressing specific frequency content before the main limiter sees it. Best for: producers who want maximum control over the limiting process at minimal cost (free).

Limiter gain reduction and quality: The amount of limiting is the single most important quality variable. Every limiter degrades the signal quality when gain reduction exceeds approximately 3–4 dB — not because the limiter is poorly designed, but because significant limiting necessarily removes transient information, reduces dynamics, and introduces distortion products. The relationship between gain reduction and quality is non-linear: 3 dB of limiting produces artefacts that are much less than twice as audible as 1.5 dB of limiting. Push any limiter hard enough and the artefacts become unavoidably obvious. The commercial loudness targets that require 6–10 dB of limiting on some genres are working against audio quality by definition — the question is how much degradation is acceptable for the loudness benefit.

LUFS Targets for Every Streaming Platform in 2025

LUFS (Loudness Units relative to Full Scale) is the measurement unit used for integrated loudness — the average loudness of an audio signal over its full duration, weighted to reflect human hearing sensitivity. It replaced RMS as the standard loudness measurement because LUFS correlates better with perceived loudness across different programme material.

Streaming platforms use LUFS-based normalisation — they analyse the integrated loudness of each track and adjust its playback level to match a target loudness. Tracks louder than the target are turned down; tracks quieter than the target may be turned up (though most platforms don't turn quiet tracks up significantly, to avoid amplifying noise). The loudness war strategy of making tracks as loud as possible is therefore self-defeating on normalised platforms: a track mastered at -8 LUFS is turned down by 6 dB on Spotify to match the -14 LUFS normalisation target, and sounds identical in level to a track mastered at -14 LUFS — but with far more limiting artefacts and reduced dynamic range.

The three LUFS measurements you need to understand:

Integrated LUFS: The average loudness over the entire duration of the track, gated to exclude silences below a threshold. This is the measurement that streaming platforms use for normalisation. Target for streaming masters: -14 LUFS for Spotify/YouTube/Tidal, -16 LUFS if primarily targeting Apple Music. Genre context: EDM and hip-hop often master to -9 to -12 LUFS for competitive loudness on non-normalised platforms (Bandcamp, some broadcast contexts) while accepting the normalisation turn-down on streaming.

Short-term LUFS: The average loudness over the last 3 seconds. Useful for monitoring loudness during real-time listening and for identifying sections that are significantly louder or quieter than the integrated average. Short-term LUFS gives a moment-to-moment picture of the track's loudness trajectory.

Momentary LUFS: The average loudness over the last 400 ms. The most responsive measurement — closest to instant loudness perception. Useful for identifying brief loud moments that spike the short-term or integrated readings. Less directly useful for making mastering decisions than integrated LUFS but valuable for understanding the track's dynamic structure.

The normalisation misunderstanding: A common misconception is that loudness normalisation "penalises" loud masters by turning them down. The actual effect is that normalisation levels the playing field — every track at or near the target plays at approximately the same perceived loudness, so the quality of the music itself determines listener experience rather than which track was mastered loudest. The producer who spent time on limiting to hit -8 LUFS is working against themselves twice: they've invested effort in loudness that gets normalised away, and they've degraded their audio quality with unnecessary limiting to achieve it. Master for the target. Let the music compete on its own merits.

The Pre-Mastering Checklist: 10 Things to Check Before You Start

Before placing a single processor on the mastering chain, a systematic pre-mastering assessment ensures the mix is genuinely ready to be mastered and identifies any issues that should be addressed at the mix stage. This checklist is not ceremonial — each item corresponds to a real class of mastering problem that is better caught before processing begins than discovered mid-session.

□Mix level: Peaks at -3 to -6 dBFS. No clipping on the mix bus. No limiting on the mix bus (unless specifically requested). Average RMS/VU around -18 to -14 dBFS.
□Format: WAV or AIFF, 24-bit minimum, sample rate matching the recording session (44.1 kHz or 48 kHz for standard delivery; 96 kHz for archival or high-res distribution).
□Mono check: Sum to mono and listen for phase cancellation, frequency response changes, or elements that disappear. Stereo effects (chorus, wide reverbs) should survive mono without the mix collapsing.
□Low-end mono check: Sub-bass content below 80–100 Hz should be essentially mono. Stereo sub-bass wastes headroom and may cause vinyl cutting problems.
□No DC offset: Open the file in a DAW and check the waveform centre. DC offset (an asymmetrical waveform that sits above or below zero) causes problems at limiting and should be corrected in the mix with a high-pass filter or DC offset removal plugin.
□Silence at head and tail: Clean silence (not just empty audio) at the start and end of the file. At least 0.5 seconds of silence before the first audio, and the tail completely natural (no artificial cutoffs on reverb or delay tails).
□No digital clipping: Check the mix file for clipped samples (waveform flattened at the top). Any clip samples should be addressed in the mix before mastering — limiters applied to clipped content produce worse results than limiters applied to unclipped content.
□Reference tracks loaded: Two or three commercially released reference tracks in the same genre, loaded and gain-matched to the mix level, ready for A/B comparison before starting any processing.
□Monitoring level set: Monitor at a consistent, calibrated level for mastering decisions. 83 dB SPL (the film industry mixing standard, also appropriate for music mastering) produces the most reliable tonal balance perception at extended listening. Use a dB SPL meter to calibrate.
□Metadata confirmed: Artist name, track title, ISRC code, BPM, key, composer credits — all confirmed and ready to embed in the master file.

The A/B Referencing Protocol

Reference tracks are the most important tool in mastering and the most commonly misused. Loading a reference track and glancing at it occasionally while working is not the referencing protocol used by professional mastering engineers. The correct protocol is systematic, disciplined, and repeated throughout the session.

Step 1 — Gain matching: Before comparing, match the loudness of the reference to your master in progress. A louder signal always sounds better — comparing at different levels produces misleading impressions. Use a loudness meter to match both signals to the same integrated LUFS before listening. Most DAW reference plugins (ADPTR Metric AB, Tonal Balance Control) handle gain matching automatically.

Step 2 — The first listen: Play the reference track from its most representative section — typically the chorus or the densest arrangement — while noting specific characteristics: where the bass sits, how bright the top end is, how forward the vocal is, how wide the stereo image is. Form a specific mental model of what the reference sounds like.

Step 3 — Immediate switch: Switch to your master at the same section without pause. The comparison is most revealing in the first few seconds before your ears adapt to the character of the new track. Listen specifically to the same characteristics you noted in the reference: the bass, the top, the vocal, the width.

Step 4 — Identify the gap: The differences between the reference and your master are specific and actionable. "My master sounds muddier in the low-mids" is a corrective EQ task. "My master sounds narrower" is an M-S width task. "My master doesn't have the same sense of air" is a high-shelf EQ task. Write down the specific gap, not a general impression.

Step 5 — Address and re-reference: Apply the correction, then return to the reference comparison. Has the gap closed? Has a new gap opened? Repeat until the master sits in a similar tonal space to the reference without sounding like an imitation of it. Your master should have the same perceived quality as the reference while retaining its own character.

Step 6 — Multiple references: Use two or three references from artists whose mastering quality represents the target. Different references reveal different aspects of your master's character — a punchy hip-hop reference reveals low-end balance; a detailed acoustic reference reveals high-frequency resolution; an electronic reference reveals limiting quality and transient handling.

Format-Specific Mastering: Streaming, Vinyl, CD, and Sync

Streaming Mastering

The standard target for a streaming master: -14 LUFS integrated, -1 dBTP true peak ceiling, 24-bit WAV at 44.1 kHz. This target works for Spotify, YouTube, and Tidal. For Apple Music, consider -16 LUFS to match their more conservative normalisation target. For releases on Bandcamp or other non-normalised platforms, genre-appropriate competitive loudness applies.

The deliverable format for most distribution services: WAV, 24-bit, 44.1 kHz. FLAC is accepted by some distributors (DistroKid accepts FLAC; others require WAV). Never deliver MP3 to a distribution service — they encode from your master to their streaming format, and MP3-to-AAC transcoding produces generation loss and audible artefacts. Always deliver lossless.

Vinyl Mastering

Vinyl mastering is a separate technical discipline with specific requirements that differ fundamentally from digital mastering. Understanding these requirements before submitting files to a cutting engineer avoids expensive mistakes and rejected lacquers.

Mono bass below 200 Hz: The phono cartridge reads stereo information as horizontal movement of the groove wall and mono information as vertical movement. Stereo bass frequencies create extreme vertical groove modulation that can cause the cartridge to skip and damages the lacquer. All bass content below 150–200 Hz must be mono before vinyl cutting. This is accomplished with M-S processing (high-pass the Side channel below 200 Hz) or with a dedicated vinyl mastering tool.

Sibilance control: High-frequency sibilance (S, T, and SH consonants in vocals) causes extremely rapid high-frequency groove modulation. The cutting head's coils can overheat at extreme sibilance levels, and the stylus may distort during playback. A de-esser in the mastering chain is standard for vocal-heavy vinyl masters. The de-esser threshold is typically set more conservatively for vinyl than for digital.

Loudness limitations: The louder a vinyl record, the wider the grooves, and the fewer grooves fit on a side. A very loud side can hold approximately 18–20 minutes of music before the inner groove distortion (caused by shorter groove circumferences) becomes unacceptable. A quieter side can hold 22–25 minutes. The vinyl cutting engineer will advise on appropriate loudness for the desired side length. Do not apply the same limiting to a vinyl master as a streaming master — vinyl masters should be more dynamic (typically -12 to -18 LUFS) than streaming masters.

The RIAA curve: Vinyl records are recorded with bass frequencies reduced and high frequencies boosted (the RIAA equalisation curve), and phono preamps apply the inverse curve during playback. This pre-emphasis and de-emphasis system is handled by the cutting engineer and the phono stage — the mastering engineer doesn't need to apply the RIAA curve manually. But the RIAA pre-emphasis is why sibilance and bass phase problems are amplified in vinyl cutting: the bass cut in the RIAA curve makes bass content relatively louder in the groove, and the treble boost amplifies sibilance.

Side sequencing: The order of tracks on a vinyl side matters. Loud, energetic tracks placed at the beginning of the side (outer grooves) where the circumference is largest can be cut louder than the same tracks placed at the end of the side (inner grooves). Quieter, more dynamic tracks should be placed at the end of each side to maintain the highest possible quality through the inner groove region.

CD Mastering

CD mastering produces a 16-bit/44.1 kHz master with a 0 dBFS peak ceiling. The conversion from the 24-bit master to 16-bit requires dithering — the addition of low-level noise that reduces the distortion introduced by bit-depth reduction. POW-r dithering (algorithms 1, 2, and 3 for progressively more complex material) and TPDF dithering are the standard choices. Dithering is applied as the last process in the mastering chain, after limiting. Never dither twice — if the 24-bit master is correctly dithered to 16-bit, no further dithering should be applied at any stage.

CD does not use loudness normalisation. Competitive loudness on CD is determined entirely by the mastering engineer's limiting decisions. Contemporary CD masters in mainstream genres are typically limited to -8 to -12 LUFS — louder than streaming targets because CD playback doesn't normalise. The loudness war peaked on CD and has been partially ameliorated by streaming normalisation for digital distribution.

Sync Licensing Deliverables

Music supervisors and post-production facilities have specific technical requirements for licensed music. Delivering the wrong format or specification can delay or prevent a placement. Standard requirements for sync delivery: WAV, 24-bit, at the highest quality sample rate available (preferably 48 kHz for film/TV, as this matches the video production standard). Separate stems (without the full mix) are frequently requested — instrumental only, vocals only, or separated music stems.

Loudness for sync delivery: broadcast loudness standards are different from streaming targets. The EBU R128 standard (used in European broadcast) targets -23 LUFS. The ATSC A/85 standard (US broadcast) targets -24 LUFS. If a sync placement specifies broadcast delivery, master to the appropriate broadcast standard rather than streaming targets. Delivering a -14 LUFS streaming master for broadcast use will result in the broadcaster's normalisation system turning it down significantly, potentially affecting the creative impact of the placement.

Stem Mastering

Stem mastering is an alternative to traditional full-mix mastering where the mix is delivered to the mastering engineer in stems — grouped submixes — rather than a single stereo file. The mastering engineer processes each stem independently before summing them to the final master, providing more control over specific elements than full-mix mastering allows.

When to use stem mastering: Stem mastering makes sense when the mix has specific elements that would benefit from independent mastering treatment — a bass stem that needs separate compression and limiting from the rest of the mix, a vocal stem that needs individual high-frequency enhancement, or drums that need independent transient control. It also makes sense when the mix is going to be adapted for different formats with different requirements — the stems can be summed differently for vinyl (mono bass processing applied only to the bass stem) versus streaming (full stereo mix).

What stems to request: The most useful stem groupings for mastering are: drums (all drum elements summed), bass (bass guitar, 808, and other low-frequency melodic elements), instruments (all melodic and harmonic content excluding bass), and vocals (lead and backing vocals combined). Four stems is the standard. More stems (kick separate from drums, lead vocal separate from backing vocals) provide more control at the cost of more complexity and time.

The stem mastering limitation: Stem mastering cannot replicate the precision of individual track mixing. The drum stem is already a fixed balance of kick, snare, and cymbals — the mastering engineer can apply compression to the drum stem but cannot change the kick-to-snare ratio within it. If the drum balance is wrong, stem mastering does not solve the problem. Stem mastering is a tool for adding mastering-grade processing to specific groups, not for fixing mix-level balance problems.

AI Mastering (LANDR, Ozone, eMastered): An Honest Assessment

AI mastering services — LANDR, eMastered, iZotope's Ozone Master Assistant, Matchering — have improved significantly and are producing results that compete with budget human mastering for typical pop, electronic, and hip-hop material. An honest assessment requires separating marketing claims from actual capability.

What AI mastering does well: AI mastering analyses the uploaded audio and applies automated processing calibrated to genre context and reference track analysis. For well-mixed tracks in conventional genres with standard structures, AI mastering produces technically correct masters — appropriate loudness targets, balanced frequency response, clean limiting — faster and at lower cost than human mastering. LANDR at its highest quality setting and Ozone's Master Assistant both produce masters that pass the technical requirements for streaming distribution and would not be identifiable as AI-mastered in a blind test by most listeners on most material.

What AI mastering does poorly: AI mastering does not understand the music's creative intention. It cannot distinguish between a deliberately dark mix (where a low-mid heavy frequency balance is intentional) and an accidentally dark mix (where low-mid buildup is a recording problem). It cannot make creative decisions about dynamic range — whether this specific song calls for a loud, compressed master or a wide-dynamic, restrained one. It does not handle edge cases (unconventional structures, extreme genres, heavily distorted material, experimental music) with the nuance of an experienced human mastering engineer.

What Mastering Cannot Fix (And What Needs to Go Back to the Mix)

This is the most important section for independent producers who are considering whether to spend more time on the mix or send it to mastering. Being specific about what mastering genuinely cannot address saves time, money, and the disappointment of expecting a problem to be solved at mastering that belongs in the mix.

Frequency problems specific to individual instruments: A bass guitar that sounds muddy at 300 Hz is a bass guitar EQ problem. A snare drum ring at 600 Hz is a snare EQ problem. A mastering EQ cut at these frequencies improves the problem element but simultaneously reduces the same frequencies in every other element in the mix — the kick drum, the piano, the vocal reverb, the room mics. For broad tonal balance issues that affect the entire mix, mastering EQ is appropriate. For problems specific to individual elements, only the mix session can address them correctly.

Timing and pitch problems: A performance with timing inconsistencies or pitch issues cannot be corrected at mastering. Pitch correction and timing correction are individual-track processes that require access to the original stems. A mastering engineer receiving a stereo mix with a flat vocal phrase has no way to correct it without stem access.

Poor arrangement or production decisions: A mix that sounds empty in the chorus because the arrangement is too sparse, or that sounds cluttered because too many elements compete simultaneously, is an arrangement problem. Mastering can make a sparse arrangement louder and perhaps slightly more present, but it cannot add the musical elements that the arrangement lacks.

Severe dynamic problems in the mix: A mix where the vocal rides wildly — 10 dB louder in some phrases than others — is a vocal automation problem. Mastering compression can reduce the peak-to-average ratio of the mix globally, but applying enough compression to control an unmixed vocal makes the entire mix compressed. Only volume automation on the vocal track in the mix session solves this correctly.

Reverb and delay that don't suit the arrangement: A reverb that is too long, too short, too wet, or whose character doesn't match the other elements in the mix is a mixing decision. Mastering cannot selectively adjust the reverb level or character on individual elements.

When to Hire a Professional Mastering Engineer vs Self-Master

Self-mastering is appropriate, achievable, and produces excellent results in many contexts. Professional mastering is appropriate in specific contexts where the cost justifies the added value. Being honest about when each is the right choice saves money in situations where self-mastering is sufficient and prevents self-mastering in situations where a professional is genuinely needed.

Professional mastering is justified when: The release is commercially important — a debut album, a single with significant marketing investment, or a release going to physical retail. When the music is destined for vinyl — the specific technical requirements and quality expectations of vinyl cutting require mastering engineers with specialised equipment and experience. When an independent creative perspective on the mix's final state adds genuine value — experienced mastering engineers have heard enough successful records to provide meaningful feedback on whether a mix is ready and how it compares to commercial releases in its genre. When technical delivery requirements are complex — multiple format deliverables, broadcast specifications, STEM mastering for multiple versions.

Self-mastering is appropriate when: The release is a demo, a reference master, or an early-career release where quality expectations are realistic. When the production budget doesn't support professional mastering costs without compromising the production quality (it's better to have an excellent mix with basic mastering than a mediocre mix with expensive mastering). When the producer has developed genuine mastering skills — the ability to hear at a mastering level, make accurate corrections, and evaluate masters against commercial references reliably. When turnaround speed is critical and the mastering session can be completed correctly without the delay of external mastering.

The Lasting Mastering Philosophy

The engineers who have been mastering records for decades — Bob Ludwig, Bernie Grundman, Greg Calbi, Emily Lazar — share a set of habits and principles that distinguish their work from that of engineers who last a few years and fade. These habits are learnable and applicable at any level of experience.

The work is the music: Every technical decision serves the music's emotional and artistic intention. A mastering engineer who is optimising for technical benchmarks rather than musical communication has confused the means with the end. The technical targets — LUFS, true peak, frequency balance — are tools for serving the music, not goals in themselves. When a technical target conflicts with the music's character, the music wins.

The best work is invisible: A masterpiece of mastering engineering is a record that listeners play repeatedly without ever noticing the mastering. The moment a listener notices that the master is very loud, very wide, very bright, or very compressed, the mastering has become part of the experience rather than serving it. Restraint is not timidity — it is discipline applied in service of the music.

Every session starts with listening: The worst mastering decisions are made before the engineer has genuinely listened to the mix. Equipment is calibrated and ready. The mix plays in its entirety without touching a single processor. Notes are written. Only after this initial listening does processing begin. This practice is inconvenient and easy to skip. It is also the single habit most consistently separating excellent mastering from good mastering.

The reference is a compass, not a destination: A reference track shows where the target tonal space is. The master being processed should move in that direction — toward the reference's tonal balance and dynamics — without becoming an imitation of it. The mastered track retains its own identity and the creative decisions of the mix while inhabiting the same quality space as the reference. References guide; they don't dictate.

Know when to stop: The most experienced mastering engineers develop a clear sense of when the work is done and are willing to stop even when more processing feels possible. The question is never "what more can I do?" but "has the music been served?" When the answer is yes, the session is over.

How to Calibrate Your Monitors for Mastering: The 83 dB SPL Method

The monitoring level at which mastering decisions are made affects those decisions profoundly. Monitoring too loud causes ear fatigue and causes engineers to reduce high frequencies to compensate — producing masters that sound dull at moderate volumes. Monitoring too quietly reduces the apparent bass response and causes engineers to boost low end unnecessarily. Professional mastering rooms calibrate their monitoring levels specifically, and the most widely adopted standard is 83 dB SPL — the film industry mixing standard also adopted by many music mastering engineers as a reliable reference level.

Calibrating to 83 dB SPL: Use a calibrated SPL meter (a dedicated hardware meter or a calibrated iOS/Android SPL meter app). Play pink noise at -20 dBFS from your DAW. Position the SPL meter at the listening position, aimed at the center of the stereo image between the monitors. Adjust the monitor volume control until the SPL meter reads 83 dB SPL (C-weighted, slow response). This is your calibrated reference monitoring level. Mark this position on your monitor volume control and return to it at the start of every session.

At 83 dB SPL, the equal-loudness curves of human hearing produce a perceived frequency balance that is reasonably representative of how the music will be heard at different levels. The bass is not over-represented (as it would be at very high monitoring levels) and the high frequencies are not exaggerated (as they might be at very low levels). Mastering decisions made at this level tend to translate reliably to other playback systems and listening levels.

Periodic low-volume check: After making mastering decisions at 83 dB SPL, drop the monitoring level to 70 dB SPL (or a conversational background level) and listen to the master. At low volume, frequency balance perception shifts — the Fletcher-Munson equal-loudness curves mean that bass and very high frequencies are perceived as quieter relative to the midrange. A master that sounds balanced at 83 dB but has the vocal disappear or the bass thin out dramatically at low volume has midrange clarity problems that low-volume checking reveals.

How to Build a Mastering Template in Logic, Ableton, and FL Studio

A mastering session template standardises the starting conditions for every mastering session — ensuring the signal chain is set up correctly, monitoring is calibrated, and reference tools are loaded before any audio arrives. Templates eliminate setup time and reduce the likelihood of processing omissions. Here is the complete mastering template structure for the three most common DAWs.

Logic Pro mastering template: Create a new empty project. Add a stereo audio track for the mix import. Insert on the mix track, in order: a gain plugin (for input level adjustment), a linear-phase high-pass filter (cleaning sub-bass rumble), Corrective EQ (Logic's Channel EQ or a third-party parametric), a vintage compressor (Logic's Compressor in Vintage Opto mode), a stereo imager (for M-S width adjustment), an additive EQ (second Channel EQ instance), and the Adaptive Limiter as the final insert. Add a second stereo audio track for the reference track with its own gain plugin for loudness matching. Save as a template and open from the template selector for every new mastering session.

Ableton Live mastering template: Create a new set with a single audio track for the mix. In the effects chain: Utility (for input gain and mono sum testing), EQ Eight (corrective), Redux or a third-party saturator (optional tape colour), Glue Compressor (mastering compression), EQ Eight second instance (additive), and Limiter (ceiling and loudness). Create a second audio track with the same Utility gain control for the reference track. Create a macro-mapped rack that exposes the most important controls — input gain, compressor ratio, limiter ceiling — for quick adjustment without navigating individual plugins.

FL Studio mastering template: Create a new project with an empty mixer. Load the mix audio onto Mixer track 1. Set up the plugin chain on Track 1: Parametric EQ 2 (corrective), Maximus (compression and limiting, two separate instances — one for gentle compression, one for final limiting), and a second Parametric EQ 2 (additive). Create Mixer track 2 for the reference with its own level control. In the master mixer track, insert a level meter plugin showing both LUFS and true peak values. Save as template.

Codec Preview: Hearing Your Master as Streaming Will Deliver It

Your master will be encoded by streaming platforms — typically to AAC (Spotify, Apple Music, Tidal) or Opus (YouTube Music) for delivery. The encoding process introduces artifacts: subtle high-frequency smearing, occasional transient distortion, and slight changes to the stereo image. In most cases these artifacts are inaudible at the bit rates used by premium streaming services (256–320 kbps AAC, high-quality Opus). At lower bit rates (used by some free tiers and mobile data-limited playback), the artifacts become more significant.

Codec preview — rendering your master to the streaming codec and comparing it to the original — is a quality check that catches problems before delivery. iZotope Ozone Advanced includes Codec Preview (available in the standalone mastering application). Alternatively, use FFmpeg (free, command-line) to render your WAV master to AAC at 256 kbps and compare in your DAW. Listen specifically for: high-frequency loss or smearing, transient distortion on percussive elements, and changes to the stereo image on wide material. If significant artifacts appear at 256 kbps, the master has content that the codec struggles with — typically very wide stereo content, extreme high-frequency energy, or complex transient stacks that confuse the perceptual encoder.

The practical fix for codec sensitivity: reduce extreme stereo width in the Side channel (wide content is harder for codecs to encode efficiently), apply gentle high-frequency limiting above 15 kHz (ultra-high frequencies that are inaudible but require codec bandwidth), and avoid stacking multiple highly dynamic transient sources at the same moment (codec bit allocation is spread across simultaneous transients, reducing each one's resolution).

Album Mastering: Track Sequencing, Spacing, and Tonal Consistency

Mastering an album requires decisions that single mastering doesn't — track order, spacing between tracks, tonal consistency across the album, and the overall dynamic arc of the listening experience. These decisions are as musically important as the processing applied to individual tracks.

Track sequencing: The order of tracks shapes the emotional narrative of the album. Standard principles: the opening track should immediately establish the album's character and quality (it's the first impression — don't open with the weakest track or the most experimental one unless that's intentional). The closing track should feel like an ending — a resolution, a statement, or a departure. The most energetic or commercially accessible track is typically placed second or third, not first. Quieter or more reflective tracks should be placed after high-energy tracks to give the listener space to breathe.

Track spacing: The silence between tracks communicates musical relationship. No silence (seamless crossfade) suggests the tracks are continuous movements of the same piece. Short silence (0.5–1 second) groups tracks as related but distinct. Standard silence (2–3 seconds) is the conventional gap between separate tracks. Longer silence (4+ seconds) creates a deliberate pause that emphasises the separation between sections. The spacing between tracks is mastered into the DDP image (for CD) or specified in the sequence of separate WAV files for digital delivery. Most distribution services determine their own streaming gap — the gap specified in the master file may not be preserved exactly in streaming playback.

Tonal consistency across the album: Every track in an album should feel like it belongs to the same body of work — similar tonal balance, similar loudness, similar dynamic character. Tracks that were produced at different times, in different studios, or by different mix engineers may have significant tonal differences that mastering must reconcile. Apply consistent broad tonal adjustments across the album before addressing individual track issues, then correct individual tracks from that baseline. A track that's significantly brighter or darker than the others should be brought toward the album's tonal centre, even if that means compromising its optimal individual sound slightly.

Dynamic arc: The loudness of individual tracks relative to each other creates a dynamic arc for the album. Not every track needs to be mastered to the same LUFS — a ballad in the middle of an album might be appropriately 1–2 LUFS quieter than the surrounding uptempo tracks. What matters is that the loudness differences feel intentional rather than inconsistent. A quiet track that feels quieter than it should (not a deliberate ballad pause but just a weak master) disrupts the listening experience more than a carefully planned dynamic gradient.

How to Develop Your Mastering Ear: A Practical Training Programme

Mastering-level listening is a skill that develops over time through deliberate practice. The ability to hear a mix and identify what it needs — before touching a processor — is what separates effective mastering from technically competent but musically disconnected processing. This training programme accelerates that development.

Exercise 1 — Spectrum identification: Load ten commercial tracks from different genres. For each, without using a spectrum analyser, describe the tonal balance in writing: "This track has elevated upper-mids around 3-4 kHz and a controlled bass that doesn't extend below 60 Hz. The high end has gentle air above 10 kHz without any harshness." Then load the spectrum analyser and compare your written description to what the analyser shows. Discrepancies identify the gaps in your listening perception. Repeat weekly.

Exercise 2 — Loudness perception calibration: Load two tracks from the same genre, one a recent commercial release and one a track you've mixed or mastered. Without any loudness meters, adjust the levels until they sound equal in perceived loudness. Measure the integrated LUFS of both at the level you set. The difference between your perceived match and the measured LUFS difference reveals your loudness perception bias — whether you perceive low-end heavy signals as louder or quieter than measurements suggest, and whether you're systematically over- or under-compensating for frequency balance differences when judging loudness.

Exercise 3 — Bypass comparison speed: Load a compressor on a bus. Set it to 2:1, 50 ms attack, auto release, 3 dB gain reduction. Rapidly bypass and un-bypass the compressor every 2–3 seconds while making notes about what changes. Initially, producers notice only loudness — the compressed signal sounds different at different levels. With practice, you begin to hear the transient shaping (the kick's initial hit changes), the low-frequency response (compression affects perceived bass weight), and the stereo image (compression can narrow the stereo field slightly). Continue this exercise with different compressors and settings until you can describe the character difference within 3 seconds of switching.

Exercise 4 — Reference deconstruction: Take a professionally mastered track you know well. Load it into your DAW on a stereo audio track. Apply EQ, compression, saturation, and limiting to a separate identical instance of the same track to match the sound of the master. The goal is not to literally re-master a mastered track but to develop an ear for what each processor contributes to the overall sound — you're reverse-engineering the mastering decisions by matching them with conscious choices. This exercise is challenging and revealing, and produces significant perceptual improvement after several sessions.

Mastering File Delivery: Formats, Naming Conventions, and Metadata

The final step of mastering is correct file delivery — ensuring the master arrives at its destination in the right format, with the right naming convention, and with the correct metadata embedded. Incorrect delivery creates problems that may not be discovered until after the release is already distributed.

File format by destination:

  • Digital distribution (streaming): WAV, 24-bit, 44.1 kHz. Some distributors accept 48 kHz — check your specific distributor's requirements. Never MP3.
  • CD: WAV or AIFF, 16-bit, 44.1 kHz, dithered from 24-bit master. Delivered as a DDP (Disc Description Protocol) image for manufacturing, or as individual WAV files with a cue sheet for independent CD-R use.
  • Vinyl: WAV, 24-bit, 44.1 kHz or 48 kHz as specified by the cutting engineer. With vinyl-specific processing applied (mono bass, sibilance control, appropriate loudness). Include a reference MP3 for the cutting engineer to compare against.
  • High-res download (Bandcamp, Qobuz): WAV or FLAC at 24-bit, 96 kHz. Note: mastering at 96 kHz requires a recording session and mix that was conducted at 96 kHz — upsampling a 44.1 kHz mix to 96 kHz for delivery does not improve quality.
  • Sync licensing: WAV, 24-bit, 48 kHz (matching the video production standard). Broadcast-loudness masters at -23 LUFS (EBU R128) if specified.

Naming conventions: Consistent, descriptive file naming prevents confusion across different versions and deliverables. A robust naming convention: Artist_Title_Version_SampleRate_BitDepth.wav. Example: KateMonroe_WhenItRains_MasterStreaming_44k_24b.wav. Avoid special characters, spaces, and ambiguous version numbering. If delivering multiple versions (streaming, vinyl, instrumental, TV Mix), the version descriptor must clearly differentiate each file.

Metadata embedding: Embed metadata directly into the WAV or FLAC file using a dedicated metadata editor (Kid3, Yate on Mac, Mp3tag on Windows — all handle WAV metadata correctly). Required fields: artist name, track title, album name (if applicable), year, genre, ISRC code, and track number. Optional but recommended: composer, producer, BPM, key, copyright notice. Embedded metadata survives the distribution process and is read by streaming platforms, improving the accuracy of reporting and royalty attribution.

ISRC codes in masters: The ISRC (International Standard Recording Code) uniquely identifies each sound recording and is the link between a specific recording and the royalties it generates. Embed the ISRC in the WAV file's metadata and register the same ISRC with your PRO and the MLC. Distributors assign ISRCs if you don't provide them — but keeping your own ISRC registry ensures continuity if you change distributors. An ISRC is permanent and specific to the recording; re-recordings, remixes, and alternate versions each require their own unique ISRC.

Frequently Asked Questions About Music Mastering

What LUFS should I master to for Spotify?

Master to -14 LUFS integrated for Spotify, with a true peak ceiling of -1 dBTP. Spotify normalises all tracks to this target, so masters louder than -14 LUFS are turned down to match — meaning extra limiting to get louder only degrades your audio quality without benefit. If you're targeting Apple Music specifically, their target is -16 LUFS. For YouTube Music and Tidal, -14 LUFS matches Spotify's target. For Bandcamp (no normalisation), genre-appropriate competitive loudness applies.

What is the difference between mixing and mastering?

Mixing is the process of combining and balancing individual tracks — adjusting the level of the kick drum, EQing the vocal, adding reverb to the snare, panning guitars left and right. It works on individual elements. Mastering is applied to the finished stereo mix as a whole — EQ, compression, limiting, and loudness adjustment applied to the combined output of the entire mix to prepare it for distribution. Mastering cannot selectively process individual elements; it affects everything simultaneously. A good mastering engineer needs a good mix to work with — mastering cannot fix problems that belong in the mix.

How loud should a master be?

For streaming distribution: -14 LUFS integrated is the standard target, matching Spotify and YouTube's normalisation levels. For tracks primarily targeting Apple Music: -16 LUFS. For CD (no normalisation): -10 to -14 LUFS is typical for most popular genres. For vinyl: -12 to -18 LUFS, significantly quieter than streaming masters, because vinyl cutting has physical loudness limitations and dynamic range is part of the vinyl listening experience. The key principle: don't master louder than the platform's normalisation target — the loudness gets normalised away and you only introduce limiting artefacts.

Can I master my own music at home?

Yes — self-mastering at home produces excellent results when the mix is genuinely good, the monitoring environment is reasonably accurate, and the producer has developed mastering-level listening skills. The biggest challenge is monitoring: untreated rooms introduce acoustic problems that make accurate mastering decisions difficult. Calibrated headphones (Beyerdynamic DT 770 Pro, Sony MDR-7506) supplemented by speaker listening help compensate. Self-mastering is appropriate for demos, early-career releases, and high-volume independent releases. Professional mastering is worth the cost for commercially important releases, vinyl masters, and situations where a trained second opinion on the mix's quality adds genuine value.

What format should I export my master for streaming?

Export your master as a WAV file, 24-bit, 44.1 kHz for streaming distribution. Never deliver MP3 to a distributor — they encode from your master to their streaming codec (AAC, Opus), and MP3-to-AAC transcoding introduces generation loss and audible artifacts. Some distributors accept FLAC, which is lossless and acceptable. The standard 44.1 kHz sample rate is correct for streaming delivery; 48 kHz is used for sync licensing deliverables to match the film and TV production standard. High-res download platforms (Bandcamp, Qobuz) accept 24-bit/96 kHz if your recording and mix were conducted at that sample rate.

What is true peak limiting and why does it matter?

True peak measures the level of the reconstructed analogue waveform between digital samples — not just the sample values themselves. Because streaming codecs (AAC, MP3) reconstruct audio from digital samples, the reconstructed waveform can reach higher levels between samples than the samples indicate — called inter-sample peaks. A master with sample peaks at -0.1 dBFS may have true peaks of +0.5 to +1 dBTP, which clips during codec encoding and causes audible distortion in the delivered stream. Set your limiter's output ceiling to -1 dBTP true peak (not sample peak) to prevent this. All professional limiters (FabFilter Pro-L2, iZotope Ozone Maximizer) have a true peak mode — always use it.

How do I know if my master is ready for release?

A master is ready when: (1) It passes the mono compatibility test — summing to mono doesn't lose important elements or cause phase cancellation. (2) It sounds competitive when A/B compared against three commercial references in the same genre at matched loudness. (3) It translates across multiple playback systems — studio monitors, earbuds, a car stereo, and a phone speaker — without losing its essential character. (4) The true peak ceiling is at -1 dBTP or below. (5) The integrated LUFS matches your target platform's normalisation level. (6) There is no clipping, distortion, or digital artifacts anywhere in the file. (7) You have listened with fresh ears after at least a night's sleep since the mastering session.

What is stem mastering and when should I use it?

Stem mastering is when the mix is delivered to the mastering engineer in grouped submixes (stems) rather than a single stereo file — typically drums, bass, instruments, and vocals as four separate stereo files. The mastering engineer processes each stem independently before summing them. Stem mastering is useful when specific elements need treatment that would negatively affect the rest of the mix if applied to the stereo bus — heavy bass compression that would over-compress the vocal, or high-frequency enhancement on the vocal that would make the cymbals harsh. It's also used for vinyl mastering (applying mono bass processing only to the bass stem) and for creating multiple versions from the same session. Stem mastering costs more than full-mix mastering and requires stems to be delivered correctly — check with your engineer before requesting stems.

Is AI mastering good enough to use professionally?

AI mastering (LANDR, iZotope Ozone Master Assistant, eMastered) produces technically correct masters for well-mixed tracks in conventional genres — appropriate loudness, balanced frequency response, clean limiting. For frequent releases, demos, and independent releases where professional mastering cost isn't justified, AI mastering is a legitimate option. It falls short on: material with complex technical requirements, vinyl masters (AI services don't handle vinyl-specific requirements), unconventional music where creative judgment matters more than technical correctness, and any situation where a human mastering engineer's second opinion on the mix's quality adds genuine value. Use AI mastering where it's appropriate; use human mastering where the stakes and budget justify the difference.

How much compression should I use when mastering?

Mastering compression is measured in single-digit dB of gain reduction — 1–3 dB is the standard range for transparent mastering compression. Settings: ratio 1.2:1 to 2:1, attack 30–100 ms (slow, allowing transients through), release auto or 150–300 ms, gain reduction 1–3 dB. The compressor type matters as much as the settings — optical and Vari-Mu compressors (Tube-Tech CL 1B, Waves PuigChild) behave more musically at low gain reduction than VCA compressors at the same settings. If you're applying more than 4–5 dB of mastering compression, the mix needs more work before mastering — mastering compression is for subtle cohesion, not dynamic control that belongs in the mix.
