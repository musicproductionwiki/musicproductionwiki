Stereo width is one of the most powerful tools in modern music production, capable of transforming flat, narrow mixes into immersive sonic experiences that captivate listeners. Whether you're producing electronic music, mixing rock bands, or mastering commercial tracks, understanding how to control and enhance stereo imaging separates amateur productions from professional releases. The challenge with stereo widening isn't simply making tracks sound wider—it's doing so while maintaining phase coherence, preserving low-end focus, and ensuring your mix translates across all playback systems from earbuds to club sound systems. Professional engineers rely on sophisticated plugins that employ multiple widening techniques simultaneously, offering precise control over frequency ranges, stereo correlation, and harmonic content.

Understanding Stereo Widening Technologies

Before diving into specific plugins, it's essential to understand the fundamental technologies that create the perception of width in stereo recordings. Different plugins employ different methods, and knowing these distinctions helps you choose the right tool for each situation.

Mid-side processing forms the foundation of most professional stereo widening workflows. This technique separates audio into two components: the mid channel (mono information identical in both left and right speakers) and the side channel (the difference between left and right). By increasing the level of the side channel relative to the mid channel, you create the perception of width. The beauty of M/S processing is its inherent mono compatibility—when summed to mono, the side information cancels out, leaving only the mid channel intact. This makes it the safest widening method for commercial productions that must translate to mono playback scenarios.

The Haas effect, also called precedence effect or delay-based widening, creates width by introducing subtle time delays between left and right channels. When one channel is delayed by approximately 5-35 milliseconds relative to the other, our auditory system perceives the sound as wider rather than as a distinct echo. However, this technique can introduce phase problems when summed to mono, potentially causing partial or complete signal cancellation. Modern plugins using this method often include phase monitoring and correction tools to minimize these issues.

Stereo chorusing and microshifting techniques create width through pitch modulation and multiple delayed copies. These methods, popularized by hardware units like the Eventide H3000, generate multiple slightly detuned copies of the signal and pan them across the stereo field. The subtle pitch variations create a natural sense of space and dimension without the harshness that can result from simple delay-based widening. This approach works particularly well on vocals, guitars, and synthesizers where you want organic movement rather than static width.

Harmonic enhancement and subtle saturation can contribute to perceived width by adding frequency content that didn't exist in the original signal. When these harmonics are generated differently in the left and right channels, they create additional stereo information that our ears interpret as width. Some advanced plugins combine harmonic generation with spatial processing for a richer, more dimensional sound.

Professional Stereo Widening Plugins

The plugin displays stereo width across the frequency spectrum in real-time, making it easy to identify narrow elements and apply targeted widening. While primarily a compressor at $179, Pro-C 2's stereo imaging capabilities deserve recognition in any serious widening discussion.

Mono Compatibility: The Non-Negotiable Rule

No matter which widening plugin you choose or how dramatic your width enhancement, your mix must maintain its balance and impact when summed to mono. Nearly 40% of music listening still occurs in mono or near-mono conditions: smartphone speakers, Bluetooth speakers, club sound systems, retail environments, and broadcast. Before finalizing any widening treatment, check your mix in mono using a correlation meter and critical listening. If essential elements disappear, become thin, or change tonally in mono, reduce your widening or switch techniques. Professional engineers often employ high-pass filters before widening processors, keeping everything below 200-300 Hz in mono to prevent bass phase issues while freely enhancing width above that threshold.

Frequency-Conscious Widening Strategies

The most effective stereo widening applications recognize that different frequency ranges require different approaches. A frequency-conscious strategy produces mixes that sound wide yet powerful, translating across all playback systems.

Low frequencies below 200 Hz should remain largely mono in professional mixes. Bass information has long wavelengths that interact poorly with stereo widening, creating phase problems that reduce power on large sound systems and cause unpredictable behavior in mono. When bass is too wide, it loses punch and can even disappear entirely when summed to mono. Professional engineers typically keep kick drums, bass guitars, and sub-bass synthesizers strictly mono, using width only on bass harmonics above 200-300 Hz. This maintains the physical impact of low frequencies while allowing the melodic content to benefit from spatial enhancement.

The midrange between 200 Hz and 4 kHz is where width creates depth and space in your mix. This frequency range contains the fundamental frequencies of most instruments and the body of vocals. Moderate width enhancement here separates instruments in the stereo field, reducing masking and improving clarity. However, extreme widening in the midrange can make mixes sound hollow or phasey. The key is balance: rhythm guitars, pad synthesizers, and background vocals benefit from width enhancement, while lead vocals, snare drums, and primary melodic elements often sound best when kept relatively centered. Using mid-side EQ in combination with width control allows you to enhance specific frequency ranges within the midrange for surgical spatial sculpting.

High frequencies above 4 kHz respond beautifully to width enhancement, creating air, dimension, and polish. Cymbals, vocal breath, string harmonics, and synthesizer air frequencies can be widened dramatically without mono compatibility concerns or phase issues. Many professional mixing engineers apply substantial width to everything above 8 kHz, creating a halo of space around the mix that sounds expansive on stereo systems while contributing minimal information to mono playback. This high-frequency width is the "secret sauce" in many commercial productions, providing the perception of an expensive, spacious mix without risking phase problems or compromising low-frequency power.

Genre-Specific Widening Applications

Different musical genres have distinct stereo imaging expectations, and successful widening respects these stylistic conventions while pushing creative boundaries.

Electronic dance music demands width for immersive club and festival experiences while maintaining devastating bass impact on large sound systems. EDM producers typically keep everything below 150 Hz strictly mono, ensuring kick drums and sub-bass hit with maximum power regardless of speaker placement or room acoustics. Synthesizer leads often receive dramatic width enhancement through mid-side processing or stereo chorus effects, creating the expansive, enveloping quality that defines modern EDM production. Build-ups and breakdowns benefit from automation of stereo width, collapsing to mono before drops for maximum impact when width suddenly returns. Sidechain compression frequently works in tandem with width control, creating rhythmic pumping that enhances the sense of space. Ambient pads and atmospheric elements can be widened extremely, sometimes using 150-200% width settings that exceed the normal stereo field through Haas-effect processing, though this requires careful mono checking.

Rock and indie productions require a more grounded, organic approach to width. Drums typically receive width primarily from natural overhead and room microphone placement rather than artificial processing, though subtle width enhancement on cymbals and room mics can increase size without sounding synthetic. Electric guitars benefit from natural width created by double-tracking and hard-panning, with additional subtle width from stereo chorus or doubling plugins on individual performances. Lead vocals stay centered and relatively narrow to maintain power and intelligibility, while background vocals and harmonies can be widened to create depth and separation from the lead. The mixing philosophy in rock tends toward "width through arrangement" rather than heavy plugin processing, using the stereo field to create space between multiple real performances rather than artificially expanding individual tracks.

Hip-hop and R&B productions balance wide, lush harmonics with powerful, focused low end. Kick drums and 808 bass remain strictly mono and often are enhanced with subtle harmonic distortion that adds width to upper harmonics while keeping the fundamental centered. Vocal production in these genres has evolved toward intimate, present lead vocals with wide, ambient backgrounds created through reverb, delay, and doubling effects. Melodic samples and loops often receive mid-side EQ treatment that narrows low-mids for vocal clarity while widening highs for air and space. The snare drum typically stays centered, but the use of stereo reverb on the snare creates width in the decay without compromising the attack's punch. Modern R&B production frequently employs extreme automation of width on vocals during choruses, creating dynamic expansion that draws listeners into emotional peaks.

Orchestral and acoustic productions rely heavily on natural room acoustics and microphone technique for width, with plugin enhancement serving subtle supportive roles. The goal is maintaining realistic spatial relationships between instruments as they would appear in concert halls. String sections benefit from gentle width enhancement that reinforces their natural spread across the stage, while solo instruments remain appropriately positioned within that soundscape. Room microphones and hall reverb provide most of the width information, with stereo widening plugins used sparingly to enhance rather than create spatial characteristics. When processing classical or jazz recordings, correlation meters become critical tools, ensuring that plugin processing doesn't create artificial phase relationships that damage the natural acoustic space captured during recording. Many classical mixing engineers avoid stereo widening plugins entirely, relying instead on reverb techniques and careful balance of spot microphones against room perspective.

Advanced Widening Techniques and Creative Applications

Beyond basic width enhancement, advanced techniques combine multiple processors and creative routing for unique spatial effects that define modern productions.

Serial widening chains use multiple plugins in sequence, each contributing different aspects of spatial enhancement. A typical professional chain might start with FabFilter Pro-Q 3 in mid-side mode, applying narrow EQ boosts to the side channel in frequency ranges where you want enhanced width—perhaps boosting 8 kHz by 2 dB on the sides to enhance air and sparkle. Next in the chain, a subtle stereo chorus like Soundtoys MicroShift adds organic movement and dimension without obvious modulation. Finally, iZotope Ozone Imager provides final width adjustment with careful monitoring of mono compatibility. This layered approach creates dimension that sounds natural rather than obviously processed, as each plugin contributes a subtle piece of the overall spatial picture rather than any single processor doing heavy lifting.

Parallel widening techniques split the signal into separate processing chains for mid and side information, allowing completely different treatment of each. By sending your source to two channels—one processing only the mid component, the other only the sides—you can apply compression, EQ, saturation, and effects independently before recombining them. This technique allows aggressive processing of the side channel to create width while leaving the mid channel clean and powerful. For example, you might heavily compress and saturate the side channel of a vocal bus, creating dense harmonic width, while keeping the mid channel dynamic and present. The result is a vocal that cuts through the mix with clarity while existing in a wide, lush space.

Dynamic width processing uses envelope followers or MIDI control to modulate width parameters in response to audio level or musical events. Plugins like Waves Center or custom builds in modular environments like Max for Live allow width to expand during quiet passages and contract during loud sections, or vice versa. This creates mixes that breathe and move, with spatial characteristics that support the emotional arc of the music. In mastering, dynamic width can increase the perception of dynamics by making quiet sections sound more expansive and intimate while loud sections focus power in the center. The technique requires careful calibration to avoid obvious pumping, but when done subtly, it adds professional polish that keeps listeners engaged.

Frequency-specific harmonic widening uses distortion, saturation, or exciters in mid-side mode to generate new harmonic content exclusively in the side channel. By processing only the side information through subtle saturation plugins like Soundtoys Decapuncher or FabFilter Saturn 2, you create harmonics that exist only in the stereo field, dramatically increasing perceived width without affecting the mono component. This technique works exceptionally well on synthesizers, electric guitars, and full mixes where you want to add dimension and excitement. The key is keeping the saturation subtle—typically 10-20% mix—so the effect enhances rather than dominates. Some engineers combine this with high-pass filtering on the side channel saturation, ensuring that any distortion products remain above the bass region where they might cause phase issues.

Stereo width automation throughout arrangements creates dramatic production moments and maintains listener interest. Rather than setting static width values, professional producers automate width parameters to support song structure: narrow, focused verses that expand into wide, spacious choruses; bridge sections that collapse almost to mono before exploding back to full width for the final chorus; instrumental breaks where width increases gradually as tension builds. This requires detailed automation lanes and careful listening, but the results are productions that use space as a compositional element rather than just a mixing consideration. In digital audio workstation environments, width automation has become as fundamental as volume and pan automation for creating engaging modern mixes.

Common Widening Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

Even experienced engineers can fall into stereo widening traps that compromise mix quality. Understanding these common mistakes helps you develop better spatial processing habits.

Over-widening is the most frequent error, occurring when engineers push width controls beyond what serves the music. More width doesn't automatically mean better mixes—it means different spatial characteristics that may or may not be appropriate. Excessive width creates phasey, hollow sounds that lack impact and often disappear partially in mono. The telltale sign of over-widening is when your mix sounds impressive in headphones but weak and diffuse on speakers, particularly in untreated rooms where stereo imaging is less defined. Professional engineers typically use width enhancement as a subtle tool, with most settings falling between 110-130% of the original width rather than the extreme 200% settings that many plugins allow. If you're unsure whether you've widened too much, the mono button provides instant clarity—if your track loses significant energy or presence in mono, you've gone too far.

Widening low frequencies remains a persistent problem, particularly among less experienced producers who apply broadband widening without frequency-conscious filtering. When bass and kick drums contain significant stereo information, they lose the physical impact that drives music forward. On club sound systems with subwoofer arrays, wide bass can create cancellation zones where certain audience members hear dramatically reduced low-end. The solution is simple but requires discipline: always high-pass filter your widening processors or use multiband tools that allow you to keep bass frequencies mono. A high-pass filter at 200-300 Hz before any widening processor ensures that spatial enhancement affects only frequencies that benefit from width. Some engineers go further, using steep brick-wall filters at 120 Hz to absolutely guarantee mono low-end regardless of processing chain complexity.

Ignoring phase correlation leads to mixes that sound great in headphones but translate poorly to speakers and completely fall apart in mono. The correlation meter should be your constant companion during widening work, displaying the phase relationship between left and right channels. Perfect mono registers as +1 on a correlation meter, perfect stereo as 0, and phase-reversed signals as -1. When widening pushes correlation below approximately +0.5, you're entering dangerous territory where mono compatibility suffers and speaker playback becomes unpredictable. Some experimental electronic music intentionally uses negative correlation for surreal effects, but commercial pop, rock, EDM, and hip-hop productions should maintain correlation above +0.5 at all times, with most material sitting between +0.7 and +1.0. Plugins like Waves PAZ Analyzer or Voxengo SPAN provide excellent correlation metering that should remain visible throughout your widening workflow.

Applying width as a fix for arrangement problems rarely succeeds. If your mix sounds cluttered or lacks separation, the solution is typically better arrangement, EQ techniques, or panning decisions rather than stereo widening. Widening cannot create space in a frequency range that's already overloaded with competing elements—it only spreads that overload across a wider area. Professional mixing requires honest assessment: if three guitar parts occupy the same frequency range and play similar rhythms, no amount of widening will create separation. The answer is arrangement editing, removing one guitar, or using EQ to carve distinct frequency spaces for each part. Once arrangement and frequency allocation are optimized, strategic width enhancement reinforces the clarity you've created rather than attempting to manufacture it from problematic sources.

Widening vocals inappropriately is another common error. Lead vocals in most genres should remain relatively narrow and centered to maintain intimacy, power, and intelligibility. Excessive width on lead vocals makes them sound distant, weakens emotional connection, and causes lyrics to become less clear. The exception is specific production styles like dream pop or ambient electronica where distant, ethereal vocals serve the artistic vision. For most commercial music, keep lead vocals narrow and centered, applying width only to vocal reverb, delay, and background harmonies. When you do widen lead vocals, use subtle settings around 110-120% and focus width enhancement above 4 kHz where it adds air without affecting vocal body and presence. Some engineers use dynamic width on vocals, allowing them to widen slightly during sustained notes while remaining focused during consonants and transient-rich passages, though this requires precise automation or sophisticated dynamic processing.

Measuring and Analyzing Stereo Width

Professional stereo widening requires objective measurement tools alongside subjective listening. Visual analysis reveals phase problems, frequency imbalances, and width distribution issues that aren't always immediately audible.

Correlation meters display the phase relationship between left and right channels as a single value or time-varying graph. As discussed earlier, correlation of +1 indicates perfect mono, while 0 indicates completely uncorrelated stereo information. Professional mixing engineers monitor correlation continuously during widening work, ensuring it never drops below safe thresholds. Many correlation meters include peak hold functions that capture the lowest correlation value during playback, alerting you to momentary phase issues that might not be audible on headphones but could cause problems on speakers. Some advanced meters like NuGen Stereoizer or TB Isone include frequency-dependent correlation displays, showing how phase relationships vary across the spectrum—invaluable for identifying specific frequency ranges where widening has created problems.

Goniometers (also called vectorscopes or Lissajous meters) provide visual representation of stereo width by plotting left and right channel relationships in two-dimensional space. Mono material appears as a diagonal line from lower left to upper right, while stereo material creates wider patterns that fill more of the display area. Perfect left-right balance produces symmetrical patterns, while imbalanced material leans toward one side. Goniometers instantly reveal over-widening—when the pattern extends beyond the safe stereo field or shows concave curves indicating negative correlation. Professional engineers reference the goniometer before and after widening processing, ensuring that enhancement creates appropriate expansion without phase reversal. Plugins like iZotope Insight 2, Nugen Visualizer, or free options like YOULEAN Loudness Meter include high-quality goniometer displays that should remain visible during critical mixing sessions.

Spectral width analyzers display stereo width across the frequency spectrum, showing exactly which frequency ranges contain stereo information and which are mono. Tools like iZotope Ozone Imager, Voxengo SPAN in mid-side mode, or Blue Cat's StereoScope Multiband provide color-coded displays where width varies across frequency. These displays make frequency-conscious widening dramatically easier—you can see at a glance whether your bass is truly mono, whether your mid-range has appropriate width, and whether high frequencies are sufficiently enhanced. Many spectral width analyzers include target curves or overlays showing recommended width distribution for various genres, helping less experienced engineers develop better spatial processing instincts. Advanced versions allow you to set target curves manually and apply matching processing, though most professional engineers prefer manual control over automated matching.

Side-to-mid ratio metering measures the balance between mono and stereo information, providing another perspective on mix width. This measurement expresses the level relationship between side and mid components, typically in decibels. A ratio of 0 dB indicates equal mid and side levels, positive values indicate more side than mid (very wide), and negative values indicate more mid than side (narrow). Professional mastering engineers often target side-to-mid ratios between -6 dB and +3 dB for balanced commercial mixes, though creative productions may intentionally exceed these ranges. By monitoring this ratio during widening work, you ensure that stereo enhancement remains proportional and doesn't overwhelm the mono foundation that provides mix power and mono compatibility.

Reference comparison tools allow you to load commercial tracks with similar genre and style, analyzing their stereo width characteristics for comparison with your own work. Plugins like ADPTR Metric AB, Magic AB, or Reference include stereo width measurement and display, making it easy to see whether your widening approaches commercial standards or deviates in ways that might compromise competitiveness. This isn't about copying reference tracks exactly—it's about understanding where your spatial processing sits relative to professional benchmarks. If your reference tracks show consistently narrower low-mids than your mix, that's valuable information suggesting you might need to reduce width in that range. Combined with critical listening, reference comparison builds your ability to judge appropriate width without relying entirely on visual meters.

The Mono Check Workflow

Implementing a systematic mono checking workflow prevents phase problems from reaching final mixes. Professional engineers check mono compatibility at multiple stages: after initial widening processing, after adding reverb and delays (which often add stereo information), during parallel processing bus adjustments, and before final export. Use a dedicated mono button plugin on your master bus or configure a keyboard shortcut in your DAW to toggle mono instantly. Listen for any mix elements that become noticeably quieter, thinner, or more harsh in mono—these are phase problems that require attention. Check mono at both comfortable listening levels and quietly, as phase issues sometimes become more apparent at lower volumes where ear sensitivity changes. If critical elements like vocals, snare, or bass change character significantly in mono, reduce width processing, adjust mid-side EQ, or reconsider your widening approach entirely. The goal isn't making your mix sound identical in mono and stereo, but ensuring it remains balanced and impactful regardless of playback system.

Creating professional stereo width requires balancing creative vision with technical rigor. The plugins reviewed in this guide provide powerful tools for spatial enhancement, but tools alone don't create great mixes—understanding the principles behind stereo widening, applying frequency-conscious processing, respecting genre conventions, and continuously measuring your results creates the foundation for mixes that translate across all playback systems while delivering the immersive width that modern listeners expect. Whether you're enhancing subtle air on acoustic recordings or creating massive EDM soundscapes, the combination of appropriate plugin selection, proper technique, and objective measurement ensures your spatial processing serves the music rather than compromising it. As you develop your widening skills, remember that restraint often produces better results than excess—professional width enhancement is the art of knowing when to widen, where to widen, and most importantly, when to leave well enough alone and let arrangement, panning techniques, and natural instrument characteristics create the spatial interest your production requires.

Practical Exercises

Beginner Exercise

Mono Bass, Wide Highs Foundation

Load a complete mix or full instrumental track and insert iZotope Ozone Imager (free version). Set the low crossover to 200 Hz and reduce width to 0% below that frequency. Gradually increase width above 200 Hz while monitoring in mono—find the maximum width setting where the track still sounds balanced in mono. This teaches you the fundamental relationship between frequency and safe widening.

Intermediate Exercise

Serial Processing Width Chain

On a vocal bus, create a three-plugin widening chain: first, FabFilter Pro-Q 3 in mid-side mode with a gentle 2 dB boost at 10 kHz on the sides only; second, Soundtoys MicroShift on Focus setting at 30% mix; third, a width plugin with multiband capability set to narrow below 300 Hz and widen above 3 kHz. Compare this layered approach to single-plugin widening—notice how the combination creates more natural, dimensional width than aggressive settings on any individual processor.

Advanced Exercise

Dynamic Width Automation Production

Take a complete song arrangement and create dynamic width automation that supports the musical structure: narrow verses to 90% width, expand choruses to 120%, create a pre-chorus build by gradually increasing width from 90% to 120% over 8 bars, and collapse the final chorus first bar to 50% width before exploding back to 130% for maximum impact. Monitor correlation throughout to ensure it never drops below +0.5. This exercise develops your ability to use width as a compositional and production element rather than a static mix parameter.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ What is the difference between stereo widening and panning?
Panning positions individual sounds in specific left-right locations within the stereo field, while stereo widening increases the apparent spatial extent of sounds that already contain stereo information or creates stereo information from mono sources. Panning is a placement tool, widening is an enhancement tool. Both work together in professional mixes: panning creates initial stereo positioning, while widening adds dimension and space to those positioned elements.
FAQ Can I widen vocals without losing clarity and power?
Yes, but lead vocals should remain relatively narrow and centered for most commercial genres. The best approach is widening only the high frequencies above 4-6 kHz using multiband processing, which adds air and dimension without affecting vocal body and presence. Alternatively, keep the main vocal narrow but widen vocal reverb, delays, and harmonies to create space around the centered lead. Dynamic width can also work, allowing vocals to expand slightly during sustained notes while remaining focused during consonants.
FAQ Why does my mix sound great in headphones but weak on speakers after widening?
This typically indicates phase problems from excessive widening or inappropriate widening of low frequencies. Headphones present left and right channels completely separately, hiding phase issues that become obvious when speakers mix the channels acoustically in your listening environment. The solution is monitoring correlation meters to ensure values stay above +0.5, checking your mix in mono frequently, and keeping everything below 200 Hz strictly mono. Also verify you're not over-widening—subtle enhancement usually translates better than extreme settings.
FAQ Should I widen individual tracks or the master bus?
Both approaches have merit in professional workflows. Individual track widening provides precise control, allowing you to widen specific elements like synth pads or background vocals while keeping others narrow. Master bus widening affects the entire mix and works well for subtle final enhancement or genre-specific spatial character. Most professional mixes use a combination: strategic widening on individual tracks or instrument buses, with conservative master bus widening for final polish. Avoid aggressive widening on both individual tracks and master simultaneously, as this compounds processing and often creates phase problems.
FAQ What correlation meter reading should I target when widening?
Professional mixes typically maintain correlation above +0.5 at all times, with most material sitting between +0.7 and +1.0. Readings consistently below +0.5 indicate phase problems that will cause issues in mono and on speakers. Some experimental genres intentionally use lower correlation for creative effects, but commercial pop, rock, EDM, and hip-hop should stay well above +0.5. Rather than targeting a specific number, focus on ensuring correlation doesn't drop dramatically when you add widening—if adding a widening plugin drops correlation from +0.9 to +0.3, you've gone too far and need to reduce the effect or change your approach.
FAQ Can stereo widening damage mono compatibility completely?
Yes, aggressive widening using delay-based methods or extreme mid-side processing can create severe phase cancellation that causes mix elements to partially or completely disappear in mono. However, properly implemented frequency-conscious widening with appropriate monitoring maintains excellent mono compatibility. The key is keeping low frequencies mono, using correlation meters to monitor phase relationships, and frequently checking your mix in mono during the widening process. Plugins that offer mono compatibility modes or automatic phase correction help prevent destructive phase issues.
FAQ Which frequencies should always remain mono in professional mixes?
Everything below 100-120 Hz should remain strictly mono in virtually all commercial music, ensuring powerful bass on all playback systems and avoiding phase issues on club sound systems. Many professional engineers extend this to 200 Hz for maximum mono compatibility and power. The exact frequency depends on genre: hip-hop and EDM often keep everything below 150-200 Hz mono, while rock and acoustic music might allow some stereo information down to 120 Hz. A safe universal approach is mono below 150 Hz, narrow from 150-300 Hz, and increasingly wide as frequency rises.
FAQ Do I need expensive plugins for professional stereo widening?
No, excellent widening is possible with free and affordable tools. iZotope Ozone Imager is free and provides professional-grade multiband width control used in countless commercial productions. Many DAWs include capable stereo imaging tools—Logic's Stereo Spread, Ableton's Utility in Width mode, and Studio One's Binaural Pan all produce quality results. The difference between affordable and expensive widening plugins lies more in workflow efficiency, visual feedback quality, and specific sonic character than fundamental capability. Understanding widening principles and applying them carefully matters far more than plugin price.
