Best Studio Headphones for Music Production 2025

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2025 18 min read
Quick Answer

For mixing: Sennheiser HD 600 (~$330, open-back) or Beyerdynamic DT 990 Pro (~$180, open-back). For tracking (recording): Audio-Technica ATH-M50x (~$149, closed-back) or Beyerdynamic DT 770 Pro (~$170, closed-back). For both mixing and tracking on a single pair: Sony MDR-7506 (~$100, closed-back) — not the best at either, but a legendary reliable workhorse that sounds right at any price.

Studio headphones are a critical monitoring tool in home production — they reveal detail in ways studio monitors can't in an untreated room, allow late-night work without disturbing others, and are essential for recording vocalists who need to hear themselves without microphone bleed. Choosing the right headphones requires understanding the fundamental trade-off between open-back and closed-back designs, and matching the headphone's character to your specific use.

What we'll cover: Open-back vs closed-back explained, what impedance means and why it matters, frequency response and what to look for, the best headphones at every price for mixing, the best for recording and tracking, reference headphones for mastering, and a decision framework for choosing the right pair.

Open-Back vs Closed-Back: The Most Important Decision

The single most important specification in studio headphones is not frequency response, not driver size, not brand — it's whether the headphone is open-back or closed-back. This design choice fundamentally changes the acoustic behaviour and appropriate use of the headphone.

Open-back headphones have perforated or mesh ear cups that allow air and sound to pass freely in both directions. Sound from outside enters the ear cups, and sound from the drivers leaks outward. The acoustic benefit: the drivers behave more like loudspeakers in a room, with a more natural, spacious soundstage and accurate stereo imaging. The ear doesn't feel pressurised or fatigued during long listening sessions. The trade-off: sound leaks from the headphone. Anyone nearby can hear what you're listening to. You can hear significant ambient noise through the open backs.

Open-back headphones are the preferred choice for mixing and critical listening because of their more natural, speaker-like stereo presentation. The Sennheiser HD 600, Beyerdynamic DT 990 Pro, and Audeze LCD-2 are all open-back — as are virtually all premium audiophile and reference headphones. They cannot be used in recording sessions because the microphone would pick up the headphone's sound.

Closed-back headphones have sealed ear cups that physically isolate the driver from the environment in both directions. Sound from outside is blocked — useful in noisy environments and for focusing. Sound from inside doesn't leak — essential for recording. The acoustic trade-off: the sealed design creates pressure buildup in the ear cup, causing some bass emphasis and a feeling of the sound being inside the head rather than around it. Stereo imaging is typically less accurate than open-back equivalents.

Closed-back headphones are the required choice for recording sessions — when a vocalist or instrumentalist monitors while recording, their headphone sound must not bleed into the microphone. They're also the right choice for working in shared spaces where open-back leakage would disturb others, and for listening in noisy environments where outside noise would interfere with open-back monitoring.

Impedance: What It Means and Why It Matters

Impedance (measured in ohms, Ω) is the electrical resistance that the headphone presents to the amplifier driving it. Higher impedance headphones require more voltage from the amplifier to reach the same loudness as lower impedance headphones. This matters for how headphones pair with different sources.

Low impedance headphones (16–32Ω): designed to work with consumer electronics — smartphones, laptops, portable media players. They're easy to drive to full volume from any source but can be more susceptible to hiss from high-output-impedance amplifiers (some cheap audio interfaces and stereo receivers).

Mid impedance headphones (80–150Ω): the sweet spot for audio interface headphone outputs (which provide moderate amplification). The Beyerdynamic DT 770 Pro 80Ω and DT 990 Pro 250Ω (the most common studio version) and Sony MDR-7506 (63Ω) fall in this range. These headphones work well with audio interfaces without requiring a dedicated headphone amplifier.

High impedance headphones (250–600Ω): the Sennheiser HD 600 (300Ω), HD 650 (300Ω), and Beyerdynamic DT 990 Pro 600Ω require a dedicated headphone amplifier or a high-power interface output to reach adequate listening volumes. Plugged directly into a phone or laptop, they sound quiet and dynamically limited. With an appropriate amplifier, they reveal their full performance — notably better transient response and lower distortion than the same headphones at inadequate drive levels.

For most home studio applications: 80–250Ω headphones paired with a quality audio interface (Focusrite Scarlett 2i2, SSL 2+, etc.) is the practical sweet spot — no dedicated headphone amp required, but significantly better than consumer-grade sources.

Frequency Response and What to Look For

Studio headphones aim for the flattest possible frequency response — the same neutrality goal as studio monitors. In practice, truly flat headphone response is rare and difficult to achieve because of the way headphones interact with the ear's anatomy. A headphone that measures perfectly flat in a test rig (an artificial ear) may not sound flat to a human listener because of ear canal resonances and the HRTF (Head-Related Transfer Function) — the complex way the human head, ears, and anatomy interact with sound.

The Harman target curve — developed by Sean Olive and researchers at Harman International — is the most scientifically validated frequency response target for headphones, based on what large groups of listeners actually preferred when presented with blind A/B comparisons. The Harman target is not flat — it includes elevated bass relative to midrange, following the equal loudness contours of human hearing. Headphones engineered toward the Harman target tend to sound subjectively balanced to most listeners.

For mixing purposes, frequency response consistency matters more than absolute neutrality. A headphone that sounds a certain way consistently — where you've learned what its bass emphasis or high-frequency brightness means in terms of the actual signal — is more useful than an "accurate" headphone you haven't learned to interpret. Many professional engineers mix on Sony MDR-7506 or Beyerdynamic DT 770 Pro because they know exactly how those headphones translate, not because they're the most accurate.

Best Headphones for Mixing

Beyerdynamic DT 990 Pro (~$180) — Best open-back under $250

The DT 990 Pro is the most widely used open-back mixing headphone in professional studios at its price point. Wide, spacious stereo imaging, detailed high-frequency response, and comfortable fit for extended sessions. The 250Ω version pairs well with most audio interface headphone outputs. One caveat: the DT 990 Pro has a pronounced high-frequency emphasis (particularly around 8–10 kHz) that can cause overly bright mixes if not accounted for. Learn to reference against monitor speakers or other headphones to calibrate your ear to the DT 990 Pro's character.

Sennheiser HD 600 (~$330) — The reference benchmark

The HD 600 has been a professional studio reference for over 25 years — an extraordinary lifespan in an industry where technology advances rapidly. Its combination of accurate frequency response, natural stereo imaging, and smooth, fatigue-free character makes it the gold standard for long mixing sessions. Requires adequate amplification (300Ω impedance) — a quality audio interface handles it adequately, though a dedicated headphone amp ($100–200) reveals its full capability. If budget allows one open-back headphone investment, the HD 600 is the one that remains relevant regardless of career stage.

Audeze LCD-2 (~$800) — Professional planar magnetic reference

Audeze's planar magnetic driver technology produces a fundamentally different sound from conventional dynamic drivers — more even distribution of the magnetic field across the diaphragm produces less distortion and more accurate transient response. The LCD-2's bass extension, midrange clarity, and high-frequency detail are exceptional. At $800 for the Classic version, this is the entry into professional planar magnetic monitoring territory. Requires a powerful headphone amplifier. Used in professional mastering facilities alongside or instead of studio monitors.

AKG K702 (~$200) — Best for analytical mixing

The AKG K702 has a bright, analytical character with a wide soundstage — it reveals detail in the high frequencies that some warmer headphones obscure. This makes it excellent for identifying sibilance, harsh frequencies, and high-frequency balance issues. Less natural-sounding than the HD 600 for extended listening, but useful as a complementary reference. Many engineers own both the K702 and a warmer reference like the HD 600 for cross-checking.

Best Headphones for Tracking and Recording

Audio-Technica ATH-M50x (~$149) — Best all-around closed-back

The ATH-M50x is the most recommended closed-back studio headphone in home recording communities, and its position is earned — it sounds balanced, has good isolation, and is comfortable for extended recording sessions. The bass response is slightly elevated compared to ideal neutrality, which means mixes made purely on the M50x can sound thin on other systems, but for tracking (monitoring while recording) this character is not a problem. Detachable cable is a practical improvement over earlier versions. Wide availability of replacement ear pads makes the M50x a long-lived investment.

Beyerdynamic DT 770 Pro (~$170) — Best isolation for recording

The DT 770 Pro offers excellent passive isolation — more than most closed-back headphones at this price — making it particularly useful in noisy recording environments and for preventing headphone bleed into sensitive microphones. The 80Ω version works directly from audio interfaces without additional amplification. The bass response is fuller than the ATH-M50x, which some vocalists prefer for the impression of a richer monitor sound during tracking. Available in 32Ω (for portable devices), 80Ω (most versatile for studio use), and 250Ω (requires more amplification).

Sony MDR-7506 (~$100) — The industry workhorse

The MDR-7506 has been in continuous production since 1991 and is used in virtually every professional recording environment in the world — television studios, radio stations, live sound, and home recording. It's not the most accurate or the most comfortable or the most isolating, but it's reliable, widely available, inexpensive, durable, and consistent. Its character is well-documented and widely understood — engineers who know how MDR-7506 sounds can make production decisions confidently from them. For a secondary pair, a reference pair when in unfamiliar rooms, or a first studio headphone on a tight budget, the MDR-7506 remains a legitimate professional choice.

Shure SRH840A (~$149) — Best tracking + light mixing hybrid

The SRH840A offers a more neutral frequency response than most closed-back alternatives — closer to the flat response that makes open-back headphones better for mixing. This neutrality makes the SRH840A useful for both tracking and light mixing work, though it doesn't match the best open-back options for critical mixing. A good choice for producers who need a single closed-back pair that also works reasonably well for mix checking.

Reference Headphones for Mastering

Mastering requires the most accurate monitoring available. At the headphone level, this means investing in reference-grade open-back headphones (HD 600, LCD-2, or higher) combined with headphone measurement and correction software (Sonarworks SoundID Reference for Headphones — $79). Sonarworks measures each headphone individually using the included measurement microphone, creating a custom correction profile that compensates for the headphone's frequency response deviations from neutral. The result is significantly more accurate monitoring than any headphone achieves uncorrected.

For mastering engineers who work primarily on headphones (common for remote and freelance mastering work), the Sonarworks + HD 600 or Sonarworks + Audeze LCD-2 combination provides monitor-level accuracy. The correction software is as important as the headphone in this context — it's the equivalent of room correction software for studio monitors.
