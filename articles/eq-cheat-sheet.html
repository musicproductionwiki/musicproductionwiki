EQ Cheat Sheet: Frequency Guide for Every Instrument

A complete reference for what to cut, boost, and filter on every instrument — with actionable EQ starting points for cleaner, more professional mixes.

By MusicProductionWiki • Updated May 2026 • ~3,200 words
Quick Answer: EQ moves that improve almost every mix immediately: (1) High-pass filter everything except kick and bass at 80–120 Hz. (2) Cut 2–4 dB at 200–400 Hz on most tracks to remove mud. (3) Cut harsh frequencies at 2–5 kHz on vocals and guitars. (4) Add air at 10–16 kHz on vocals and overheads. Subtractive EQ first — fix problems before you boost anything.

The Frequency Spectrum — What Every Range Does

Before looking at individual instruments, understand what each frequency range contributes to a mix. When you know what a range does, you can make EQ decisions by ear rather than by memorising fixed settings.

Universal EQ Rules That Always Apply

Beyond instrument-specific moves, these principles apply universally in a mix context:

  • High-pass everything that doesn't need low end. Every track except kick and bass should have a high-pass filter cutting below 80–120 Hz. Sub-bass you cannot hear on laptop speakers is still consuming mix bus headroom and causing distortion. This single move often transforms a muddy mix.
  • Subtractive before additive. Find and cut what sounds bad before boosting what you want more of. You often need less of both than you think.
  • Wide cuts, narrow boosts — or the opposite. Wide Q cuts remove broad ranges of problematic energy without creating obvious notches. Narrow Q boosts emphasise specific character frequencies without adding unwanted neighbours. Some engineers prefer the inverse — experiment.
  • EQ in context. Make every EQ decision with the whole mix playing, not the track soloed. A snare that sounds perfect soloed often disappears in a mix and a snare that sounds harsh soloed often sits perfectly in context.
  • Reference constantly. A/B your mix against commercial reference tracks throughout the EQ process. If your bass is twice as heavy as the reference, you are working with inaccurate monitoring, not making a creative choice.
  • Trust the low-cut. A high-pass filter on the mix bus itself — set very low, at 20–30 Hz — removes truly inaudible sub-sonic energy that can cause intermodulation distortion and limit headroom. Use a gentle slope (12 dB/oct).

EQ Plugins Worth Knowing

The tools you use matter less than how you use them, but some EQ plugins offer significant advantages over standard stock options:

  • FabFilter Pro-Q 4: The most versatile surgical EQ available. Linear phase mode eliminates phase shift for mastering. Dynamic EQ mode allows frequency-specific compression. The spectrum analyser shows exactly what's happening in real time. Essential if you do a lot of mixing or mastering. (~$200)
  • Waves SSL E-Channel: Models the EQ from the SSL 4000E console used on countless hit records. The proportional Q design means boosts and cuts are narrower at extreme settings and wider at gentle settings — very musical. (~$30–50 on sale)
  • UAD API 550: Warm, punchy analogue EQ character from the API 500 series console. Excellent on drums and guitars. Requires a UAD interface or satellite.
  • Your DAW's stock EQ: Logic's Channel EQ, Ableton's EQ Eight, and FL Studio's Parametric EQ 2 are all excellent and free. Do not underestimate them.

Practical Exercises

Beginner: The Mud Hunt

Open a multitrack session or your own mix. Set up a narrow-Q boost of +12 dB on a parametric EQ. Sweep slowly from 100 Hz to 500 Hz while the mix plays. You will hear mud, boxiness, and boominess become dramatically exaggerated as the boost sweeps through the problem frequencies. When you find the worst-sounding frequency, switch from boost to cut — 2–4 dB at the same point. Apply this technique to each major track in your mix. This sweep-and-cut approach is one of the fastest ways to clean up a muddy mix.

Intermediate: The High-Pass Challenge

Take any of your existing mixes and apply a high-pass filter at 80–100 Hz to every track except the kick drum and bass instrument. Use a 24 dB/octave slope. Listen to how the mix changes — it should feel cleaner and more controlled without losing warmth, because the frequency range you're cutting on mid and high-frequency instruments contains mostly rumble and mud, not musical content. This is one of the most impactful single EQ moves in mixing.

Advanced: EQ Matching

Use an EQ matching plugin (FabFilter Pro-Q 4 has this built in, as does iZotope Neutron) to analyse the frequency spectrum of a professional reference track and your mix simultaneously. The tool will show you exactly where your mix differs from the reference — where you have more or less energy in each frequency range. Use this as a diagnostic tool (not a fix-all) to identify systematic problems with your monitoring or mixing decisions. Note what you see, make targeted EQ adjustments, and repeat. This exercise builds the calibrated ear that professional mix engineers develop over years of comparing their work to commercial releases.

FAQ — EQ Cheat Sheet

What frequencies should I cut on every track?

Apply a high-pass filter to every track except kick drum and bass — set it between 80–120 Hz to remove low-frequency rumble and mud that you can't hear but that clutters the mix bus. This single EQ move will make most beginner mixes cleaner immediately.

What is the 'mud' frequency range in EQ?

The mud range is generally 200–400 Hz. Most instruments produce energy in this range, and when multiple tracks accumulate here, mixes sound thick, undefined, and unclear. Cutting 2–4 dB in this range on multiple tracks simultaneously often dramatically clarifies a mix.

What frequencies make vocals sound harsh?

Vocal harshness typically occurs at 2–5 kHz. Small boosts here increase intelligibility, but too much creates a hard, strident sound. A de-esser or gentle cut at 3–5 kHz can tame harshness. Sibilance (the 's' sound) lives at 6–10 kHz.

How do I make kicks and bass not clash?

The most common technique is sidechain compression — the bass is compressed every time the kick hits, ducking slightly so the kick punches through. Alternatively, EQ the kick to have its fundamental below 80 Hz and cut that range on the bass. Giving each element its own frequency slot prevents masking.

What is additive vs subtractive EQ?

Subtractive EQ means cutting frequencies to remove problems — mud, harshness, boxiness. Additive EQ means boosting frequencies to enhance character — adding brightness, warmth, or presence. Most engineers prefer subtractive EQ first: fix problems before you add anything.

What is a high-pass filter?

A high-pass filter (HPF) removes frequencies below its cutoff point, allowing only high frequencies to pass through. It's used to remove low-frequency rumble, handling noise, and sub-bass energy from tracks that don't need it — basically everything except kick drums and bass instruments.

What does boosting at 10–16 kHz do?

Boosting at 10–16 kHz adds air — an open, airy, expensive-sounding quality to vocals and cymbals. Applied gently (1–3 dB), it can make a mix feel more professional and open.

Should I EQ before or after compression?

Both approaches are valid and common. EQ before compression means the compressor responds to the shaped frequency content. EQ after compression shapes the tone after dynamics are controlled. Many engineers do both: a corrective EQ before and a creative EQ after compression.
