How to Make Drum and Bass: Complete Production Guide (2026)

Updated May 2026 12 min read Technique Guide
Quick Answer: Drum and bass is produced at 160–180 BPM with complex break-based or programmed drum patterns, deep rolling basslines, and atmospheric pads or leads. Ableton Live is the dominant DAW. The core technique involves programming a two-step kick-snare pattern, layering breaks or programmed percussion for complexity, and designing a bass sound (rolling sub, reese, or modulating neuro bass) that sits tightly with the kick. DnB has subgenres with distinct production styles — liquid, jump-up, neurofunk, and jungle each require different approaches.

Drum and bass has had one of the longest sustained runs of any electronic music genre. Born from the UK rave scene of the early 1990s, it has evolved continuously without losing its core identity — high-tempo, percussion-driven, bass-heavy music that rewards careful listening and demands precise production. In 2024, DnB surged in popularity on sample platforms by over 700%, and its influence is audible across mainstream pop, hip-hop production, and soundtrack work.

Making DnB is genuinely challenging — the high BPM amplifies every production mistake, the drum programming is complex, and the bass design requires both technical knowledge and trained ears. This guide breaks down the full production process from the ground up, covering all the major subgenres, the essential techniques, and the specific DAW workflows that make DnB production efficient.

Drum and bass is a genre family, not a single sound. Before you start producing, knowing which subgenre you're targeting is essential — the production techniques differ significantly.

The BPM and Time Signature

DnB runs at 160–180 BPM in 4/4 time. Most tracks sit at 172–174 BPM — this has been the genre's "sweet spot" for decades. At these speeds, individual drum hits that would feel comfortable at 130 BPM become extremely tight, and any timing imprecision becomes immediately obvious. For this reason, DnB producers typically work with quantised MIDI or precisely edited audio rather than loose, human-feel timing.

An important workflow note: many DnB producers write their drum patterns at half the actual BPM (86–87 BPM) and then double the tempo. This makes drum programming feel more like writing at a normal hip-hop tempo, and patterns that feel "correct" at half tempo will translate authentically at full DnB speed. Ableton Live handles this halftime/fulltime switching particularly well.

DnB Drum Programming: The Foundation

The Two-Step Pattern

The defining drum pattern of drum and bass is the two-step. Unlike four-on-the-floor (kick on every beat) or straight boom-bap (kick on 1 and 3), the DnB two-step places kicks in syncopated positions — typically on beat 1, the "and" of beat 2, beat 3 (or the "and" of 3), and occasionally the "and" of beat 4. The snare falls reliably on beats 2 and 4. This syncopated kick placement is what gives DnB its forward momentum and sense of rolling motion.

Learning to programme a convincing two-step takes time. The most useful approach is to listen to classic DnB tracks and identify the exact kick placement by ear, then recreate it in your step sequencer or piano roll. Start with a simple 4-bar pattern: kick on beat 1, snare on 2 and 4, kick on the "and" of 3. This is the most basic two-step and works immediately. From there, add complexity one kick at a time.

The Amen Break

The Amen break is a 6-second drum loop from "Amen, Brother" by The Winstons (1969). It became the foundational sample of jungle and drum and bass — the most sampled 6 seconds in music history. Learning to work with the Amen break is essential for anyone serious about DnB and jungle production.

The break contains a kick, snare, hi-hat, and cymbal pattern all locked together in a swinging shuffle groove. DnB producers chop the break into individual hits or short phrases, rearrange them, time-stretch them to target BPM, process them with filtering and compression, and layer the result beneath programmed drums to add organic texture and swing.

In Ableton Live: load the Amen break into a Simpler or Drum Rack, warp it to 172 BPM, slice it at transients (right-click → Slice to MIDI), and then rearrange the slices in a MIDI pattern. This gives you full control over every hit while keeping the organic character of the original break. Pitch individual slices up or down for variation. Apply compression, EQ, and saturation to make the break sit in the mix rather than dominating it.

Programming Without Breaks

Not all DnB uses the Amen break. Many neurofunk, minimal, and rollers tracks use fully programmed drums — synthesised or sampled individual hits, precisely placed in a piano roll without reference to any break loop. For programmed DnB drums:

  • Kick: Fast attack, punchy mid-range body, short sustain. Layer a sine sweep (pitch drops from 200Hz to 60Hz over 80ms) with a transient clap for definition. Keep sub energy below 80Hz.
  • Snare: Layer a noise burst with a tuned resonant body. The classic DnB snare has a "crack" character — sharp attack, medium decay, present in the 200Hz–2kHz range. Tune the body to a musical pitch (usually the root note of the track).
  • Hi-hats: Tight closed hats on 8th notes with velocity variation. Open hat accents on off-beats at lower volume. Apply a high-pass filter above 8kHz for a crisp, clean character.
  • Ghost hits: Add ghost snare hits at low velocity between main snare hits. This creates the rolling, organic feel of a real drummer rather than a mechanical grid.

DnB Bass Design: The Core Techniques

The Rolling Sub Bass

The rolling sub bass is the foundation of liquid DnB and rollers. It is a clean, pure sub-bass sound that follows a rhythmic pattern aligned with the kick drum, creating a sense of continuous forward motion. Here is the technique:

  1. Use a sine wave oscillator or a sampled sub bass hit as your source
  2. Programme notes in the piano roll that roughly follow the root notes of your chord progression
  3. Set note lengths so the bass rolls — notes overlap slightly and use portamento for smooth pitch transitions (glide time: 50–100ms)
  4. Apply a slow-attack LFO to the amplitude for a subtle pulsing effect that creates the "roll" sensation
  5. Keep all bass energy below 150Hz — no mid-range content, pure sub
  6. Sidechain the sub to the kick with a fast attack and medium release

The Reese Bass

The reese bass is the most iconic DnB bass sound. Named after Kevin "Reese" Saunderson (who created it for his techno productions), it was adopted by early DnB and jungle producers and became the genre's signature sound for darker styles. The recipe:

  1. Load two sawtooth oscillators — detune one up by 5–15 cents, the other down by the same amount
  2. Add a third oscillator (sine or sawtooth) one octave below for sub weight
  3. Apply a low-pass filter with a resonance of 30–50% and set the cutoff in the 400–800Hz range
  4. Add a slow LFO (0.5–2Hz) modulating the filter cutoff — this creates the characteristic "breathing" movement
  5. Apply portamento for gliding between notes (150–300ms for classic reese, shorter for tighter modern styles)
  6. Route through a chorus or slight flanger for additional width and movement
  7. Compress heavily on the bass bus — reese basses benefit from high-ratio limiting to even out the dynamic variation from the LFO

The Neuro Bass (Neurofunk)

Neurofunk bass design is more complex than rolling sub or reese. The defining characteristic is aggressive, modulating mid-range content layered above a sub foundation — often called a "brain-melting" or "talking" bass. The technique involves:

  • A sub layer (sine wave) for the foundation, separate from the mid layer
  • A mid layer with complex FM synthesis, ring modulation, or extreme filter modulation creating distorted, formant-like movement
  • The mid layer is processed through distortion, bit crushing, comb filtering, and dynamic EQ for maximum aggression
  • Precise automation of filter cutoff, resonance, and drive throughout the pattern creates the characteristic "talking" effect
  • The sub and mid layers are kept on separate channels with separate compression and EQ — they are mixed together in the final mix rather than processed as one sound

Synthesisers commonly used for neuro bass: Serum (its wavetable and FM capabilities make it ideal), Vital (free and powerful), Native Instruments Massive, and FM8. The Serum neuro bass approach using distortion in the FX chain and wavetable oscillator modulation is one of the most widely taught techniques in DnB production tutorials.

Atmosphere and Pads

DnB — particularly liquid DnB — relies heavily on atmospheric pads, chord stabs, and ambience to create emotional depth. The drums and bass are the engine; the atmosphere is what makes a track feel like something. Common approaches:

  • Pad chords: Long, sustained chord pads in a minor key using lush synthesiser patches (Arturia Analog Lab, Serum pads, or sampled strings). Apply heavy reverb (large hall, 4–6 second decay) and gentle chorus for width.
  • Vocal chops: Liquid DnB frequently uses chopped vocal samples — either original recordings or samples processed beyond recognition. Pitch and time-stretch vocal chops to fit the key, then filter and reverb them into the texture.
  • Piano or keys: Liquid DnB often features piano, Rhodes, or electric piano as a melodic element. Keep it in the mid-upper range to avoid clashing with the bass.
  • Foley and texture: Neurofunk and dark DnB use industrial textures, metallic hits, and ambient recordings to create atmosphere. Layering subtle, processed field recordings under the main elements adds depth without cluttering the mix.

DnB Production in Ableton Live

Ableton Live is the standard DnB DAW. Its workflow suits the genre particularly well:

  1. Set project BPM to 172. Or set to 86 BPM and programme in halftime — switch to 172 when the pattern is complete.
  2. Build a Drum Rack: Load individual kick, snare, hat, and clap samples. Programme the two-step pattern in MIDI. Add a separate audio track for your break loop.
  3. Load the Amen break (if using): Drop into a new audio track, warp to 172 BPM, slice at transients, and create a MIDI clip from the slices. Rearrange the MIDI pattern for your variation.
  4. Build the bass in a Simpler or Instrument Rack: For sub rolling bass, use a long-decay sine wave patch with portamento. For reese, use two detuned sawtooth oscillators in Operator (Ableton's native FM synth).
  5. Layer your atmosphere: Pads on a return track with heavy reverb. Vocal chops on individual channels. Use Ableton's Session View to experiment with different chord sections before committing.
  6. Use return tracks for shared reverb and delay: DnB mixes benefit from shared reverb spaces across multiple elements — a single large hall reverb on a return track with multiple elements sending to it creates cohesion.
  7. Group drums, bass, and atmosphere separately: Drum group → drum bus compression and saturation. Bass group → separate sub and mid processing. Atmosphere group → shared reverb tail management.

Arrangement and Structure

DnB tracks follow a club-functional structure designed for DJ mixing. Standard structure for a club track: Intro (drums and atmosphere only, 16–32 bars for DJ mix-in), Build-up (bass enters, tension builds, 8–16 bars), Drop (full elements hit simultaneously, 32–64 bars), Breakdown (drums reduce, atmosphere sustains, 16–24 bars), Drop 2 (variation of the original drop, 32–64 bars), Outro (elements strip back for DJ mix-out, 16–32 bars).

The drop is the most important section. It should hit hard and immediately. The breakdown that precedes it serves to create tension — the more effectively you strip back elements during the breakdown, the more powerful the drop feels when it arrives.

Mixing DnB

DnB mixing requires careful attention to the sub-bass range. At 172 BPM, kick and bass interact constantly, and without careful sidechain management, the low end becomes muddy. Key mixing principles for DnB:

  • Sidechain kick to sub bass: Fast attack (1ms), medium release (100–200ms), ratio 4:1 to 8:1. The sub should duck noticeably on each kick hit.
  • Separate sub and mid bass: Use a crossover or mid-bass/sub split. Keep sub energy below 100Hz, mid bass 100Hz–500Hz. Process separately.
  • High-pass everything except kick and bass: Everything else in the mix should be high-passed at 150–200Hz minimum. DnB has a clean, uncluttered low end.
  • Drum compression: Parallel compression on the drum group — mix in 30–50% of a heavily compressed version for punch without killing transients.
  • Master loudness: DnB masters typically run -8 to -9 LUFS integrated for club playback. Match label reference tracks.

Practical Exercises

Beginner: Programme Your First Two-Step

Set your DAW to 172 BPM. Load any kick, snare, and hi-hat sample into a drum machine or Drum Rack. Programme a pattern: kick on beat 1, snare on beat 2, kick on the "and" of beat 2 (the 8th note between beats 2 and 3), snare on beat 4. Add closed hi-hats on every 8th note. Loop this 2-bar pattern. Now listen to a reference liquid DnB track (Hospital Records catalogue is ideal). Identify where their kick falls differently from yours. Make one adjustment. This iterative comparison-and-adjustment process is how DnB drum programming improves — not from theory alone, but from referencing and responding.

Intermediate: Build a Reese Bass from Scratch

Open your synthesiser (Serum, Vital, or any subtractive/wavetable synth). Load two sawtooth oscillators. Detune oscillator 2 by +10 cents and oscillator 1 by -10 cents. Add a third oscillator one octave below as a sine wave for sub weight. Open a low-pass filter and set cutoff at 500Hz, resonance at 35%. Add an LFO at 1Hz modulating the filter cutoff by 30%. Set portamento to 200ms. Play a two-note pattern a fifth apart (e.g., D and A). Record yourself adjusting the LFO rate from 0.5Hz to 3Hz while the bass plays. Listen to how the character changes. Now apply light chorus and compress the output. Compare your result to a classic DnB track with a prominent reese bass. Identify three differences and adjust to close the gap.

Advanced: Full DnB Track from Drop to Breakdown

Build a 64-bar drop section for a liquid DnB track. Requirements: two-step kick-snare pattern with break layer for texture, rolling sub bass following a minor chord progression, reese or neuro bass element for mid-range movement, two atmospheric pad chords with large reverb, one melodic element (piano or lead), and a vocal chop element. The drop must be followed by a 16-bar breakdown that strips back to pads and bass only. Then bring the drop back with one variation (different bass pattern, new melodic element, or additional percussion layer). Master the 96-bar section (drop + breakdown + drop 2) to -9 LUFS integrated and compare to a reference Hospital Records or Metalheadz release. Identify where your low end, stereo width, and high-end air differ most and fix those three specific issues before considering the exercise complete.

Frequently Asked Questions

What BPM is drum and bass?

Drum and bass runs at 160–180 BPM, with most tracks sitting at 172–174 BPM. Liquid DnB tends toward the slower end (170–172), while neurofunk and jump-up often run 174–178 BPM.

What is the Amen break?

The Amen break is a 6-second drum loop from "Amen, Brother" by The Winstons (1969). It became the foundational sample of drum and bass and jungle, chopped and rearranged into thousands of variations across four decades of music.

What is a reese bass?

A reese bass is a detuned, modulating bass sound made from two detuned sawtooth oscillators with a modulated low-pass filter. Named after Kevin "Reese" Saunderson, it became one of the defining sounds of drum and bass.

What DAW is best for making DnB?

Ableton Live is the most widely used DAW for DnB production, particularly for its clip-based workflow, excellent warp tools, and Drum Rack. FL Studio and Logic Pro are fully capable alternatives.

What is the difference between liquid DnB and neurofunk?

Liquid DnB is melodic, warm, and soulful — emphasising lush pads, vocal chops, and rolling sub bass. Neurofunk is darker and more technical — featuring complex modulating bass sounds, distorted reese basses, and clinical drum programming.

How do I programme DnB drums without samples?

Use a synthesiser to design kick and snare sounds from scratch. A DnB kick uses a sine sweep (200Hz → 60Hz over 80ms) layered with a transient clap. A DnB snare uses a noise burst with a tuned resonant body in the 200Hz–2kHz range.

Is drum and bass hard to make?

DnB has a steeper learning curve than many genres because of its high BPM, complex drum programming, and demanding bass design. Starting with liquid DnB and simple rolling patterns is the most accessible entry point for intermediate producers.

What sample packs are best for DnB?

Loopmasters has an extensive DnB catalogue with packs from Metalheadz, Hospital Records, and Dispatch. Splice has strong DnB collections. For break-based production, Amen break libraries and custom break recordings are essential starting points.
