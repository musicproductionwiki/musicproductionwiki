ASCAP vs BMI: Which PRO Should You Join?

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2025 14 min read
Quick Answer

For most US songwriters and composers, ASCAP and BMI are essentially equivalent in royalty collection and distribution. The practical differences come down to: BMI is free to join for songwriters (ASCAP charges a one-time $50 fee), SESAC is invite-only and smaller, and ASCAP has slightly better international royalty collection relationships in some territories. Join one — you cannot be a member of both simultaneously — and stay consistent.

ASCAP (American Society of Composers, Authors and Publishers) and BMI (Broadcast Music, Inc.) are the two largest Performing Rights Organizations in the United States. They collect royalties when your music is performed publicly — played on radio, streamed online, performed in venues, broadcast on TV, used in film — and distribute those royalties to the songwriters and publishers who own the compositions.

If you write original music and don't belong to a PRO, you are leaving money on the table. Every time your music plays on the radio, streams on Spotify or Apple Music, plays in a restaurant, bar, hotel, or fitness class, or is used in a TV show or film, royalties are being generated. Without PRO membership, those royalties accumulate in the PRO's general pool rather than finding their way to you.

What we'll learn: How PROs work and what they collect, the specific differences between ASCAP and BMI in fees, royalty calculation, payment schedules, member services, and international collection, plus SESAC and newer digital-only options, and how to decide which to join.

How PROs Work

Performing Rights Organizations operate on a licensing model. They negotiate blanket licenses with businesses that use music publicly — radio stations, streaming services, TV networks, venues, restaurants, fitness studios, retail chains — and collect licensing fees from these businesses. They then distribute the collected fees to the songwriters and publishers in their membership, based on a combination of actual usage data and statistical sampling.

The two key parties in every music copyright are the songwriter (who writes the melody and lyrics) and the publisher (who administers and licenses the copyright). PROs collect and distribute performance royalties separately for each — the songwriter's share goes directly to the songwriter, the publisher's share goes to the publisher. If you have not signed with a publisher, you can register your own publishing company with the PRO and collect both shares yourself.

PROs do not collect all music royalties. They specifically collect performance royalties — royalties generated when music is publicly performed or broadcast. Mechanical royalties (generated when a recording is reproduced, including on-demand streaming) are collected by separate organizations — in the US, the Music Licensing Collective (MLC) for digital mechanicals, and the Harry Fox Agency and others for physical reproduction. Sync licensing royalties are negotiated directly or through licensing agents, not through PROs.

ASCAP: Songwriter-Owned

ASCAP was founded in 1914 and is the only major US PRO that is member-owned — it is run by and for its member songwriters and publishers. The board of directors is elected by the membership, and ASCAP's charter commits it to distributing all collected royalties minus operating costs, with no profit taken by external shareholders.

ASCAP membership for songwriters costs a one-time $50 registration fee. Publisher registration is separate ($50 for each publishing entity registered). After the initial fee, there are no ongoing annual fees for membership. ASCAP distributes royalties four times per year (quarterly).

ASCAP's royalty calculation methodology: ASCAP uses a combination of actual performance data (from cue sheets, digital performance logs, and broadcast monitoring) and statistical sampling to determine what was performed and how often. The precise weighting formula is complex and varies by performance type. ASCAP provides a detailed royalty statement with each payment that breaks down the sources of income.

BMI: Free to Join, Same Function

BMI was founded in 1939, partly to provide an alternative to ASCAP and to represent genres — country, jazz, blues, R&B — that ASCAP was slower to embrace in its early decades. BMI is a non-profit corporation (not member-owned like ASCAP, but not operating for profit either) that distributes royalties to its affiliated songwriters and publishers.

BMI is free to join for songwriters — there is no registration fee. Publisher affiliate registration at BMI costs $150 for a business entity or $150 for a sole proprietor publishing company. BMI distributes royalties quarterly (four times per year), on a schedule offset from ASCAP's distribution dates.

BMI's royalty calculation uses a similar combination of actual performance data and statistical sampling. The methodologies differ in detail — the specific weighting systems for different performance types, the minimum performance thresholds for payment, and the international reciprocal collection agreements — but the practical outcomes for most songwriters are comparable.

The fee difference is the most straightforward practical distinction: BMI costs nothing to join as a songwriter, while ASCAP charges $50. For publishers, ASCAP is actually cheaper ($50 vs BMI's $150). The fee difference rarely drives the decision for established songwriters, but it matters for newer artists on tight budgets.

Royalty Calculation Comparison

Both organizations use weighting systems that value certain performance types differently. Radio airplay, TV broadcast, live performance, and streaming all generate different royalty amounts per play based on the PRO's weighting formulas, the size of the audience, and the licensee's fee structure.

The practical result: royalty amounts for the same number of plays may differ slightly between ASCAP and BMI due to their different calculation methodologies. Independent research has not consistently shown one organization to pay meaningfully more than the other across diverse catalogs. Individual songwriters may find they receive slightly different amounts depending on their specific performance mix and genre — but there's no universal answer about which pays more.

SESAC: The Third Option

SESAC (originally the Society of European Stage Authors and Composers) is the third US PRO and the only one that is invitation-only. SESAC is considerably smaller than ASCAP or BMI, is privately owned (acquired by private equity in 2013), and claims higher per-performance royalties for some writers as a result of its smaller, curated membership base competing for a meaningful pool of royalties.

SESAC represents notable songwriters including Bob Dylan, Neil Diamond, Adele (UK admin through PRS, US admin through SESAC), and others. If SESAC approaches you with an invitation, it's worth evaluating — the per-performance rates can be favorable. Without an invitation, it's not an option.

International Royalty Collection

US PROs collect performance royalties for US performances. When your music is performed internationally, the collecting society of that country collects local royalties and remits them to your US PRO through reciprocal agreements. Both ASCAP and BMI have international reciprocal relationships with most major collecting societies worldwide.

The practical implication: the quality of international collection matters most when a significant portion of your performance income comes from outside the US. For most US-based songwriters with primarily US performances, the international collection difference between ASCAP and BMI is negligible. For songwriters with substantial European or international activity, reviewing the specific reciprocal agreements with the territories of interest is worthwhile.

How to Join and What to Register

Joining ASCAP: go to ascap.com, click "Join ASCAP," complete the songwriter application, pay the $50 fee. Your works can be registered immediately after account activation. Register works by title, co-writers, publishers, and the splits (percentages of ownership).

Joining BMI: go to bmi.com, click "Affiliate with BMI," complete the songwriter application, no fee for songwriters. Works are registered through the BMI portal after account activation.

What to register: every song you write, including collaborations. Register the title, all co-writers and their splits, the publisher(s) and their splits, and the ISRC code if available. Register works before releasing or performing them to ensure any royalties generated from day one are captured.

The Decision

For most songwriters just starting out: join BMI for the zero-cost entry point. Register your works immediately. Set up your publisher affiliate account if you're self-publishing. Start registering every song you release.

For songwriters already on ASCAP or BMI: stay where you are unless you have a specific reason to switch. The disruption of switching PROs — transferring works, updating publisher registrations, ensuring no royalty gaps during transition — is rarely worth the marginal difference between the two organizations. Consistency matters more than optimization at this level.

For songwriters with substantial international income or specific genre considerations: consult an entertainment attorney or music business advisor about which organization may better serve your specific catalog and performance mix.
