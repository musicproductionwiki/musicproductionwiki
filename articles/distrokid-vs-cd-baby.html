Distrokid vs Cd Baby

DistroKid is the better choice for artists who release music frequently — its flat annual subscription of $22.99/year with unlimited releases and 100% royalty retention beats CD Baby's per-release fees and 9–15% royalty cut once you release more than two or three tracks per year. CD Baby is better suited to artists who release infrequently and want a one-time fee model, or who need integrated publishing administration and physical CD distribution without managing separate accounts.

Getting your music onto Spotify, Apple Music, Tidal, Amazon Music, and every other major streaming platform requires a digital distributor — a service that acts as the intermediary between independent artists and the global streaming ecosystem. DistroKid and CD Baby are the two most widely used distributors for independent artists worldwide, and choosing between them is one of the first meaningful business decisions a self-releasing musician makes.

This comparison covers every dimension that matters: pricing models, royalty splits, distribution speed, platform reach, publishing administration, customer support, and the specific scenarios where each service makes more financial sense. By the end, you'll have a clear answer based on your actual release cadence and career goals — not marketing copy from either company.

The core trade-off in one sentence: DistroKid charges a flat annual fee with no royalty cut, while CD Baby charges per release with a percentage of ongoing royalties — making DistroKid cheaper for prolific artists and CD Baby potentially cheaper for artists who release rarely and generate significant per-release streaming income.

Pricing Models: Subscription vs. Per-Release

The most fundamental difference between DistroKid and CD Baby is how they make money, and understanding that difference helps you predict your total cost of distribution over time.

DistroKid Pricing

DistroKid operates on an annual subscription model. The core tiers as of May 2026 are:

  • Musician: $22.99/year — unlimited releases for one artist
  • Musician Plus: $35.99/year — unlimited releases, customizable release dates, presave pages, more label name options
  • Label plans: $79.99–$179.99/year — unlimited releases for 5 to 100 artists

The critical implication: once you pay the annual fee, you can release as many singles, EPs, and albums as you want at no additional per-release cost. DistroKid takes 0% of your royalties — the company makes its money entirely from subscription fees. If you cancel your subscription without paying the "leave music up" fee ($4.95 per album, $0.99 per single), your music is removed from all platforms. This is a key consideration for long-term catalog management.

CD Baby Pricing

CD Baby operates on a per-release model with ongoing royalty percentages:

  • Standard Single: $9.95 one-time fee + 9% of streaming royalties
  • Standard Album: $29.95 one-time fee + 9% of streaming royalties
  • CD Baby Pro Single: $14.95 one-time fee + 15% of streaming royalties (includes publishing administration)
  • CD Baby Pro Album: $49.95 one-time fee + 15% of streaming royalties (includes publishing administration)

CD Baby keeps the one-time fee as a distribution charge, then takes a percentage of every royalty payment your music generates indefinitely. The upside: there's no ongoing subscription, so your music stays on platforms forever after the initial payment regardless of your relationship with CD Baby.

Break-Even Analysis

The math on which service is cheaper depends on how many releases you make per year and how much streaming income you generate. For the annual DistroKid Musician plan at $22.99/year with 0% royalties, vs. CD Baby Standard at 9%:

  • If you generate under ~$255/year in streaming royalties and release fewer than 2-3 tracks, CD Baby's lower upfront cost per release may result in less total spending in year one
  • At $255/year in streaming royalties, DistroKid's 0% cut saves exactly $22.99 compared to CD Baby's 9% — recovering the subscription cost
  • Beyond that threshold, every dollar of streaming income favors DistroKid more strongly
  • Artists releasing 4+ tracks per year favor DistroKid almost universally, since each CD Baby release adds $9.95–$49.95 to the total cost

Platform Reach and Distribution Speed

Both DistroKid and CD Baby deliver to all the major streaming platforms you'd expect: Spotify, Apple Music, Amazon Music, YouTube Music, Tidal, Deezer, Pandora, iHeartRadio, TikTok, Instagram/Facebook, Beatport, and dozens of other stores globally. The platform coverage is functionally equivalent for most artists — both services reach 150+ stores and platforms. The meaningful differences are in speed and a few specialty platforms.

Distribution Speed

DistroKid is consistently faster. After submission, music typically appears on Spotify within 1–3 business days and on Apple Music within 1–5 business days. Expedited delivery is available for an additional fee if you need faster turnaround. CD Baby typically takes 3–7 business days for Spotify and similar timelines for Apple Music. Both services recommend submitting at least two weeks before your planned release date to ensure availability, account for any content review delays, and have time to complete Spotify for Artists editorial pitching.

Spotify's editorial pitching requirement — pitching unreleased tracks to Spotify's playlist curators through Spotify for Artists — requires submission at least 7 days before release. Both distributors support this, but DistroKid's faster delivery gives artists more buffer time.

Specialty Platform Access

CD Baby has a long-standing physical distribution arm — it can manufacture and distribute physical CDs and vinyl to brick-and-mortar stores and online retailers. This remains relevant for artists who sell merchandise at shows or want physical product. DistroKid is digital-only.

Both distributors now deliver to TikTok and Instagram Reels, which is essential for viral discoverability. Both also deliver to YouTube Music and can generate Content ID claims for YouTube, though the specifics of Content ID management differ between them.

Pre-Save Campaigns

DistroKid's Musician Plus plan and higher include built-in presave page creation, which allows fans to pre-save an upcoming release on Spotify and Apple Music before it goes live. CD Baby does not offer a native presave feature — artists need third-party tools like Feature.fm or Submithub for presave campaigns. This is a notable gap for artists focused on launch strategy, where presave numbers influence algorithmic momentum on release day.

Royalty Splits and Payout Structure

Royalties are where the financial stakes are highest for successful artists. Understanding how each distributor handles the money that flows from streaming platforms is essential before signing up.

DistroKid's 100% Royalty Model

DistroKid passes 100% of streaming royalties directly to artists on all plans. When Spotify pays out, DistroKid receives the payment and forwards the full amount to your account. The only deductions are banking fees for certain payout methods and, in some territories, applicable taxes withheld at source by the streaming platform itself (not by DistroKid).

Royalties are paid monthly, and DistroKid's minimum payout threshold is $2.00 — very accessible for artists who are just beginning to accumulate streaming income. Payments can be received via bank transfer, PayPal, or Payoneer depending on your country.

DistroKid also offers a "Bank" feature that splits royalties automatically between collaborators — co-writers, producers, featured artists, and managers — at percentages you set. This eliminates manual splitting and is one of the most practically useful features for collaborative artists.

CD Baby's Royalty Cut

CD Baby takes 9% of all streaming royalties on its Standard plan and 15% on its Pro plan. This is a perpetual cut — it applies to every royalty payment for the lifetime of your music on the platform, not just for the first year or until a certain threshold is reached.

The 15% cut on CD Baby Pro is higher than the standard 9%, but Pro includes publishing administration (more on that below), which can recover significant royalties that would otherwise go uncollected — particularly performance royalties from international markets and mechanical royalties from streaming.

CD Baby's minimum payout threshold is $25.00 — substantially higher than DistroKid's $2.00 minimum. For artists generating modest streaming income, this means waiting longer between payouts from CD Baby. Payments are made monthly once the threshold is reached.

Collaborative Splits

Both platforms support royalty splits for collaborations, but DistroKid's implementation is more integrated. DistroKid's Bank feature allows automated splitting directly in the platform. CD Baby handles splits through a separate payment arrangement and is less seamlessly integrated for ongoing collaborations.

Publishing Administration: The Most Important Differentiator

Many independent artists — even experienced ones — leave money on the table by not collecting all their royalties. Publishing administration is the mechanism that collects songwriting royalties separate from the recording royalties that distributors typically handle. This is where CD Baby and DistroKid diverge most significantly.

The Two Royalty Streams

Every time a song is streamed, there are two distinct royalty streams:

  1. Master recording royalties — paid to whoever owns the recording (typically the artist). These are handled by your distributor and go directly to you.
  2. Publishing/songwriting royalties — paid to whoever wrote the song. These are paid by streaming platforms to PROs (Performance Rights Organizations like ASCAP or BMI) and to mechanical royalty organizations like the MLC (Mechanical Licensing Collective in the US).

If you write your own music and aren't registered with a PRO or the MLC, you're not collecting publishing royalties at all. Understanding the full picture requires knowing the difference between ASCAP and BMI and how to register properly.

CD Baby Pro Publishing

CD Baby Pro ($14.95/single, $49.95/album one-time) includes full publishing administration. CD Baby registers your songs with relevant PROs and mechanical rights organizations in over 40 countries, then collects performance royalties, mechanical royalties, and sync royalties on your behalf. This is a comprehensive service that handles international collection — important because many royalties generated when your music is played in Europe, Asia, or Latin America go uncollected by artists who only register with a US PRO.

The trade-off is the 15% royalty cut and the higher upfront fee. But for songwriters who haven't set up independent publishing administration, CD Baby Pro often recovers far more in uncollected royalties than the 15% cut costs.

DistroKid and Publishing

DistroKid's standard plans do not include publishing administration. Artists need to register independently with their PRO (ASCAP at $50 one-time membership or BMI at no cost) and with the MLC (free) to collect mechanical royalties. DistroKid does offer a publishing administration add-on service, but it's a separate product with its own fee structure, and the integration is less seamless than CD Baby Pro's built-in publishing.

For artists who have already set up their own publishing — registered with ASCAP or BMI, affiliated with the MLC, and potentially working with a publishing administrator — DistroKid's lack of built-in publishing isn't a problem. But for newer artists who want a single-stop solution, CD Baby Pro's integrated publishing administration is a meaningful advantage.

To understand the full scope of what you might be missing, read our complete guide on how to register your music for publishing royalties.

Artist Tools, Dashboard, and Platform Features

Beyond core distribution, both services have invested in artist-facing tools that affect day-to-day usability.

DistroKid Dashboard and Features

DistroKid's interface is clean and fast. Core features include:

  • HyperFollow — a smart link landing page that auto-populates with all platform links after your release goes live
  • Bank — automated royalty splitting for collaborators
  • Spotify for Artists verification — DistroKid integrates directly with Spotify's artist verification flow
  • Cover song licensing — DistroKid can obtain mechanical licenses for cover songs (additional fee) and distribute them legally
  • Presave pages — available on Musician Plus and higher plans
  • TikTok scheduling — direct scheduling of sound delivery to TikTok
  • YouTube Content ID — DistroKid offers Content ID enrollment, which monetizes your recordings on YouTube and manages claims

CD Baby Dashboard and Features

CD Baby's dashboard is more feature-rich in some areas but can feel cluttered compared to DistroKid's streamlined interface. Key features include:

  • CD Baby Music — an integrated store where fans can purchase your music directly
  • Physical distribution — CD manufacturing and distribution to physical stores
  • Sync licensing pitching — CD Baby actively pitches music to sync licensing opportunities (TV, film, ads) on behalf of its artists
  • YouTube Content ID — available as standard
  • Music video distribution — CD Baby can distribute music videos to platforms including Vevo
  • Publishing administration — built into Pro tier

CD Baby's sync licensing pitching is worth highlighting. While no distributor guarantees sync placements, CD Baby has an active sync team that pitches eligible music to music supervisors. This is a passive opportunity for catalog artists and can generate meaningful income. For a deeper understanding of sync opportunities, see our guide on how to get sync licensing deals.

Analytics and Reporting

Both platforms provide streaming analytics showing plays, saves, and revenue by platform and territory. Neither matches the depth of a dedicated analytics platform like Chartmetric or Soundcharts. DistroKid's analytics are real-time for Spotify data via Spotify for Artists integration; CD Baby's reporting is accurate but typically updates with a 24-48 hour lag.

Customer Support and Catalog Management

Customer support quality is consistently one of the most cited frustrations with digital distributors, and both DistroKid and CD Baby have mixed reputations here — though for different reasons.

DistroKid Support

DistroKid's support has historically been criticized for slow response times and heavy reliance on automated systems. Support is primarily via email ticket, with no phone support. The help center (automated FAQs and chatbot) handles the majority of common questions adequately. For urgent issues — metadata corrections after a release, disputes about Content ID claims, urgent takedown requests — wait times can be frustrating.

DistroKid's subscription model means support interactions are frequent: artists contact support for everything from ISRC corrections to label name changes. The volume of inquiries relative to DistroKid's rapid growth has strained support capacity.

CD Baby Support

CD Baby offers phone support during business hours — a meaningful differentiator. The ability to speak with a human being about distribution issues is valuable when something goes wrong with a release. CD Baby also provides email support and a knowledge base. Response times via phone are generally faster than email; email ticket response times average 2-4 business days.

CD Baby's longer history (founded in 1998) means its team has more institutional knowledge about edge cases in distribution — unusual metadata situations, rights disputes, catalog migration issues — than some newer services.

Catalog Portability

One of the most important long-term considerations is what happens to your music catalog when you want to switch distributors or if a platform shuts down.

With CD Baby, your music stays on streaming platforms indefinitely after your one-time payment, regardless of your ongoing relationship with CD Baby. If you want to move your music to a new distributor, you need to take down the CD Baby release, migrate ISRCs if possible, and re-upload through the new service — causing a temporary gap in availability.

With DistroKid, music stays up as long as your subscription is active. If you cancel, you can pay the "leave music up" fee ($4.95 per album, $0.99 per single) to maintain availability without an active subscription. The fees are reasonable for small catalogs but can add up for artists with extensive back catalogs.

Which Distributor Should You Choose?

The right choice depends on your specific situation. Here's a framework for making the decision clearly.

Choose DistroKid If:

  • You release music regularly — more than 2-3 tracks or releases per year
  • You want to keep 100% of your streaming royalties
  • You already have publishing set up (registered with ASCAP/BMI and the MLC) or don't need publishing administration yet
  • You prioritize fast delivery speed and a clean, simple interface
  • You need automated royalty splitting for collaborations (Bank feature)
  • You want integrated presave campaign pages (Musician Plus)
  • Cost predictability matters — the flat annual fee is easy to budget

Choose CD Baby If:

  • You release infrequently — one or two releases per year or less
  • You want a one-time fee with no ongoing commitment and no risk of music being removed
  • You want publishing administration included without managing it separately
  • You need physical CD or vinyl distribution
  • You want passive sync licensing pitching for your catalog
  • Music video distribution to Vevo is important to your strategy
  • You value phone customer support access

The Publishing-Focused Artist

If you're a songwriter who writes for other artists, or a producer who co-writes on placements, publishing administration is genuinely important and can represent significant uncollected income. In this case, CD Baby Pro's integrated publishing makes sense — or, alternatively, use DistroKid for distribution and work with a dedicated publishing administrator like Songtrust, SongFile, or work directly with your PRO.

Frequently Overlooked Considerations

ISRC codes: Both distributors assign ISRC (International Standard Recording Code) codes to your releases automatically if you don't provide your own. ISRCs are tied to the specific recording and travel with your music. When switching distributors, retaining your original ISRCs preserves your streaming history and playlist saves — contact your new distributor about ISRC portability before migrating.

UPC codes: Both services assign UPC/EAN codes to releases. Same portability considerations apply.

Neighboring rights: Neither DistroKid nor CD Baby standard plans collect neighboring rights royalties — royalties paid to performers and record labels when recordings are played on radio, TV, and in public venues. Artists generating significant broadcast income should work with a neighboring rights collector separately.

Label services vs. distribution: Neither DistroKid nor CD Baby is a label — they don't advance money, provide A&R support, or fund marketing. They are pure distribution and collection services. Artists who want label-style support should look at label services companies or evaluate the record deal landscape as a separate question from distribution.

Final Verdict: DistroKid vs CD Baby in 2026

For the majority of independent artists in 2026, DistroKid is the stronger default choice. The combination of unlimited releases, 0% royalty cut, fast delivery, automated collaboration splits, and a low annual subscription makes it mathematically superior for anyone releasing more than a handful of tracks per year. The presave feature, HyperFollow smart links, and direct Spotify for Artists integration add practical value for modern release strategy.

CD Baby remains the better choice in specific situations: infrequent releasers who want a genuine one-time commitment, artists who need physical distribution, songwriters who want publishing administration bundled into their distribution without managing separate accounts, and artists who value access to CD Baby's sync pitching network. The CD Baby Pro tier is genuinely valuable for songwriters who haven't already established publishing infrastructure — the publishing royalties it can unlock often exceed the cost difference.

The worst outcome is paralysis — spending months researching rather than releasing. Both services are legitimate, professional, and will get your music onto Spotify and Apple Music reliably. The financial difference between choosing "wrong" is real but recoverable; the opportunity cost of delaying your releases while overthinking distribution is larger.

For artists just starting out, understanding where distribution fits within the larger context of independently promoting your music and building streams is equally important as choosing the right distributor.

Practical Exercises

Beginner Exercise

Calculate Your Break-Even Point

Estimate how many releases you plan to put out in the next 12 months and your approximate current monthly streaming income. Multiply your annual streaming income by 9% (CD Baby's cut) and compare that number to DistroKid's $22.99/year subscription fee — whichever is lower tells you which service costs less for your current scale. Do this calculation before signing up for either service.

Intermediate Exercise

Audit Your Publishing Registration

Check whether you are registered with a PRO (ASCAP or BMI), affiliated with the MLC for mechanical royalties, and collecting royalties in international markets. If you find gaps — especially missing MLC registration or no international collection — evaluate whether CD Baby Pro's publishing administration would recover more in uncollected royalties than it costs, versus setting up independent publishing administration through Songtrust or similar. Document your current royalty registration status in writing.

Advanced Exercise

Model a 3-Year Total Cost of Ownership

Build a spreadsheet comparing DistroKid vs CD Baby over 36 months using your actual release cadence (projected), current streaming revenue, and expected growth rate. Include DistroKid's annual subscription, CD Baby's per-release fees, and both services' royalty percentage cuts applied to projected income at 6-month intervals. Factor in the value of publishing royalties if you're considering CD Baby Pro, and include the cost of separate publishing administration if choosing DistroKid. This model will give you a defensible financial answer tailored to your actual career trajectory rather than a generic recommendation.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ Should I use DistroKid or CD Baby?
Use DistroKid if you release music more than 2-3 times per year and want to keep 100% of royalties — the $22.99/year unlimited plan becomes more cost-effective the more you release. Use CD Baby if you release infrequently and prefer a one-time per-release fee with no annual subscription, or if you want CD Baby's additional services like physical distribution, sync licensing pitching, or built-in publishing administration.
FAQ Does DistroKid keep 100% of royalties?
Yes — DistroKid passes 100% of streaming royalties to artists on all plans. DistroKid makes its money from annual subscription fees, not royalty percentages. CD Baby takes 9% of streaming royalties on its Standard plan and 15% on its Pro plan, making DistroKid mathematically better for artists generating meaningful streaming income once the subscription cost is recovered.
FAQ How fast does DistroKid distribute music?
DistroKid is the fastest major distributor — music typically appears on Spotify within 1-3 days and on Apple Music within 1-5 days after submission. CD Baby typically takes 3-7 business days. Both recommend submitting at least 2 weeks before a planned release date, and Spotify editorial pitching requires submission at least 7 days before release.
FAQ Does CD Baby collect publishing royalties?
CD Baby Pro ($14.95/single, $49.95/album) includes publishing administration — CD Baby registers your songs with relevant PROs and collects performance and mechanical royalties from over 40 countries. DistroKid's basic plans do not include publishing administration; artists need to register separately with ASCAP or BMI and the MLC.
FAQ What happens to my music if I cancel DistroKid?
If you cancel your DistroKid subscription without paying the 'leave music up' fee ($4.95 per album or $0.99 per single), your music is removed from all streaming platforms. CD Baby uses a one-time fee model, so your music stays on platforms indefinitely after paying the initial distribution fee regardless of any ongoing relationship with CD Baby.
FAQ Which distributor pays royalties faster?
Both DistroKid and CD Baby pay royalties monthly. DistroKid's minimum payout threshold is $2.00 versus CD Baby's $25.00 minimum, meaning smaller earners receive payouts sooner with DistroKid. DistroKid royalties are typically paid within 30-60 days of being reported by streaming platforms.
FAQ Can I use both DistroKid and CD Baby?
Yes, but not for the same releases — you cannot distribute the same song through two distributors simultaneously. You could use DistroKid for new releases and leave older CD Baby releases in place. Switching distributors requires removing music from one service before distributing through another, causing temporary unavailability on streaming platforms.
FAQ Which distributor is better for getting on playlists?
Both DistroKid and CD Baby provide the same Spotify for Artists access and editorial playlist pitching capabilities — playlist placement is determined by Spotify's editorial team, not the distributor. The key factor is releasing through any legitimate distributor with at least 7 days lead time and completing the pitch form in Spotify for Artists.
