Best Audio Interfaces for Home Studio 2025: Every Budget Covered

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2025 20 min read
Quick Answer

For most home studio producers: the Focusrite Scarlett 2i2 4th Gen (~$200) is the default recommendation — clean preamps, reliable drivers, excellent software bundle, and the most-used interface in home recording for good reason. Step up to the SSL 2+ (~$230) for SSL preamp character and dual headphone outputs, or the Universal Audio Volt series (~$200–350) for UA's DSP and plugin bundle. For professional multi-channel recording, the Focusrite Scarlett 18i20 or PreSonus Studio 1824c at ~$500 handles entire band sessions.

Choosing the right audio interface is one of the most consequential decisions in building a home studio. The interface is the hub of everything — your microphones, instruments, and monitors all connect through it, and its preamp quality, driver stability, and I/O configuration determine what's possible in your recording setup. This guide covers the best options at every price point with honest assessments of who each is for.

What we'll cover: What to look for before buying, best interfaces under $150, best interfaces $150–$300, best interfaces $300–$600, best professional interfaces $600+, specialist interfaces for specific applications, and a decision framework for choosing the right one.

What to Look For Before Buying

Input count: How many things do you need to record simultaneously? Solo vocalist: 1 mic input is sufficient. Vocalist + acoustic guitar: 2 inputs. Recording a band: 8+ inputs. Don't buy more inputs than you need — more inputs mean more cost and more complexity. Do buy enough for your realistic use — buying a single-input interface and immediately wishing for two is a common frustration.

Preamp quality and gain: Preamp quality determines how clean and low-noise your recordings are. More expensive interfaces have lower noise floors and more gain. Look for at least 60 dB of gain — sufficient for most condenser microphones. 65+ dB is needed for low-output dynamic microphones (SM7B, RE20) without additional gain boosters.

Driver stability: An interface is useless if it crashes your DAW or introduces dropouts. Focusrite, Universal Audio, and Apogee have strong driver reputations. Some budget brands have driver stability issues that make recording sessions frustrating.

Connection type: USB-C is the current standard. Thunderbolt provides lower latency and higher bandwidth for professional multi-channel work. Avoid older USB-A-only interfaces when buying new — USB-C compatibility ensures future compatibility with current and future computers.

Software bundle: Many interfaces include DAW licenses and plugins worth significantly more than the interface itself. The Scarlett range includes Pro Tools Artist and Splice. The SSL 2+ includes SSL's own plugins and Ableton Live Lite. Factor bundle value into the price comparison.

Best Under $150

Focusrite Scarlett Solo 4th Gen (~$120) — Best single-input choice

The world's best-selling audio interface at its price point for good reason. One XLR mic input, one instrument input, clean preamps with up to 56 dB gain, Air mode presence lift, USB-C, and a software bundle including Pro Tools Artist and Ableton Live Lite. The limitation is inherent: one mic input. If you'll ever need to record two things simultaneously, buy the 2i2 instead. For solo vocalists and single-instrument producers, the Solo is excellent.

PreSonus AudioBox USB 96 (~$99) — Best budget two-input option

Two mic/instrument inputs, 48V phantom power, solid build quality, and USB power. Preamps are slightly noisier than Focusrite's at high gain settings but competitive for the price. Includes Studio One Artist — a capable DAW that outclasses most bundled software at this price. A reliable entry-level option for producers who need two inputs on a tight budget.

Behringer UMC22 (~$49) — Ultra-budget option with caveats

At $49, the UMC22 provides a functional recording interface with one XLR mic input and one instrument input. Preamp quality and build quality are entry-level — expect some noise at high gain settings and a less premium feel. Driver stability on Windows can be inconsistent. Not recommended as a long-term studio tool, but functional for absolute beginners testing whether home recording is for them before investing more.

Best $150–$300

Focusrite Scarlett 2i2 4th Gen (~$200) — The benchmark recommendation

Two mic inputs with 56 dB gain each, Air mode on both channels, USB-C, independent phantom power per channel, and the same Pro Tools Artist + Ableton Live Lite + Splice bundle as the Solo. The 2i2 is the most recommended audio interface in music production communities for good reason — it delivers professional-quality recordings reliably, has excellent driver stability, and the software bundle represents exceptional value. For the majority of home studio producers who record vocals and instruments, the 2i2 is the correct choice without needing to consider alternatives.

SSL 2+ (~$230) — Best for producers wanting character

Same input/output count as the Scarlett 2i2 but with SSL's 4K Legacy mode — a high-frequency presence enhancement inspired by the SSL 4000 console. Dual headphone outputs with independent volume controls, MIDI in/out, and a software bundle including SSL Native plugins (the SSL bus compressor alone is worth significant value). The SSL 2+ is the right choice when you want a more characterful sound with some SSL analogue flavour rather than pure neutrality. Build quality is excellent — denser and more substantial than the Scarlett range.

Universal Audio Volt 276 (~$230) — Best for hardware compression character

The Volt 276's defining feature is an onboard 76 compressor — a hardware compressor circuit modelled on the UREI 1176 — that processes the input signal before it reaches the computer. Recording through hardware compression produces a specific warmth and dynamic character that software emulations approximate but don't fully replicate. For producers who want to track with hardware compression baked in, the Volt 276 is unique at this price point. The preamps are clean and capable. UA's software bundle includes their older plugin library.

MOTU M2 (~$170) — Best for monitoring accuracy

The MOTU M2 stands out for its exceptional DAC quality — the monitoring output is cleaner and more accurate than Focusrite and SSL competitors at similar prices, making it a favourite for producers who prioritise monitoring quality. The preamps are solid but not the main selling point. If you're making mixing and mastering decisions that depend on accurate monitoring, the M2 deserves consideration.

Best $300–$600

Universal Audio Apollo Solo (~$499) — Best DSP interface in this range

The Apollo Solo is where UA's Unison technology becomes accessible — preamp emulations that load directly into the interface hardware, changing not just the sound but the physical impedance and gain characteristics of the preamp to match the hardware being emulated. The Apollo Solo includes UA's Console software, real-time DSP processing with zero latency for monitoring, and access to the UA plugin ecosystem. A tier above its competitors in what it offers technically, but the value is fully realised only if you invest in UA plugins.

Focusrite Scarlett 18i20 (~$500) — Best for multi-channel recording

Eight mic/instrument inputs, eight balanced outputs, ADAT connectivity for expanding to 18 inputs with external preamp units, and the same Scarlett preamp quality across all channels. The 18i20 is the right choice when you need to record an entire drum kit, a full band simultaneously, or multiple room microphone setups. The Scarlett preamp quality that works on the 2i2 scales consistently across all eight inputs.

PreSonus Studio 1824c (~$450) — Best multi-channel alternative

Eight mic inputs, multiple output options, ADAT expansion, and robust build quality. The Studio 1824c includes Studio One Professional — the most fully-featured DAW bundled with any interface at any price. For producers who want to avoid additional DAW licensing costs while getting serious multi-channel recording capability, the Studio 1824c's bundle value is exceptional.

Professional Interfaces $600+

Universal Audio Apollo Twin X (~$899) — Professional desktop standard

Thunderbolt connectivity for ultra-low latency, two Unison-capable mic preamps, extensive DSP for real-time processing, and access to UA's full plugin ecosystem. The Apollo Twin X is the professional desktop interface standard used in high-end home studios and professional production environments. The Thunderbolt requirement means Mac users have a smoother experience (Thunderbolt more universal on Mac), though Windows Thunderbolt support has improved significantly.

Apogee Symphony Desktop (~$1,295) — Best for Mac-based professional studios

Apogee's conversion quality is widely considered among the best available at any price. The Symphony Desktop provides two high-quality mic preamps, exceptional DAC quality for monitoring, and Apogee's proprietary DSP for real-time processing. Mac-only. For producers who have invested in the Apple ecosystem and want the best possible conversion quality, the Symphony Desktop is a benchmark product.

RME Babyface Pro FS (~$849) — Best for touring and mobile recording

RME's driver stability and low latency performance on Windows is unmatched in the industry — RME interfaces work reliably in high-performance computing environments where other brands introduce issues. The Babyface Pro FS is compact, bus-powered, and provides professional-grade preamps in a road-ready form factor. The choice for producers who need reliability and performance on Windows without compromise.

Specialist Interfaces

Rode AI-1 (~$129) — Best for single USB microphone conversion

For producers who want to use a standard XLR condenser microphone with the convenience of USB connectivity. The AI-1 is a single-input interface with a clean Focusrite-quality preamp and minimal footprint. Pairs particularly well with the Rode NT1 for a clean, compact vocal recording setup.

iRig Pro I/O (~$150) — Best for mobile iOS recording

Designed specifically for iOS and Android recording with Lightning and USB-C connectivity. Works as a standard interface connected to a computer but excels in mobile applications — recording on an iPhone or iPad with a professional microphone and proper 48V phantom power. For producers who record on the go or in non-studio environments.
