Fl Studio Vs Pro Tools

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 · 21 min read · Verified May 7, 2026
Quick Answer — Updated May 2026

FL Studio is the better choice for beat makers, electronic producers, and anyone prioritizing MIDI workflow, lifetime free updates, and an included plugin suite. Pro Tools is the industry standard for professional recording studios, post-production, and audio engineers who need rock-solid session compatibility and advanced audio editing tools. Your genre, workflow, and budget should drive the decision — most producers who record live instruments heavily lean Pro Tools, while most beat makers and electronic artists prefer FL Studio.

Updated May 2026 — FL Studio 21 / Pro Tools 2024.x

Choosing between FL Studio and Pro Tools is one of the most consequential decisions you'll make as a producer or engineer. Both are professional-grade digital audio workstations, but they were built for fundamentally different workflows, different users, and different points in the music creation process. This guide breaks down every major dimension — MIDI, audio recording, mixing, bundled instruments, pricing, and real-world industry use — so you can make an informed decision rather than defaulting to whatever your favorite producer happens to use.

Bottom Line Up Front

FL Studio dominates beat production and electronic music. Pro Tools dominates professional recording and post-production. If you're primarily making beats, FL wins on value and workflow. If you're recording bands, voiceover, or working in commercial studios, Pro Tools is the professional standard you'll need to know.

Background, Philosophy, and Who Each DAW Is Built For

FL Studio — originally known as FruityLoops — was developed by Belgian company Image-Line and first released in 1997. Its core design philosophy has always been pattern-based composition: you build loops and patterns in the step sequencer or piano roll, then arrange those patterns on a timeline. This approach is perfectly suited to beat making, hip-hop, trap, EDM, and any genre where repetitive, loop-driven structures are the norm. Over the decades, FL Studio evolved into a full-featured DAW capable of serious audio recording and mixing, but its MIDI-first DNA remains its defining characteristic.

Pro Tools, developed by Avid Technology (originally Digidesign), launched in 1991 and was engineered from the ground up as a professional audio recording and editing platform. Its architecture mirrors the workflow of a traditional mixing console and tape machine — you record audio to tracks, edit it on a timeline, and mix it down. Pro Tools became the standard in commercial recording studios throughout the 1990s and early 2000s, and that dominance has been self-reinforcing: studios use it because engineers know it, engineers learn it because studios use it.

This divergence in origin explains nearly every practical difference between the two DAWs. FL Studio is optimized for composition and sound design from scratch. Pro Tools is optimized for capturing, editing, and refining performances that have already happened in the real world. Neither is objectively better — they just excel at different stages and styles of music making.

Key Insight

Many professional producers and engineers own both. FL Studio is used to sketch beats and write MIDI arrangements; Pro Tools is used to record live sessions and do final mix work. The two tools complement each other more than they compete.

MIDI Workflow and Pattern-Based Composition

This is where FL Studio's advantage is most decisive. The FL Studio piano roll is widely considered the best MIDI editor in any DAW — and that's not marketing copy, it's a genuine consensus among producers who have used multiple platforms. The piano roll features per-note properties (velocity, pan, pitch, release, modulation) that can be edited with mouse gestures or drawn directly, a robust chord tool, arpeggio and strum controls built in, and a note grouping system that makes editing complex MIDI arrangements fast and intuitive. The step sequencer remains one of the fastest ways to program drum patterns in the industry.

FL Studio's pattern-based workflow means you can create dozens of variations of a loop — different drum patterns, alternate chord progressions, breakdown sections — and audition them in real time by triggering patterns from the playlist. This is a fundamentally different compositional approach than a purely linear timeline, and for electronic music and hip-hop, it maps more naturally to how those genres are structured.

Pro Tools has a functional MIDI editor, but it was clearly built as a secondary feature to the audio workflow. The piano roll (called the MIDI Editor in Pro Tools) does the job, but it lacks the ergonomic polish and feature depth of FL Studio's implementation. Pro Tools also lacks a pattern-based workflow entirely — everything lives on a single linear timeline. For producers who think in loops and patterns, this is a genuine limitation. For engineers recording a band's live performance, the linear timeline is exactly what they need and the pattern approach would only add friction.

FL Studio also ships with an excellent MIDI controller integration framework. The Novation Launchpad, Akai MPK series, and virtually every class-compliant MIDI controller map cleanly to FL Studio's mixer, step sequencer, and piano roll. The MIDI workflow in a DAW becomes noticeably more fluid when the software was designed with live MIDI performance in mind, as FL Studio was.

Winner — MIDI Workflow: FL Studio, by a significant margin for beat makers and electronic producers. Pro Tools is adequate for basic MIDI but was never designed to be a MIDI-first tool.

Audio Recording, Editing, and Comping

This section inverts the previous verdict. Pro Tools' audio recording and editing capabilities are class-leading and have been for over two decades. Key features that define Pro Tools' recording advantage:

Elastic Audio is Pro Tools' time-stretching and pitch correction engine built directly into the edit window. You can slip audio clips to the grid, warp timing without destructive edits, and conform recordings to tempo changes with surgical precision. This is not a plugin — it's a core part of the editing workflow.

Clip Gain allows you to adjust the gain of individual audio clips before they hit the channel fader or any plugin, giving you precise control over level matching and dynamics management before the mix stage. This is an underrated feature that saves enormous time when editing multi-take sessions.

Comping (composite editing — stitching together the best parts of multiple takes into one final performance) is where Pro Tools absolutely dominates. The playlist system in Pro Tools lets you stack multiple takes on lanes within a single track, audition sections by looping, and drag-and-drop the best phrases into a comp track. This workflow is essential for recording vocals, guitar solos, and any performance that requires multiple takes. FL Studio has a version of this but it's considerably less mature and harder to use.

Session file compatibility is a Pro Tools exclusive advantage that cannot be overstated for commercial work. When a client sends you a Pro Tools session file (.ptx), or when you deliver stems to a mixing engineer, the chance that they're working in Pro Tools is extremely high — industry surveys consistently put Pro Tools usage in professional recording studios at 70–80% of facilities. If you're doing commercial recording work — film scoring, studio sessions, album production for signed artists — you will encounter Pro Tools session files, and being native in the software matters.

FL Studio's audio recording capabilities have improved substantially in recent versions. It handles multi-track recording competently, and the Edison audio editor built into the software is genuinely excellent for sample editing, pitch shifting, and spectral analysis. However, FL Studio's architecture treats audio clips as secondary to MIDI patterns, and the comping and take management systems reflect that priority order. For a home studio producer recording their own vocals or a guitar line, FL Studio is perfectly capable. For a session engineer tracking a full band with 24+ inputs, Pro Tools is the right tool.

For anyone setting up a home recording environment, check our guide on how to record vocals in a home studio — the techniques apply regardless of which DAW you choose, but the specific workflow steps differ between the two platforms.

Winner — Audio Recording and Editing: Pro Tools, decisively for professional and multi-take recording scenarios. FL Studio is adequate for basic home studio recording.

Mixing Engine, Routing, and Mastering Tools

Both DAWs include fully capable mixing environments, but their architecture and feature sets reflect their different design priorities.

Pro Tools Mixing Environment: The Pro Tools mixer is modeled directly on a professional SSL or API-style console, and engineers who have worked in hardware-based studios find the transition to Pro Tools intuitive. The signal flow is predictable and transparent. Pro Tools supports up to 768 kHz sample rates (in HDX hardware configurations) and 32-bit floating point processing on the native engine. The I/O routing system is powerful and scales to large session configurations with dozens of buses and auxiliary channels. For post-production and film work, Pro Tools' metering (including loudness metering conforming to ATSC A/85, EBU R128, and related broadcast standards) and its surround sound routing (up to Dolby Atmos 9.1.6 in Pro Tools Ultimate) are unmatched in any competing DAW at the native software level.

Pro Tools' mixer also benefits from decades of third-party plugin development specifically targeting its architecture. The AAX plugin format (used by Pro Tools since version 11) is supported by every major plugin developer, and Avid's own plugins — particularly the Avid Channel Strip and various console emulation tools — are genuinely well-regarded. The automation system in Pro Tools is among the most sophisticated available, with Write, Touch, Latch, and Trim modes that mirror the feel of riding faders on a physical console.

FL Studio Mixing Environment: FL Studio's mixer has evolved substantially and is now a capable professional tool. It supports up to 512 insert and send tracks, flexible routing between tracks via the mixer routing matrix, sidechain routing, and full automation of all mixer parameters. The mixer supports 32-bit and 64-bit float processing, and Image-Line has invested significantly in reducing latency and improving stability in recent versions. The included Parametric EQ 2 and Maximus multiband compressor/limiter are genuinely competitive tools that many producers use as their primary mixing processors.

Where FL Studio's mixer trails Pro Tools is in the depth of its automation system, the scalability for large sessions (100+ tracks with complex routing), and the integration with professional hardware control surfaces. FL Studio does support Mackie Control and HUI-compatible controllers, but the experience is less seamless than Pro Tools' deep integration with Avid's own S3 and S6 control surfaces — hardware that you will find in virtually every major commercial facility.

For mastering, FL Studio includes the excellent Maximus plugin and the Parametric EQ 2, which are sufficient for basic mastering tasks. Pro Tools users typically rely on third-party mastering plugins (FabFilter Pro-L 2, iZotope Ozone, etc.) as Avid's own mastering-specific tools are less comprehensive. If you want to go deeper on mastering tools across platforms, our iZotope Ozone 12 review covers a plugin that works equally well in both DAWs.

Winner — Mixing Engine: Pro Tools for professional studio applications, large sessions, and post-production. FL Studio is competitive for mixing hip-hop and electronic music where session complexity is lower.

Bundled Instruments, Plugins, and Sound Libraries

This is a major differentiator that significantly affects the real-world cost of each platform. FL Studio's included plugin suite is genuinely impressive and forms the core toolkit that most FL users work with on a daily basis:

FL Studio's Included Instruments (Producer/Signature Edition and above):

  • Sytrus — a powerful FM/subtractive hybrid synthesizer that is competitive with commercial FM synths costing hundreds of dollars
  • Harmor — a resynthesis and additive/subtractive synthesizer with image synthesis capabilities
  • Harmless — an additive synthesis engine optimized for pads, leads, and basses
  • Gross Beat (Producer Edition and above) — a time and volume manipulation effect widely used in trap and hip-hop production
  • Parametric EQ 2 — a full-featured parametric equalizer used by professionals as a primary mixing tool
  • Maximus — a three-band dynamics processor (compressor/expander/limiter) that doubles as a mastering limiter
  • Fruity Convolver — a convolution reverb included in the standard package
  • Fruity Love Philter and various modulation effects
  • FLEX — a sample-based rompler instrument with a free sample library that includes drums, basses, keys, and synth patches
  • DirectWave — a full sampler instrument for building custom sample libraries

The Signature Bundle adds Vintage B3 (B3 organ emulation), Sakura (physical modeling string synthesizer), Poizone (subtractive synth), and several other instruments. This suite is genuinely capable of producing commercial-quality music without purchasing a single additional plugin, which is a significant advantage for budget-conscious producers.

Pro Tools' included plugin suite is considerably more modest. The standard Pro Tools package includes Avid's own channel strip, a basic EQ, compressor, gate, and some utility tools. The Pro Tools Ultimate tier includes additional dynamics processing and some third-party licenses (Avid bundles vary by version and promotional period), but there is no equivalent to FL Studio's comprehensive synthesis and effects library. If you want professional-quality reverb, spectral editing, or advanced synthesis in Pro Tools, you are purchasing third-party plugins — which adds cost quickly.

The plugin format issue deserves emphasis: Pro Tools only supports the AAX plugin format. This means that free VST plugins — of which there are thousands — do not work in Pro Tools. If you're building a plugin collection, every plugin you buy for Pro Tools must have an AAX version, which excludes many smaller developers and all free VST tools. FL Studio supports VST2, VST3, and AU (on Mac), giving it access to the full breadth of the plugin ecosystem. Our guide to the best free VST plugins covers dozens of tools that will work in FL Studio but not in Pro Tools.

Winner — Bundled Instruments and Plugins: FL Studio, by a wide margin. The included plugin suite represents thousands of dollars in equivalent third-party value.

Pricing, Licensing, and Long-Term Cost

Pricing is one of the most discussed differences between the two DAWs, and the comparison looks very different depending on your time horizon.

FL Studio Pricing (as of May 2026):

  • Fruity Edition — $99 (MIDI patterns and built-in instruments only; no audio recording to playlist)
  • Producer Edition — $199 (full audio recording, all native plugins, VST/AU support)
  • Signature Bundle — $299 (Producer Edition plus Gross Beat, Harmor, Newtone, Pitcher, and additional instruments)
  • All Plugins Bundle — $499 (everything Image-Line makes, including all third-party licensed instruments)

The critical pricing advantage for FL Studio is its lifetime free updates policy. When you buy any edition of FL Studio, you receive every future update to that software — including major version upgrades — at no additional cost. A producer who purchased FL Studio Producer Edition in 2015 for $149 has received FL Studio 12, 20, 20.8, 20.9, 21, and every subsequent version for free. This policy is essentially unique in the DAW market and represents enormous long-term value. [CORRECTED: FL Studio Producer Edition price in 2015 was approximately $149-$159 → current price is $199]

Pro Tools Pricing (as of May 2026):

  • Pro Tools Intro — Free (limited to 16 tracks, basic plugin set; no AAX third-party plugin support)
  • Pro Tools Artist — $9.99/month or $99/year (32 tracks, core AAX plugin support)
  • Pro Tools Studio — $39.99/month or $399/year (unlimited tracks, full Avid plugin suite, Dolby Atmos mixing, cloud collaboration)
  • Pro Tools Ultimate (now called Pro Tools Studio in recent rebranding, with HDX hardware) — pricing varies by hardware configuration and subscription tier; HDX systems start at $10,000+ for hardware

Pro Tools moved to a subscription-first model in 2022, which has been controversial among its user base. While the monthly entry point seems affordable, the annual cost compounds over time. A producer who maintains a Pro Tools Studio subscription for five years at $399/year spends $1,995 — and owns nothing if they cancel. Compare this to FL Studio's one-time $199 Producer Edition with lifetime updates, and the long-term value proposition for FL Studio is dramatically better for independent producers who don't need the professional studio compatibility that justifies Pro Tools' cost.

The calculus changes for professional recording studios and engineers. A studio charging $500/day for session work can absorb the annual Pro Tools subscription cost easily, and the session file compatibility and industry-standard status justify the ongoing expense. For a hobbyist or independent beat maker, FL Studio's pricing model is simply better suited to the budget reality.

Genre Fit, Industry Use Cases, and Real-World Workflows

The genre breakdown for FL Studio vs Pro Tools reflects their design philosophies almost exactly:

FL Studio Dominates In:

  • Hip-hop and trap — The pattern-based workflow, the 808 bass tools (Sytrus, 3xOsc, and the step sequencer's pitch automation), and the overall ergonomics map perfectly to how trap and hip-hop beats are constructed. Metro Boomin, Southside, Wheezy, and many other chart-topping producers have publicly cited FL Studio as their primary DAW.
  • EDM and electronic music — The synthesizer suite, the pattern arranger for building buildups and drops, and the MIDI workflow make FL Studio the dominant tool in the EDM production community. See our guide on how to make EDM for workflow details that align specifically with FL's strengths.
  • Lo-fi hip-hop — The accessibility of FL Studio, combined with its sample manipulation tools (Slicex, DirectWave), makes it the dominant tool in the lo-fi production community.
  • Hyperpop and experimental electronic — The synthesis depth (Harmor especially) and modulation capabilities suit producers working in more experimental territories.

Pro Tools Dominates In:

  • Rock, pop, and country recording — Any genre where live instruments are tracked in a professional studio environment will almost certainly be recorded in Pro Tools. The comping, editing, and session management tools are purpose-built for this workflow.
  • Film scoring and post-production — Pro Tools is the industry standard for audio post-production for film and television. Its timecode support, video playback engine, and surround sound capabilities are unmatched. If you want to work in sync licensing or film scoring professionally, Pro Tools knowledge is essentially mandatory. Our guide on how to get sync licensing deals explains why Pro Tools compatibility matters in this market.
  • Commercial recording studios — When a major label artist comes in to record vocals on their album, the studio is running Pro Tools. Period. Engineers trained in Pro Tools can move from studio to studio seamlessly because the session format is universal.
  • Podcast and voiceover production — Pro Tools' audio editing capabilities, noise reduction integration, and loudness metering tools make it a preferred choice for professional podcast and voiceover engineers, though competitors like Adobe Audition have made inroads here.

Platform Compatibility, Stability, and System Requirements

Both DAWs run on Windows and macOS. FL Studio added native Apple Silicon (M1/M2/M3/M4) support in FL Studio 21, delivering significant performance improvements on modern Macs. Pro Tools has similarly updated its Apple Silicon support across the 2023 and 2024 releases, with full native M-series support now available across the product line.

Neither DAW officially supports Linux, though FL Studio runs on Linux via Wine with varying degrees of stability. This is an unofficial workflow that Image-Line acknowledges but does not support.

System Requirements (Minimum, May 2026):

  • FL Studio 21 (Windows): Windows 10 or 11, Intel Core i5/AMD Ryzen 5 equivalent, 8 GB RAM (16 GB recommended), 4 GB free disk space for installation
  • FL Studio 21 (macOS): macOS 10.15.7 (Catalina) or later, Apple Silicon natively supported, 8 GB RAM (16 GB recommended)
  • Pro Tools Studio (Windows): Windows 10 or 11 (specific build requirements apply), Intel Core i7/AMD Ryzen 7 recommended, 16 GB RAM minimum (32 GB recommended for large sessions)
  • Pro Tools Studio (macOS): macOS 12 (Monterey) or later, Apple Silicon natively supported (since Pro Tools 2023.3), 16 GB RAM minimum

Pro Tools has historically had more stringent system qualification requirements — Avid maintains a list of tested and approved hardware configurations, and running Pro Tools on unqualified hardware can result in stability issues. This is partly a legacy of its hardware-centric origins (the original Pro Tools required Digidesign hardware to function), and while the software now runs without dedicated DSP hardware, the qualification culture persists. FL Studio is generally more permissive about hardware compatibility and tends to run stably on a wider range of consumer configurations.

For producers building out a studio setup, your choice of audio interface should be compatible with your DAW. Our best audio interfaces of 2026 guide covers options that work well with both platforms, though Avid's own MBOX and HD I/O interfaces are optimized specifically for Pro Tools.

Stability: Both DAWs are mature, stable products in their current versions. FL Studio 21 has an excellent reputation for stability on both platforms. Pro Tools has historically struggled with stability issues that its users are sometimes resigned to — crash reports during large sessions are not uncommon, and Avid's update cadence sometimes introduces new bugs. This is a known quality of the Pro Tools experience that engineers factor into their workflows (frequent session saves, incremental session backups). FL Studio's stability is generally considered superior for typical production workflows.

If you want to compare FL Studio against another strong alternative that bridges both worlds, our FL Studio vs Ableton Live comparison covers that head-to-head in detail, including Ableton's unique Session View workflow that some producers find more intuitive than either FL's pattern system or Pro Tools' linear timeline.

Industry Perspective

Many working producers maintain accounts on both platforms — using FL Studio for beat writing and initial composition, then importing stems into Pro Tools for mixing or delivering to a mixing engineer who works in a commercial facility. This hybrid approach leverages each DAW's strengths without forcing a compromise on either end of the workflow.

Learning Curve, Community, and Educational Resources

FL Studio has a shallower initial learning curve for producers coming from a beat-making background. The step sequencer is immediately intuitive — you click squares to activate drum hits, adjust velocities by dragging, and you have a functional drum pattern in minutes. The piano roll opens directly when you double-click a MIDI clip, putting the composer's tool front and center. Image-Line's official YouTube channel and the broader FL Studio community on YouTube represent thousands of hours of tutorial content ranging from absolute beginner to advanced sound design.

Pro Tools has a steeper initial learning curve, particularly for users who have never worked with a traditional mixing console workflow. Concepts like routing sends and returns, hardware buffer settings, and the difference between clips, regions, and tracks can feel opaque to beginners. However, Pro Tools' learning curve is well-documented: Avid offers official certification programs (Pro Tools Certified Operator and Pro Tools Expert credentials), major universities and recording schools teach Pro Tools as a core curriculum element, and the number of professional-grade educational resources is enormous. If your goal is a career in professional audio engineering, learning Pro Tools is a career investment that pays dividends in the job market.

The communities surrounding both DAWs are large and active. FL Studio's community skews toward self-taught producers sharing beats, tutorials, and sample packs. The r/FL_Studio subreddit, FL Studio Discord servers, and YouTube tutorial ecosystem are highly accessible. Pro Tools' community skews toward engineers, studio professionals, and students — more technically rigorous but also more specialized.

Both DAWs offer free trial versions. FL Studio's trial is fully functional — every feature works, but you cannot reopen saved projects (you must start fresh each session). This is a generous trial policy that lets you test the software thoroughly before purchasing. Pro Tools Intro is the free tier, which is permanently limited rather than time-limited, giving you a perpetual but feature-restricted version to learn the interface on.

Practical Exercises

Beginner Exercise

One Beat in Each DAW's Trial

Download the free trials of both FL Studio and Pro Tools Intro and attempt to create a simple four-bar drum loop in each. Notice which interface feels more intuitive for programming your first drum pattern — this visceral first impression is a genuine signal about which workflow suits your brain. Document which steps felt confusing in each DAW and look those specific steps up in tutorials before drawing conclusions.

Intermediate Exercise

Record and Edit a Vocal Take in Both Platforms

If you have access to both DAWs, record three takes of a short vocal phrase (even spoken word is fine) in FL Studio and then in Pro Tools. Practice the comping workflow in Pro Tools (stacking takes on playlist lanes and assembling a comp) and compare it to FL Studio's take management. This exercise will reveal the real-world difference in audio editing ergonomics that makes Pro Tools the professional recording standard — or confirm that FL Studio's simpler approach meets your needs.

Advanced Exercise

Hybrid Workflow — Beat in FL, Mix in Pro Tools

Build a full beat in FL Studio (drums, 808, melodies, atmospheres) and export individual stems at 24-bit/48 kHz. Import those stems into Pro Tools and build a complete mix using only Pro Tools' native tools plus one third-party AAX compressor and one AAX EQ. This hybrid workflow exercise teaches you the session handoff process used by professional studios and exposes you to the specific strengths of Pro Tools' mixing environment when applied to beat-based music. Document any latency compensation or level-matching issues you encounter during the import process.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ Is FL Studio good enough for professional music production?
Yes — FL Studio is used by chart-topping producers across hip-hop, trap, EDM, and pop. Metro Boomin, Southside, and Martin Garrix are among the high-profile names associated with FL Studio. The question is never whether FL Studio is "good enough" in terms of output quality, but whether its workflow suits your specific production style.
FAQ Can you record live instruments in FL Studio?
Yes, FL Studio supports multi-track audio recording via any ASIO-compatible audio interface on Windows or Core Audio on Mac. The workflow is functional but less mature than Pro Tools for take management, comping, and large multi-track sessions with live bands.
FAQ Why is Pro Tools so expensive compared to FL Studio?
Pro Tools has moved to a subscription model that reflects its position as an industry-standard professional tool. The ongoing cost is justified for commercial studios and engineers doing paid session work. For independent producers and beat makers, FL Studio's one-time purchase with lifetime free updates is dramatically better value.
FAQ Does Pro Tools support VST plugins?
No — Pro Tools only supports the AAX plugin format. This means the vast library of free and paid VST/VST3 plugins cannot be used in Pro Tools without an AAX version being available from the developer. This is one of Pro Tools' most significant practical limitations for producers accustomed to the broader plugin ecosystem.
FAQ Which DAW do professional recording studios use?
The majority of professional commercial recording studios run Pro Tools as their primary DAW. Industry surveys consistently put Pro Tools usage at 70–80% of professional facilities. This institutional dominance makes Pro Tools knowledge essentially mandatory for engineers who want to work in commercial studios.
FAQ Can I use FL Studio for mixing and mastering?
Yes — FL Studio's mixer, Parametric EQ 2, and Maximus plugin are capable professional tools. Many independent producers mix and master entirely in FL Studio. For large commercial projects or post-production work with strict loudness standards, Pro Tools or a dedicated mastering tool offers more specialized capabilities.
FAQ Is the FL Studio lifetime free update policy still active?
As of May 2026, yes — Image-Line still honors the lifetime free updates policy for all FL Studio purchases. This policy means you pay once and receive all future major version upgrades at no cost, making it one of the best long-term value propositions in the DAW market.
FAQ Should I learn FL Studio or Pro Tools first?
If your goal is making beats, electronic music, or hip-hop, start with FL Studio — the learning curve is lower and the workflow is better suited to those genres. If your goal is becoming a recording engineer or working in professional studios, learn Pro Tools from the start since it's the industry standard you'll encounter in every commercial facility.
