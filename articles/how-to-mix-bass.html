How to Mix Bass: The Complete Mixing Guide

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 13 min read Intermediate

Bass is the most technically difficult element to mix well. The low frequencies that carry it are invisible on many playback systems, phase problems hide in them, translation from studio to listener is inconsistent across speaker sizes, and the kick drum occupies the same frequency range — creating a constant competition for space. Getting bass right is the difference between a mix that sounds professional on every system and one that sounds great in the studio and thin or boomy everywhere else.

This guide covers the complete bass mixing process: EQ priorities, compression settings, sidechain compression for kick interaction, sub vs mid bass layering, harmonic saturation for small speaker translation, mono compatibility checking, and genre-specific approaches for bass guitar, 808, and synth bass.

What you'll learn:

EQ and compression for every bass type, kick-bass sidechain relationships, sub and mid bass layering, harmonic saturation for translation, mono compatibility checking, and the full bass mixing workflow from raw recording to finished mix.

The Low-End Fundamentals

Before touching a plugin, understand the physics. Bass frequencies below 80Hz are not reproduced by laptop speakers, phone speakers, or most earbuds. If your bass exists entirely in this sub range, it sounds enormous on studio monitors and disappears on the speakers most listeners use. Commercial bass mixes work on every system because they contain two components: sub bass content that provides physical weight on large systems, and mid bass harmonic content (100–300Hz) that provides presence on small systems.

The second fundamental: all bass content below 100Hz should be mono. Our ears cannot perceive stereo at these frequencies — the left and right channels simply sum. Stereo bass creates phase cancellation problems when summed to mono, which happens on club systems, many streaming platform normalizations, and virtually all phone speakers. Mono bass below 100Hz is not a creative limitation — it is a technical requirement for translation.

EQ: Frequency by Frequency

Step 1 — High-Pass Filter: 20–40Hz

Cut everything below 30–40Hz with a 12–24dB/octave high-pass filter. These sub-infrasonic frequencies are below the range of reproduction on any practical speaker, add no audible content, and waste headroom that the audible bass frequencies need. Every bass channel starts with this cut.

Step 2 — Fundamental Boost: 50–100Hz

The fundamental frequency of your bass — where its root note sits — lives here. For a bass guitar tuned to E standard, the open E sits at approximately 41Hz; the highest note on a 4-string sits around 330Hz. For an 808 sub, the note frequency determines where the fundamental is. Boost the fundamental 2–4dB with a broad bell to reinforce the body. Use a spectrum analyzer to identify where the fundamental is for your specific bass, rather than boosting at a fixed number.

Step 3 — Mud Cut: 150–400Hz

The 150–400Hz range is where bass gets muddy. Too much energy here makes the low end sound thick and indistinct — individual notes lose definition, the kick loses space, and the mix sounds heavy rather than powerful. Apply a gentle but meaningful cut (3–6dB with a moderate Q) somewhere in this range. The exact frequency depends on your specific bass and arrangement — sweep a narrow boost slowly through this range while listening; the frequency that makes it sound worst when boosted is the one to cut.

Step 4 — Presence Boost: 600Hz–1.5kHz

This is the bass's "growl" or "bite" range — the frequency content that carries through small speakers and makes bass audible when the sub can't be reproduced. A 2–4dB boost in this range makes bass present on laptop speakers and earbuds. For aggressive genres (metal, hard rock, trap), boost higher in this range (1–2kHz). For smooth genres (R&B, soul, jazz), keep it lower (600–800Hz) for warmth rather than bite.

Step 5 — High-Pass the Top End: 2–4kHz+

Roll off or cut the bass channel above 2–4kHz. Above this point, bass content only competes with guitars, keys, and vocals without contributing bass character. A gentle high-shelf cut cleans up the high-frequency content without affecting the bass's audible presence range.

Compression

Bass compression serves two purposes: consistent note-to-note dynamics (some notes on a bass guitar or bass keyboard are naturally louder than others) and sustain control (shaping how long each note rings out).

Bass guitar (fingerstyle)10–30ms80–150ms3:1–5:13–5dB
Bass guitar (pick / slap)5–15ms50–100ms4:1–6:14–6dB
808 / synth sub5–15ms100–300ms4:1–8:14–8dB
Synth bass (pluck)0–5ms80–200ms4:1–6:13–6dB

The release time on bass compression determines the pumping character. Short release (under 80ms) creates audible pumping on each note — sometimes intentional in electronic music. Longer release (100–300ms) creates smoother, more sustained compression that feels transparent. For bass guitar in rock or R&B, transparent compression is usually the goal. For 808 in trap, the pumping interaction with the kick is often an intentional part of the sound.

Parallel compression on bass: A classic technique — send the bass to a parallel compression return track compressed very hard (10:1 ratio, 10–15dB GR, medium attack), then blend this heavily compressed signal low under the main bass channel. The heavy compression brings up the quiet notes and sustain without squashing the loud notes and attack that go through the main channel. The blend adds density and consistency without obvious pumping.

Sidechain Compression: Kick and Bass

The kick drum and bass occupy the same low frequency range. On every beat in most genres, both the kick transient and the bass note hit simultaneously — two large low-frequency signals competing for the same acoustic space. Without management, this creates a congested, muddy low end where neither element has impact. Sidechain compression is the standard solution: the kick triggers a compressor on the bass, briefly ducking the bass on each kick hit so the kick's transient has space to be heard clearly.

Sidechain settings for kick-to-bass:

  • Attack: 0–5ms (instant response — duck immediately when kick hits)
  • Release: 50–150ms (controls how quickly the bass returns — shorter = tighter interaction, longer = audible pumping)
  • Ratio: 4:1 to 10:1 for electronic music; 2:1 to 4:1 for organic music
  • Threshold: Set so the kick triggers 3–8dB of gain reduction on the bass

The release time is the creative parameter. At 50ms the bass returns quickly and the effect is subtle — mainly giving the kick transient a cleaner window. At 150ms the bass ducks noticeably on each beat and returns slowly, creating the pumping rhythmic effect central to house and electronic music. Set to taste based on genre and feel.

Layering Sub and Mid Bass

The professional approach to bass in most electronic genres is to run two separate bass elements: a sub layer and a mid layer.

Sub layer: A pure sine wave or very low-pass filtered synth patch carrying the root note at 40–80Hz. This is the physical weight — felt on large speakers, inaudible on small ones. High-pass this layer above 30Hz and low-pass below 100–120Hz to keep it focused on the sub range only. Apply sidechain compression from the kick to the sub. Keep it mono.

Mid layer: A more complex bass sound — a distorted synth bass, a bass guitar, a Moog-style patch — carrying the harmonic content from 100–300Hz upward. High-pass this layer above 80–100Hz so it doesn't conflict with the sub layer. This is what translates to small speakers. Apply the presence boost EQ here (600Hz–1.5kHz). Can have gentle stereo width above 200Hz.

Mix the two layers together. The sub gives weight; the mid gives presence and translation. Adjust the balance by checking both on large monitors (where the sub should be prominent) and on laptop speakers (where the mid layer should carry the bass impression). On laptop speakers you should hear the bass clearly even though the sub is not reproduced — this is the mid layer working as intended.

Harmonic Saturation for Small Speaker Translation

Even without a dedicated mid layer, adding harmonic saturation to a sub-heavy bass creates overtones in the audible range (100–500Hz) that represent the bass on small speakers. Saturation distortion is even-order (second, fourth, sixth harmonics) — it adds harmonically related content above the fundamental that is audible on any speaker.

Apply saturation plugins to the bass channel after EQ and compression: Soundtoys Decapitator, FabFilter Saturn 2 in multiband mode (saturating only the high end of the bass without affecting the sub), or a tape saturation plugin. Drive until you can hear the harmonic character — the bass should sound slightly warm and present rather than thin and clean. Check again on laptop speakers: the saturated bass should be more audible than the unsaturated version.

Mono Compatibility Check

The most important quality check for bass is mono compatibility. Every professional mixing session includes a mono reference check. The process: click your DAW's stereo-to-mono button (or use a utility plugin), listen to your bass in mono, and compare it to the stereo version.

If the bass thins dramatically in mono, you have a phase problem. Common causes: stereo widening plugins applied below 100Hz, chorus or flanger effects on the bass without a low-pass limit, or poorly aligned parallel compression tracks with phase offset. Fix the cause rather than just fixing the symptom — if a stereo effect is creating phase issues, limit it to frequencies above 100Hz where stereo is perceptible.

Frequently Asked Questions

Why does my bass disappear on small speakers?

Sub frequencies below 80Hz aren't reproduced by small speakers. Add harmonic saturation to create overtones in the 100–300Hz range that small speakers can reproduce. Or layer a mid bass element above the sub to carry the bass presence on small systems.

Should I sidechain the bass to the kick?

Yes, in most genres. Sidechain compression ducks the bass briefly on each kick hit, giving the kick's transient space to cut through without competing with the bass in the same frequency range. Essential in electronic music; gentler in organic genres.

Should bass be mono or stereo?

Bass below 80–100Hz should be mono. Our ears don't perceive stereo at those frequencies, and stereo content in the sub range causes phase cancellation when summed to mono. You can have subtle stereo width in the mid bass harmonics above 100Hz.

How do I mix sub bass and mid bass together?

Run two separate layers: a sub layer (sine or filtered synth, below 100Hz, mono) for physical weight, and a mid layer (more complex bass sound, high-passed above 80Hz) for small speaker translation. The sub provides weight on big systems; the mid provides presence everywhere.

What EQ should I use on bass?

High-pass at 30–40Hz. Boost the fundamental (50–100Hz). Cut the mud (150–400Hz). Boost presence (600Hz–1.5kHz). High-pass the top above 2–4kHz. Adjust frequencies by ear using a spectrum analyzer and reference track comparison.

How do I check if my bass translates?

Four checks: sum to mono (bass should not thin dramatically), listen on laptop speakers (should still be present), compare to a reference track in your genre, and use a spectrum analyzer to compare low-end energy profiles.
