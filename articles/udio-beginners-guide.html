Udio AI Beginner's Guide: How to Use Udio to Make Music in 2026

Everything you need to know to start generating music with Udio — from account setup and prompting to audio conditioning, track extensions, and how Udio compares to Suno for different workflows.

By MusicProductionWiki · Updated May 2026 · 13 min read
Quick Answer: Udio is an AI music generator that creates full songs from text prompts, exports WAV files (unlike Suno's MP3), and offers strong genre fidelity with detailed audio conditioning controls. Free tier gives 100 credits/month. Paid plans start at $10/month for commercial rights. Udio excels at complex and niche genres, nuanced instrumentation, and instrumental-first workflows. It's slightly more technical than Suno but rewards detailed prompts and is the preferred tool among producers who care about audio quality.

What Is Udio AI?

Udio is an AI music generation platform developed by a team of former Google DeepMind researchers. It launched in public beta in April 2024 and rapidly established itself as the primary competitor to Suno in the AI music generation space. Unlike most first-wave AI music tools that produced instrumental loops or basic backing tracks, Udio generates complete, structured songs with vocals, harmonies, and layered instrumentation from text prompts.

What distinguishes Udio from its competitors is its emphasis on audio quality and genre authenticity. While Suno optimizes for accessibility and consistent output across a wide range of users, Udio rewards more technical prompting with output that demonstrates deeper stylistic understanding — particularly for music genres that require specific cultural or sonic nuance.

Udio exports WAV files rather than MP3, which is a significant practical advantage for producers who intend to work with the output in a DAW. WAV is an uncompressed format that preserves full audio fidelity and is the standard for professional mixing and mastering workflows. This single detail makes Udio the preferred tool among producers who treat AI generation as one stage in a larger production process.

Udio Plans and Pricing (2026)

Udio uses a credit system. Each song generation costs credits, with the exact credit cost varying by generation length and settings.

The free tier is more limited than Suno's — 100 credits per month versus Suno's 50 per day. For users primarily interested in exploring the tool, Suno's free tier is more generous. For users who take audio quality seriously and plan to use output in professional workflows, Udio's Standard plan at $10/month is the entry point worth the cost. The WAV export alone justifies it for producers who would otherwise need to convert audio files after downloading.

Udio Interface Overview

Udio's interface is more information-dense than Suno's, reflecting its positioning toward users comfortable with technical controls. The creation panel shows a text prompt field, tag chips, audio conditioning upload, and several toggles — all visible at once rather than hidden behind menus.

The Prompt Field and Tag Chips

The main text field accepts free-form prompt text. Below it, Udio displays suggested tag chips based on your input — clickable genre, mood, and instrument descriptors that are appended to your prompt automatically. These chips are one of Udio's most useful interface elements because they represent terms from Udio's internal vocabulary that the model responds to strongly. Clicking a suggested chip often improves output quality compared to writing the same term by hand, because the chip uses Udio's exact training terminology.

Audio Conditioning

The audio conditioning upload field appears directly in the creation panel on paid plans. You can upload any audio file (MP3, WAV, FLAC) as a reference, and Udio will use it to inform the style, texture, and energy of its generation. This is Udio's most distinctive feature and the one that produces the most reliably targeted results for specific sonic goals.

Lyrics Input

Like Suno, Udio allows you to input your own lyrics. In Udio, the lyrics field appears as a toggle-able panel below the main prompt. Udio processes lyrics input somewhat differently from Suno — it tends to be more literal about following lyrical content and less likely to improvise or substitute lines mid-generation. This can be an advantage (more precise lyrical control) or a limitation (less natural musical flow when lyrics are rigid).

Your Library and Discovery

Udio has a strong social discovery component. Generations from all users are surfaced in a public feed by default unless you mark them private. This community dimension is both a source of inspiration (you can see what others are generating and use it to improve your prompts) and a feature to be mindful of if you're generating commercial content you don't want publicly visible before release.

How to Write Udio Prompts

Udio prompting follows similar principles to Suno but with some important differences in how the model interprets input. The core framework remains: genre + mood + tempo + instrumentation + vocal style. But Udio's model tends to respond more strongly to technical music production terminology and less to casual descriptive language.

Udio Responds Well To

Specific subgenre terms (not just "jazz" but "West Coast cool jazz" or "late 1950s hard bop"), production-specific language ("dry room reverb," "808 sub bass," "vintage tape saturation"), instrument specificity ("nylon string acoustic guitar," "Fender Rhodes electric piano," "muted trumpet"), and explicit tempo ("92 BPM," "half-time feel at 130 BPM"). The more technically precise your Udio prompt, the more accurately the model can match your intention.

Using Negative Prompts

Udio supports negative prompting — terms you can add to steer the model away from unwanted elements. Add "no drums," "no vocals," "no reverb," or "avoid distortion" to exclude those elements from your generation. This control layer doesn't exist in Suno's standard prompt field and is a significant precision advantage for users who know what they don't want as clearly as they know what they do.

Audio Conditioning: Udio's Killer Feature

Audio conditioning is what separates Udio from every other AI music generator in terms of stylistic precision. The ability to upload a reference audio clip and have Udio match its style is fundamentally different from prompt-based control — it lets you communicate sonic intent through sound itself rather than through language.

What Audio Conditioning Does

When you upload a reference file, Udio's model analyzes it for stylistic features: the frequency balance, the reverb characteristics, the rhythmic pattern, the harmonic complexity, the tempo, and the overall sonic texture. It then uses this analysis to condition its generation — meaning the output will attempt to match those characteristics while still being original content (not a reproduction of the reference).

The effect is powerful in practice. Upload a track with a specific drum machine sound and room ambience, and Udio will generate music with comparable production aesthetics. Upload a reference with a specific guitar tone and chord progression style, and Udio's output will lean toward that harmonic and timbral territory.

Best Practices for Audio Conditioning

Use clean, well-mixed audio as your reference. Tracks with a lot of reverb, distortion, or vocal-heavy mixes can produce inconsistent conditioning results. Keep your reference clips to 15–30 seconds — Udio doesn't need a full song to extract stylistic information, and shorter clips process faster. Use instrumental references when targeting instrumental output; the model picks up on vocal characteristics from a reference even when you've also specified "instrumental" in the prompt.

Audio conditioning strength can be adjusted with a slider in the conditioning panel. Lower conditioning strength produces output that's loosely inspired by the reference; higher strength produces output more tightly bound to the reference's characteristics. For most creative uses, 40–70% strength gives the best balance between reference fidelity and original creativity.

Extending and Building Full Tracks

Udio generates approximately 30 seconds of audio per generation — shorter than Suno's 30–120 second outputs. This means building a full-length track requires more extension steps in Udio than in Suno, which is worth factoring into your credit planning.

The Extend Workflow

The Extend function in Udio works from the end of any generated clip, adding approximately 30 more seconds of audio in the same style. You can optionally modify the prompt or add new lyrics for the extended section, allowing you to evolve the song's direction. Udio's extensions tend to maintain strong musical coherence with the original clip — transitions between sections are generally smooth and stylistically consistent.

Intro Generation

Udio offers an Intro generation feature that doesn't exist in Suno. You can generate an intro for any existing clip — Udio analyzes the clip and creates a 15–30 second opening that leads naturally into it. This is valuable for building tracks that feel compositionally complete: an instrumental introduction that builds tension before the main musical idea arrives.

Song Structure Planning

Plan your track structure before starting to generate. A typical Udio approach to a 3-minute track: generate the core section (verse or chorus) first, identify the strongest clip, generate an intro for it, then extend into verse 2, extend into bridge, extend into final section, add outro. Six to eight credits and steps from blank page to complete structure. More steps than Suno but each gives precise control.

Udio vs Suno: Which Should You Use?

This is the most common question from new AI music creators, and the honest answer is that most serious users end up using both. They serve overlapping but distinct use cases.

Use Udio When

You're targeting a specific, niche, or technically complex genre where accurate sonic character matters. You want WAV files for use in a DAW mixing workflow. You have a reference track you want to style-match using audio conditioning. You're generating primarily instrumental content where Udio's superior instrumental output quality shines. You want negative prompting control to exclude specific sonic elements.

Use Suno When

You're a beginner and want the fastest path to a complete, polished-sounding track. You want to write your own complete lyrics using a structured Custom Mode. You need higher volume output at lower cost (Suno's free tier is more generous). You want consistent, radio-friendly vocal production. You're generating content for social media where quick turnaround matters more than audio quality precision.

The Combined Workflow

Many professional users generate initial concepts in Suno (faster, more consistent vocals for early-stage ideation), then use Udio for final versions where audio quality and genre specificity matter more. The tools are complementary rather than competitive at the level of serious creative production.

Downloading Udio Output

Udio outputs download as WAV files on paid plans — a significant practical advantage over Suno's MP3 exports. WAV files are uncompressed, preserving full audio fidelity and making Udio output directly usable in professional DAW workflows without conversion steps. Free tier users may receive MP3 exports on some generation types.

To download any generation, click the download icon on the generation card in your library or in the public feed. Your browser downloads the file immediately. Udio does not currently offer stem separation (separate vocal and instrumental tracks) on the same basis as Suno Pro — stem separation in Udio works differently and is handled through Udio's internal tools rather than a dedicated stems export feature.

Copyright and Commercial Use

Udio's commercial use terms are similar to Suno's: paid subscribers receive commercial rights to output they generate. Free tier output is restricted to personal, non-commercial use. The same legal grey areas around copyright ownership of AI-generated music apply to Udio output as to Suno — the contractual commercial license from Udio enables you to release and monetize, but does not constitute copyright registration with the US Copyright Office.

Udio was named alongside Suno in the RIAA's 2024 copyright infringement lawsuits regarding training data. The status of those suits as of mid-2026 continues to affect how the AI music industry talks about rights and ownership. Pro Tips for Better Udio Results

Use the community feed for prompt research. Browse Udio's public feed and click through generations in your target genre. You can see the prompts used for public generations — studying what prompts produce the outputs you're looking for is the fastest way to develop your own prompt skill.

Generate multiple variations of your best prompts. Like Suno, Udio is nondeterministic — the same prompt produces different results each time. Generate 3–5 variations from your strongest prompt and cherry-pick the best clip before extending. This raises your floor quality significantly.

Adjust seed for consistency. Udio allows you to set a specific generation seed number. If a generation produces an output you love, note the seed number from its metadata. Generating with the same seed and same prompt will produce the same output — useful for recreating a specific result exactly, or for sharing reproducible generation settings with collaborators.

Use short, precise prompts for instrumental content. For instrumental generation specifically, Udio rewards brevity and precision over long descriptive paragraphs. A tightly focused 30-word prompt with specific instrument and production terminology consistently outperforms a 200-word narrative description.

Make generations private if they're commercial. Udio's default is to share generations publicly in the community feed. If you're generating commercial content, toggle to Private before generating to prevent your output from appearing in the public feed before you're ready to release it.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is Udio AI?

Udio is an AI music generator that creates full songs from text prompts, similar to Suno. It launched in April 2024 and is known for strong genre fidelity, detailed audio conditioning features, and high-quality WAV audio output.

Is Udio free to use?

Udio offers a free tier with 100 credits per month. Paid plans start at $10/month for Standard (1,200 credits/month) and $30/month for Pro (4,800 credits/month), both with commercial rights included.

How is Udio different from Suno?

Udio tends to produce stronger genre fidelity — particularly for complex or niche genres — and offers more detailed audio conditioning controls. Suno is generally faster, easier for beginners, and produces more consistently polished vocals. Most serious AI music users maintain accounts on both platforms.

What is audio conditioning in Udio?

Audio conditioning lets you upload a reference audio clip that Udio uses to match the style, energy, and texture of your generation. It's Udio's most distinctive feature and produces the most precisely targeted stylistic results of any control mechanism available in current AI music tools.

Can Udio generate music without vocals?

Yes. Adding "instrumental" to your Udio prompt or using the Instrumental toggle suppresses vocal generation. Udio's instrumental output is widely regarded as one of its strongest features and is the primary reason many producers choose Udio over Suno for backing track generation.

How long are Udio generations?

Udio generates clips of approximately 30 seconds by default. You use the Extend feature to build these into longer tracks, adding sections one generation at a time. A complete 3-minute track typically requires 5–7 extensions from the initial clip.

What file format does Udio export?

Udio exports WAV files on paid plans — an uncompressed format suitable for professional mixing and mastering workflows. This is a significant advantage over Suno's MP3 export and a primary reason Udio is preferred by producers working in DAW-based workflows.

Can I use Udio music commercially?

Yes, on paid plans (Standard and Pro). Free tier output is limited to personal, non-commercial use under Udio's Terms of Service. The same copyright grey areas that apply to Suno apply to Udio — the commercial license from Udio enables release but does not constitute registered copyright ownership.

Practical Exercises

Beginner Exercise: Your First Udio Generation

Create a free Udio account. Browse the community feed for five minutes and find a public generation in a genre you like. Click through to see its prompt. Use that prompt as inspiration (not a copy) to write your own prompt for a different song in the same genre. Generate two variations. Compare them. Note what the prompt successfully communicated to the model and what was lost in translation. The goal is to develop prompt intuition by working with the community feed as a learning resource — something Suno doesn't offer in the same way.

Intermediate Exercise: Audio Conditioning Experiment

Upgrade to Udio Standard. Choose a piece of music you love — ideally an instrumental track where you want to capture the general production aesthetic, not copy the specific song. Export a 20-second clean clip of the track. Upload it as an audio conditioning reference in Udio. Write a prompt that matches the broad genre and mood without describing the specific song. Generate five variations at 50% conditioning strength. Then generate five more at 80% conditioning strength. Compare the results at each strength level against your reference track. Document what the audio conditioning successfully captured and what it missed. This exercise builds the intuition for using audio conditioning effectively across different reference types.

Advanced Exercise: Full Track from Scratch in Udio

Choose a target genre and write a detailed Udio-style prompt (see the prompt comparison table above for reference). Generate 6 variations and select the best clip. Generate an intro for it. Then extend it through a full song structure: intro, verse 1, chorus, verse 2, chorus, bridge, final chorus, outro. Import each WAV file into your DAW on a separate track. Arrange them in sequence. Add transitions, volume automation, and any additional production elements you want to contribute. Export the final mix. The goal is to complete the full Udio production pipeline from generation to finished DAW session, developing the workflow skills that make Udio genuinely powerful rather than just a generation toy.
