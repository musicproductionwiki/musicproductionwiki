How to Mix Music: Complete Beginner's Guide

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2025 22 min read

Mixing is the process of balancing and processing individual tracks in a recording to create a cohesive, clear, and emotionally compelling stereo or surround sound presentation. A well-mixed song sounds natural and effortless — every element has its place, nothing is fighting for attention, and the overall picture serves the music rather than drawing attention to the technical process. Getting there requires understanding both the tools and the decisions behind them.

What we'll cover: The mindset and workflow for approaching a mix, gain staging and why it matters, reference tracks, EQ for separation and clarity, compression for control and character, reverb and delay for space, stereo width and imaging, automation, and how to know when a mix is finished.

Mixing Mindset: What You're Actually Trying to Do

Before touching a single plugin, understand what mixing is and what it isn't. Mixing is not making sounds louder. It's not applying as many effects as possible. It's not a technical exercise in frequency management. Mixing is creating the best possible emotional experience from the raw recorded material — every decision serves that goal.

The three things a good mix achieves: clarity (every element can be heard and understood without confusion), balance (the relative levels of elements serve the song's emotional intention — the lead vocal is the most important thing, the hi-hat is not), and translation (the mix sounds good on studio monitors, consumer headphones, phone speakers, car stereos, and every other playback system the listener will use).

Before starting any mix, listen to the raw, unprocessed tracks from beginning to end at a moderate volume. You're listening for: what the arrangement is doing, what the most important elements are, what problems exist (noise, timing, pitch issues), and where the mix should go emotionally. A mix that doesn't serve the song is a technical exercise, not a craft.

Reference Tracks: Your Most Important Tool

A reference track is a commercially released song in the same genre that represents the sound quality and character you're targeting. Load it into your DAW at matched loudness and alternate between it and your mix throughout the session. Reference tracks provide: a reality check on your low-end balance (does your kick have the same weight as the reference?), a tonal benchmark (is your vocal as present as the reference vocal?), and a loudness target (how loud is the reference at the same perceived volume as your mix?).

Choose reference tracks that: were released within the last five years in your specific genre, are from artists your listeners would recognise as successful, and represent the specific emotional register of the song you're mixing (a dark trap banger references differently from an uplifting dance track). Load two or three references and cycle through them — different references reveal different aspects of your mix's strengths and weaknesses.

Match the loudness of your reference to your mix when comparing — louder always sounds better to human perception, and comparing at mismatched levels produces misleading impressions. Most DAWs allow loading a reference track on an aux return with its level matched to your mix's loudness using a loudness meter (Youlean Loudness Meter is free and excellent).

Gain Staging: The Foundation of Everything

Gain staging is the practice of setting levels correctly at every stage of the signal path so no stage clips or introduces noise, and so each processor in the chain receives an appropriate input level. Poor gain staging is the most common cause of mixes that sound noisy, distorted, or lose dynamic range through the processing chain.

The practical gain staging workflow: before inserting any plugins, set your track faders so that the mix bus meters peak at approximately -6 to -12 dBFS on the loudest moment of the song. Individual tracks should average around -18 to -12 dBFS with occasional peaks not exceeding -6 dBFS. If any track is much louder than this, turn it down at the track fader before the plugins — not by adding a gain reduction plugin or relying on processors to pull the level back.

Why this matters: every plugin in the chain receives an input signal. A limiter or compressor on an already-too-loud signal is doing more work than it should and producing more artifacts. An EQ on a signal with very high peaks can distort internally. Plugins designed for professional use are typically calibrated to work best at around -18 dBFS average — the standard professional recording level. Start here and you'll encounter fewer clipping and noise problems as the mix builds.

EQ: Creating Space and Clarity

EQ is the most-used tool in mixing. It removes frequency content that clutters the mix and enhances frequency content that serves the element's role. The most important principle: subtractive EQ (removing frequencies) is more powerful and more musical than additive EQ (boosting frequencies). Cut first, boost sparingly.

High-pass filtering: Apply a high-pass filter (low-cut) to every track that doesn't need sub-bass content — which is most tracks. Vocals: high-pass at 80–100 Hz. Guitars: high-pass at 80–120 Hz. Hi-hats and cymbals: high-pass at 200–400 Hz. Synth pads: high-pass at 80 Hz. This cleanup of unnecessary low-frequency content dramatically clarifies the low end and gives the kick and bass room to breathe. It's the highest-impact single EQ move in most mixes.

Cutting problem frequencies: Every recorded track has frequency ranges that are acoustically problematic — often caused by room resonances, proximity effect, or the specific instrument's acoustic properties. Common problems and their approximate frequency ranges: vocal boxiness (200–400 Hz), guitar harshness (2–5 kHz), bass muddiness (150–300 Hz), snare ring (500–900 Hz). Use a narrow Q (high Q value) to identify the problem frequency by boosting and sweeping, then switch to a cut when you find it. Cut with a medium-to-narrow Q to address the problem without affecting too much surrounding frequency content.

Frequency separation: When two instruments occupy overlapping frequency ranges, they compete in the mix — one obscures the other. The solution: EQ them apart. If the lead guitar and vocal both have prominent 3 kHz content, boost 3 kHz slightly on whichever is more important (the vocal) and cut 3 kHz on the other (the guitar). This separation creates perceived clarity even when the volume levels are the same. Frequency separation is the key technique for making busy arrangements sound open and clear rather than congested.

Adding character: After solving problems, add character sparingly. A high-shelf boost on the vocal (above 10 kHz) adds air and presence. A low-shelf boost on the kick (below 80 Hz) adds weight and fullness. A presence boost on an acoustic guitar (2–5 kHz) pushes it forward in the mix. Keep additions modest — 1–3 dB is typically sufficient. Large boosts introduce phase shift, increase the risk of intersecting with other elements, and often expose recording problems that are better solved at the source.

Compression: Control and Character

Compression reduces the dynamic range of audio — quieting the loud parts and allowing the average level to be raised. In mixing, compression serves two distinct purposes: control (keeping elements dynamically consistent so they don't jump in and out of the mix) and character (adding the punch, glue, and warmth that make sounds feel polished rather than raw).

Drums: Drum compression controls the wide dynamic range of live drum recordings and adds punch and power. On the kick drum: ratio 4:1, fast attack (2–3 ms, preserving the initial transient click), medium release (50 ms), 4–6 dB gain reduction. This tightens the kick's sustain while preserving its punch. On the snare: ratio 4:1, slightly slower attack (10 ms, allowing the initial snap through), short release, 4–6 dB gain reduction. On the overhead mics: light compression (2:1, 2–3 dB) to control the room's dynamic range without killing the cymbal's natural sustain.

Vocals: Two-stage vocal compression is the professional standard. First compressor: optical emulation (LA-2A style) at 2:1–3:1, 15–25 ms attack, auto release, 3–5 dB gain reduction. This smooths the overall performance level. Second compressor: VCA type at 3:1–4:1, faster attack and release, 2–4 dB gain reduction. Together they produce a controlled, polished vocal without the squashed quality of heavy single-stage compression.

Bass: Medium ratio (4:1), attack fast enough to catch the initial transient (5–15 ms), release set to musical tempo (auto works well), 4–8 dB gain reduction. Bass compression keeps the level consistent — crucial for maintaining a stable low-end foundation under the rest of the mix.

Mix bus compression: Light compression (2:1, 2–3 dB of gain reduction) on the mix bus adds cohesion — the elements feel like they belong together in the same space rather than existing on independent planes. Apply mix bus compression from the beginning of the mix session so you're hearing the compressed result as you make decisions, not adding it at the end and changing the balance you've built.

Reverb and Delay: Creating Space

Reverb and delay create the sense of acoustic space in a mix — placing sounds in virtual rooms, halls, and environments that give the mix depth and dimension. The key principle: shared reverb spaces create cohesion. When multiple elements share the same reverb return at different send levels, they sound like they're in the same acoustic environment.

Setting up reverb returns: Create two reverb return tracks — one with a short plate or room reverb (0.5–1.2 seconds decay), one with a longer hall reverb (1.5–3 seconds). Use the short reverb as a general mixing reverb for drums, guitars, and rhythm instruments. Use the long reverb for lead vocals, main instruments, and for creating spacious, emotional moments. Control the amount of each element in each reverb by adjusting the send levels from each track to the reverb return.

Pre-delay: Set pre-delay on every reverb return — typically 15–30 ms for the short reverb, 20–50 ms for the long reverb. Pre-delay creates a gap between the dry signal and the reverb onset, preserving the clarity and presence of the dry signal before the reverb adds space. Without pre-delay, reverb smears the dry signal and pushes elements backward in the mix.

Delay: A tempo-synced delay (eighth-note or dotted eighth-note) on the lead vocal adds rhythmic depth and width without sounding like obvious echo. Panning the delay return to the opposite side of the dry signal creates stereo width. Set the delay feedback to 2–3 repeats and the level low enough to be felt rather than heard as a distinct echo.

Stereo Width and Imaging

Stereo width is the perceived left-right spread of the mix. Elements panned to the centre (0°) feel present and grounded. Elements panned hard left or right create width and separation. The stereo field of a mix should be intentional: important elements centred, supporting elements spread, and the stereo width consistent across the frequency spectrum.

Panning basics: Centre: kick drum, bass, lead vocal, snare drum (it can be slightly off-centre). Left and right: rhythm guitars, keyboard pads, backing vocals, hi-hats. Use a graduated pan — not hard left/right for everything, but varying pan positions (30L, 50L, 70R, etc.) to create a natural stereo spread rather than a binary left-right split.

Mono compatibility: Check your mix in mono regularly (most DAWs have a mono sum button on the master output). Elements that are very wide (processed with stereo wideners or panned extremely hard) can disappear or cancel in mono — a problem when listeners hear the mix on mono speakers (smart speakers, phone speakers, some Bluetooth devices). The fundamental elements — kick, bass, snare, lead vocal — should be present and strong in mono. Width processing on other elements should survive mono without completely disappearing.

Stereo width processing: The Haas effect (a copy of a signal panned opposite the original with 20–40 ms delay) creates stereo width. Mid-side EQ (boosting the sides while leaving the mid unchanged) adds perceived width. Stereo wideners on individual tracks add width but risk mono compatibility problems. Use these tools on supporting elements, not on the fundamental rhythm and vocal elements.

Automation: Making the Mix Move

Automation is the process of programming parameter changes over time — levels that rise for the chorus, reverb that increases during the bridge, a filter that opens gradually during the intro. Automation transforms a static mix into a dynamic performance that responds to the song's emotional arc.

Volume automation: The most important and most used automation type. Draw volume automation on the lead vocal to maintain consistent presence throughout despite the performance's natural dynamic variation. Automate the master bus level for the final fade-out. Automate individual track levels between verse and chorus (typically +2–3 dB on rhythm elements in the chorus for energy increase). Automate specific moments that need attention — a phrase that disappeared into the mix, a word that was slightly too loud.

Effect automation: Automate reverb send levels to increase spatial depth in the chorus and reduce it in intimate verse sections. Automate a filter on a synth pad to open during the chorus and close during the verse. Automate delay feedback to increase during specific phrases for dramatic effect. These automations make the mix feel produced and intentional rather than static.

Clip gain vs track automation: Use clip gain (pre-fader level adjustment on individual clips or sections) for correcting performance-level inconsistencies — the guitarist played a phrase much louder than the rest. Use track automation for intentional, structural level changes — the chorus needs to be louder overall. Clip gain is surgical; track automation is structural.

Knowing When a Mix Is Finished

The most common mixing mistake is over-mixing — continuing to adjust after the mix has reached its best state, introducing new problems rather than solving existing ones. Signs a mix is finished: you're making changes and then immediately undoing them; you're adding processing to elements that already sound good; you've been in the session for more than three hours without a break.

The practical test: export the mix, load it in a different player or device, and listen without looking at the screen. Listen as a music fan, not as an engineer. If you can listen through without being pulled out of the music by technical problems, the mix is done or close to it. If something specific bothers you — the vocal is slightly buried in the chorus, the bass is too heavy — go back and address that specific issue only.

Take breaks. Return with fresh ears. Listen at low volume (if elements disappear at low volume they need more presence). Check on multiple playback systems. When you're satisfied on three different systems and a day after you finished, the mix is done.
