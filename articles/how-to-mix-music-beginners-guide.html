How to Mix Music: A Complete Beginner's Guide

Mixing is not magic. It is not an innate talent that some people are born with. It is a learnable skill built from a small number of core concepts applied consistently and intelligently. Here is everything you need to start.

MP The Music Production Wiki Team Updated April 2026 15 min read Beginner

Every record you have ever loved has been mixed. The process of mixing — combining individual recorded tracks into a single cohesive stereo file — is what separates a collection of sounds from a piece of music that moves people. And while the tools and techniques used by professional mixing engineers can seem impossibly complex from the outside, the underlying principles are simple enough to learn and start applying today. This guide covers all of them.

What Mixing Actually Is

Before anything else, let's be precise about what mixing means — because confusion about this causes enormous amounts of wasted effort for new producers.

Mixing is the process of combining multiple recorded tracks — drums, bass, guitars, keyboards, vocals, and everything else — into a single balanced stereo file called a mixdown. The mixing engineer's job is to make every element audible, give each element its appropriate space and weight in the sonic picture, and create a cohesive result where the whole is greater than the sum of its parts.

Mixing is not the same as mastering. Mastering is the subsequent process of preparing the finished mixdown for distribution — optimizing its loudness, ensuring it translates well across playback systems, and making final tonal adjustments. Mixing happens first. Mastering happens last. Confusing the two leads producers to try to master their mixes before the mix is finished, which creates problems that compound on each other.

Mixing is also not the same as producing. Production is the creative process of building and arranging the elements of a track — writing the parts, choosing the sounds, crafting the arrangement. Mixing is the technical and aesthetic process of making those elements work together sonically. They overlap, especially for self-producing artists, but they are distinct disciplines with distinct goals.

The Foundation: Gain Staging Before You Touch a Single Plugin

The single most important concept in mixing that nobody teaches beginners is gain staging — and the reason most amateur mixes sound muddy, distorted, or lifeless has nothing to do with plugins. It has everything to do with levels that were never set correctly before the mixing process began.

Gain staging means setting the level of every signal at every stage of your signal chain so that nothing clips, nothing is too quiet to use effectively, and your plugins receive signals in the range they were designed to process. In practice, this means starting your mix with every fader at unity (0dB) and adjusting the gain of each individual track — using the clip gain, the input gain, or a trim plugin — so that your mix bus peaks at around -18dBFS to -12dBFS during the loudest sections.

Why does this matter? Because every plugin in your DAW — compressors, EQs, saturators, limiters — was designed to receive a signal within a specific level range and behaves differently outside that range. Feed a compressor a signal that's too hot and it will behave unpredictably. Feed an EQ a signal that's too quiet and your boosts will introduce noise. Get your gain staging right before you open a single plugin and every subsequent mixing decision becomes more predictable and more effective.

Step One: Balance — The Most Underrated Mixing Skill

Before you reach for an EQ or a compressor, spend time getting your levels right. A surprisingly large percentage of what makes a professional mix sound professional is not processing — it's the relative level of each element in the mix. The kick drum sits at the right level relative to the snare. The bass sits at the right level relative to the kick. The lead vocal sits at the right level relative to everything else. These relationships are the architecture of your mix, and no amount of plugin processing will fix them if they're wrong.

Start with your most important element — usually the lead vocal or the kick drum, depending on the genre — and set it at a level that feels right in the context of the whole mix. Then build the rest of the mix around it. Ask yourself constantly: is this element too loud or too quiet relative to what matters most? Trust your ears. Move faders. Listen from the beginning of the track. Listen at low volume — a mix that sounds balanced at low volume is usually well-balanced. A mix that only sounds right at high volume has problems that loudness is masking.

The professional standard: mix at low to moderate volumes. Your ears make more accurate frequency judgments at lower volumes, and the Fletcher-Munson curve — the well-documented phenomenon of human hearing becoming more sensitive to bass and treble at higher volumes — means that mixing loud consistently produces mixes with too little low end and too little high end when played quietly.

Step Two: EQ — Subtracting Before You Add

EQ — equalization — is the tool that adjusts the frequency content of individual tracks. Most beginners use it to add: boost the bass to make it sound bigger, boost the high frequencies to add air and presence, boost the midrange to bring something forward. This instinct is wrong, and understanding why will immediately improve your mixes.

Professional mixing engineers subtract far more than they add. The reason is that every frequency band you boost on one track competes with the same frequency band on every other track. Boost the low midrange of your guitars and they'll fight with your keyboards for the same space. Boost the high frequencies of your synths and they'll clash with your cymbals. The result is a mix that sounds crowded, fatiguing, and confused — because everything is trying to be heard in the same space simultaneously.

The professional approach: use EQ primarily to remove frequency content that doesn't serve each element's role in the mix. High-pass filter everything that doesn't need low end — acoustic guitars, keyboards, room microphones, overhead cymbal microphones — to clear space for the kick drum and bass. Cut the frequencies where elements clash rather than boosting to compensate. Find the frequency range where each element lives and protect that space while clearing the frequency ranges where it competes with more important elements.

Once you've done the subtractive work, additive EQ becomes more effective and more targeted. A small boost on the lead vocal at 3–5kHz adds presence and intelligibility. A boost on the kick at 60–80Hz adds weight. A boost on acoustic guitars at 10–12kHz adds sparkle. These additions work because you've already cleared the space for them.

Step Three: Compression — Control Without Killing Dynamics

Compression is the most misunderstood tool in music production, used badly far more often than it's used well. Understanding what it actually does — and what it doesn't do — transforms how you use it.

A compressor reduces the dynamic range of a signal. When the signal exceeds a set threshold, the compressor reduces its gain by a ratio you specify. A 4:1 ratio means that for every 4dB the signal exceeds the threshold, only 1dB passes through. The result is a more consistent level — peaks are controlled, quiet passages are brought closer to loud passages, and the overall signal sits more consistently in the mix.

What compression does not do: make things sound louder (though the makeup gain applied after compression can restore level), fix a bad performance, or add character by itself (unless you're using a compressor specifically chosen for its harmonic character, like an 1176 or an LA-2A).

The most common beginner mistake with compression is over-compression — crushing the dynamics of a signal so completely that it loses the life and movement that made it interesting in the first place. A compressed vocal that never breathes sounds robotic. A compressed drum bus with no dynamic variation sounds mechanical. Compress enough to control the most problematic peaks and create consistency, then stop. The gain reduction meter is your guide — a few dB of gain reduction working musically is almost always better than heavy gain reduction working against the performance.

Starting settings for common sources:

Lead vocals: Ratio 3:1 to 4:1. Attack 10–30ms (let the initial consonants through before compression engages). Release 100–200ms (let the tail of each phrase recover naturally). Aim for 3–6dB of gain reduction on the loudest passages.

Kick drum: Ratio 4:1 to 6:1. Attack 5–20ms. Release 50–100ms. The goal is to control the peak transient without eliminating the punch that makes a kick drum feel physical.

Bass: Ratio 4:1 to 8:1. Slower attack (20–40ms) to preserve the initial pluck or attack of the note. Medium release (100–200ms) to let the note breathe between hits.

Step Four: Panning — Creating Width and Separation

Panning places elements in the stereo field — left, right, or anywhere between center and the extreme edges. Used intelligently, panning creates a sense of width and space that makes a mix feel immersive rather than flat. Used randomly, it creates an unbalanced mix that sounds unstable on different playback systems.

The professional approach to panning follows a few consistent principles. Keep the most important elements — lead vocal, kick drum, bass, snare — at or near center. These elements carry the most energy and need to be anchored in the middle of the stereo field for the mix to feel stable. Pan supporting elements — rhythm guitars, keyboard pads, backing vocals, percussion — away from center to create width without compromising the stability of the center image.

A technique used on virtually every professional record: double tracking. Record the same part twice — or copy a part and pitch-shift it subtly — and pan one version left and one version right. The slight differences between the two performances create a natural width that a single panned track cannot replicate. It's why guitars on rock records feel so wide and present simultaneously — they're almost never a single track panned to one side.

Step Five: Reverb and Delay — Space Without Mud

Reverb and delay are the tools that create a sense of space and depth in a mix. Used well, they make a mix feel three-dimensional — some elements close and dry, others further back and surrounded by space. Used badly, they make a mix sound muddy, cluttered, and like everything was recorded in a bathroom.

The most important rule for reverb: less than you think. Beginners consistently over-reverb their mixes because reverb sounds beautiful in isolation. The moment you put reverb in the context of a full mix, it fills frequency space and pushes elements backward. The professional approach is to use the minimum amount of reverb that achieves the spatial effect you're after, then automate it — increase reverb during quiet passages and instrumental breaks, reduce it during dense sections where clarity is paramount.

Use sends rather than inserting reverb directly on individual tracks. A send routes a portion of each track's signal to a shared reverb — so your drums, guitars, and vocals can all feel like they're in the same room because they're using the same reverb return. This creates cohesion and uses far less CPU than inserting individual reverb plugins on every track.

Step Six: Reference Tracks and Translation

The final and most important habit of professional mixing engineers: constantly reference your mix against commercial recordings in the same genre. Import a track you love into your DAW session and switch between it and your mix at matched levels. The differences you hear are your guide. If your mix sounds narrow where the reference is wide, address the panning and stereo processing. If your mix sounds muddy where the reference is clear, address the low-mid EQ. If your mix sounds harsh where the reference sounds smooth, address the high-frequency EQ and compression.

Also check your mix on multiple playback systems before calling it done. Earbuds, car speakers, laptop speakers, your phone — each reveals different problems. A mix that sounds good on all of them is a mix that translates. A mix that only sounds good on your studio headphones has problems that other systems will expose. Translation is the final test of a mix, and nothing replaces it.

The Most Important Mixing Advice We Can Give

Take breaks. Your ears fatigue faster than you realize, and fatigued ears make poor mixing decisions — particularly in the high frequencies, where tired ears consistently hear things as brighter than they are. Mix for 45 minutes. Take a 15-minute break away from speakers and headphones. Come back fresh. The decision you make in the first five minutes after a break is almost always better than the decision you would have made in the last five minutes before it.
