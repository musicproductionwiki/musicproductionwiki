How to Mix Music: Beginner's Guide

First published May 2026 · Updated May 2026
MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 Beginner → Advanced
Quick Answer

Mixing is the process of combining multiple recorded tracks into a balanced, cohesive final stereo track. It involves balancing levels, shaping tones with EQ, controlling dynamics with compression, creating depth with reverb and delay, positioning elements in the stereo field with panning, and adding movement with automation. A good mix makes every element clearly audible, creates the right emotional character for the song, and translates consistently across different playback systems.

Before You Touch a Fader Beginner

The most common mixing mistake is starting to mix before you've done the preparation that makes mixing possible. Preparation isn't exciting, but it determines whether your session is manageable or chaotic.

Organize your session first. Color code your tracks (drums in one color, bass in another, guitars, vocals, etc.). Name every track descriptively. Group similar elements together. Delete any unused takes or regions. A disorganized session creates cognitive overhead — every decision takes longer because you're also navigating chaos.

Gain stage before you touch EQ or compression. Every track should be at an appropriate level — peaking around -6 to -12 dBFS — before you add any processing. If tracks are hitting close to 0 dBFS, reduce the input gain or add a gain plugin at the top of the chain. This single step prevents most of the technical problems that plague home studio mixes.

Import a reference track. Put a commercially released, professionally mixed and mastered song in the same genre on a separate track in your session. Level-match it to your mix (both should play at roughly similar perceived volumes). Use this reference constantly throughout the mixing process to calibrate your expectations and identify where your mix differs from a professional standard.

Step 1: The Rough Balance Beginner

Start with no EQ, no compression, no effects. Just faders. Your goal: using only the volume fader on each track, create a version of the song where every element is audible and the overall feel is roughly right. This takes longer than it sounds and matters more than most producers realize.

Start with your most important element — typically the kick drum or the lead vocal depending on genre — and set it to a level around -18 to -15 dBFS. This is your reference point. Everything else gets balanced relative to this anchor. Add elements gradually: kick → snare → bass → other drums → guitars/keys → vocals → effects. Listen after each addition and adjust.

The rough balance reveals immediately which elements are fundamentally too loud or quiet in the raw recording, which ones need significant EQ work, and where the biggest mixing challenges will be. Many experienced mixers spend 30–60 minutes on the rough balance before touching anything else.

Step 2: EQ in Context Intermediate

EQ in the context of a full mix (not in solo) is the essential discipline that separates professional mixing from amateur mixing. Every EQ decision should be made while hearing the element in relation to everything competing with it.

The standard EQ workflow for mixing: apply a high-pass filter to every element that doesn't need sub-bass (everything except kick and bass). Start with corrective cuts — identify problem resonances, remove them with narrow cuts. Then make broad tonal shaping decisions — add warmth, presence, or air as needed. Save creative EQ for last.

The most important frequency relationships to manage: kick and bass in the 60–120Hz range (one element leads, the other follows); midrange competition between vocals and guitars in the 1–4kHz range (cut guitars where the vocal needs to live); high-frequency buildup across multiple elements (cymbals, guitars, pads, and reverbs all add energy above 8kHz — too much combined creates harshness).

Step 3: Compression for Density Intermediate

Most mixing compression is subtle — 2–4dB of gain reduction on most elements, more on dynamic sources like vocals and bass. The goal isn't to make things louder or to limit peaks. It's to control the dynamic range of individual elements so they sit consistently in the mix, and to add density and sustain that makes elements feel fuller.

Track-level compression goals by element: drums get a fast compressor (1176-style) to control transient peaks and add punch; bass gets moderate compression with auto-release to tighten the dynamic range between notes; lead vocal gets 3–6dB of compression to even out the performance without restricting emotional dynamics; guitars and keys typically need less compression than most producers apply.

Bus compression — a compressor on your drum bus, mix bus, or instrument groups — is where a lot of the "glue" in professional mixes comes from. A very gentle 1–2dB of bus compression (2:1 ratio, slow attack, moderate release) makes elements feel like they belong together rather than like isolated recordings stacked in a session.

Step 4: Space and Depth — Reverb and Delay Intermediate

Professional mixes have a front-to-back dimension — some elements feel close and present (kick drum, lead vocal), others feel further away (room reverb on drums, backing vocals), and others feel like they occupy a mid-distance (rhythm guitars, keys). This depth comes primarily from reverb and delay.

The standard mixing approach: use two or three shared reverb sends across the entire mix rather than individual reverb plugins on every channel. A short plate (0.6s decay) for snare and percussion; a medium hall (1.5s) as the main ambient environment; occasionally a long special reverb for specific dramatic moments. Route each element to these shared reverbs at different levels — drums get a small amount of the plate, vocals get a medium amount of the hall, etc. Everything shares the same acoustic environment, which makes the mix feel cohesive.

Delay is a rhythmic effect that works with tempo. Use tempo-synced delay (eighth notes, quarter notes, dotted eighths) to add space without cluttering the mix with non-rhythmic echoes. A quarter-note delay on vocals is a classic mixing technique — it adds space between phrases without audibly repeating the words.

Step 5: Panning — The Stereo Field Intermediate

Panning places elements left to right in the stereo field. The general rule: keep low-frequency elements (kick, bass, bass synths) at or near the center — panned bass is a classic amateur mixing mistake that causes problems on mono playback and creates an unbalanced sense of weight. Keep lead elements (lead vocal, lead synth, lead guitar) at or near center. Pan supporting elements (rhythm guitars, backing keyboards, hi-hats, shakers) to the left and right to create width.

The stereo image should feel balanced — roughly equal energy on the left and right side of the mix at any given moment. If your arrangement has a rhythm guitar on the left, something should fill a similar role on the right (another guitar, a keyboard pad, a shaker) to maintain balance.

Step 6: Automation — Making the Mix Move Advanced

Automation is what separates a mix that feels alive from a mix that feels static. Professional mixing engineers automate constantly — the level of the lead vocal rises slightly in the chorus, drops back in verses. The reverb sends increase for dramatic moments. The hi-hats pan slightly through the measure. These subtle, continuous adjustments create the sense of a mix that's emotionally engaged with the song.

Start automation with volume automation on the lead vocal. Draw in or record level adjustments so every part of the performance sits at the ideal level throughout the song — not just on average, but phrase by phrase. This single automation pass is often the biggest improvement in a mix. From there, automate reverb sends at key moments, automate filter sweeps on synthesizers, automate the appearance of delay tails at the end of phrases.

Step 7: Checking Translation Intermediate

A mix that sounds great on one system but terrible on another has a translation problem. Professional engineers check their mixes on multiple systems as a matter of course: studio monitors, consumer headphones, earbuds, a phone speaker, a car stereo if available. Each system reveals different problems — studio monitors might not reveal a bass imbalance that's obvious on consumer earbuds; studio headphones might make the high end feel brighter than it will on speakers.

The single most revealing check for translation: listen at very low volume (barely audible). Elements that are properly balanced will still be audible and in correct proportion at very low volumes. Elements that are over-reliant on sheer level to be heard will disappear or become unclear at low volumes. This is the Fletcher-Munson curve in action — our hearing's frequency sensitivity changes at different volumes, and a mix calibrated for one level will sound different at another.

📀 Record Reference

Billie Eilish — "bad guy" (2019), produced and mixed by Finneas O'Connell — This record is a masterclass in modern minimal mixing. Every element is given extreme space — the mix is essentially empty compared to most commercial pop productions, with individual elements occupying distinct spaces rather than stacking into a dense arrangement. The bass sits completely alone in the low end; the vocal is close and intimate; the percussion is crisp and defined. Study this mix specifically for how few elements there are and how deliberately each one is placed. Less is almost always more in mixing.
