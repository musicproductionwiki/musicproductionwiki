Best Audio Interfaces Under 100

Updated May 2026 — The sub-$100 audio interface market has matured dramatically. What used to mean compromised preamps, plastic build quality, and driver headaches now means genuinely capable recording hardware that can take your productions from bedroom demos to release-ready tracks. Whether you're recording vocals over beats, tracking a guitar, cutting podcast episodes, or building your first home studio, there's a sub-$100 interface that will serve you without embarrassing you.

This guide is for music producers, home recording engineers, and content creators who need real performance advice — not a rehash of spec sheets. Every interface here has been evaluated against three core criteria: audio quality (preamp noise floor, converter accuracy), workflow practicality (driver stability, DAW compatibility, bus power reliability), and value density (what you actually get for the money including bundled software). We've also noted where certain interfaces punch above their price and where they quietly cut corners you'll notice in six months.

Before diving into the picks, one framing note: the gap between a $80 interface and a $150 interface is meaningful, but the gap between a $80 interface and zero interface (recording directly into a laptop's built-in card) is enormous. If you're still using your MacBook's 3.5mm input or your PC's onboard Realtek sound card for anything serious, any interface on this list will be a transformative upgrade.

What Actually Matters in a Budget Audio Interface

Budget interfaces share similar-looking spec sheets, which makes comparison deceptively difficult. Here's what actually separates a great sub-$100 interface from a mediocre one:

Preamp Quality and Noise Floor

The preamp is the most expensive component in any audio interface. Budget units cut costs here first. When you see an Equivalent Input Noise (EIN) spec, lower numbers mean quieter preamps — pro-grade is around -128 dBu, while most budget interfaces land between -120 and -129 dBu. The practical implication: if you're recording a quiet acoustic guitar or a singer who doesn't project, a noisier preamp forces you to crank gain, which amplifies that noise floor into an audible hiss. For louder sources (electric guitar cabinet, loud vocalist, drum overhead close-miked), even mediocre preamps are fine.

Converter Quality and Bit Depth/Sample Rate

Every interface on this list records at 24-bit/48kHz minimum, and most go up to 192kHz. For practical home studio work, 24-bit/48kHz is more than sufficient — streaming platforms and most DAW workflows operate at 44.1kHz or 48kHz. The 192kHz spec is largely a marketing number for this price tier; the converters in sub-$100 interfaces aren't accurate enough to capture ultrasonic content meaningfully. What matters more is the dynamic range (measured in dB) — look for 100dB or above for clean recordings.

Latency and Driver Stability

USB audio latency is a real concern for producers who want to monitor their voice or instrument through the interface while recording. Most interfaces in this range offer direct monitoring — a hardware mix of your live input and your DAW playback — which bypasses latency entirely. On Windows, ASIO driver quality varies dramatically between manufacturers; on Mac, Core Audio handles most interfaces natively, reducing driver drama considerably. Behringer and M-Audio have historically had Windows driver issues; Focusrite has consistently been the most stable in this department.

Build Quality and Bus Power

Bus-powered interfaces (powered entirely by USB) are convenient but introduce a subtle risk: if your USB port is marginal (old hubs, power-limited ports on some ultrabooks), you may get dropouts or hum. Interfaces with external power adapters eliminate this but add a cable. Most sub-$100 interfaces are bus-powered; the better ones include a USB-C connection which provides more stable power delivery than legacy USB-A.

Bundled Software Value

At this price tier, the bundled DAW and plugins can genuinely double the value of your purchase. Focusrite bundles Ableton Live Lite plus a suite of plugins. PreSonus bundles Studio One Artist, which is arguably the best bundled DAW in the industry. These aren't demos — they're fully functional recording environments. If you're just starting out, checking out our guide to audio interfaces for beginners alongside this article will help you understand how the full ecosystem fits together.

Pro Tip: The 48V Phantom Power Test

If you plan to use a condenser microphone — including popular budget condensers like the Audio-Technica AT2020 or any large-diaphragm condenser — your interface must supply 48V phantom power. Every interface on this list provides it, but verify the phantom power switch is separate from the gain knob. Some ultra-budget interfaces (not on this list) implement phantom power via a hidden menu or firmware setting, which creates workflow friction during sessions. A dedicated physical button is the standard you should insist on at any price.

The Top Picks: Best Audio Interfaces Under $100 in 2026

1. Focusrite Scarlett Solo Gen 4 — Best Overall

The Focusrite Scarlett Solo Gen 4 is the default recommendation for a reason, and it's not just brand recognition. The fourth generation brought a genuinely redesigned preamp stage — the "Air" mode now engages an impedance-shifting circuit that adds a subtle harmonic presence boost modeled on vintage transformer-based preamps, which helps condenser microphones cut through on recordings without aggressive EQ. The EIN on the Gen 4 preamp is rated at -129 dBu, which is class-leading at this price point.

Practically, the Solo offers one XLR/TRS combo input (mic or line), one instrument input on the front, headphone output with its own volume knob, and stereo monitor outputs. The USB-C connection is a significant quality-of-life upgrade from Gen 3's micro-USB. Converter quality is rated at 119dB dynamic range on inputs, which is excellent for the price — you'll hear the difference versus cheaper alternatives when recording anything dynamic.

The bundled software package includes Ableton Live Lite, a suite of Focusrite Red plug-ins, and access to Splice samples. If you're new to production and want to get started immediately, this bundle genuinely has everything you need. For a deeper look at the hardware, our Focusrite Scarlett Solo review covers the Gen 4 preamp improvements in detail.

Who it's for: Singer-songwriters, home studio beginners, podcasters, bedroom producers who record one source at a time.

Price: $99 street price (frequently discounted to $89)

Pros: Best preamp quality at this price, rock-solid drivers on both Mac and Windows, USB-C, Air mode is genuinely useful for condensers, excellent bundled software.

Cons: Only one mic input limits simultaneous recording; no MIDI I/O; monitor outputs are unbalanced on the Solo (balanced on the 2i2).

2. Focusrite Scarlett 2i2 Gen 4 — Best Two-Input Under $100 (Sometimes)

The Scarlett 2i2 Gen 4 typically hovers around $119-$129, but it frequently goes on sale below $100, particularly during holiday periods and back-to-school promotions. If you catch it on sale, it's the best value on this list by a significant margin. Two independent preamps (both with the Gen 4 preamp circuit and Air mode), balanced monitor outputs, a headphone output, and the same USB-C connection as the Solo. The addition of a second input means you can record a vocalist and a guitarist simultaneously, or dual-mic a guitar cabinet, or run a stereo keyboard rig directly.

The 2i2 Gen 4 also adds independent direct monitoring with a mono/stereo toggle, which is a meaningful workflow upgrade when recording two sources. Our Scarlett Solo vs 2i2 comparison breaks down every practical difference between the two units if you're deciding between them.

Who it's for: Anyone who occasionally records two sources simultaneously, producers who want balanced outputs for studio monitors, duos and small band tracking sessions.

Price: $129 (regular) — watch for sales below $100

Pros: Two preamps, balanced outputs, superior headphone output vs Solo, best-in-class drivers, Air mode on both channels.

Cons: Usually above $100 at full price; no MIDI I/O; slightly bulkier than Solo.

3. PreSonus AudioBox USB 96 — Best for Studio One Users

The PreSonus AudioBox USB 96 has held its position as a legitimate budget contender for years, largely because of one thing: it ships with Studio One Artist, which is a full-featured DAW that competing bundles don't match. Studio One's workflow — particularly its drag-and-drop plugin chaining and consolidated mixing environment — is genuinely easier for beginners to learn than Ableton Live's session/arrangement split-view paradigm.

The AudioBox USB 96 itself is a two-channel interface with two combo XLR/TRS inputs, MIDI I/O (a rare feature at this price), headphone output, and USB-A connectivity. The hardware is notably sturdy — the metal chassis has survived more studio backpack drops than its price would suggest. The preamps are competent rather than exceptional; EIN is around -129 dBu, competitive with the Scarlett Solo but without any equivalent of Air mode.

The 96kHz sample rate support (hence the name) is functional, and 24-bit/96kHz is a legitimate working resolution for some post-production workflows. MIDI I/O makes this the only interface on this list that lets you connect a hardware synthesizer or external MIDI controller without a separate USB-MIDI adapter — a meaningful advantage if you own any vintage gear.

Who it's for: Producers who want MIDI connectivity, Studio One users, home studio setups with hardware synths, anyone who values the bundled DAW over interface aesthetics.

Price: $79 street price

Pros: MIDI I/O, excellent bundled Studio One Artist DAW, metal chassis build quality, two inputs, competitive preamps.

Cons: USB-A only (no USB-C), slightly higher latency than Scarlett on some systems, headphone amp is the weakest link on the unit.

4. Behringer UMC202HD — Best Budget Two-Input Value

The Behringer UMC202HD is the most polarizing recommendation on this list, and understanding why requires nuance. The preamps in the UMC202HD are based on Midas circuits — Behringer acquired Midas (the respected British console manufacturer) and licensed the preamp topology into their budget interfaces. The result is preamp quality that meaningfully outperforms what the price suggests. Side-by-side with a Focusrite Scarlett, the Midas preamps have a slightly warmer character — less hyped in the upper midrange — which some producers prefer for recording guitar amplifiers and acoustic instruments.

The unit offers two combo XLR/TRS inputs, two balanced outputs (on TRS jacks), headphone output, 192kHz sample rate support, and a direct monitoring mix knob. Build quality is where Behringer's cost-cutting shows: the knobs feel plasticky and the chassis is lighter than the PreSonus. Driver stability on Windows has historically been the other weakness — check your OS version against Behringer's driver support page before purchasing.

At $59, the UMC202HD is roughly 40% cheaper than the Scarlett Solo while offering two preamp channels. That math is hard to ignore if you're on a strict budget and need two inputs.

Who it's for: Budget-conscious producers who need two channels, users who prefer a warmer preamp character, secondary or travel interface.

Price: $59 street price

Pros: Midas preamps for the price, two channels, balanced outputs, extremely affordable, 192kHz support.

Cons: Build quality feels budget, historical Windows driver instability, no MIDI I/O, USB-A only.

5. M-Audio Air 192|4 — Best for Producer Workflows

The M-Audio Air 192|4 is positioned as the producer-friendly option in this price range, and it earns that positioning through two specific features: the dedicated instrument input with a high-impedance (HI-Z) circuit optimized for direct guitar and bass recording, and the Pro Tools | First bundled license. If you're a guitarist who records direct into amp simulation plugins — using something like a neural processing amp sim or a traditional convolution IR loader — the HI-Z quality on the Air 192|4 is noticeably better than what generic instrument inputs on budget interfaces provide.

The interface itself is compact and USB-C equipped, with two combo inputs, monitor outputs, and a headphone output. At $89, it competes directly with the Scarlett Solo. The preamp quality is slightly behind Focusrite in measurable terms, but the HI-Z instrument circuit is better suited for direct electric guitar recording. For a deep dive into guitar-specific interface considerations, our guide to audio interfaces for guitarists covers high-impedance circuit design in more detail.

Who it's for: Electric guitarists and bassists recording direct, producers who want Pro Tools | First in their bundle, bedroom producers who record one instrument at a time.

Price: $89 street price

Pros: Excellent HI-Z instrument input, USB-C, Pro Tools | First bundle, compact footprint.

Cons: Preamps slightly behind Scarlett in noise performance, M-Audio support has been inconsistent historically, no MIDI I/O.

6. MOTU M2 — Premium Budget Pick (Frequently Under $100 on Sale)

The MOTU M2 retails for around $169 but has been available for $99 on sale regularly through Amazon and Guitar Center in 2025-2026. If you see it at or below $100, it's the best audio interface in this price range without qualification. MOTU's M2 features the lowest measured latency of any USB interface in this category (round-trip latency of 2.5ms at 96kHz with 32-sample buffer), the best headphone amplifier (capable of driving high-impedance headphones like the Beyerdynamic DT 990 Pro properly), an integrated hardware metering display with level meters on the front panel, and genuinely high-quality preamps with -131 dBu EIN.

The M2 also supports loopback recording natively — a feature useful for streamers, podcasters, and producers who need to route internal audio. The build quality is substantially better than anything else at this price tier: a solid metal chassis with a clean industrial design. For producers doing critical monitoring work, the headphone amp on the M2 is the only budget interface that can properly drive planar magnetic headphones or high-impedance reference cans.

Who it's for: Producers who want the absolute best performance at the sub-$100 price point (when on sale), anyone with high-impedance headphones, streamers who need loopback.

7. Audient EVO 4 — Best for Clean, Simple Workflow

Audient is a British console manufacturer whose full-size desk products cost tens of thousands of dollars. The EVO 4 is their entry-level interface, and the preamp quality shows the heritage. The EVO 4's Audient console-mic preamp circuit delivers -129 dBu EIN with a transparency and headroom that makes it feel like a more expensive product. It's a two-channel interface with USB-C connectivity, and the front panel features a single large knob (the "Smarter Gain" feature) that automatically detects whether you've plugged in a microphone, instrument, or line-level source and adjusts impedance accordingly.

Where the EVO 4 wins is in the headphone output (slightly better amp than the Solo) and the auto-impedance-detection feature, which reduces setup friction. Where it loses is in driver stability history on Windows and the bundled software package, which is less comprehensive than Focusrite's offering.

Who it's for: Producers who want high-transparency preamps, users who record a variety of sources (mic, guitar, synth) and want automatic impedance handling, Mac users where driver differences matter less.

Price: $99 street price

Pros: Console-heritage preamp design, auto-impedance detection, USB-C, excellent headphone output, clean industrial design.

Cons: Windows drivers less stable than Focusrite historically, bundled software less comprehensive, Smarter Gain feature occasionally misidentifies source impedance.

Matching the Right Interface to Your Use Case

The best audio interface isn't a universal answer — it depends heavily on what you're actually recording. Here's a use-case breakdown to help you make a final decision:

For Vocalists and Singer-Songwriters

If you're recording your own vocals over beats or as a solo artist, the Focusrite Scarlett Solo Gen 4 is the clear choice. The Air mode is specifically beneficial for condenser microphones recording voice — it adds clarity and presence without requiring you to EQ the track aggressively later. The single-input limitation is not a real constraint when you're working alone. Pair it with a large-diaphragm condenser in the $100-$200 range and you have a recording chain that can produce commercially competitive vocal tracks.

For vocal recording technique and treatment, our guide on recording vocals in a home studio covers mic placement, room treatment, and gain staging in detail — all of which interact directly with the interface you choose.

For Electronic Music Producers

If you're primarily working in a DAW with software instruments and only occasionally record external sources (a synthesizer output, a sample you're chopping, a vocal one-shot), almost any interface on this list will work. The key spec to prioritize is the monitor output quality — because you're listening back through the interface's DAC more than you're recording through its preamps. The MOTU M2 wins here when on sale, followed by the Scarlett 2i2 Gen 4 for its balanced outputs. The PreSonus AudioBox USB 96's MIDI I/O is also relevant if you have a hardware synthesizer you want to sequence from your DAW. For electronic music production workflows, see our home studio audio interface guide for a broader perspective on how interfaces fit into a full production setup.

For Guitarists Recording Direct

Direct guitar recording requires a high-impedance instrument input — a true HI-Z circuit that properly loads the guitar's passive pickups without high-frequency rolloff. All interfaces on this list have instrument inputs, but quality varies. The M-Audio Air 192|4 has the best HI-Z implementation in this price tier. The Focusrite Scarlett Solo is a very close second. Avoid connecting a passive guitar directly to the XLR mic input (some beginners do this with an XLR-to-TS adapter) — it won't properly load the guitar and the tone will sound thin and wrong.

For Podcasters and Content Creators

Podcast recording has specific needs: stable operation with multiple USB devices, reliable direct monitoring so you can hear yourself without latency, and ideally a second input for an interview guest. The PreSonus AudioBox USB 96 and Behringer UMC202HD both offer two channels at prices that leave budget for a good dynamic microphone (the Shure SM7B or Electro-Voice RE20 are common professional choices, though both require a clean preamp with adequate gain — the Focusrite Gen 4 preamps handle these better than the Behringer on high-gain demands). For podcast recording workflows, our podcast recording guide covers the complete setup from interface choice through post-production.

For Bands and Multi-Instrument Home Recording

With a budget capped at $100, you have two channels maximum. For a full band recording, this means you're recording one or two elements at a time and building up the arrangement in layers — which is actually how most home studio rock records are made. The Scarlett 2i2 (when on sale below $100) or the PreSonus AudioBox USB 96 give you the most flexibility. If you need to go beyond two channels simultaneously, you're looking at stepping up to the Focusrite Scarlett 4i4 or 18i8, which takes you above the $100 ceiling.

Setting Up Your Interface for Best Performance

Getting your interface out of the box and set up correctly has a significant impact on your actual recording experience. Here are the optimization steps that apply regardless of which unit you chose:

Driver Installation and ASIO Buffer Settings

On Windows, always install the manufacturer's ASIO driver before plugging in the interface. Connecting a USB audio device before driver installation can cause Windows to load generic WDM drivers, which increases latency and can cause instability. After installation, open the ASIO control panel and set your buffer size. For recording (especially with direct monitoring disabled), start at 128 samples and go up to 256 or 512 if you experience crackling. For mixing without live input, 512-1024 samples is fine and reduces CPU load. On Mac, Core Audio requires no driver installation for most interfaces on this list.

Gain Staging

Proper gain staging at the interface level is critical and frequently misunderstood. Your preamp gain knob should be set so your loudest signal peaks at around -18 to -12 dBFS on your DAW's input meter. Recording too hot (peaking near 0 dBFS) gives you a falsely loud reading that compresses the dynamic range and risks digital clipping. Recording too quietly means you lose resolution in the quiet parts of the performance. The sweet spot varies slightly by source — drums and loud guitars can hit -12 dBFS peaks comfortably, while quiet acoustic instruments and voice in a soft passage should sit around -18 dBFS to leave headroom for dynamics.

Direct Monitoring and Latency

Turn on direct monitoring (the hardware blend of your input signal with DAW playback) when recording performances where you need to hear yourself in real time. The direct monitor signal is zero-latency — it bypasses the analog-to-digital conversion, USB transmission, DAW processing, and digital-to-analog conversion cycle that creates audible delay. Disable direct monitoring when you want to hear yourself through your DAW with effects processing applied — and in this case, keep your buffer size at 64-128 samples to keep latency below 10ms.

Using Your Interface with iOS and iPad

Most USB-C interfaces on this list (Scarlett Solo Gen 4, EVO 4, M-Audio Air 192|4, MOTU M2) work with iPhone and iPad via a USB-C to USB-C cable (iPad Pro and current iPad Air models) or USB-C to Lightning adapter (older devices). This opens up recording with iPad DAWs like GarageBand or Cubasis — a legitimate mobile production setup. Not all interfaces pass iOS compatibility testing; Focusrite and Audient have the most reliable iOS support documentation of the brands on this list.

Common Mistakes to Avoid When Buying a Budget Interface

After helping hundreds of producers set up their first studios, these are the mistakes that waste money and cause frustration most often:

Buying an Interface Before a Microphone

The interface is only one part of your recording chain. A sub-$100 interface connected to a mediocre microphone will sound worse than the same interface with a good mic. If your total budget is $150, consider spending $50 on the interface (the Behringer UMC202HD at $59 is fine) and $100 on the best microphone you can afford. The microphone has a larger impact on recorded tone than the interface at this price tier.

Ignoring Computer Compatibility

Before purchasing, verify that your operating system version is supported by the interface's drivers. Manufacturers publish compatibility lists, and there's often a lag between a new OS release (a new Windows or macOS version) and updated drivers. Focusrite is notably fastest to update drivers for new OS releases; some smaller manufacturers take 3-6 months. If you just upgraded your OS and a deal seems too good to be true, check driver compatibility first.

Expecting 192kHz to Sound Better Than 48kHz

The 192kHz spec is marketed aggressively on budget interfaces, but the practical audio quality improvement for music production is marginal to nonexistent. Converters in sub-$100 interfaces are not accurate enough to resolve the theoretical benefits of higher sample rates. More importantly, recording at 192kHz quadruples your file size and strains your CPU during playback — most DAWs run their internal processing at 44.1 or 48kHz anyway. Record at 24-bit/48kHz and spend the freed CPU resources on more plugins and virtual instruments.

Not Testing the Headphone Output

If you mix primarily on headphones (a common setup when you're starting out or don't have treated monitoring space), the headphone amplifier quality matters enormously. Budget interfaces vary widely here — the MOTU M2 has the best headphone amp, followed by the Audient EVO 4 and Focusrite Scarlett 2i2 Gen 4. The Behringer UMC202HD has the weakest headphone amp on this list. For headphone-based mixing, check our guide to the best headphones for mixing to understand how interface output quality interacts with headphone impedance and sensitivity.

Overlooking the Bundled Software

The bundled DAW and plugin packages are real, usable software — not stripped demos in most cases. If you're choosing between two similarly specced interfaces, the software bundle can legitimately tip the decision. Focusrite's bundle (Ableton Live Lite, Loopmasters samples, Focusrite Red plugins) and PreSonus's bundle (Studio One Artist) are both excellent. Studio One Artist is particularly notable because it has no track limit and includes the basic mixing environment you need for complete productions.

When to Upgrade and What Comes Next

A sub-$100 interface is not a permanent solution for everyone, but it can last years in the right workflow. Here's how to recognize when you've outgrown your entry-level interface:

You've outgrown your interface when you consistently need more than two simultaneous inputs — for example, recording a live drumkit, a band rehearsal, or a podcast with multiple guests. At that point, you're looking at four-to-eight channel interfaces in the $200-$400 range, such as the Focusrite Scarlett 4i4 Gen 4 or the Focusrite 18i8. If you're finding preamp noise becoming audible on your recordings — particularly when recording quiet instruments with sensitive condenser microphones in a treated room — that's also a signal to upgrade. Finally, if you're doing critical mastering work or professional mixing, the D/A converter quality in a sub-$100 interface will eventually become the weakest link in your monitoring chain.

For context on what a step up looks like, our best audio interfaces under $200 article covers the next tier, where you get significantly improved preamps, balanced monitor outputs on all channels, more I/O, and in some cases dedicated DSP for onboard effects processing. The jump from sub-$100 to the $150-$200 tier is one of the best value-per-dollar upgrades in home studio gear.

Practical Exercises

Beginner Exercise

Gain Staging Practice Session

Connect a microphone to your new interface and record a short vocal passage at three different gain settings: too low (signal peaks at -30 dBFS), correct (peaks at -18 to -12 dBFS), and too hot (peaks above -6 dBFS). Import all three takes into your DAW and compare the noise floor and dynamic feel of each recording. This single exercise will permanently improve every recording session you run going forward.

Intermediate Exercise

Direct Monitor vs DAW Monitoring Latency Test

Record a rhythmic source (hand clap, vocal chop, drum pad hit) while monitoring through your DAW at 256-sample buffer, then again with direct monitoring enabled on your interface hardware. Align both recordings in your DAW timeline and zoom into the waveform — measure the offset in milliseconds between the two takes. This gives you a real-world measurement of your system's round-trip latency and helps you decide when to use direct monitoring versus DAW monitoring in your workflow.

Advanced Exercise

Preamp Character Shootout Across Interfaces

If you have access to two different interfaces (borrow a friend's if needed), record the same source — ideally an acoustic guitar with a consistent strumming pattern at the same dynamic level — through both preamps at identical gain levels verified by a reference tone. Null test the two recordings in your DAW by inverting the phase of one track and summing them; any remaining signal reveals the sonic difference between the two preamp circuits. Analyze the spectral content of the difference signal to understand precisely where each preamp's character lives in the frequency domain.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ Is a $100 audio interface good enough for professional-quality recordings?
Yes — modern sub-$100 interfaces like the Focusrite Scarlett Solo Gen 4 offer 24-bit/192kHz conversion and preamps with -129 dBu EIN, which is more than sufficient for professional home studio recordings. The limiting factor at this price tier is usually the room acoustics and microphone, not the interface itself.
FAQ Do I need phantom power in my audio interface?
If you plan to use a condenser microphone, yes — condenser mics require 48V phantom power to operate, and all interfaces on this list provide it. Dynamic microphones like the Shure SM7B or SM58 do not require phantom power, though engaging it accidentally won't damage a dynamic mic in most cases.
FAQ Can I use a budget audio interface with an iPad or iPhone?
Most USB-C interfaces on this list (Scarlett Solo Gen 4, Audient EVO 4, M-Audio Air 192|4, MOTU M2) work with iPad via USB-C to USB-C cable on current iPad models. Older Lightning-port devices need a USB-C to Lightning adapter. Always check the manufacturer's iOS compatibility documentation before purchasing specifically for mobile use.
FAQ What is the difference between the Focusrite Scarlett Solo and Scarlett 2i2?
The Scarlett 2i2 adds a second microphone preamp input, balanced monitor outputs (vs unbalanced on the Solo), and slightly better headphone output. The Solo has one XLR mic input and one instrument input. If you ever need to record two sources simultaneously or want balanced monitor connections, the 2i2 is worth the extra cost — especially when it goes on sale near the $100 price point.
FAQ Why does the Behringer UMC202HD have Midas preamps if it's so cheap?
Behringer acquired Midas (the British console manufacturer) in 2014 and licensed the Midas mic preamp circuit topology into their budget interfaces. While the UMC202HD's preamps are genuinely Midas-designed circuits, the surrounding components, build materials, and quality control are at a budget level — which is how the price stays at $59.
FAQ How do I reduce latency when recording with a USB audio interface?
The most effective approach is to use your interface's direct monitoring feature, which routes the input signal to your headphones at the hardware level with zero latency, bypassing the DAW entirely. If you need to hear DAW-processed sound, reduce your ASIO buffer size to 64-128 samples and ensure no other applications are using audio in the background.
FAQ Is the PreSonus AudioBox USB 96 the only budget interface with MIDI I/O?
Among the mainstream sub-$100 interfaces, the PreSonus AudioBox USB 96 is unusual in offering dedicated 5-pin DIN MIDI in and out ports. Most other interfaces at this price use USB MIDI only (requiring a separate MIDI interface for 5-pin DIN connectivity), which matters if you own vintage synthesizers, hardware drum machines, or any MIDI device without USB.
FAQ Should I buy the cheapest interface possible and save money for plugins and microphones?
Generally, yes — the law of diminishing returns strongly favors spreading your budget across your signal chain rather than concentrating it in the interface. A $60 Behringer UMC202HD plus a $100 microphone will produce better recordings than a $160 interface paired with a free built-in laptop mic. The interface is often the least important variable in your recorded sound quality at the entry level.
