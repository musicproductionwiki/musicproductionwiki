Music Copyright and Fair Use Explained

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2025 11 min read
Legal Disclaimer

This article provides general educational information about copyright law. It is not legal advice. Copyright law is complex and jurisdiction-specific. Consult a qualified attorney for guidance specific to your situation.

Copyright is the foundation of the music business — it determines who owns what, who gets paid, and who can legally use a piece of music. Producers who don't understand copyright basics make expensive mistakes: releasing music with uncleared samples, covering songs without mechanical licenses, or failing to register their own works properly. This guide covers the essential copyright concepts every producer needs to know.

What we'll cover: What copyright protects in music, the two copyrights in every recording, how copyright registration works, sampling law, cover song licensing, fair use in music, and common mistakes that cost producers money.

What Copyright Protects

Copyright in music protects original creative expression fixed in a tangible form. "Fixed in a tangible form" means written down, recorded, or stored — a melody you hum but never record or write down is not protected by copyright. "Original creative expression" means something you created that reflects your creative choices, not something purely factual or functional.

Copyright does not protect: chord progressions (harmonic structures are not protectable — the I-IV-V progression belongs to no one), scales and keys, tempo, rhythm patterns in isolation, or musical styles and genres. This is why a song can legally use the same chord progression as a famous hit without infringement — the specific melody, lyrics, and sound recording are protected, but the underlying harmonic structure is not.

Copyright in music specifically protects: the melody (the sequence of pitches), the lyrics, and the specific arrangement of musical elements in a composition. Separately, it protects the sound recording — the specific recorded performance captured on a particular track.

The Two Copyrights in Every Commercial Recording

Every commercially released song contains two separate copyrights: the composition (also called the musical work) and the sound recording (also called the master or phonorecord). These are owned by different parties and generate different types of royalties.

The composition copyright covers the underlying song — the melody and lyrics. It is typically owned by the songwriter(s) or assigned to a music publisher. When someone streams your song, covers your song, or syncs it in a film, the composition copyright generates royalties collected by PROs and licensing entities.

The sound recording copyright covers the specific recorded version of the song — the actual audio file or recording. It is typically owned by the recording artist or assigned to a record label. The sound recording copyright is separate from the composition — a cover of your song does not require a license from the sound recording copyright holder, only from the composition copyright holder.

This distinction matters practically: when you sample a recording, you need to clear both the composition copyright (a license from the publisher or songwriter) and the sound recording copyright (a license from the label or artist who owns the master). Both clearances are required, and either party can refuse or charge whatever they choose.

Sampling Law

Sampling — using a portion of an existing recording in a new work — requires clearing both the composition and the sound recording copyright of the sampled material. There is no de minimis exception in US copyright law for sampling — no fixed number of seconds or bars that can be sampled without permission. Even a very short sample requires clearance if it is recognisable from the original.

The landmark Bridgeport Music v. Dimension Films (2004) established in the Sixth Circuit that any digital sampling of a sound recording, no matter how brief, constitutes infringement without a license. While this decision doesn't bind all US circuits, it set a practical industry standard: clear all samples, regardless of length.

Sample clearance costs vary enormously. A sample from an unknown independent artist might be cleared for a modest flat fee. A sample from a major label artist or famous song can cost tens of thousands of dollars in upfront fees plus ongoing royalty splits. Many clearance requests are refused entirely. This is why "sample-free" beats and interpolations (re-recording the melody without using the original recording) are common in commercial production — they avoid the sound recording copyright clearance requirement.

Cover Song Licensing

Recording and releasing a cover of someone else's song requires a mechanical license for the composition copyright. Unlike sampling (where the rights holder can refuse any license), mechanical licensing for covers is governed by compulsory license provisions in US copyright law — once a composition has been commercially released, any artist can record a cover version and release it commercially by obtaining a mechanical license and paying the statutory mechanical rate.

The statutory mechanical rate in the US is set by the Copyright Royalty Board. For physical and permanent digital downloads, the rate is 9.1 cents per copy sold for songs under 5 minutes. For streaming, the rate structure is more complex and split between interactive and non-interactive streaming services.

Services that handle mechanical licensing for cover recordings include Harry Fox Agency (now part of Songfile), DistroKid's cover song licensing feature, and Easy Song Licensing. These services automate the process of identifying the rights holders and paying the statutory rate. You do not need to contact the original artist or their publisher directly for a standard cover version — the compulsory license system handles it.

Important: a cover song license gives you the right to record and distribute your cover. It does not give you the right to use the original recording or to create a music video — video synchronisation requires a separate sync license from the publisher, which is not compulsory.

Fair Use in Music

Fair use is a legal doctrine that allows limited use of copyrighted material without permission under specific circumstances. In US copyright law, four factors determine whether a use qualifies as fair use: the purpose and character of the use (commercial vs. educational, and whether it's transformative), the nature of the copyrighted work, the amount of the work used, and the effect on the market for the original work.

Fair use in music is narrow and unpredictable. Parody — using copyrighted material to comment on that specific material — has been successfully defended as fair use (the 2 Live Crew case involving a parody of Roy Orbison's "Oh, Pretty Woman"). Commentary and criticism may qualify. Purely educational uses in non-commercial contexts may qualify.

Fair use is not a safe harbour for producers who want to sample without paying. It is a defence that must be argued in court after being sued, not a pre-clearance exemption. The most important factor — market effect — typically works against producers who sample commercially, because the sample arguably substitutes for a license the rights holder could have granted. Do not rely on fair use as a sampling strategy in commercial music production.

Copyright Registration

Copyright exists automatically when a work is created and fixed — you do not need to register to have copyright protection. Registration with the US Copyright Office provides significant additional benefits: it creates a public record of your claim, allows you to sue for infringement in federal court, and (if registered before infringement or within three months of publication) entitles you to statutory damages and attorney's fees rather than just actual damages.

Registration costs $45-65 for a single work or $85 for a group registration of multiple unpublished works filed together. Given that statutory damages in copyright cases can reach $150,000 per infringement for willful infringement, registration costs are trivial relative to the legal protection they provide. Register commercially important works at copyright.gov.

Common Producer Mistakes

Releasing music with uncleared samples is the most expensive common mistake — it can result in takedowns, legal action, and demands for a share of all revenue generated. Not registering works means losing the ability to claim statutory damages if someone infringes your copyright. Using music found on YouTube or social media without verifying licensing — much content on these platforms is not licensed for commercial use despite appearing accessible. Assuming that changing a sample "enough" makes it legal — there is no legal threshold for what constitutes enough change to a sample to avoid infringement.
