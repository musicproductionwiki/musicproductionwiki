How to Make Trap Beats: Complete Beginner to Advanced Guide

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2025 10 min read

Trap beat production has defined mainstream hip-hop and influenced virtually every other popular music genre since the early 2010s. The sonic signature — rolling hi-hats, booming 808 bass, snapping snares, minor key melodies — is immediately recognisable and widely copied. Making trap beats that sound professional rather than generic requires understanding both the technical fundamentals and the creative decisions that separate distinctive beats from template beats.

What we'll cover: The essential elements of trap production, hi-hat programming and rolls, 808 bassline design and tuning, snare and clap layering, kick patterns, melody and chord construction in trap, mixing the key elements, and how to develop a recognisable sound.

DAW and Essential Tools

The most used DAWs for trap production are FL Studio (Metro Boomin, Southside, Wheezy, Zaytoven all use FL), Ableton Live (increasingly common for hybrid trap production), and Logic Pro. FL Studio's step sequencer and piano roll are particularly well-suited to trap's pattern-based programming. All three DAWs produce professional results — the tool matters less than the knowledge of how to use it.

Essential plugins for trap: a sampler or drum machine plugin for layering drum sounds (Native Instruments Battery, XO by XLN Audio, or your DAW's built-in sampler), a synthesizer for 808 bass (Serum handles 808 design well; many producers use real 808 samples pitched to key), a piano or keys plugin for melodies (Nexus, Omnisphere, or sampled pianos are common), and a basic mixing chain (EQ, compressor, limiter).

Sample packs vs. original sound design: many professional trap producers use high-quality sample packs for drums and melodic elements, chopping and layering rather than synthesising from scratch. Using commercial sample packs intended for this purpose (not unlicensed samples of released music) is standard professional practice in trap production. Sound kits sold by established producers often include the specific drum sounds associated with their style.

Hi-Hat Programming: The Heartbeat of Trap

Trap hi-hat patterns are the most distinctive element of the genre and the area where individual producer identity is most apparent. Understanding the building blocks allows you to develop patterns that feel musical rather than mechanical.

The basic trap hi-hat framework: Start with a 16th note hi-hat grid. Trap hi-hats typically operate at 130–160 BPM, with the hi-hat pattern often programmed at a half-time feel — the kick and snare feel like they're at 65–80 BPM while the hi-hats operate at 130–160 BPM. This creates the characteristic spacious, slow groove with frenetic hi-hat activity above it.

Hi-hat rolls: The defining trap hi-hat technique is the roll — rapid consecutive hi-hat hits that accelerate into a phrase. Rolls are typically programmed by subdividing the hi-hat grid: 8th notes become 16th notes, 16th notes become 32nd notes or 64th notes for the fastest rolls. In FL Studio, the step sequencer allows fractional step sizes. In Ableton and Logic, the piano roll handles subdivisions through note length adjustments.

Velocity variation: Mechanical, equal-velocity hi-hats sound programmed. Accenting different hits — making every other 16th note slightly louder, or creating velocity patterns within rolls — humanises the pattern. Ghost hi-hats (very low velocity hits) between the main hits add texture without cluttering the pattern. Vary velocity on every hi-hat event rather than leaving them all at 100.

Open and closed hi-hats: Alternating between closed hi-hat (short, tight) and open hi-hat (sustained, washy) adds dynamic interest and is a standard trap technique. Open hi-hats are particularly effective at the end of phrases before the snare, creating a brief wash that sets up the snare hit.

Hi-hat sound selection: Crisp, clear hi-hat samples with defined attack and minimal sustain work best for the tight, punchy trap sound. Avoid samples with too much room sound or excessive high-frequency content. Layer two hi-hat samples — a bright, clicky sample and a slightly darker one — slightly offset for a thicker, more textured sound.

808 Basslines: Tuning and Design

The 808 bass drum — originally from the Roland TR-808 drum machine — is the foundation of trap's low-end character. A properly tuned, properly mixed 808 is the difference between a professional-sounding trap beat and an amateur one. This element receives more attention from professional producers than any other.

Tuning the 808: The 808 must be tuned to the key of the track. An untuned 808 clashes with the melody harmonically — the collision between the melody's notes and the 808's fundamental frequency creates dissonance that signals an unfinished, amateur production. Most 808 samples are tuned to a specific root note (typically C or G) — identify the sample's root note and pitch it to match each note in your bass pattern.

Programming the 808 pattern: The 808 pattern typically follows the root note of the chord progression, changing pitch with the chords. The most impactful 808 hits are on beat 1 and beat 3 (or in half-time, beats 1 and 2 of a two-bar pattern). Long, sustained 808 notes — pitching down or sliding between notes — are characteristic of trap's bass design. Use portamento (pitch sliding between notes) for the signature trap bass slide effect.

808 length and envelope: Trap 808s are typically long — two to four beats — not short like a conventional kick drum. The sustained note allows the harmonic and sub-bass content to develop over the length of the phrase. Use a long sustain in the sampler envelope rather than a short decay-gated sample.

808 layering: Layer the 808 with a short kick drum sample that provides the click and attack the 808's slower attack lacks. The kick handles the transient punch; the 808 handles the tonal body and sub-bass. Crossfading between the kick's attack and the 808's sustain using volume automation or envelope settings produces the characteristic trap kick-808 sound.

808 distortion and saturation: Applying mild saturation to the 808 adds harmonic content above the fundamental frequency, making the 808 audible on speakers and headphones that can't reproduce sub-bass (anything below 40–60 Hz). Saturation plugins (Decapitator, Saturn, or a simple tape saturation) on the 808 channel, with the drive set to add 2nd and 3rd harmonics without destroying the smooth character, are standard in professional trap mixing.

Snare and Clap Layering

Trap snares snap, crack, and cut through dense mixes. The characteristic sound comes from layering rather than using a single sample. A typical trap snare consists of three to five layered elements: a tight, punchy snare sample (the body), a sharp clap sample (the attack and crack), a snappy rim or stick sample (upper frequency definition), sometimes a noise burst (adds white noise transient for extra brightness), and sometimes a low-end thud (a very short, pitched sub hit to add weight).

Each layer is EQed and balanced within the stack: the snare sample contributes the body (200–600 Hz); the clap contributes the crack (2–5 kHz); the rim adds definition above 5 kHz. High-pass filter every layer to remove any conflicting low frequencies, then blend to taste. The combined snare stack should hit hard, ring briefly, and snap clearly without being harsh.

Snare placement: Trap snares typically fall on beats 2 and 4 in 4/4 time, but with notable variation. Half-time trap (snare on beat 3 only, or beats 2 and 4 in a half-time feel) creates a heavier, more spacious groove. Snare rolls — rapid snare hits before the main snare — add energy to transitions.

Kick Drum Patterns

Trap kick drums are punchy, short, and sub-heavy. Unlike hi-hats, kick patterns in trap are relatively simple — the 808 bass provides the harmonic and dynamic interest in the low end, so the kick mainly serves a rhythmic and transient function. Common kick patterns: on beat 1 alone (simplest, most spacious), beats 1 and 2.5 (syncopated pattern that creates momentum), or a more complex syncopated pattern with additional kicks on the "and" of 2 and 3.

The kick sound: short attack, minimal sustain, with a punch centered around 60–100 Hz and click at 3–5 kHz. The 808 handles the sub-bass, so the kick's low end primarily needs to complement the 808's attack without competing with its sustain.

Trap Melody and Chord Writing

Trap melodies are predominantly minor key, often pentatonic or with minor scale embellishments. The emotional vocabulary of trap leans toward the dark, melancholy, triumphant, and menacing — major key melodies are less common but appear, especially in more melodic sub-genres like emo rap and melodic trap.

Common scales: Natural minor (Aeolian mode) for dark, direct melodies. Harmonic minor for more exotic, dramatic qualities (the raised 7th creates a characteristic tension). Minor pentatonic (five-note subset of minor) for simpler, more loop-friendly melodies. Phrygian mode for the most ominous, threatening character.

Instrument choices: Piano samples (especially prepared piano or slightly detuned pianos for a woozy quality), flute samples (common in drill and trap hybrids), strings (lush string pads under the main melody), and synthesizer leads (Serum, Nexus, Omnisphere). Brass — trumpet, trombone — appears in southern trap variants. Music box or bell sounds add an eerie, unsettling quality.

Melodic structure: Keep melodies short and repetitive. A 2-bar or 4-bar melodic loop repeated throughout the beat is standard. Variation comes from layering — adding a counter-melody, introducing a new instrument in the second half — rather than changing the core melody.

Mixing Trap Beats

Mixing trap requires balancing the 808's dominance in the low end with the hi-hats and snare's presence in the high end, while keeping the melody elements audible in between. Key mixing decisions:

808 and bass EQ: High-pass everything that isn't the 808 above 80–100 Hz. The 808 should own the sub-bass region exclusively. Saturation on the 808 to add mid-range harmonics. Sidechain compression from the kick to the 808 for clean kick-808 interaction (or simply program them so they don't clash in time).

Hi-hat brightness: Trap hi-hats are bright and crisp. Don't be afraid of high frequency content on hi-hats — a high-shelf boost above 10 kHz adds the shimmer and air that makes them cut through. High-pass below 500 Hz to remove any low-frequency content from the hi-hat channel.

Snare presence: The snare stack should sit prominently in the mix at around -6 to -9 dBFS peak. A transient shaper on the snare bus (increase attack, decrease sustain) sharpens the snap without additional volume. Light parallel compression on the snare bus adds density and punch.

Loudness targeting: Most distributed trap beats target -9 to -11 LUFS integrated loudness, with a limiter ceiling at -0.3 to -1 dBTP. This is louder than the streaming platform normalisation targets, which means Spotify and YouTube will turn the master down to approximately -14 LUFS — but for beats intended for rap vocals, the louder master is appropriate for the mixing session context.
