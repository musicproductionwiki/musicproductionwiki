How to Distribute Your Music in 2026: Every Platform Compared

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated April 2026 13 min read Beginner

Getting your music onto Spotify, Apple Music, Tidal, Amazon Music, and every other streaming platform requires a distributor — a company that acts as the intermediary between you and the platforms, delivering your audio files and metadata, collecting your royalties, and paying them back to you. The distributor you choose affects how much of your money you keep, how quickly you get paid, what tools you have access to, and — in ways that most producers don't think about until it's too late — who owns what rights to your music. This is a decision worth understanding before you make it.

What a Music Distributor Actually Does

Streaming platforms do not accept music directly from independent artists. Spotify, Apple Music, Tidal, and their peers have agreements with a small number of licensed distributors who are authorized to deliver content to their platforms. When you release music through a distributor, the distributor delivers your audio files, metadata — your song title, artist name, album art, ISRC codes, and all associated information — to every platform you've selected, monitors your streams and downloads, collects the royalties those platforms owe you, and pays those royalties into your account on a schedule that varies by distributor.

What a distributor does not do, in most cases: collect your publishing royalties. Streaming generates two types of royalties — master royalties (paid to whoever owns the recording) and publishing royalties (paid to whoever owns the composition — the melody and lyrics). Most distributors collect master royalties on your behalf. Publishing royalties — mechanical royalties from streaming, performance royalties from broadcast — require separate registration with a Performing Rights Organization (PRO) like ASCAP, BMI, or SESAC in the United States, and often a publishing administrator like Songtrust or DistroKid's Songwriter portal to collect mechanical royalties from streaming platforms. This distinction costs independent artists significant money every year because nobody explains it clearly when they sign up for distribution.

The Revenue Share Question: What You Actually Keep

Distributor pricing models fall into two categories: annual subscription with 100% royalty retention, and free or low-cost tiers with revenue share. Understanding the mathematics of each model for your specific release volume determines which is more economical for your situation.

An annual subscription model charges you a flat fee — typically $20–50 per year — regardless of how much music you release, and retains 0% of your royalties. Everything the platforms pay goes directly to you. For producers who release music consistently — multiple singles per year, EPs, albums — this model is almost always more economical than revenue share, because the subscription cost becomes smaller per release the more you release.

A revenue share model charges you little or nothing upfront and takes a percentage — typically 15–30% — of every royalty payment you receive. For producers who release very little music or who are testing the waters, this model has lower risk — if the music doesn't generate royalties, you haven't spent money on a subscription. But for any producer generating meaningful streaming income, revenue share is significantly more expensive than subscription over time.

DistroKid — The Speed and Volume Choice

DistroKid is the most popular music distributor among independent artists for a reason: it is fast, inexpensive, and designed for producers who release music frequently. The basic plan — currently $22.99 per year — allows unlimited releases to all major platforms with 100% royalty retention. No per-release fees. No revenue share. Unlimited singles, EPs, and albums for a flat annual subscription.

DistroKid's turnaround time is among the fastest in the industry — releases typically appear on Spotify and Apple Music within 2–5 days. Its dashboard is clean, its royalty reporting is clear, and its additional features — HyperFollow landing pages, leave-on streaming when you cancel, collaboration splits — add genuine value beyond basic distribution.

The limitations: DistroKid's customer support has historically been its weakest point — response times can be slow and resolution of complex issues inconsistent. Its publishing royalty collection — through its Songwriter portal — is available but requires an additional annual fee. And if you cancel your DistroKid subscription, your music is removed from streaming platforms unless you pay for the leave-on feature or migrate to another distributor — a dependency that some producers find uncomfortable.

Who DistroKid is best for: producers and artists who release music regularly and want the lowest annual cost per release with maximum royalty retention. The volume model — unlimited releases for one annual fee — rewards consistent output and makes it the most economical choice for active independent artists.

TuneCore — The Artist-First Legacy Choice

TuneCore has been distributing independent music longer than most of its current competitors and has built a reputation for reliable royalty collection, thorough platform coverage, and artist-friendly policies. Its pricing model has evolved over the years — the current structure charges per release rather than a flat annual fee, which makes it more expensive per release than DistroKid for high-volume releasers but more transparent for artists releasing only a few projects per year.

TuneCore's publishing administration — TuneCore Publishing — is one of its strongest differentiators. It collects both master and publishing royalties in territories around the world, addresses the gap that costs many independent artists significant income, and does so within the same platform you're already using for distribution. For songwriters who want comprehensive royalty collection without managing multiple accounts across different services, TuneCore's integrated approach has genuine value.

TuneCore's analytics are among the most detailed available from any distributor — granular data on streams by territory, platform, time period, and demographic. For artists making decisions about where to invest their marketing efforts, this data is actionable in ways that less detailed analytics are not.

Who TuneCore is best for: songwriters who want integrated publishing administration alongside distribution. Artists who value detailed analytics. Independent artists releasing music at a pace where per-release pricing is not significantly more expensive than a subscription model.

CD Baby — The Comprehensive Legacy Distributor

CD Baby was founded in 1998 — before streaming existed — and has distributed music for longer than any other independent distributor. That history gives it relationships, processes, and reach that newer distributors are still building. It distributes to over 150 platforms and stores, offers physical distribution for artists who still sell CDs and vinyl, and provides publishing administration through CD Baby Pro.

CD Baby's pricing model takes a one-time fee per release — currently $9.95 for a single, $29 for an album — plus a 9% commission on royalties. This model is different from both DistroKid's subscription and TuneCore's per-release annual fee — you pay once per release forever, but CD Baby keeps a percentage of everything you earn on that release indefinitely.

The 9% commission is CD Baby's most significant disadvantage for successful artists. An artist generating $1,000 per month in streaming royalties pays CD Baby $90 every month forever, on music they paid to release years ago. For artists with catalog that generates ongoing income, this adds up to a meaningful sum over time.

Who CD Baby is best for: artists who want physical distribution alongside digital. Artists with a long-term catalog strategy who value CD Baby's longevity and reach. Artists who prefer a one-time payment model over annual subscriptions.

UnitedMasters — The Brand Partnership Choice

UnitedMasters occupies a different position in the distribution market than its competitors. Founded with backing from Alphabet (Google's parent company) and with partnerships with major brands including the NBA, ESPN, and others, UnitedMasters offers distribution alongside a brand partnership marketplace — a platform where artists can connect with brands looking for music for commercials, content, and campaigns.

Its free tier offers distribution with a 10% revenue share. Its SELECT tier — $5 per month — offers 100% royalty retention and access to the brand partnership features. For artists who see sync and brand licensing as a meaningful revenue stream, UnitedMasters' brand marketplace differentiates it meaningfully from pure distribution plays.

Who UnitedMasters is best for: urban and hip-hop artists who want access to brand partnership opportunities alongside distribution. Artists for whom sync licensing potential is a primary consideration. Artists who want free distribution and are comfortable with a 10% revenue share at low streaming volumes.

Amuse — The Free Distribution Option

Amuse offers free music distribution with no annual fee and no revenue share on its basic tier — a proposition that sounds too good to be true but is legitimate with important limitations. Its free tier distributes to major platforms, retains 100% of royalties, and charges nothing. The catch: slower delivery times than paid competitors, fewer platforms than comprehensive paid services, and limited customer support.

Amuse's paid tiers — Boost and Pro — add faster delivery, more platforms, and additional features. Its artist development programs — Amuse has signed artists directly to record deals based on streaming performance data — make it an interesting option for artists who want distribution and a potential path to label attention.

Who Amuse is best for: artists who are truly starting out and want to test the market without financial commitment. Artists who are comfortable with slower release timelines and limited support in exchange for zero cost.

The Metadata Issue Nobody Talks About

Regardless of which distributor you choose, the most important thing you will do at the point of release is not choose the right platform — it is enter your metadata correctly. Metadata is the information attached to your release: song title, artist name, featured artists, producers, songwriters, ISRC codes, UPC codes, genre, language, explicit content flag, and more.

Incorrect metadata costs independent artists money every single day. A song released without the correct songwriter credits cannot have its publishing royalties collected. A song released with a misspelled artist name creates a separate artist profile on streaming platforms, splitting your audience. A song released without proper ISRC codes may not be trackable across platforms, creating royalty collection gaps that are difficult to fix retroactively.

Spend the time to get metadata right before you release. Check every field. Confirm that your artist name is spelled exactly as it appears on your existing releases. Register your songs with your PRO before releasing. Enter your songwriter splits if you collaborated. The five minutes this takes before release prevents problems that can take months to resolve after the fact.

The Recommendation

For most independent artists releasing music consistently: DistroKid at $22.99 per year is the most economical choice. Unlimited releases, 100% royalty retention, fast delivery, and a clean dashboard. Register separately with a PRO and consider DistroKid's Songwriter portal for publishing royalty collection.

For songwriters who want integrated publishing administration: TuneCore's comprehensive royalty collection approach justifies its higher per-release cost. The value of collecting publishing royalties that would otherwise be lost often exceeds the cost difference versus DistroKid.

For artists just starting out with zero budget: Amuse's free tier gets your music on streaming platforms with no financial risk. When your releases start generating meaningful income, migrate to a paid service with better features and support.

The One Thing Most Artists Miss

Your distributor collects your master royalties. Nobody automatically collects your publishing royalties unless you take separate action. Register with ASCAP or BMI before you release your first song. Register your songs with your PRO after signing up. Consider a publishing administrator like Songtrust to collect mechanical royalties from streaming. The publishing royalties sitting uncollected because independent artists don't know about this system represent hundreds of millions of dollars globally every year. Make sure none of that money is yours.
