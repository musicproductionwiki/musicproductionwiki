How to Make Lo Fi Beats

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 · 23 min read · Verified May 7, 2026
Quick Answer — Updated May 2026

Lo-fi beats are built on four pillars: slow tempos (70–90 BPM), jazz-influenced extended chords (7ths and 9ths), humanized drums with swing, and analog texture effects like vinyl crackle and tape saturation. You don't need expensive gear — set your DAW to 75–85 BPM, program a simple kick-snare pattern with swing, layer warm jazz chords, and apply a low-pass filter and vinyl noise to finish the aesthetic.

Lo-fi has grown from an internet niche into one of the most searched and streamed genres in music production. YouTube's lo-fi radio channels have accumulated billions of listens. Lofi Girl's 24/7 stream regularly attracts millions of concurrent listeners. Spotify's lo-fi playlists generate consistent streams that accumulate meaningful royalties for producers who understand the genre. And unlike many electronic genres, lo-fi is genuinely accessible — you do not need an expensive sample collection, a complex synthesizer rig, or years of theory to make a convincing lo-fi beat from scratch.

What you do need is an understanding of what makes lo-fi sound like lo-fi. The genre has a specific set of characteristics — tempo, harmonic language, drum feel, texture processing — that define it as a recognizable aesthetic rather than just quiet music. This guide covers all of them in detail, from setting your tempo through to applying the final texture effects that give a lo-fi beat its characteristic warm, worn sound. Updated May 2026.

What Makes Lo-Fi Sound Like Lo-Fi

Lo-fi — short for low fidelity — is a production aesthetic that intentionally embraces the sonic characteristics of older, degraded recordings. It draws from the sound of vinyl records (surface noise, gentle distortion, frequency rolloff at the extremes), tape recordings (warmth, slight saturation, occasional pitch wobble), and early hip-hop sampling (grit, texture, the feel of music that has been recorded, played back, and re-recorded through multiple generations of media).

The genre combines these vintage textures with jazz-influenced harmony and relaxed, swinging rhythms to produce music that feels warm, nostalgic, and undemanding — perfect for studying, working, or relaxing. The imperfection is intentional and essential. A lo-fi beat that is too clean, too bright, or too perfectly on-grid stops feeling like lo-fi and becomes something else entirely.

The five core characteristics of lo-fi music are:

  • Slow, relaxed tempo — 70–90 BPM, with 75–85 BPM as the classic sweet spot
  • Jazz-influenced extended chords — 7ths and 9ths rather than basic triads
  • Humanized, slightly swinging drums — acoustic sample character, slight grid imprecision
  • Warm, low-pass filtered frequency response — rolls off the harsh high frequencies of digital production
  • Texture layering — vinyl crackle, tape noise, ambient room sounds, rain samples

Understanding these five elements is the foundation of everything that follows. Each one reinforces the others. A lo-fi beat that nails the chords but has machine-perfect drums and no texture processing will still feel hollow. A beat with perfect texture but wrong harmony will feel misplaced. The magic is in the combination.

The four interlocking pillars of a lo-fi beat. Each element reinforces the others — the genre sound emerges from their combination, not any single ingredient.

Step 1 — Set Your Tempo and Project

Open your DAW and set the project tempo between 70 and 90 BPM. For a classic chill lo-fi hip hop feel, 75–85 BPM is the sweet spot — slow enough to feel relaxed but with enough momentum to hold attention. Going slower than 70 BPM tends to feel sluggish rather than chill. Going faster than 90 BPM starts to lose the genre's characteristic laid-back quality.

Set your time signature to 4/4. Most lo-fi music is in straight 4/4. If you want to explore later, 3/4 and 6/8 produce interesting variations, but they are not where you should start when learning the genre.

Enable a swing or shuffle setting if your DAW has one. Around 50–60% swing adds the gentle push-pull feel that makes lo-fi drums sound human rather than mechanical. In Ableton Live, this is the Groove setting applied from the Groove Pool. In FL Studio, it is the Swing knob in the Step Sequencer. In Logic Pro, apply a Groove template to your MIDI regions via the Region Inspector.

If you are new to using groove and swing in your DAW, take a moment to understand how quantization and swing interact before you start programming drums — it will save you a lot of frustration later. Our guide on using groove and swing in music production covers this in detail for all major DAWs.

Choose your key. Minor keys dominate lo-fi — they have a natural melancholy that suits the nostalgic aesthetic. D minor, F minor, and G minor are particularly popular starting points. The minor key combined with jazz extensions produces the characteristic bittersweet warmth that lo-fi is known for. That said, lo-fi in major keys works well too — Cmaj7 and Fmaj7 progressions can feel warm and hopeful rather than melancholic while still hitting the lo-fi aesthetic perfectly.

Step 2 — Program Your Lo-Fi Drums

The drums are the heart of a lo-fi beat, and getting the right sound and feel is more important than harmonic complexity. Lo-fi drums have three defining qualities: they sound like real acoustic drums (not synthesized), they feel slightly imprecise and human (not quantized to a perfect grid), and they are simple in pattern — restraint is the rule.

Choosing the Right Drum Sounds

Find drum samples that sound like they were recorded in a real room, ideally with some vintage character — slightly muffled, with natural room ambience, and with a compressed quality that suggests they came from a sampled record rather than a dry recording session. Splice, Looperman, and dedicated lo-fi sample packs from producers like MSXII Audio, Basement Freaks, and Kits Kreme all offer suitable material. If you want completely free options, the free sample packs on Splice's free tier and Looperman's community uploads include excellent lo-fi-ready drum hits.

What you are looking for in a kick drum: a punchy but not overpowering low-end hit, slightly rounded attack, possibly with a gentle room tail. Avoid modern trap kicks with long 808-style sub tails — those belong to a different genre. A good lo-fi kick sounds like someone playing a real kick drum in a small room in the 1990s.

For snares and claps: lo-fi benefits from snares that have a natural crackle and room sound. A snare that sounds like it was recorded in a medium-sized room with slight reverb already baked in is ideal. Layer a snare with a soft clap hit to add body if needed.

Hi-hats are crucial. Closed hi-hats should be slightly muted and not overly bright. Open hi-hats should have a natural shimmer, not a sharp metallic sheen. Vary the velocity of your hi-hats significantly — this single change makes a drum pattern feel dramatically more human.

Building the Basic Pattern

Start with a simple foundation pattern. The most common lo-fi drum pattern is:

  • Kick: beats 1 and 3 (or beat 1 only for a more minimal feel)
  • Snare: beats 2 and 4 (the classic backbeat)
  • Closed hi-hat: every eighth note, or every quarter note for a more minimal pattern
  • Open hi-hat: placed sparingly on the upbeats between snare hits

Program this pattern first with straight quantization, then humanize it. Do not try to humanize and program at the same time — it is easier to get the rhythm right first, then loosen it up.

Humanizing Your Drums

Humanizing lo-fi drums is one of the most important steps in the entire production process. Here is a systematic approach:

  1. Timing offset: Move individual hits 5–15ms early or late from the grid. The kick can sit slightly behind the beat for a heavier, dragging feel. Hi-hats can push slightly ahead for energy. Random small offsets on all hits creates an organic feel.
  2. Velocity variation: No two hi-hat hits should be the same velocity. Set a range of roughly 60–100 (on a 0–127 MIDI scale) and vary each hit individually. Snare hits can vary less dramatically (80–110), but should not be identical.
  3. Pattern variation: Change the pattern slightly every 4 or 8 bars — add a ghost note on the snare, remove a hi-hat hit, add a kick variation. This prevents the loop from feeling mechanical over long stretches.
  4. Ghost notes: Add very quiet snare hits (velocity 20–40) between the main snare hits. This adds texture and rhythm depth that sounds like a real drummer's brush technique.

For a deeper look at drum programming technique that applies across genres, our guide on mixing drums in a DAW covers the processing side after you have your patterns right.

The Drag Technique: One of the most effective lo-fi drum humanization tricks is the "drag" — setting your entire drum pattern to sit 10–20ms behind the tempo grid. This creates a laid-back, heavy feeling that is characteristic of sampled hip-hop. In Ableton Live, nudge the timing of your drum clip backward. In FL Studio, use the Step Sequencer's pattern offset. The result is subtle but transforms the feel from mechanical to groove-driven.

Step 3 — Lo-Fi Chords and Harmony

Harmony is what gives lo-fi beats their emotional character. The genre draws heavily from jazz — not full bebop complexity, but the harmonic language of jazz: extended chords, voice leading, and the warmth of 7th and 9th intervals. Understanding this harmonic language is the single most valuable skill you can develop as a lo-fi producer.

The Essential Lo-Fi Chord Types

Forget basic triads for lo-fi. The following chord types form the backbone of the genre:

Chord Type Construction Example in D minor Character
Minor 7th 1 – b3 – 5 – b7 Dm7 (D F A C) Melancholic, warm, introspective
Major 7th 1 – 3 – 5 – 7 Fmaj7 (F A C E) Dreamy, nostalgic, bittersweet
Dominant 7th 1 – 3 – 5 – b7 A7 (A C# E G) Tense, bluesy, creates movement
Minor 9th 1 – b3 – 5 – b7 – 9 Dm9 (D F A C E) Rich, lush, jazz-inflected
Major 9th 1 – 3 – 5 – 7 – 9 Fmaj9 (F A C E G) Open, airy, sophisticated
Half-Diminished (m7b5) 1 – b3 – b5 – b7 Em7b5 (E G Bb D) Unstable, passing, moody

Building a Lo-Fi Chord Progression

The most common lo-fi progressions are short loops — two, three, or four chords that repeat throughout the track, often with subtle variation. Here are three progressions that work well and are used frequently in the genre:

Progression 1 — The ii-V-i in D minor: Em7b5 → A7 → Dm7. This is a jazz ii-V-i (two-five-one) in D minor. The Em7b5 creates tension, the A7 creates expectation, and the Dm7 resolves. Two bars each, looped continuously.

Progression 2 — Drifting Majors in F: Fmaj7 → Em7 → Am7 → Dm7. Four chords, one bar each. The progression moves gently through the key of F major with a sense of unresolved longing — it does not land on a definitive tonic, which creates the floating quality lo-fi is known for.

Progression 3 — Two-Chord Loop: Cmaj7 → Am7. Simple, hypnotic, and effective for longer ambient lo-fi tracks. The simplicity is a feature — it creates space for melody and texture to breathe.

Voicing and Spacing

How you voice your chords matters as much as which chords you use. Several principles apply consistently to lo-fi:

  • Drop the root in the bass range when you have a separate bass instrument. If your chord is Dm7, your piano or Rhodes left hand plays the bass note D, while the right hand plays just the upper extensions: F, A, C (and possibly E for the 9th).
  • Open voicings — spread the chord tones across a wider range rather than stacking them tightly — sound warmer and less cluttered. For Fmaj7, try F in the low register, then A and E in the mid range, with C at the top.
  • Omit the fifth where needed. In jazz and lo-fi, the fifth of the chord is often the least important note. Dropping it allows more room for the extensions (7ths and 9ths) that give the music its character.

Lo-Fi Melody

Lo-fi melodies are typically simple, sparse, and scale-based rather than complex or technically demanding. The pentatonic minor scale, natural minor scale, and Dorian mode (a minor scale with a raised 6th) are the most useful starting points. A good lo-fi melody uses space as much as it uses notes — silence between phrases is not weakness, it is style. Three to five notes per phrase, with gaps for breathing, is the right density. Avoid fast passages or virtuosic runs — they feel out of place and break the relaxed character.

The most common lo-fi melody instruments are: muted piano or Rhodes electric piano, acoustic guitar (fingerpicked), melodic synth pads, vibraphone, and solo saxophone or flute samples. For a more detailed look at harmonic concepts that will strengthen your lo-fi progressions, our guide on ear training for music producers covers chord recognition and scale awareness from the ground up.

Step 4 — Bass Programming

Lo-fi bass is understated by design. Unlike trap or funk where the bass is often a lead element, lo-fi bass sits comfortably underneath the chords and reinforces the harmony without calling attention to itself. This subtlety is part of what creates the genre's relaxed, non-aggressive character.

Lo-Fi Bass Sound Design

The classic lo-fi bass sound is a round, warm, slightly compressed tone with minimal attack transient. Think of a vintage Fender P-Bass or J-Bass recorded through a slightly overdriven DI box — punchy in the low-mids but not harsh. Alternatively, a simple sine or triangle wave synth bass with a short attack and medium decay mimics an upright bass and works well for jazzier progressions.

Apply these processing choices to your bass:

  • Low-pass filter: Cut frequencies above 2–3kHz. Lo-fi bass should not have a bright, present high end. Roll it off.
  • Subtle saturation: A gentle saturation plugin adds harmonic warmth and helps the bass feel present without being loud. 5–15% drive on a tape-style saturation plugin is usually enough.
  • Compression: Medium-fast attack (10–30ms), medium release (80–150ms), moderate ratio (3:1 to 4:1). This gently smooths the bass dynamics without removing the natural feel. For a full breakdown of compression settings, our beginner's guide to compression covers the essential concepts.

Bass Pattern Principles

Keep the bass pattern simple. The most effective lo-fi bass lines follow these rules:

  1. Root note anchoring: Start each chord change on the root note. This grounds the harmony and ensures the progression reads clearly.
  2. Minimal movement: Most lo-fi bass lines move only when the chord changes. Between chord changes, the bass holds or plays simple rhythmic variations on the same note.
  3. Syncopation sparingly: Occasionally anticipating a chord change by hitting the root a half-beat early adds feel and groove without complicating the pattern.
  4. Follow the kick drum: Aligning bass hits with kick drum hits on beats 1 and 3 creates a locked, solid low-end foundation.

Step 5 — Sampling vs Original Production

Lo-fi has historically been built on sampling — taking a snippet of a jazz or soul record, chopping it, pitching it down slightly, and layering drums on top. Many of the genre's most recognizable tracks are built this way. However, the landscape for producers has changed significantly, and it is important to understand both approaches.

The Sampling Approach

Sampling a real record gives you instant access to the character, warmth, and imperfection of vintage recordings. A two-bar chop from a 1970s jazz record already has all the analog texture, frequency character, and room sound that you would otherwise spend time trying to recreate with plugins. The emotional resonance of a well-chosen sample is hard to replicate from scratch.

The process: find a source recording (jazz, soul, classical, or any melodic/harmonic material), identify a usable two to four-bar phrase, import it into your DAW, pitch it down 1–3 semitones if needed for the right key and feel, chop it into a sampler instrument, and layer your own drums and bass on top.

The critical issue with sampling: Using a copyrighted recording without clearance is copyright infringement. Publishing platforms like DistroKid and CD Baby will distribute your music, but if the original rights holder identifies the sample, your track can be taken down and you may face legal action. For commercial release or sync licensing, uncleared samples are a serious risk. Our article on how to license your music covers this in detail.

Safe sampling options include: samples from records released under Creative Commons licenses, samples from dedicated sample packs with a royalty-free license (such as those from MSXII, Kits Kreme, and similar producers who create original material specifically for this purpose), and samples from public domain recordings (generally pre-1928 in the US, though this threshold changes as copyright terms expire).

The Original Production Approach

Creating original lo-fi beats from scratch — using MIDI instruments, original recordings, and effect processing rather than uncleared samples — sidesteps all clearance issues and is increasingly the preferred approach for producers who want to release commercially.

The challenge is capturing the warmth and imperfection of real recordings. This is exactly what the texture effects in Step 7 are designed to address. With the right processing chain, an original lo-fi beat built entirely from MIDI and soft synths can be genuinely indistinguishable from a sampled production. The key tools are: tape saturation, vinyl noise simulation, subtle pitch wobble, low-pass filtering, and room reverb on individual elements.

Many producers use a hybrid approach: original chord progressions and melodies programmed with high-quality vintage-style instrument plugins (Arturia Wurlitzer V, Native Instruments Una Corda, Spitfire LABS), combined with vintage-character drum samples from cleared sample packs, and processed with the full lo-fi texture chain.

Step 6 — The Lo-Fi Texture Effects

The texture effects are what transform a reasonably pleasant jazz-influenced beat into something that unmistakably sounds like lo-fi. This is the processing stage where the genre's defining sonic character is created. Work through these effects systematically.

1. Low-Pass Filtering

Digital recordings naturally have a bright, extended high-frequency response. Vinyl records and tape do not — they roll off the top end. Applying a low-pass filter to your master bus (or to individual elements) removes this brightness and gives the mix a warm, slightly dull quality that suggests an older recording medium.

Settings: Apply a gentle low-pass filter at 12–16kHz with a soft slope (6–12dB per octave). This is not meant to be obvious — it should feel like the mix naturally has less sheen, not like you applied a heavy filter. On individual elements (piano, melody), you can filter more aggressively, down to 8–10kHz, to give them a vintage character.

2. Vinyl Crackle and Surface Noise

Vinyl crackle is the most recognizable lo-fi texture effect. The surface noise of a vinyl record — the constant low-level hiss and occasional crackle of a stylus moving through a groove — tells the listener's brain that this sounds like a record.

How to add vinyl crackle:

  • Sample method: Find a vinyl noise sample (quiet crackle with no music on it), loop it across the entire track, and set its volume to sit 15–20dB below your main elements. It should be felt rather than heard — present enough that removing it makes the mix feel sterile, but not so loud that it becomes the primary sound.
  • Plugin method: iZotope Vinyl (free) is the most widely used option. It simulates vinyl surface noise, crackle, and the frequency response of a needle in a groove. RC-20 Retro Color by XLN Audio (paid) adds vinyl wobble, noise, and degradation with more control. Apply either plugin on a return track or directly on the master bus at low intensity.

3. Tape Saturation and Warmth

Tape saturation adds subtle harmonic distortion that makes elements feel warm and three-dimensional rather than cold and digital. Applied gently to the master bus, it is one of the most effective ways to give an entire mix a vintage character.

Tape saturation plugins to use:

  • iZotope Vinyl (free) — includes basic tape-style color
  • Softube Tape — professional tape emulation with multiple machine types
  • Waves J37 Tape — models the EMI J37 tape machine used in classic recordings
  • RC-20 Retro Color — combines vinyl, tape, and multiple degradation modes in a single plugin
  • Your DAW's built-in saturation — Ableton Live's Saturator, Logic Pro's Tape Delay feedback saturation, and FL Studio's built-in clipping are all capable of adding gentle warmth without a paid plugin

Settings: Apply tape saturation at low drive settings — enough to add warmth but not enough to introduce obvious distortion. On the master bus, 5–15% drive on a tape plugin is usually the right range. You should be able to A/B the plugin on and off and notice that the version with saturation feels warmer and more present, without being able to point to obvious distortion artifacts.

4. Tape Wobble and Pitch Instability

Tape playback has slight, random pitch variation caused by inconsistencies in the tape transport mechanism. This gentle pitch wobble is part of what makes vintage recordings feel alive rather than static. In lo-fi production, simulating this adds a subtle humanizing quality to the entire mix.

The easiest approach: use a chorus or flanger plugin set to a very slow rate (0.1–0.3 Hz) with minimal depth and mix. This creates the impression of slight pitch drift without obvious chorus effect. Dedicated wow and flutter plugins (RC-20's wobble section, iZotope Vinyl's mechanical noise section) offer more authentic simulation. Apply this to the master bus or to your primary chord/melody instrument at very low intensity — less than you think you need.

5. Ambient Sounds and Room Tone

Many lo-fi tracks include ambient environmental sounds layered quietly underneath the music — rain on a window, coffee shop background noise, birdsong, distant traffic, or the sound of a room with air conditioning. These ambient layers create a sense of place and strengthen the relaxed, immersive quality of the music.

Layer ambient sounds at very low volume — roughly 20–25dB below the loudest element in the mix. They should not be consciously noticeable as separate sounds; they should contribute to a felt atmosphere. Rain is the most classic choice and the most widely available as a free sound effect. Layer two or three rain recordings at slightly different qualities (close rain, distant rain, rain on glass) to create a more three-dimensional ambient texture.

6. Reverb for Space and Cohesion

Lo-fi mixes benefit from a shared reverb that places all the elements in the same acoustic space. The impression is of listening to music playing in a room — not a dead studio space, but somewhere with natural reflections. A medium-sized room reverb (not a large hall) with a decay time of 0.8–1.5 seconds applied as a send effect works well. Keep reverb wet signal subtle on the drums (5–10% send) and slightly more present on the melodic elements (10–20%). For detailed reverb technique, our guide on how to use reverb in a mix covers all the essential concepts.

Step 7 — Mixing a Lo-Fi Beat

Lo-fi mixing is in many ways the opposite of modern pop or electronic music mixing. Where modern pop aims for loudness, clarity, and maximum separation, lo-fi mixing aims for warmth, cohesion, and a sense of gentle imperfection. The mix should feel like a complete, integrated whole rather than a collection of individually processed elements fighting for space.

Frequency Balance in Lo-Fi

The target frequency response for a lo-fi mix is warm and slightly dark — strong in the low-mids, full in the midrange, and rolled off in the highs. Use an EQ on the master bus to apply a gentle high shelf cut (2–3dB cut above 10kHz) and a very slight low-mid boost (1–2dB around 200–400Hz). This shifts the mix's overall color toward vintage warmth.

Individual element EQ priorities:

  • Kick: Boost gently around 80Hz for weight, roll off sharply above 10kHz
  • Snare: Keep the mid presence (1–3kHz) while rolling off the harsh upper frequencies above 8kHz
  • Piano/Rhodes: Cut boxiness at 300–500Hz if needed, roll off above 10–12kHz for a vintage muted quality
  • Bass: Keep the fundamental (60–100Hz) and low-mids (150–250Hz), cut most of the content above 2kHz

Dynamics and Compression in Lo-Fi

Lo-fi mixes should have some dynamic range — they should not be crushed to maximum loudness. Light bus compression on the master (2:1 ratio, moderate attack and release, 1–3dB of gain reduction) gives the mix a gentle cohesion without flattening the dynamics. The analog-style compressors (1176-style, LA-2A-style) work well here because they add gentle harmonic coloring in addition to compression.

For the individual drum bus: a vintage-style compressor with moderate attack (30–50ms to let the transients through), medium release (200–300ms), and 2:1 to 3:1 ratio adds glue and warmth. Parallel compression (blending a heavily compressed signal with the uncompressed original) is particularly effective on lo-fi drum busses, adding punch while maintaining dynamics.

Volume Balance

The rough volume hierarchy for a lo-fi beat from loudest to quietest:

  1. Drums (kick and snare at roughly equal volume, sitting as the loudest consistent elements)
  2. Bass (prominent but not fighting the kick)
  3. Primary chord instrument (piano, Rhodes, guitar)
  4. Melody instrument (slightly behind the chord layer)
  5. Texture effects (vinyl crackle, ambient sounds — barely audible but felt)

Loudness Targets for Lo-Fi

Lo-fi is not a loudness-maximized genre. Targeting an integrated loudness of around −14 LUFS (the Spotify normalization target) is appropriate and actually suits the genre's aesthetic — a lo-fi beat at maximum loudness loses the intimate, quiet quality that makes it work. Avoid heavy limiting that crushes the peaks. A ceiling limiter set at −0.3 dBTP is appropriate for protecting against intersample clipping, but use it gently.

Step 8 — Releasing Lo-Fi Music

Understanding how to release lo-fi music commercially is the final piece of the puzzle. The lo-fi market is large and playlist-driven, which creates real opportunities for independent producers who approach it systematically.

Distribution

Use a digital distribution service to get your lo-fi beats onto Spotify, Apple Music, YouTube Music, and other platforms. DistroKid, CD Baby, and TuneCore are the three most widely used options for independent producers. DistroKid charges a flat annual fee and keeps 100% of your royalties — a good deal if you release frequently. CD Baby charges per-release but takes a small percentage. For a full comparison, our DistroKid vs CD Baby comparison covers the key differences in detail.

Playlist Strategy

Lo-fi's playlist ecosystem is particularly strong. The genre is heavily playlist-driven — listeners find lo-fi through curated playlists and radio channels rather than artist discovery. This means getting your music onto lo-fi playlists is one of the most effective marketing actions you can take.

For Spotify: submit to Spotify editorial playlists through Spotify for Artists at least seven days before your release date. Pitch specifically to the lo-fi and chill study playlists by describing your music accurately in the pitch form. Beyond editorial playlists, identify independent curators who run lo-fi playlists and reach out directly — many accept submissions through SubmitHub or through their social media profiles.

For YouTube: lo-fi radio channels actively look for new music to add to their streams and compilations. Lofi Girl, College Music, Chillhop Music, and similar channels all have submission pages or email addresses for music submissions. Getting placed on one of these streams generates consistent passive exposure and streaming revenue.

Copyright Registration

Register your original lo-fi beats with a Performing Rights Organization (PRO) — ASCAP, BMI, or SESAC in the US — to collect performance royalties when your music is played publicly. If your beats are entirely original (no uncleared samples), register the copyright as well. For a full breakdown of royalty collection, our article on how music royalties work explains the full system from registration through collection.

Building a Lo-Fi Brand

Lo-fi music has a strong visual aesthetic — animated art, muted color palettes, cozy imagery (rain, books, late nights, coffee). Consistent visual presentation across your releases, playlist covers, and social media significantly strengthens brand recognition in this genre. The Lofi Girl model — a single, iconic visual associated with a continuous stream — has proven enormously effective and can be adapted at a smaller scale by individual producers.

Consider creating your own lo-fi compilation album — a collection of five to ten lo-fi beats under a consistent visual and sonic theme. This performs better on streaming platforms than individual singles in the lo-fi genre because listeners favor longer listening sessions. A compilation gives them 30–45 minutes of continuous content, which drives higher average stream counts per listener.

Practical Exercises

Beginner Exercise

Build Your First Lo-Fi Pattern

Open your DAW, set the tempo to 80 BPM with 55% swing, and program a simple kick-snare pattern (kick on beat 1, snare on beat 2) with closed hi-hats on every eighth note using acoustic drum samples. Add a Dm7 chord on a piano or Rhodes plugin held for two bars, then loop the four-bar section and listen back — focus on whether the swing makes the pattern feel human rather than mechanical.

Intermediate Exercise

Build a Full Lo-Fi Beat With Texture Processing

Create a complete eight-bar lo-fi beat with drums, bass, chords (use a ii-V-i progression in D minor: Em7b5 → A7 → Dm7), and a simple melody. Once the musical elements are complete, apply iZotope Vinyl (free) to the master bus at low intensity, add a vinyl crackle sample underneath the mix at −18dB relative to the kick, and apply a low-pass filter at 14kHz. Compare the before and after — notice how the texture processing transforms the feel of the track.

Advanced Exercise

Create a Release-Ready Lo-Fi Compilation Track

Produce a three-to-four-minute lo-fi track entirely from original material (no uncleared samples) using a hybrid approach: original chord progressions programmed with a vintage instrument plugin, cleared acoustic drum samples humanized with timing offsets and velocity variation, layered ambient sounds (rain, room tone), and the full texture processing chain (tape saturation, vinyl noise, low-pass filter, tape wobble). Mix to −14 LUFS integrated loudness and export at 24-bit/44.1kHz, then submit it to a lo-fi playlist on SubmitHub or directly to a lo-fi YouTube channel.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ What BPM is lo-fi music?
Lo-fi beats typically range from 70 to 90 BPM, with 75–85 BPM as the classic sweet spot. Going slower than 70 BPM feels sluggish rather than chill; going faster than 90 BPM begins to lose the laid-back lo-fi character.
FAQ What chords are used in lo-fi music?
Lo-fi music draws heavily from jazz harmony, using major 7th, minor 7th, dominant 7th, major 9th, and minor 9th chords. Avoid basic triads — the 7th and 9th extensions are what give lo-fi its characteristic warm, sophisticated sound.
FAQ How do I get the vinyl crackle sound?
Add vinyl crackle by layering a dedicated vinyl noise sample under your mix at around 15–20dB below your main elements, or use a plugin like iZotope Vinyl (free) or RC-20 Retro Color. It should be felt underneath the music, not heard as a primary sound.
FAQ Do I need to sample to make lo-fi beats?
No — you can create authentic lo-fi entirely from original material using MIDI instruments and effect processing. Many producers prefer original production to avoid sample clearance issues, especially for commercial release or sync licensing.
FAQ What is the best DAW for making lo-fi beats?
Lo-fi beats can be made in any DAW. FL Studio and Ableton Live are the most popular choices, and Logic Pro is excellent on Mac. The DAW matters less than understanding the genre's core techniques — the lo-fi sound comes from specific processing choices that work anywhere.
FAQ How do I humanize my lo-fi drums?
Humanize lo-fi drums by slightly offsetting hits from the grid (5–15ms early or late), varying hi-hat velocities across a wide range (60–100), adding ghost notes on the snare at very low velocity, and applying 50–60% swing to the grid.
FAQ What plugins are used in lo-fi music?
The most used lo-fi plugins are RC-20 Retro Color (vinyl texture, wobble, noise), iZotope Vinyl (free vinyl emulation), and analog saturation plugins for warmth. Most essential lo-fi effects — low-pass filtering, bitcrushing, tape saturation — are available as stock plugins in any DAW.
FAQ How do I add warmth to my lo-fi beats?
Add warmth through tape saturation on the master bus (5–15% drive), a gentle high-frequency rolloff with a low-pass filter above 12–14kHz, analog-style compression, and room reverb on the main elements. The combination creates the warm, slightly dull quality associated with vintage recordings.
