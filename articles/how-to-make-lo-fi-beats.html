How to Make Lo-Fi Beats (2026): The Complete Step-by-Step Guide

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026
16 min readBeginner – Intermediate

Lo-fi has grown from an internet niche into one of the most searched and streamed genres in music production. YouTube's lo-fi radio channels have accumulated billions of listens. Lofi Girl's 24/7 stream has been running for years and regularly holds millions of concurrent listeners. Spotify's lo-fi playlists generate consistent streams that accumulate meaningful royalties for producers who understand the genre. And unlike many electronic genres, lo-fi is genuinely accessible — you do not need an expensive sample collection, a complex synthesizer rig, or years of theory to make a convincing lo-fi beat from scratch.

Quick Answer

Lo-fi beats require understanding the genre's specific characteristics: slower tempos (typically 70-100 BPM), warm jazz-influenced chord progressions, loose drum programming with swing, and texture effects like vinyl crackle and tape saturation. You don't need expensive gear or samples—focus on the right harmonic language, drum feel, and analog-inspired processing to create an authentic lo-fi sound.

What you do need is an understanding of what makes lo-fi sound like lo-fi. The genre has a specific set of characteristics — tempo, harmonic language, drum feel, texture processing — that define it as a recognizable aesthetic rather than just quiet music. This guide covers all of them in detail, with step-by-step instruction from setting your tempo through to applying the final texture effects that give a lo-fi beat its characteristic warm, worn sound.

What you'll learn: The defining characteristics of lo-fi music, how to program drums with the right feel, lo-fi chord theory and melody, bass programming, sampling vs original production, the key texture effects (vinyl crackle, tape wobble, saturation), how to mix a lo-fi beat, and how to release lo-fi music commercially.

What Makes Lo-Fi Sound Like Lo-Fi

Lo-fi — short for low fidelity — is a production aesthetic that intentionally embraces the sonic characteristics of older, degraded recordings. It draws from the sound of vinyl records (surface noise, gentle distortion, frequency rolloff at the extremes), tape recordings (warmth, slight saturation, occasional pitch wobble), and early hip-hop sampling (grit, texture, the feel of music that has been recorded, played back, and re-recorded through multiple generations of media).

The genre combines these vintage textures with jazz-influenced harmony and relaxed, swinging rhythms to produce music that feels warm, nostalgic, and undemanding — perfect for studying, working, or relaxing. The imperfection is intentional and essential. A lo-fi beat that is too clean, too bright, or too perfectly on-grid stops feeling like lo-fi and becomes something else.

The five core characteristics of lo-fi music are: slow, relaxed tempo (70–90 BPM); jazz-influenced extended chords (7ths and 9ths); humanized, slightly swinging drums with acoustic sample character; warm, low-pass filtered frequency response that rolls off the extremes; and texture layering — vinyl crackle, tape noise, ambient sounds — that adds the sense of physical, imperfect media.

Step 1: Set Your Tempo and Project

Open your DAW and set the project tempo between 70 and 90 BPM. For a classic chill lo-fi hip hop feel, 75–85 BPM is the sweet spot — slow enough to feel relaxed but with enough momentum to hold attention. Going slower than 70 BPM tends to feel sluggish rather than chill. Going faster than 90 BPM starts to lose the genre's characteristic laid-back quality.

Set your time signature to 4/4. Most lo-fi music is in straight 4/4. If you want to explore, 3/4 and 6/8 produce interesting variations but are not where you should start. Enable a swing or shuffle setting if your DAW has one — around 50-60% swing adds the gentle push-pull feel that makes lo-fi drums sound human rather than mechanical. In Ableton Live, this is the Groove setting. In FL Studio, it is the Swing knob. In Logic Pro, apply a Groove template to your MIDI regions.

Choose your key. Minor keys dominate lo-fi — they have a natural melancholy that suits the nostalgic aesthetic. D minor, F minor, and G minor are particularly popular. The minor key combined with jazz extensions produces the characteristic bittersweet warmth that lo-fi is known for.

Step 2: Program Your Lo-Fi Drums

The drums are the heart of a lo-fi beat, and getting the right sound and feel is more important than harmonic complexity. Lo-fi drums have three defining qualities: they sound like real acoustic drums (not synthesized), they feel slightly imprecise and human (not quantized to a perfect grid), and they are simple in pattern — restraint is the rule.

Choosing the Right Drum Sounds

Find drum samples that sound like they were recorded in a real room, ideally with some vintage character — slightly muffled, with natural room ambience, and with a compressed quality that suggests they came from a sampled record rather than a dry recording session. Splice, Looperman, and dedicated lo-fi sample packs from producers like Mr. Bill, MSXII Audio, and Drumvault all provide suitable material. Avoid clean, bright, modern trap drum samples — they fight against the lo-fi aesthetic at every turn.

Your basic drum pattern needs a kick, snare, and hi-hat. The kick hits on beats 1 and 3 (or just beat 1 with a variation on the 3). The snare hits on beats 2 and 4. The hi-hat plays quarter notes or eighth notes with occasional 16th note fills. Keep it simple — lo-fi drums are background elements, not showcases.

Humanizing Your Drums

This is where most beginners go wrong. Programming drums to a perfect grid and then trying to fix the sound with effects does not work. The human feel needs to be in the timing itself. Offset each hit slightly from the grid — nudge some hits 10–20ms early or late randomly. Vary the velocity of every hit, particularly the hi-hats: let them breathe between hits by making some quieter and some louder. The goal is to make the drums feel like they were played by a slightly tired, relaxed drummer who has been playing for a while — not a machine.

Most DAWs provide a humanization or randomization function that applies small random timing and velocity variations automatically. Use this as a starting point, then listen critically and adjust. The best lo-fi drums feel organic but not sloppy — there is a difference between natural variation and missed beats.

Add a subtle snare roll or ghost note on the e or ah of beat 4 occasionally. This small variation breaks up the pattern and adds the kind of moment-to-moment rhythmic interest that keeps a looping beat from feeling static.

Step 3: Lo-Fi Chord Progressions

Lo-fi music is built on jazz harmony. The defining characteristic of lo-fi chords is the use of extended voicings — 7th, 9th, and occasionally 11th chords — rather than basic triads. A plain C major chord played in a lo-fi context sounds wrong; a Cmaj7 or Cmaj9 sounds right because the added notes create the warm, sophisticated texture the genre requires.

Essential Lo-Fi Chord Types

Minor 7th (min7): The most common chord in lo-fi. In D minor: Dm7 (D-F-A-C). Provides a relaxed, melancholy warmth. Play this chord across a full bar or two and it already sounds like lo-fi.

Major 7th (maj7): Bright but soft — the jazz major chord. In F major: Fmaj7 (F-A-C-E). Creates a warm, floating quality. Excellent for resolving tension.

Minor 9th (min9): Adds an extra layer of richness to the minor 7th. In D minor: Dm9 (D-F-A-C-E). More complex and sophisticated — the kind of chord that immediately signals jazz influence.

Dominant 7th (7): Creates movement and tension. In A: A7 (A-C#-E-G). Creates a pull toward the tonic that is characteristic of jazz-influenced progressions.

Lo-Fi Chord Progressions That Work

The simplest and most effective lo-fi progressions are two to four chord loops that repeat without resolution. Here are four that work immediately:

ii-V-I in major: Dm7 → G7 → Cmaj7 → Cmaj7 (in C major). The most fundamental jazz progression. Sounds instantly sophisticated and warm.

Minor loop: Dm7 → Am7 → Gm7 → Am7. A four-chord minor progression that loops indefinitely without feeling repetitive. The Gm7 creates a moment of movement that makes the return to Dm7 feel satisfying.

Two-chord chill: Dm7 → Gm7. The simplest possible lo-fi progression. Stays on each chord for a full two bars. The lack of harmonic movement creates maximum calm.

Jazz ii-V with extension: Em7 → A9 → Dmaj9 → Dmaj9. A slightly more complex version using 9th chords. The 9th chords add the extra sophistication that distinguishes bedroom lo-fi from professional-quality lo-fi.

Play your chords in a mid-range voicing — not too low (muddy) and not too high (thin). Aim for the middle register of the keyboard where the notes sit warmly. Avoid playing chord inversions that put the 3rd or 7th in the bass — root-position voicings work best for lo-fi.

Adding Melody

Not every lo-fi beat needs a lead melody — many successful lo-fi tracks are purely atmospheric, with the chord loop providing the melodic interest. If you add a melody, keep it simple and sparse. A few notes here and there, sitting in the spaces between chord changes, is more effective than a busy melodic line that competes with the harmonic texture.

Use a Rhodes electric piano, a detuned synth piano, a sampled flute or vibraphone, or a nylon-string guitar for your melodic instrument. These timbres fit the lo-fi aesthetic naturally. Quantize the melody only loosely — let some notes be slightly late or early to maintain the human feel. Add a touch of legato articulation and expression rather than playing every note at the same velocity.

Step 4: Bass Programming

The bass in lo-fi is understated — its job is to ground the harmony and add low-end warmth without drawing attention to itself. A simple bass line that follows the root notes of the chord progression, occasionally adding a passing note or a brief walking figure, is all that is needed.

Use an upright bass sample or a warm, round electric bass tone for the most authentic lo-fi character. Avoid bright, modern bass tones with prominent attack — they fight against the warm, rolled-off frequency response that lo-fi requires. If using a synthesized bass, add gentle saturation and roll off the high frequencies above 500Hz to give it the warm, slightly undefined quality of a real bass recorded to tape.

Keep the bass rhythm simple. Quarter notes following the root note of each chord is a perfectly valid lo-fi bass line. Adding an occasional 8th note fill or a chromatic passing note approaching a chord change gives it just enough movement to feel musical. Quantize the bass loosely — let it breathe with the drums rather than sitting in a perfectly rigid position on the grid.

Step 5: Texture Effects — The Lo-Fi Sound

This is where a collection of good-sounding MIDI parts becomes a lo-fi beat. The texture processing layer — vinyl crackle, tape wobble, frequency shaping, and saturation — is what transforms clean digital production into something that feels warm, worn, and vintage. Done well, listeners should not be able to separate the texture effects from the music itself; it should all feel like one cohesive sound.

Vinyl Crackle and Surface Noise

Add a vinyl crackle layer to a separate audio track set to loop throughout the track. This can be a recorded vinyl noise sample (available in most lo-fi sample packs and on Looperman for free) or generated by a plugin. Volume is critical — the crackle should feel like a light layer underneath the music, present but not dominant. A good level is where removing it makes the beat feel slightly too clean. Set it louder than that and it becomes a distraction.

Plugins for vinyl texture: iZotope Vinyl (free, excellent) simulates the full range of vinyl playback characteristics including surface noise, mechanical noise, electrical noise, and pitch wobble. RC-20 Retro Color by XLN Audio (~$99) provides vinyl noise alongside other lo-fi texture tools including bitcrushing and tape noise. Chop and Mangle by Sugar Bytes provides more creative lo-fi processing. For beginners, iZotope Vinyl is free and sufficient for most lo-fi production needs.

Tape Wobble and Pitch Variation

Tape wobble — a subtle, slow variation in pitch and speed that simulates a tape machine with inconsistent motor speed — is one of the most effective lo-fi texture elements. Applied at a very low intensity (often 10-20% of the available range), it adds a gentle organic quality to the entire mix that is difficult to describe but immediately felt. The pitch variation should be slow (0.5–2Hz modulation rate) and very subtle — if it sounds like an effect, it is too strong.

Most lo-fi plugins include a tape wobble or wow and flutter control. In Ableton Live, the Corpus or Chorus effects can simulate gentle pitch modulation. In FL Studio, the Parametric EQ 2's LFO can be used to modulate a filter cutoff for a similar effect. Logic Pro's Spreader and Modulation effects offer comparable functionality.

Low-Pass Filtering and Frequency Shaping

One of the most recognizable characteristics of lo-fi music is its rolled-off high-frequency response. Real vinyl and tape recordings have limited high-frequency extension compared to modern digital recordings — they naturally lack the sharp, airy presence above 12–14kHz that digital production has by default. Emulating this is essential for authentic lo-fi sound.

Apply a gentle low-pass filter to your master channel or to a return/bus channel that your entire mix routes through. Set the cutoff at around 12–14kHz and use a gentle slope (6–12dB per octave) so the rolloff is gradual rather than a hard wall. The result should make the mix feel warmer and slightly less defined at the top end — not muffled, just less bright than a modern production.

Also consider a gentle high-pass filter below 50Hz to remove sub-bass content that adds no musical information to lo-fi (which does not have the sub-heavy character of trap or bass music). This cleans up the low end and makes the bass feel more defined within the mix.

Tape Saturation and Warmth

Tape saturation — gentle harmonic distortion modeled on the behavior of magnetic tape when driven moderately hard — is the final texture element that ties the lo-fi sound together. Applied subtly to individual channels and to the master bus, it adds a warmth and density that digital production lacks by default. The effect is similar to what vinyl pressing adds to a recording, or what analog tape naturally does to a signal passing through it: a slight compression of transients, the addition of harmonic overtones (primarily second harmonics, which are musically pleasant), and a gluing quality that makes elements feel like they belong together.

Apply gentle saturation to your drum bus, your chord instrument bus, and your master channel. The key word is gentle — lo-fi saturation should be felt more than heard. If you can clearly identify the distortion artifact, you have applied too much. A good starting point is driving a tape saturation plugin until you can just hear the warmth change versus bypassed, then backing off by 20-30%.

Most DAWs provide free saturation tools. Logic Pro's Tape Delay and Pedalboard include usable saturation. Ableton's Saturator device is straightforward and effective. FL Studio's Fruity Wave Candy and Maximus provide saturation options. For dedicated tape saturation, free plugins like Softube's Saturation Knob, the free version of RC-20, and YouLean's free bundle include capable saturation processing.

Step 6: Ambient Layer — Field Recordings and Room Sounds

Many lo-fi producers add a subtle ambient layer — the distant sound of rain, a café environment, a fan, birds, or a quiet room — underneath the music. This layer adds a sense of physical space and immersion that pure instrumental production cannot achieve on its own. It reinforces the aesthetic of music being heard from another room, or listened to on a rainy afternoon with a coffee, or discovered on a forgotten cassette tape.

The ambient layer should be very quiet — often 15–20dB below the music level. It should not be immediately obvious to the listener that it is there; it should only be noticeable when removed. Free ambient samples are widely available on Freesound.org (all Creative Commons licensed) and dedicated lo-fi sample packs. Rain, café ambience, and quiet room recordings are the most commonly used.

Apply a generous reverb and a low-pass filter to the ambient sample to push it further into the background and make it feel diffuse rather than sharp and present. The goal is environmental immersion, not a sound design showcase.

Step 7: Mixing a Lo-Fi Beat

Lo-fi mixing is simpler than most genres because the aesthetic embraces imperfection. The goal is warmth, balance, and feel — not clinical precision.

Level balance: The chord instrument (piano, Rhodes, vibraphone) is typically the loudest element, followed by drums, bass, then melody if present. Texture and ambient layers sit underneath everything else.

Reverb: Apply a short room reverb to drums for space without wash. Apply a longer reverb to the chord instrument and melody — lo-fi chords often have a generous tail that blurs transitions between chords and adds spaciousness. Use the same reverb for related elements to keep the mix cohesive.

Compression: Gentle bus compression on the master — 2:1 ratio, slow attack, medium release, 2–3dB of gain reduction at peaks. This glues elements together and adds the controlled, compressed feel of a mix through analog equipment. Retain the natural dynamics of the drums.

Master bus chain: Low-pass filter, gentle saturation, tape wobble if using a master bus plugin. Export at 24-bit WAV for distribution and archiving.

Releasing Lo-Fi Music

Lo-fi is one of the best genres for independent release. Spotify's editorial lo-fi playlists, YouTube lo-fi radio channels, and independent curators actively seek new tracks to keep their continuous streams fresh. Pitch releases to Spotify editorial playlists through Spotify for Artists at least seven days before release. Reach out directly to YouTube lo-fi radio channel curators — many accept submissions and will feature quality tracks, generating YouTube Content ID royalties over time.

For commercial release, ensure your beat contains no uncleared samples — create everything from scratch using original recordings and MIDI, or use cleared royalty-free sample libraries. Sample-based lo-fi beats using uncleared records cannot be commercially released or monetized on YouTube without risking copyright claims and takedowns.

Practical Exercises

Beginner Exercise

Apply Lo-Fi Processing to a Clean Beat

Open any beat you've already made. Apply a lo-fi processing chain: a vinyl simulation plugin or a gentle saturation to add warmth and harmonic distortion, a low-pass filter cutting frequencies above 12kHz to dull the top end, a noise or vinyl crackle layer at -30 to -25dBFS, and a slight pitch modulation (warble) on the whole mix. Reduce your stereo width slightly so it feels mono-adjacent. Compare processed and unprocessed versions. The processed version should feel warm, intimate, and nostalgic — like music heard through old speakers.

Intermediate Exercise

Sample Chop a Jazz Record to Build a Lo-Fi Loop

Find a royalty-free or Creative Commons jazz recording (Freemusicarchive.org is a good source). Import it into your sampler. Find a 2–4 bar section with a strong chord or melodic phrase. Chop it, slow the playback to around 75–85 BPM, and pitch it to your key. Add a drum pattern using a dusty lo-fi drum kit: kick on beats 1 and 3, snare on 2 and 4, and off-beat hi-hats with velocity variation. The chopped jazz sample should be processed with vinyl simulation and filtered warmth. The result is the classic lo-fi hip-hop aesthetic built from source material rather than a preset.

Advanced Exercise

Build a Complete Lo-Fi Beat EP

Produce 4 lo-fi beats — each 2.5–3 minutes long — that form a cohesive EP. Each track should use a different source material (one sample-based, one entirely synthesised, one using live recorded elements, one combining multiple approaches). Define a consistent processing palette across all 4 tracks so they feel like a unified body of work: the same vinyl noise character, a consistent frequency rolloff, and matching reverb qualities. Sequence the 4 tracks so they have a narrative arc — from more energetic to more contemplative. Master each track to -14 LUFS for streaming and package them as a cohesive release.

Frequently Asked Questions

+ FAQ What is the ideal tempo range for creating lo-fi beats?

Lo-fi beats typically sit between 70–90 BPM, which creates the slow, relaxed tempo that defines the genre. This tempo range allows for the swinging, laid-back feel characteristic of lo-fi while providing enough space for expressive drum programming and melodic elements.

+ FAQ Why is imperfection intentional in lo-fi production?

Lo-fi deliberately embraces sonic imperfections like vinyl crackle, tape wobble, and frequency rolloff to create a warm, nostalgic aesthetic. A beat that is too clean, bright, or perfectly grid-aligned loses its lo-fi identity and becomes a different genre entirely.

+ FAQ What are the main sonic sources that influence lo-fi's characteristic sound?

Lo-fi draws from three primary sources: vinyl records (surface noise and gentle distortion), tape recordings (warmth and pitch wobble), and early hip-hop sampling (grit and multi-generation re-recording artifacts). These vintage textures combine with jazz-influenced harmony to create the genre's signature sound.

+ FAQ Do you need expensive equipment or sample collections to make lo-fi beats?

No. Unlike many electronic genres, lo-fi is genuinely accessible and doesn't require expensive plugins, complex synthesizers, or extensive sample libraries. What matters is understanding the specific characteristics that define the lo-fi aesthetic rather than having premium gear.

+ FAQ What role does jazz-influenced harmony play in lo-fi production?

Jazz harmony is a core characteristic of lo-fi, providing the chord progressions and melodic sophistication that give the genre depth beyond its vintage texture processing. This harmonic language, combined with relaxed rhythms, creates the contemplative and nostalgic feel that makes lo-fi suitable for studying and relaxation.

+ FAQ What are the key texture effects that define a lo-fi beat's characteristic sound?

The three essential texture effects are vinyl crackle (surface noise), tape wobble (pitch instability), and saturation (warmth and harmonic coloration). These effects work together to create the warm, worn aesthetic that distinguishes lo-fi from clean digital production.

+ FAQ How does drum programming contribute to the lo-fi sound?

Drums in lo-fi require the right feel rather than mechanical precision; they should sound relaxed and swinging rather than rigidly quantized. Proper drum programming is one of the five core characteristics that defines lo-fi and prevents beats from sounding like other genres.

+ FAQ Can lo-fi producers earn meaningful royalties from streaming platforms?

Yes. Spotify's lo-fi playlists generate consistent streams that accumulate meaningful royalties for producers who understand the genre, and YouTube's lo-fi radio channels have accumulated billions of listens. The genre has become one of the most searched and streamed formats in music production.

What BPM is lo-fi music?

Lo-fi beats typically range from 70 to 90 BPM. The sweet spot for classic lo-fi hip hop is 75–85 BPM. Slower than 70 feels sluggish; faster than 90 loses the laid-back character.

What chords are used in lo-fi music?

Lo-fi draws from jazz harmony: major 7th, minor 7th, dominant 7th, major 9th, and minor 9th chords. ii-V-I progressions using these extended voicings create the characteristic warm sophistication. Avoid basic triads — the 7th and 9th extensions are essential.

How do I get the vinyl crackle sound?

Layer a vinyl noise sample or use a plugin like iZotope Vinyl (free) or RC-20 Retro Color. Run it lightly — just enough to feel present underneath the music, not loud enough to be distracting.

Do I need to sample to make lo-fi beats?

No. You can create the complete lo-fi aesthetic from scratch using original recordings, MIDI instruments, and texture processing. Many producers prefer this approach to avoid sample clearance issues for commercial release.

What is the best DAW for making lo-fi beats?

Any DAW works. FL Studio and Ableton Live are most popular among lo-fi producers. Logic Pro is excellent on Mac. The DAW matters less than understanding the genre's production techniques.

How do I humanize my lo-fi drums?

Offset hits slightly from the grid (10–20ms), vary hit velocities especially on hi-hats, enable swing or shuffle, and use real acoustic drum samples rather than synthesized sounds. The goal is removing the machine-perfect feel digital production defaults to.

What plugins are used in lo-fi music?

RC-20 Retro Color (vinyl texture, wobble, noise), iZotope Vinyl (free, excellent vinyl emulation), and analog saturation plugins for warmth. Most essential lo-fi effects are also available as stock plugins in any DAW.

How do I add warmth to my lo-fi beats?

Apply tape saturation gently to the master bus, roll off high frequencies above 12–14kHz with a low-pass filter, add analog-style compression, and use room reverb. The combination creates the warm, slightly dull quality of older recordings.
