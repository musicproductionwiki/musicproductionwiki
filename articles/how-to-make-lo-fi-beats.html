How to Make Lo-Fi Beats: The Complete Production Guide

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 13 min read Beginner–Intermediate

Lo-fi hip-hop is one of the most widely streamed genres on the internet — and one of the most misunderstood from a production standpoint. The aesthetic of warmth, imperfection, and nostalgic texture that defines the genre didn't emerge from low-budget accident. It was developed deliberately by producers who understood exactly which elements of degraded audio create an emotionally resonant listening environment.

This guide breaks down every element of a lo-fi beat: the drum pattern, the harmonic vocabulary, the sampling and texture layer, the processing chain, and how to combine them into something that sounds intentional rather than just degraded. Whether you're working in Ableton, FL Studio, Logic, or any other DAW, the principles are identical.

What you'll learn:

The complete lo-fi production process: tempo and feel, drum patterns with swing, jazz chord voicings, melody construction, vinyl and tape texture processing, the full lo-fi signal chain, and how to mix and master for the genre's aesthetic.

What Makes Lo-Fi Sound Lo-Fi

Lo-fi is defined by specific forms of audio degradation that evoke warmth, age, and intimacy. Understanding which degradation types are characteristic — and which just sound bad — is the foundation of producing the genre convincingly.

The characteristic elements are: tape saturation (even-harmonic distortion that adds warmth without harshness), vinyl noise (surface noise and crackle suggesting an analog playback source), low-pass filtering (rolling off high frequencies to remove the clinical brightness of digital audio), pitch flutter (very slight, slow pitch variation simulating tape wow and flutter), bit reduction (subtle digital degradation in the 12–16 bit range rather than full 8-bit), and acoustic reverb (small room ambience rather than large concert hall decay).

What lo-fi is not: distorted to the point of unpleasantness, so degraded that the harmonic content is lost, or mixed carelessly under the assumption that "lo-fi" means "no standards." The best lo-fi beats are precisely engineered to sound warm and imperfect, which is a different thing from being produced without care.

Tempo and Feel

Lo-fi hip-hop almost universally works in the 70–90 BPM range, with the sweet spot at 75–85 BPM. This tempo range creates the characteristic slow, contemplative groove that serves the genre's studying-and-relaxing listener context. At these tempos, a 16th-note hi-hat pattern feels unhurried; a half-time snare on beat 3 (or beats 2 and 4 with variation) creates the laid-back swing that defines the genre.

Set your DAW to 80 BPM as a starting point. You can adjust from there based on the feel of your source samples — if you're working with a jazz loop sampled from vinyl, match your project tempo to the loop's natural tempo rather than time-stretching it aggressively. Lo-fi's warmth depends on natural, unstretched audio.

Swing Quantization

Straight (100% quantized) grids kill the lo-fi feel. Every DAW has swing or groove quantization — use it. The target is 65–75% swing on 16th notes, which pushes the off-beats (the "e" and "a" of each beat) slightly late, creating the characteristic head-nod asymmetry of hip-hop rhythms.

Building Lo-Fi Drums

Lo-fi drums are built from acoustic samples — jazz kits, vintage soul recordings, boom bap hip-hop drums — rather than electronic or synthesized sounds. The acoustic origin gives them natural room ambience, transient character, and frequency content that responds correctly to lo-fi processing. An 808 or a TR-808 sample processed to sound lo-fi sounds wrong; a vintage jazz snare processed to sound lo-fi sounds right.

The Core Pattern

Start with a four-bar loop. The fundamental lo-fi drum pattern is simpler than most producers expect:

  • Kick: Beats 1 and 3, with occasional variation (a ghost kick on the "and" of 2 in bar 3)
  • Snare: Beat 3 for a half-time feel, or beats 2 and 4 with velocity variation for a more driving pattern
  • Hi-hat: 8th notes as a foundation, with occasional 16th-note flourishes on offbeats. Vary the open and closed pattern for feel.
  • Ghost snare: Quiet ghost notes (velocity 20–40% of the main snare) between the main hits create a human shuffle feel that is central to the genre's organic character

Drum Processing Chain

  1. Low-pass filter on the drum bus: cut above 10–12kHz to remove digital brightness
  2. Tape saturation: gentle harmonic coloring (3–6dB drive on a tape emulation plugin)
  3. Room reverb: small room or plate, short decay (0.4–0.8s), 15–25% wet
  4. Gentle bus compression: 2:1 ratio, slow attack (30–50ms), 2–3dB gain reduction for glue
  5. Bit crusher (optional): reduce to 14–16 bit for digital character without destroying detail

Keep velocity variation across every hit. A hi-hat that is exactly the same velocity every 8th note sounds programmed, not played. Randomize velocities by ±15–25% on ghost notes and ±10% on main hits. Most DAWs have a humanize function that does this automatically.

Jazz Chords and Harmonic Language

The harmonic identity of lo-fi hip-hop comes from jazz. The wistful, emotionally complex feeling of the genre's best work is created by extended chord voicings — seventh, ninth, and eleventh chords — that carry ambiguity and color in ways simple major and minor triads cannot.

The Essential Chord Types

Chord TypeExampleNotes
Minor 7thCm7 (C–Eb–G–Bb)The most foundational lo-fi chord. Dark, warm, unresolved.
Minor 9thDm9 (D–F–A–C–E)Minor 7th with added color. More complex and layered.
Major 7thFmaj7 (F–A–C–E)Dreamy, bright. Contrast against minor chords.
Dominant 7thG7 (G–B–D–F)Creates tension resolving to the tonic minor chord.
Half-diminishedBø7 (B–D–F–A)Maximum wistfulness. Handle carefully — powerful.
Minor 11thAm11 (A–C–E–G–B–D)Lush, dense. Best as a sustained pad rather than a voicing.

Simple Progressions to Start

The ii-V-i jazz movement is everywhere in lo-fi hip-hop because it creates natural emotional tension and resolution without ever feeling bombastic:

  • ii-V-i in C minor: Dm7b5 → G7 → Cm7
  • i-VII-VI-VII loop: Cm7 → Bbmaj7 → Abmaj7 → Bbmaj7 (circular, hypnotic)
  • i-iv loop: Dm7 → Gm7 (minimal two-chord meditation — hugely common in ambient lo-fi)
  • I-vi-ii-V: Fmaj7 → Dm7 → Gm7 → C7 (the jazz "rhythm changes" adapted for lo-fi)
Chord voicing tip:

Don't play chords in root position with a full bass note at the bottom of each voicing. Use inverted voicings and drop the bass notes down an octave or more from the chord body. A Cm7 voicing might have C in the bass two octaves below, with Eb–G–Bb voiced higher up as a three-note cluster. This creates the warm, open sound of jazz piano rather than the muddy, crowded sound of chords played in root position across multiple octaves.

Melody Construction

Lo-fi melodies are understated, repetitive, and emotionally direct. Unlike lead melodies in genres that reward complexity, lo-fi melody works best when it is simple enough to feel familiar by the second loop and emotionally evocative on the first listen.

Instruments that work best for lo-fi melody: electric piano (Rhodes or Wurlitzer samples), acoustic piano run through lo-fi processing, vibraphone and marimba samples, fretless bass as a lead voice, guitar run through tape and reverb, and simple synth patches with slow attack envelopes.

Keep the melody within a narrow range — typically a major sixth or minor seventh interval from lowest to highest note. This restraint creates intimacy. Large melodic leaps feel dramatic and out of place in a genre built on introspection.

Rhythmically, syncopation is essential. Melodies that land squarely on the beat feel mechanical. Push melodic phrases to start on the "and" of beats, or delay them slightly behind the grid using your DAW's nudge function. Quarter note and dotted quarter note values create the characteristic laid-back phrasing of the genre.

Sampling vs. Original Composition

The history of lo-fi hip-hop is built on vinyl sampling — producers like J Dilla, Nujabes, and early Madlib shaped the genre by chopping and pitching vinyl records. However, sampling copyrighted material without clearance is a legal and financial risk that has ended careers and channels. Most successful modern lo-fi producers on streaming platforms use original composition or royalty-free sources.

Royalty-Free Approach

Create your jazz chords and melodies from scratch using sampled instruments (a jazz piano sample library, a Rhodes sample pack, a live-recorded acoustic guitar). This produces work you own entirely with no clearance risk and unlimited commercial use.

Sources for royalty-free lo-fi samples: Splice (subscription, clear licensing), Looperman (free community loops with license information), BandLab Sounds (free, clear licensing), and sample packs from producers like Cxdy, Philanthrope, and other lo-fi artists who sell their toolkit packs.

If You Do Sample Vinyl

Sampling a melody or groove from a vinyl record without clearing the copyright is technically infringement regardless of how much it's processed. The lo-fi community has normalized this practice, and many bedroom producers distribute freely on YouTube or SoundCloud without incident. The risk increases significantly when monetizing on streaming platforms or YouTube's Content ID system. If you want to commercially release or monetize a sample-based beat, clear the sample or interpolate the melody rather than sampling directly.

Vinyl and Tape Texture: The Processing Chain

This is where lo-fi beats become lo-fi beats. The right processing chain applied correctly is the difference between a clean jazz beat and a lo-fi track. Here is the standard signal chain, applied at the mix bus or on individual elements:

The Lo-Fi Processing Chain (in order)

  1. EQ — High shelf cut: Roll off above 12–14kHz with a gentle shelf (-4 to -8dB). Removes digital brightness.
  2. Tape saturation: Drive tape emulation at 3–6dB. Even-order harmonics add warmth. Target the mix bus or individual stems. RC-20 Retro Color, Waves J37, or iZotope Ozone Exciter.
  3. Pitch flutter: Very slow, very subtle pitch modulation (0.01–0.03 semitones, 0.2–0.5Hz rate) simulating tape wow. RC-20 includes this. iZotope Vinyl's wow and flutter controls.
  4. Bit crusher (optional): Reduce from 24-bit to 14–16 bit. Creates subtle digital grit without destroying resolution. Audiority BitCrusher, Ableton's Redux, or Logic's Bitcrusher.
  5. Lo-pass filter: Final gentle rolloff above 10–11kHz after all processing. Smooths any harshness from the saturation chain.
  6. Vinyl noise layer: A separate audio track with vinyl crackle at -18 to -24dB below the mix bus. iZotope Vinyl (free) generates this. Or use a vinyl noise sample.

Arrangement

Lo-fi beats are typically four to eight bar loops extended to a full track duration of 2–4 minutes with very minimal arrangement changes. The listener experience is about the mood of the loop, not the development of a complex song structure. This is appropriate for the genre's primary use case — ambient focus music — and is not a failure of arrangement craft.

The arrangement toolkit for lo-fi: filter automation (slowly opening or closing a low-pass filter over 16–32 bars for organic movement), volume automation on the vinyl noise layer (increase slightly during quiet passages), occasional instrument drops (remove the melody for 4 bars to create space and anticipation), and very subtle reverb tail extensions at key points.

Avoid building to a dramatic drop or chorus structure — lo-fi beats live in a continuous, uninterrupted emotional state. The closest thing to a structural moment is a brief 2-bar half-time break (drums drop to minimal kick and snare) before the loop resumes.

Mixing Lo-Fi Beats

Mix levels in lo-fi are typically flatter (less dynamic contrast between elements) than in pop or hip-hop, because the genre's intimate listening context means listeners hear every element without the distance of a loud, punchy mix. The drums sit relatively low — not buried, but not dominant. The chords are the emotional center of the track and should be the loudest non-drum element. The melody sits just above the chord pad in the mix rather than dominating above everything else.

Stereo imaging: keep the kick and bass mono and centered. The chord pad and melody can be widened slightly (light chorus, stereo delay, or subtle panning). Avoid aggressive stereo widening — lo-fi's intimacy depends on a relatively centered, headphone-friendly image.

Mastering for Lo-Fi

Master lo-fi beats to -14 to -16 LUFS integrated — matching Spotify's normalization target and reinforcing the genre's quiet, intimate character. Aggressive limiting destroys the tape saturation character and the dynamic warmth that makes lo-fi feel analog. A gentle limiter with a ceiling at -1dBTP is sufficient — you don't need to drive into it hard.

Keep the true peak ceiling at -1dBTP for streaming platform compliance. Check your master in mono — lo-fi's centered image should hold up. If the chords collapse into mud when summed to mono, adjust your low-end balance or check for phase issues in the chord voicings.

Three Practice Exercises

Exercise 1 (Beginner): The Two-Chord Loop

Set your tempo to 80 BPM with 65% swing. Build a drum pattern with kick on 1 and 3, snare on 3 (half-time), and swung 8th-note hi-hats. Add a Dm7 chord on beat 1 and Gm7 on beat 3 of each bar using a Rhodes or piano sample. Apply iZotope Vinyl (free) to the mix bus. Loop for 4 bars and focus on getting the swing and the texture right before adding anything else.

Exercise 2 (Intermediate): The Jazz Chord Progression

Build a four-bar chord progression using the ii-V-i movement: Dm7b5 (two bars) → G7b9 (one bar) → Cm7 (one bar). Add a simple 4-note melody using C, Eb, G, and Bb — only these four notes. Process through the full lo-fi chain: tape saturation, pitch flutter, low-pass filter, vinyl noise. Add a drum pattern and mix so the chords are the loudest element, melody just above, drums below.

Exercise 3 (Advanced): Full Track in 90 Minutes

From scratch, build a complete 2-minute lo-fi beat with: a 4-bar drum loop with ghost notes and velocity variation, a 4-bar chord progression of at least 3 chord types, a 4-bar melody with syncopation and pitch variation, a vinyl noise layer, the complete processing chain on the mix bus, and a simple arrangement with at least one 4-bar section where an element is removed for contrast. Master to -15 LUFS. Evaluate in headphones and on speakers.

Distributing Lo-Fi Beats

The lo-fi hip-hop market is primarily built on YouTube streaming — channels like ChilledCow (now Lofi Girl) have built enormous audiences around continuous lo-fi livestreams. Submitting your beats to lo-fi YouTube channels is a legitimate distribution strategy that provides exposure, streaming revenue, and attribution.

For Spotify and other streaming platforms, distribute through DistroKid, TuneCore, or CD Baby. Lo-fi beats often work as standalone instrumentals rather than album tracks — a single strong beat released as a standalone single is a valid approach. Target the "lo-fi beats" and "study music" playlist categories in your Spotify for Artists editorial pitch. The audience for these categories is enormous and loyal.

Frequently Asked Questions

What BPM is lo-fi hip-hop?

70–90 BPM, with the sweet spot at 75–85 BPM. This tempo creates the characteristic slow, relaxed groove without losing forward movement.

Do I need to sample vinyl to make lo-fi beats?

No. Original jazz chords and melodies processed through lo-fi plugins create the same aesthetic. Original composition also avoids copyright clearance risk entirely.

What are the best free lo-fi plugins?

iZotope Vinyl (free vinyl texture), Ableton Redux or Logic Bitcrusher (built-in bit reduction), and royalty-free vinyl noise samples. These three cover the essential lo-fi processing chain at zero cost.

What chords are used in lo-fi music?

Extended jazz chords: minor 7th, minor 9th, major 7th, and dominant 7th. The ii-V-i jazz progression (e.g., Dm7 → G7 → Cm7) is foundational to the genre's harmonic vocabulary.

How loud should lo-fi beats be mastered?

-14 to -16 LUFS integrated — matching Spotify's normalization and reinforcing the genre's quiet, intimate character. Avoid aggressive limiting that destroys the analog warmth.

Can I make lo-fi beats without an MPC or hardware?

Yes, entirely in software. Any DAW with swing quantization, sample support, and basic plugin processing can produce professional lo-fi beats. Many successful lo-fi producers work entirely in GarageBand or FL Studio with no hardware.
