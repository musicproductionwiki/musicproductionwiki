What Is Mastering Music

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 · 9 min read · Verified May 7, 2026
Quick Answer — Updated May 2026

Mastering is the final step in music production where a finished stereo mix is processed and optimized for distribution. It uses EQ, compression, limiting, and loudness normalization to ensure a track sounds polished and consistent across every playback system and streaming platform. Mastering cannot fix a bad mix — the mix must be right before mastering begins.

Mastering is the final step in music production before a track reaches the listener. After mixing — where individual elements are balanced and processed inside a session — mastering takes the finished stereo mix and prepares it for distribution. It is the bridge between your mix and every speaker, headphone, streaming encoder, and listening environment where your music will be heard. Updated May 2026.

Mixing vs. Mastering — The Critical Distinction

Mixing happens inside your DAW session with access to every individual track — the kick drum, the lead vocal, the bass, the pads. A mix engineer shapes each element separately: EQ on the snare, compression on the vocal, reverb on the keys. The mix engineer's job is to create a coherent, balanced arrangement from all these individual elements.

Mastering works only with the finished stereo export — a single audio file. The mastering engineer cannot go back into the session and turn down the kick or add reverb to the vocal. Everything in mastering happens to the entire stereo image simultaneously. This constraint is precisely why mastering requires such refined listening skills: you cannot fix problems individually, only address them globally.

The best analogy: mixing is cooking a dish — choosing ingredients, balancing flavours, deciding what goes with what. Mastering is plating and presenting that dish — ensuring it looks right, the temperature is correct, the portion is appropriate for the context. A great mix badly mastered loses commercial viability. Learn more in our complete beginner's guide to mixing.

Critical rule: Mastering cannot fix a bad mix. If the low end is a mess, the vocals are buried, or the arrangement is fighting itself, mastering will make those problems slightly less obvious at best. The mix must be right before mastering begins.

What Mastering Actually Does

Mastering applies several types of processing to the finished stereo mix, each addressing a different aspect of the final sound.

Tonal balance correction via EQ. Even after a careful mix, the stereo export often has residual tonal imbalances. Mastering EQ corrects these with subtle moves — typically 1–3 dB rather than the dramatic sculpting used in mixing. A slight high-frequency lift adds air and presence. A 200 Hz reduction tightens a muddy low-mid buildup. The goal is translation: ensuring the track sounds tonally correct on systems that apply no EQ of their own.

Dynamics control via compression. Mastering compression is applied gently — ratios of 1.5:1 to 2:1, slow attack, slow release, limiting gain reduction to 1–3 dB. The purpose is not to squash dynamics but to glue the stereo mix together, adding weight and cohesion. Many engineers use multiple compressors in series, each doing very little, for a more transparent result. See our deep-dive on bus compression techniques for the same principles applied at the mix stage.

Stereo width and mid/side processing. Mid/side (M/S) processing lets mastering engineers work independently on the centre of the stereo image (kick, bass, lead vocals) and the sides (reverb, width, ambience). Widening the sides while keeping the centre tight creates the impression of a bigger mix without phase problems. Reducing low frequencies in the sides keeps the bass mono — critical for club systems and vinyl compatibility.

Harmonic saturation and character. Many mastering engineers pass the mix through analog hardware or saturation plugins to add subtle harmonic content — even harmonics from tape and transformers, odd harmonics from tube processing. This adds warmth and dimension that purely digital processing cannot replicate.

Brickwall limiting and loudness maximization. The final stage is a brickwall limiter, which sets the absolute ceiling of the master and allows the overall loudness to be raised to competitive levels without digital clipping. This is where limiter technique becomes critical — push too hard and you destroy dynamics; not hard enough and the track sounds quiet on streaming platforms.

The mastering signal chain: stereo mix → EQ → compression → saturation → brickwall limiter → master file

LUFS Loudness Targets for Streaming

LUFS stands for Loudness Units Full Scale — the industry-standard measurement of perceived loudness that accounts for how the human ear hears different frequencies. Streaming platforms use LUFS to normalize all tracks to a consistent playback level. Understanding these targets is essential for competitive masters. See also our article on how to master a song at home for a complete workflow.

PlatformTarget LUFS (Integrated)True Peak Ceiling
Spotify-14 LUFS-1.0 dBTP
Apple Music-16 LUFS-1.0 dBTP
YouTube-14 LUFS-1.0 dBTP
Club / EDM-9 to -7 LUFS-0.3 dBTP
CD-9 to -12 LUFS (typical)-0.3 dBTP

Always set your true peak ceiling at -1.0 dBTP to survive lossy encoding (AAC, MP3) without inter-sample clipping. For club music and EDM, -9 to -7 LUFS is standard practice.

How to Prepare Your Mix for Mastering

Leave your mix peaking around -6 dBFS with no limiting or clipping on the mix bus. Export at 24-bit at your session's native sample rate. Do not apply a brickwall limiter to the mix bus before sending for mastering — that headroom is what the mastering chain needs to work effectively. A mix with limiting already baked in gives the mastering engineer almost nothing to work with and often results in a louder but dynamically crushed master.

Tools Used in Mastering

For beginners, iZotope Ozone is the most complete and accessible mastering suite. Its Master Assistant AI analyzes your track and suggests starting settings, making it viable for self-mastering on independent releases. Pair it with Youlean Loudness Meter (free) for accurate LUFS measurement. Read the full iZotope Ozone 12 review for a detailed breakdown of its modules.

FabFilter Pro-L 2 is the gold-standard brickwall limiter for producers building a more custom chain. Its True Peak limiting mode and per-sample oversampling make it the preferred final stage for professional masters. For EQ, FabFilter Pro-Q 4 offers M/S mode and dynamic EQ capabilities that cover most mastering EQ tasks — see our FabFilter Pro-Q 4 review.

For major commercial releases, hiring a professional mastering engineer remains the gold standard. The key limitation of self-mastering is ear fatigue and the lack of fresh perspective after spending days inside the mix. A professional mastering engineer brings trained ears, a calibrated monitoring environment, and experience across thousands of tracks.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ What is mastering in music production?
Mastering is the final step in audio production where a finished stereo mix is processed and optimized for distribution. It uses EQ, compression, limiting, and loudness normalization to ensure the track sounds polished and consistent across all playback systems and platforms.
FAQ What does a mastering engineer actually do?
A mastering engineer listens critically to a finished mix, applies processing to correct tonal imbalances and dynamic issues, sets the final loudness to meet platform specifications, and delivers master files in the correct format for streaming, CD, or vinyl.
FAQ Do I need to master my music?
Yes. Streaming platforms normalize loudness, so unmastered tracks sound quieter and less polished than mastered ones. Mastering ensures consistent sound across all platforms and playback systems, which is essential for any professional release.
FAQ What is the difference between mixing and mastering?
Mixing works with individual tracks inside a session, balancing and processing each element separately. Mastering works only with the finished stereo mix as a single audio file, polishing and preparing it for distribution — you cannot return to individual elements during mastering.
