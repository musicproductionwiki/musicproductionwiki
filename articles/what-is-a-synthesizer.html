What Is A Synthesizer

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 · 7 min read · Verified May 7, 2026
Quick Answer — Updated May 2026

A synthesizer is an electronic instrument that generates sound by creating audio signals from scratch — using oscillators, filters, and amplifiers — rather than recording acoustic sources. Unlike samplers, synths build tones mathematically or electronically, giving producers total control over timbre, pitch, and dynamics. They appear as hardware units, software plugins (VSTs), and increasingly as hybrid instruments combining both worlds.

How a Synthesizer Generates Sound

At its core, a synthesizer produces sound by generating an electrical signal and then sculpting that signal into a usable tone. Every synth — whether a 1970s Minimoog or a modern software plugin — relies on the same fundamental chain: oscillator → filter → amplifier. The oscillator creates a raw waveform (sine, sawtooth, square, triangle, or noise). The filter shapes its harmonic content, most commonly by cutting high frequencies (a low-pass filter). The amplifier controls volume over time using an envelope — typically defined by attack, decay, sustain, and release (ADSR) stages.

Modulation is what brings a synth to life. An LFO (low-frequency oscillator) can wobble pitch to create vibrato, sweep the filter cutoff for that classic "wah" movement, or pulse the volume for tremolo. Envelope generators can be routed to almost any parameter, making sounds that evolve naturally from the moment a key is pressed to the moment it releases.

The classic synthesizer signal path: Oscillator → Filter → Amplifier → Output, with modulation sources (LFO, ADSR envelopes) shaping every stage.

The Main Types of Synthesis

There are several synthesis methods, each with a distinct sonic character. Understanding them helps you pick the right tool for any sound design task.

Synthesis Type How It Works Known For Famous Example
Subtractive Starts with harmonically rich waveform; filters remove content Warm basses, leads, classic pads Minimoog, Moog Sub 37
FM (Frequency Modulation) Oscillators modulate each other's frequency Electric pianos, metallic bells, punchy basses Yamaha DX7, Native Instruments FM8
Wavetable Cycles through stored single-cycle waveforms Evolving pads, modern leads, hyperpop textures Xfer Serum, Native Instruments Massive X
Additive Layers individual sine waves to build complex tones Precise timbral control, organ-like sounds Hammond organ concept, Camel Audio Alchemy
Sample-Based Uses recorded audio as oscillator source Realistic acoustic instruments, hybrid textures Native Instruments Kontakt
Physical Modeling Mathematically simulates physical acoustics Acoustic instruments, plucked strings, bowed textures Applied Acoustics Chromaphone

Analog vs. Digital Synthesizers

Analog synths generate sound using real voltage fluctuations through transistor-based circuits. Producers prize them for their warmth, slight imperfections, and the way they respond to touch — the Moog Subsequent 37 and Roland Juno-106 are perennial studio favorites. Digital synths represent sound as numbers, enabling complex algorithms like FM and wavetable synthesis that would be impractical in pure analog hardware. Software synthesizers (VSTs and AU plugins) are digital instruments running inside your DAW of choice, giving you near-infinite polyphony and recall at a fraction of the hardware cost.

Producer Tip

The "analog warmth" debate matters less than workflow. Many top producers build entire records with software synths like Serum or Massive X, then run the audio through analog hardware or saturation plugins to add harmonic richness. Choose the tool that matches your budget and creative speed.

Hardware Synths vs. Software (VST) Synths

Hardware synthesizers are standalone instruments with physical knobs, keys, and circuits. They excel for tactile, hands-on performance and are ideal for live settings. Software synthesizers run as plugins inside your DAW — Ableton Live, FL Studio, Logic Pro — and offer instant parameter recall, unlimited presets, and CPU-limited polyphony. For producers building a home studio, a strong software synth library paired with a quality MIDI controller covers virtually every production scenario without the cost or desk space of multiple hardware units.

Common beginner-friendly software synths include Vital (free), Xfer Serum ($189), and Native Instruments Massive X (included with Komplete bundles). For hardware entry points, the Arturia MiniFreak ($399) and Korg Minilogue XD ($499) offer deep sound design in compact, affordable packages.

Synthesizers Across Music Genres

Synths are genre-agnostic workhorses. In trap production, detuned 808 bass synths define the low end. In house and techno, analogue-style lead synths drive the groove. Cinematic composers rely on lush wavetable pads and physical-modeling strings, while hyperpop producers push wavetable synths into deliberately harsh, pitch-bent territory. Even country and folk producers increasingly layer subtle synth pads behind acoustic instruments for modern radio appeal. Understanding what synthesis method underpins a sound lets you reverse-engineer almost any patch you hear.

Getting Started With Synthesis

The fastest way to learn synthesis is to start with a subtractive synth — the signal path is visual and logical. Load a basic sawtooth preset, then slowly close the filter cutoff until the sound thins out. Add filter envelope depth so the tone blooms on each note. Once that click moment happens, the rest of synthesis opens up naturally. Pair your exploration with solid fundamentals: understanding MIDI routing in your DAW ensures you can trigger and record synth parts cleanly from day one. From there, dive into FM synthesis for electric piano sounds and wavetable synths for modern leads — and you will have the core toolkit to program virtually any synthesized sound in contemporary music production.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ What is the difference between a synthesizer and a sampler?
A synthesizer generates sound from scratch using oscillators, filters, and amplifiers. A sampler plays back recorded audio clips of real instruments or sounds, triggered by MIDI notes.
FAQ Do I need a hardware synthesizer to make professional music?
No. Virtually all chart-level music is produced with software synthesizers (VSTs) running inside a DAW. Hardware synths offer a tactile workflow advantage but are not a prerequisite for professional results.
FAQ What is subtractive synthesis?
Subtractive synthesis starts with a harmonically rich waveform and removes (subtracts) frequencies using a filter, shaping the final tone. It is the most common and beginner-friendly synthesis method.
FAQ What does an LFO do on a synthesizer?
An LFO (Low-Frequency Oscillator) modulates parameters like pitch, filter cutoff, or volume at slow rates, creating effects such as vibrato, filter sweeps, and tremolo.
FAQ What is FM synthesis?
FM (Frequency Modulation) synthesis creates sound by having one oscillator (the modulator) modulate the frequency of another oscillator (the carrier), producing complex, metallic, or bell-like timbres. The Yamaha DX7 is the most famous FM synthesizer.
FAQ What is wavetable synthesis?
Wavetable synthesis cycles through short recordings of single-cycle waveforms stored in a table. As the playback position moves through the table, the timbre evolves, enabling the rich, morphing sounds common in modern pop and EDM production.
FAQ Is Serum a good synthesizer for beginners?
Serum by Xfer Records is excellent for beginners due to its visual, drag-and-drop wavetable editor and large library of presets. Its interface makes synthesis concepts easy to understand visually, though FL Studio's built-in 3xOsc or the free Vital synth are more cost-effective starting points.
FAQ What is polyphony on a synthesizer?
Polyphony refers to the number of notes a synthesizer can play simultaneously. A monophonic synth plays one note at a time (common for bass and lead lines), while a polyphonic synth can sustain multiple notes for chords and pads.
