What Is A Synthesizer

What Is a Synthesizer?

A synthesizer is an electronic instrument that generates sound through electronic signal processing rather than acoustic vibration. Where a guitar creates sound by vibrating strings over a resonating body, and a piano creates sound by hammers striking strings, a synthesizer creates sound by generating electrical waveforms and shaping them through a series of modules: oscillators, filters, amplifiers, and modulation sources.

The fundamental power of synthesis is its scope — a synthesizer can produce any sound, from faithful recreations of acoustic instruments to sounds that have never existed in the physical world. This makes it simultaneously the most versatile and the most complex instrument ever created. Synthesizers appear across every genre of modern music: the bass in trap, the leads in EDM, the pads in ambient music, the electric piano in R&B, the arpeggiated sequences in synthwave — all synthesizers.

Brief History of the Synthesizer

The modern synthesizer era begins with Robert Moog's Moog synthesizer in 1964, which introduced voltage-controlled oscillators and filters in a modular format. The Minimoog (1970) made this accessible in a portable, non-modular instrument and appeared on countless records throughout the 1970s.

The 1980s brought digital synthesis. Yamaha's DX7 (1983), using FM synthesis, redefined the decade's sound — its electric piano patch is possibly the most recognizable synthesizer preset in history. The Roland Jupiter-8, Juno-106, and Sequential Circuits Prophet-5 established the analog polyphonic synthesizer as a studio staple.

The VST plugin format, introduced by Steinberg in 1996, ultimately shifted the majority of synthesis from hardware to software. By the 2010s, plugins like Native Instruments Massive and Xfer Serum redefined what a software synthesizer could do. In 2026, Serum remains the most-used synthesizer in professional pop and electronic production; Vital, its free open-source competitor, has accumulated a massive user base since its 2020 launch.

Oscillator Types and Waveforms

The oscillator is the synthesizer's sound generator — it produces the raw waveform that all other modules then shape. The shape of the oscillator's waveform determines its harmonic content, which determines its base timbre before any filtering or processing.

Synthesis Methods

Subtractive synthesis starts with a harmonically rich waveform (saw or square) and uses filters to remove harmonics, sculpting the tone. It's the foundation of classic analog synths and is ideal for bass, leads, and pads. If you're making trap beats, subtractive synthesis is behind most of the dark 808 stabs and melodic leads you hear.

FM (Frequency Modulation) synthesis uses one oscillator (the modulator) to modulate the frequency of another (the carrier), producing complex, bright, and bell-like harmonic structures. The Yamaha DX7's electric piano patch is the definitive FM sound. FM excels at metallic tones, electric pianos, and aggressive digital leads.

Wavetable synthesis stores short single-cycle waveforms and scans through them to create timbral evolution. Serum and Vital are the dominant wavetable synthesizers in 2026 — their visual wavetable displays make the concept intuitive. Wavetable synthesis is central to modern EDM and hyperpop sound design. For a deeper look at how these fit into a production workflow, see the best plugins for beginners.

Granular synthesis splits audio into tiny grains (typically 1–100ms) and reassembles them, enabling extreme time-stretching and textural morphing. It's a key tool for cinematic sound design and ambient music.

ADSR Envelope

ADSR stands for Attack, Decay, Sustain, Release — the four stages of an envelope that shapes how a sound evolves over time. Understanding ADSR is foundational to making your first beat and programming expressive synthesizer parts.

ADSR Quick Reference:
Attack — How quickly the sound rises to peak level after a key is pressed.
Decay — How quickly it falls from peak to the sustain level.
Sustain — The level held while a key is held down.
Release — How long the sound fades after the key is released.
Slow attack = pads and swells. Fast attack, short release = plucks and stabs.

Filters and LFO Modulation

The low-pass filter is the most essential tone-shaping tool in subtractive synthesis. It passes frequencies below the cutoff point and attenuates frequencies above it. Closing the filter (lowering the cutoff) makes sounds darker and warmer; opening it brightens the sound. The resonance control boosts a narrow band around the cutoff frequency, adding a characteristic peak or "squelch" — the signature sound of classic acid basslines.

An LFO (Low Frequency Oscillator) generates a slow-cycling waveform — typically below 20Hz, too slow to perceive as pitch — that modulates other synthesizer parameters. Assigning an LFO to pitch creates vibrato; to volume creates tremolo; to filter cutoff creates an automatic filter sweep. LFOs are one of the primary tools for adding movement to otherwise static synth patches. Understanding LFO-to-filter modulation is essential for producers making EDM or any genre built on evolving textures.

Hardware vs Software Synths in 2026

Software synthesizers (VST/AU plugins) dominate music production in 2026 for practical reasons: they are instantly recallable within DAW sessions, cost far less than hardware equivalents, and their visual interfaces make synthesis concepts easier to learn. Serum (~$189) and Vital (free) are the recommended starting points for new producers.

Hardware synthesizers offer tactile, hands-on control and specific analog character — the non-linear behavior of analog circuits introduces warmth and slight imperfections that many producers deliberately seek. Hardware is better approached once you understand synthesis fundamentals. Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ What is a synthesizer in simple terms?
A synthesizer is an electronic instrument that generates sound by creating and shaping electrical signals. Unlike a piano or guitar which physically vibrate strings or air columns, a synthesizer builds sounds mathematically from scratch using oscillators, filters, and amplifiers — which means it can create any sound imaginable, from convincing real instruments to sounds that have never existed in nature.
FAQ What is the difference between subtractive and FM synthesis?
Subtractive synthesis starts with a harmonically rich waveform (saw or square wave) and uses filters to remove harmonics, shaping the tone. FM (Frequency Modulation) synthesis uses one oscillator to modulate the frequency of another, creating complex, bright, and bell-like sounds. Subtractive excels at bass and lead sounds; FM excels at metallic, electric piano, and digital tones.
FAQ What does ADSR mean on a synthesizer?
ADSR stands for Attack, Decay, Sustain, Release — the four stages of an envelope. Attack: how quickly the sound rises to its peak. Decay: how quickly it falls from peak to the sustain level. Sustain: the level held while a key is pressed. Release: how long the sound fades after the key is released. ADSR shapes the volume contour of every synthesizer note.
FAQ What is a low-pass filter on a synth?
A low-pass filter passes frequencies below the cutoff frequency and attenuates frequencies above it. On a synthesizer, closing the filter (lowering the cutoff) makes sounds darker and warmer. Opening the filter (raising the cutoff) brightens the sound. It's the most fundamental tone-shaping tool in subtractive synthesis — the filter cutoff sweep is one of the most recognizable synthesizer sounds in music.
FAQ What is wavetable synthesis?
Wavetable synthesis stores short single-cycle waveforms (wavetables) and plays them back, scanning through them to create timbral evolution. Rather than generating waveforms in real time like subtractive synthesis, wavetable synthesis reads stored wave shapes. Serum and Vital are the most popular wavetable synthesizers in 2026 — their visual wavetable displays make the concept intuitive to understand.
FAQ Should I start with hardware or software synths?
Start with software (VST plugins). Software synths are free or inexpensive, instantly recallable in your DAW sessions, and offer visual interfaces that make synthesis concepts easier to learn. Hardware synths are more tactile and can inspire creativity differently, but they're better approached once you understand synthesis fundamentals. Vital (free) and Serum (~$189) are the recommended starting points in 2026.
FAQ What is an LFO on a synthesizer?
LFO stands for Low Frequency Oscillator. It generates a slow-moving waveform (typically below 20Hz — too slow to hear as pitch) that modulates other synthesizer parameters. Assigning an LFO to pitch creates vibrato; to volume creates tremolo; to filter cutoff creates an automatic filter sweep. LFOs are one of the primary modulation sources in any synthesizer.
FAQ What synth makes the bass sound in trap music?
Trap 808 bass is created by an 808 drum machine's bass drum — a sine wave pitch envelope that decays from high to low, producing the signature sub-bass slide. Most producers in 2026 use 808 samples processed through a DAW rather than actual Roland TR-808 hardware. Plugins like Serum with a sine-wave oscillator, heavy sub-bass boost, and pitch envelope automation can replicate this sound from scratch.
