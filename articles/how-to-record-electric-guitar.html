How to Record Electric Guitar at Home: The Complete Guide

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 13 min read Beginner–Intermediate

Recording electric guitar at home used to require a loud amp, a good microphone, and an acoustically treated room. In 2026, the three best options for home studio electric guitar recording are amp simulator plugins, DI recording with cabinet simulation, and — for those who can manage the volume — traditional amp and microphone recording. Each approach produces professional results; the choice depends on your living situation, budget, and workflow preferences.

What you'll learn:

The three recording methods (DI with amp sim, mic on cabinet, re-amping), signal chain setup, microphone placement for speaker cabinets, the best amp simulator plugins, gain staging for guitar DI, double-tracking for stereo width, and noise reduction techniques.

The Three Recording Methods

Method 1: DI Into Audio Interface + Amp Simulator Plugin (Recommended for Home Studios)

Connect guitar directly to the HI-Z instrument input on your audio interface. Record a clean, unprocessed DI signal into your DAW. Apply an amp simulator plugin to the recorded track — Neural DSP, Line 6 Helix Native, or similar — to create the guitar tone in the software. No amp required, no microphone required, no neighbor complaints. You can change the amp tone after recording, try different amp models on the same performance, and mix at any volume. This is the standard professional approach for home studio recording in 2026.

Method 2: Real Amp + Microphone

Use a real guitar amplifier and speaker cabinet, mic the speaker with a dynamic microphone (Shure SM57 is the industry standard), and record through an audio interface. Produces the most realistic amp character — some guitarists prefer the way real amps feel and respond to playing dynamics. Requires adequate volume (at least moderate apartment volume), a microphone, and acoustic consideration for the recording space. The traditional approach used in professional studios.

Method 3: DI Recording + Re-Amping Later

Record a clean DI signal now. Send that DI signal through a real amp and cabinet at a later time (in a different location, a professional studio session, or when the right amp is available). Re-amping separates performance from tone — the guitarist focuses entirely on playing, and the amp tone is dialed in separately. The clean DI recording preserves the performance indefinitely and can be re-amplified any number of times. Used extensively in professional production where guitarists track at home and tone decisions happen later.

Setting Up DI Recording

DI guitar recording requires three things: your guitar, an audio interface with a HI-Z (high-impedance) instrument input, and a cable. The instrument input on your interface — labeled "HI-Z," "Inst," or "Instrument" — provides the correct impedance for a guitar pickup. Do not plug directly into a line-level input without HI-Z switching, as this produces a thin, weak signal that doesn't represent the guitar's actual output level or character.

Signal chain: Guitar → standard TS instrument cable → HI-Z input on audio interface → DAW recording track

Gain staging: Set the input gain so the DI signal peaks between -18 and -12dBFS during normal playing. Guitar DI signals have very high peak-to-average ratios — a single plucked note can peak 10–15dB louder than sustained chords. Setting gain conservatively prevents clipping on dynamic passages. The amp simulator plugin adds the apparent loudness in the processed chain; the clean DI doesn't need to be loud.

Ground loop hum with single-coil pickups: Stratocasters, Telecasters, and other single-coil guitars are susceptible to electromagnetic hum from nearby electronics — computer monitors, LED lighting, phone chargers, and power supplies. If you hear a 60Hz (USA) or 50Hz (Europe) hum in the DI signal, move the guitar away from your computer screen, switch to 3-way position on a Stratocaster (hum-canceling positions are 2 and 4 — the in-between positions), or use a noise gate plugin with a fast attack to silence the noise floor between phrases.

Amp Simulator Plugins

Amp simulation quality has reached a level where professional recording guitarists regularly use software exclusively. The Neural DSP ecosystem in particular has set a new standard with convolution-based impulse responses for speaker cabinet simulation and carefully modeled circuit-level accuracy for specific amplifiers.

  • Neural DSP (various artists)~$15/mo or ~$150 eachArchetype: Plini, Gojira, Tim Henson, Cory Wong — each modeled on a specific artist's rig. Industry-leading accuracy. The recommended starting point for any genre.
  • Line 6 Helix Native~$399 or $99/yrPlugin version of the Helix hardware floor unit. Comprehensive amp, cab, and effect library covering 100+ amp models. The standard for session guitarists and touring musicians.
  • BIAS AMP 2 (Positive Grid)~$99Allows custom amp building by swapping individual components — tubes, transformers, tone stacks. Excellent for dialing in very specific tones. Tone Match feature for matching existing amp tones.

  • Guitar Rig 7 (Native Instruments)~$199 (included in Komplete)Long-established comprehensive amp sim. Good integration with Maschine and Komplete workflow for producers already in the NI ecosystem.
  • Ignite Amps (various)FreeMultiple free amp simulators of surprising quality: Emissary (high-gain), NadIR (cabinet loader). Starting point for producers who want to test amp sims before purchasing.

Microphone Placement on a Cabinet

For producers who have an amp and want to mic it, microphone placement determines the tone more than almost any other variable. The speaker cone has dramatically different frequency content from center to edge, and the off-axis angle changes the high-frequency character significantly.

Starting position (the industry standard): Shure SM57, on-axis (microphone capsule pointed directly at the speaker), touching or within 1cm of the grille cloth, aimed at the junction between the dust cap (center dome) and the main cone. This position gives a balanced tone — neither too bright nor too dark — that works for most genres as a starting point.

Two-microphone technique: A common studio approach uses two microphones: an SM57 close on the cap edge (on-axis, touching grille) and a condenser microphone (AKG C414, Rode NT1, or similar) positioned 1–3 feet from the cabinet to capture room ambience. Blend the two signals in the mix — the SM57 provides midrange presence and definition; the condenser provides space and air. Check phase alignment between the two signals; phase cancellation can thin the combined sound significantly.

Double-Tracking for Stereo Width

A single guitar recording is a mono signal. In most rock, pop, and metal productions, electric guitars are wider than mono — achieved through double-tracking: recording the same part twice, panning one take hard left and the other hard right.

Double-tracked guitars create width because the two performances are not identical — tiny timing and pitch differences between the takes create a natural stereo image that sounds organic and wide. This is why double-tracked guitars sound larger and more powerful than artificially widened mono guitars, even when a stereo widener plugin could technically create a similar width measurement.

For bedroom producers without room for performance variation: record the same part twice and pan them. The takes will be slightly different. If you have pitch correction on both takes, turn it off — the natural variation is the point. Alternatively, use a short delay (10–20ms on one side) to create artificial double-tracking, though this sounds less organic than actual performance doubling.

Mixing Electric Guitar

After recording, the processing chain for electric guitar in a mix:

  1. High-pass filter: Cut below 80–100Hz. Guitar content below this range is mostly noise and low-mid mud. The bass guitar and kick drum occupy the sub range; guitar should stay above this boundary.
  2. Presence EQ: Electric guitar's characteristic frequency range is 1–5kHz. A gentle boost here (2–4dB) gives guitars cutting power in dense mixes without sounding harsh.
  3. Compression: Light compression (2:1 to 4:1 ratio, medium attack 20–40ms, medium release 100–200ms, 2–4dB GR) evens out dynamics. Heavy compression changes the feel of the guitar significantly — use sparingly unless that compressed sustain character is intentional.
  4. Reverb (on a send): A short room or plate reverb (0.5–1.2s decay) adds space without pushing the guitar back in the mix. Use a send, not an insert.
  5. Stereo placement: For double-tracked guitars, pan L100 and R100. For a single guitar part, position where it serves the arrangement — often L40–L60 or R40–R60 to leave the center for vocals and kick.

Frequently Asked Questions

Do I need an amp to record electric guitar at home?

No. Record guitar DI (direct into your audio interface's HI-Z instrument input) and use amp simulator plugins in your DAW. Neural DSP, Line 6 Helix Native, and many others produce professional results without any physical amp.

What is the best microphone for recording guitar amp?

Shure SM57 — it appears on more electric guitar recordings than any other microphone. On-axis, touching the grille, aimed at the cap edge. For a second microphone to add room and air, add a condenser (AKG C414, Rode NT1) positioned 1–3 feet back.

What are the best amp simulator plugins?

Neural DSP leads on accuracy and realism. Line 6 Helix Native covers the widest range of amp models. BIAS AMP 2 allows custom amp building. For free options, Ignite Amps provides surprisingly capable free amp sims as a starting point.

What is DI guitar recording?

Plugging the guitar directly into an audio interface's HI-Z instrument input without going through an amp. The raw direct signal is captured cleanly and amp simulation is applied afterward in the DAW using plugins. No amp, no microphone, no volume issues.

How do I get stereo electric guitar?

Double-track — record the same part twice and pan one take hard left, one hard right. The slight timing and pitch differences between takes create a natural, wide stereo image. This is how most rock and metal recordings achieve their guitar width.

What gain staging should I use for guitar DI?

Set input gain so the DI signal peaks at -18 to -12dBFS. Guitar DI has very high peak-to-average ratios — individual plucked notes peak much louder than sustained chords. Conservative gain staging prevents clipping on dynamic passages. The amp sim adds apparent loudness in the processing chain.
