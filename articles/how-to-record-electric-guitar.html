How To Record Electric Guitar

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 · 19 min read · Verified May 7, 2026
Quick Answer — Updated May 2026

Record electric guitar at home by either miking a real amplifier with a Shure SM57 through an audio interface, or going direct (DI) into your interface and using amp simulator plugins like Neural DSP or Line 6 Helix Native. Both methods produce professional results in 2026. Choose DI for silent, flexible recording or mic a real amp for natural speaker dynamics and room character.

Updated May 2026 by The Music Production Wiki Team

Recording electric guitar at home has never been more practical. The gap between home and professional studio guitar recordings has closed dramatically over the past decade, driven by two parallel developments: amp simulator plugins that are now genuinely indistinguishable from real amplifiers in most contexts, and audio interfaces that handle guitar DI recording at prices accessible to any producer.

This guide covers both approaches — real amp recording and DI with amp sims — so you can choose the right method for your setup and produce professional-quality guitar tracks without leaving your home. Whether you are a bedroom producer adding guitar to beats, a guitarist building your first home studio, or an engineer looking to refine your recording technique, this guide gives you the complete picture.

The Two Methods: Real Amp vs. DI + Amp Sim

Every home guitar recording begins with a fundamental choice: record through a physical amplifier and microphone, or record direct (DI) through your audio interface and use an amp simulator plugin to create the tone in software. Both approaches produce excellent results in 2026. The right choice depends on your space, gear, neighbours, and the tonal character you are after.

Real Amp + Microphone

  • Natural amp dynamics and feel
  • Room acoustics add character that is difficult to replicate in software
  • Zero monitoring latency — you hear the amp directly
  • Requires physical space and meaningful volume levels
  • Neighbour and noise concerns are a real limitation
  • More setup and equipment required

DI + Amp Simulator

  • Silent recording — no amp noise whatsoever
  • Record a clean DI signal, then re-amp or re-process after the session
  • Minimal gear: just an audio interface and a plugin
  • Tone is infinitely adjustable after recording
  • Requires a low-latency monitoring setup to feel natural while playing
  • Best plugins cost $100–$400

Gear You Need

Audio Interface

The audio interface converts the analog signal from your guitar or microphone into digital audio your DAW can record. For electric guitar, you need an interface with a high-impedance instrument input — labeled Hi-Z or Inst on the front panel. This presents the correct impedance load to your guitar's pickups, preserving tone and clarity. Plugging a guitar into a standard line-level input without Hi-Z loses high-frequency content and produces a thin, lifeless tone.

The Focusrite Scarlett 2i2 Gen 4 ($199) is the most widely used interface for home guitar recording, covering both microphone and DI recording with two high-quality preamps and front-panel Hi-Z inputs. The Universal Audio Volt 2 ($169) adds a vintage 1176-style compression circuit on the input, which some guitarists use to add natural compression and saturation to their DI signal before it hits the DAW. Either is more than capable for professional-quality guitar recording. For a deeper comparison of interface options at different price points, see our guide to the best audio interfaces for guitarists.

If you are recording a miked amp and a DI signal simultaneously — which is an excellent practice for maximum flexibility — you will need an interface with at least two inputs. The Scarlett 2i2 handles this scenario perfectly.

Microphone — For Amp Recording

The Shure SM57 ($99) is the industry standard for electric guitar cabinet recording and has appeared on more guitar tracks than any other microphone in history. It handles the high sound pressure levels produced by guitar cabinets without distortion, has a frequency response that complements electric guitar's midrange character, and is physically robust enough for close-mic positioning over years of use.

If you are adding only one microphone for guitar recording, the SM57 is the correct choice without exception. Its slight presence peak in the 5–10 kHz range helps guitar cut through a dense mix, and its tighter proximity effect at close distances adds useful low-mid body to the recorded tone.

For a more complete sound, a condenser microphone positioned 30–60 cm from the cabinet captures room ambience and adds air and dimension to the SM57's close-mic signal. The AKG C414 ($399) or a budget condenser like the Audio-Technica AT2020 ($99) blend with the SM57 to create a fuller, more three-dimensional tone. If you want to explore condenser options in depth, our condenser vs. dynamic microphone guide covers the tradeoffs in detail. Start with the SM57 dominant in the blend and add the room mic gradually until you achieve the dimension you want.

Amp Simulators — For DI Recording

Amp simulator quality in 2026 is genuinely exceptional. The gap between the best software amp simulation and a real miked amplifier is now small enough that most listeners cannot identify which is which in a blind test when both are well-executed.

Neural DSP plugins are considered the benchmark for modern amp simulation and use neural network capture technology to model real amplifiers with audibly accurate results. Artist-specific plugins include Archetype: Gojira, Archetype: Plini, and Archetype: Nolly — each containing fully modeled amp, cab, and effects chains tailored to specific tonal territories. Individual plugins are priced at $99 each with a free 14-day trial available.

Line 6 Helix Native ($399) provides the full Helix amp and effects ecosystem as a DAW plugin with hundreds of amp, cab, and effects models. If you own a Helix hardware unit, Helix Native is available at a steep discount and keeps your tones consistent between live and studio work.

Free options: GarageBand on Mac includes excellent amp simulations sufficient for professional results in many contexts. Guitar Rig 7 Player from Native Instruments is a capable free amp simulator on both Mac and Windows. LePou plugins are highly regarded freeware options particularly suited to metal and hard rock tones.

Cables and Accessories

Use a standard 1/4" TS (mono instrument) cable from your guitar to the interface Hi-Z input. Cable quality matters less than many players believe — any well-shielded cable from a reputable brand keeps noise to a minimum. For amp recording, an XLR cable runs from the SM57 to the interface mic input. Keep XLR cables away from power cables to minimise hum pickup.

Recording a Real Amp: Step by Step

Microphone Placement

Microphone placement is the single most significant variable in amp recording. The same amplifier and settings produce dramatically different tones based on where the microphone sits relative to the speaker cone. Understanding the tonal effect of different positions gives you an enormous range of sounds from a single microphone and amp combination.

On-axis, near the dust cap (center): Aim the SM57 directly at the center of the speaker cone, approximately 1–3 cm from the grille cloth. This gives the brightest, most present tone with maximum high-frequency content. Best for aggressive rhythm guitar that needs to cut through a dense mix, high-gain leads, and modern rock or metal tones.

On-axis, near the cone edge: Move the SM57 to the area where the cone meets the surround. This gives a warmer, rounder tone with more low-mid body and less brightness. Best for warm cleans, classic rock tones, or parts that need a smoother character and less attack.

Off-axis: Angle the SM57 15–45 degrees from perpendicular to the speaker cone. Off-axis placement reduces brightness and harshness and gives a more natural, room-influenced sound. Use this when the on-axis tone is too aggressive or when you want the guitar to sit back in the mix rather than dominate it.

Room mic positioning: Place a condenser microphone 30–90 cm from the cabinet facing the speaker to capture the natural room sound. Phase check the room mic against the close mic — if the combined signal is thinner than either mic alone, flip the polarity on the room mic. Use the room mic subtly blended under the SM57 to add dimension without losing focus.

Phase Alignment Rule: Whenever you use two microphones on a guitar cabinet, always check phase. Play a sustained chord and flip the polarity switch on one mic input. If the combined signal sounds fuller with polarity flipped, keep it flipped. Comb filtering from out-of-phase mics is one of the most common causes of thin, hollow-sounding guitar recordings.

Gain Staging for Amp Recording

Set your interface preamp gain so that the loudest guitar passages peak between -18 dBFS and -12 dBFS on the channel meter. This leaves enough headroom to capture transient peaks without clipping while keeping the signal well above the noise floor. Do not record at maximum levels — digital distortion from clipping is unrecoverable, while a signal that is 6–10 dB quieter than you think it should be is easily corrected in your DAW.

Record at 24-bit depth and 44.1 kHz or 48 kHz sample rate. The noise floor improvement of 24-bit over 16-bit recording is audible and meaningful when recording guitar at moderate levels. Higher sample rates (96 kHz, 192 kHz) offer minimal benefit for electric guitar recording and create significantly larger file sizes.

Room Treatment Considerations

Parallel reflective surfaces cause comb filtering in room recordings. If your recording space has hard parallel walls, place the amp in a corner or against a wall and position the close mic so that the room microphone is not aimed directly at a reflective surface. Hanging a duvet or heavy blanket behind the room mic position absorbs early reflections and gives a cleaner, more controlled ambient sound. For a complete guide to treating your recording space, see our home studio acoustic treatment guide.

DI Recording with Amp Simulators: Step by Step

Setting Up the DI Signal Chain

Connect your guitar to the Hi-Z input on your audio interface using a standard instrument cable. In your DAW, create a mono audio track and set the input to the interface channel where your guitar is connected. Engage input monitoring on the track so you can hear your signal through the monitoring chain.

The critical decision at this stage is whether to record with the amp simulator plugin active on the track or to record a clean DI signal and apply the amp sim afterwards. Both approaches work, but they have different practical implications:

Recording with amp sim active (monitoring through plugin): You hear the finished tone as you play, which many guitarists find more inspiring and natural. The risk is that if you change your mind about the tone later, you are working with an already-processed signal.

Recording clean DI and applying amp sim after: Maximum flexibility — you can change amps, cab models, and entire signal chains after the performance is captured. This is the preferred approach for professional sessions. The challenge is that playing through a clean DI signal feels unnatural for many guitarists, which can affect performance quality.

The best of both worlds is to use your DAW's input monitoring path to monitor through the amp sim while recording the clean DI signal to disk. In most DAWs, this means placing the amp sim plugin on the track in monitoring mode rather than record mode. Consult your specific DAW's documentation for this feature.

Latency Management

Latency — the delay between playing a note and hearing it back — is the primary technical challenge of DI guitar recording. Even modest latency of 10–20 ms feels uncomfortable and can cause guitarists to rush or lag their timing. Managing latency effectively is essential for capturing good performances.

Buffer Size Approximate Latency CPU Load Best For
32 samples ~1.5 ms round-trip Very high Recording only — may cause dropouts
64 samples ~3 ms round-trip High Recording with amp sim monitoring
128 samples ~6 ms round-trip Moderate Recording — acceptable for most players
256 samples ~11 ms round-trip Low Mixing only — noticeable delay when playing
512+ samples 20+ ms round-trip Very low Mixing only — too much latency for recording

Set your DAW buffer size to 64 or 128 samples for guitar recording sessions. On Windows, use ASIO drivers rather than WDM or MME — the latency difference is dramatic, often 10x lower with ASIO. On Mac, Core Audio drivers are generally excellent and require no additional configuration. Close background applications, particularly browsers and streaming services, before recording to free up CPU resources.

Many modern audio interfaces include direct monitoring — a hardware-level zero-latency signal path that routes your input directly to the headphone or monitor outputs without passing through the DAW. Use direct monitoring when you want zero-latency monitoring of a clean DI signal, then separately use the amp sim plugin at low buffer size for the monitored tone. The Focusrite Scarlett series handles this particularly well through the Focusrite Control software.

Dialing In Amp Sim Tones

When using Neural DSP or similar plugins, start with a preset close to your target tone and make targeted adjustments rather than building from scratch. The most impactful parameters in order of significance are:

  1. Gain / Drive: Sets the amount of distortion. Start lower than you think you need — overdriven tones that sound impressive soloed often disappear in a full mix.
  2. Cabinet model and microphone position: The cabinet selection has an enormous impact on the character of the tone. Modern 4x12 cabs with V30 speakers give aggressive mid-forward tones. 1x12 open-back cabs give warmer, more vintage sounds.
  3. Tone stack (Bass, Mid, Treble): Set these for the mix, not for the solo tone. Cut low mids around 250–400 Hz to reduce muddiness in dense arrangements.
  4. Presence and Depth: Presence controls upper-mid frequency content and helps the guitar cut through. Depth adds low-end weight to the simulated amp response.
  5. Noise gate: Essential for high-gain tones. Set the threshold so that it closes cleanly between notes without clipping the attack of picked notes.

DAW-Specific Recording Setup

Recording Guitar in Ableton Live

  1. Connect guitar to the Hi-Z input on your audio interface.
  2. Open Ableton Live and create an Audio Track (Ctrl+T / Cmd+T).
  3. Set the track input to the interface channel your guitar is connected to. Set the input type to Mono.
  4. Click the monitoring button on the track to "In" if you want to always hear the input, or "Auto" to monitor only when armed.
  5. Go to Preferences > Audio and set the buffer size to 64 or 128 samples.
  6. Arm the track by clicking the record arm button (the circle icon on the track).
  7. Add your amp sim as an Audio Effect on the track. Set the plugin to monitor through it but ensure your DI signal is what gets recorded if you want the clean DI capture.
  8. Press the global record button and begin playing.

Recording Guitar in Logic Pro

  1. Create a new Audio Track and select your interface input channel.
  2. Enable the track for recording by clicking the red Record Enable button.
  3. Access Logic's built-in amp designer via Plugins > Amps and Pedals > Amp Designer for zero-cost amp simulation with professional results.
  4. Set your project sample rate to 44.1 kHz or 48 kHz in Project Settings.
  5. In Logic Preferences > Audio, set I/O Buffer Size to 64 or 128 samples.
  6. Press R to begin recording.

Recording Guitar in FL Studio

  1. In the Mixer, route your audio interface input to a free mixer track.
  2. Right-click the track's input button and select your interface input channel.
  3. Enable the record arm button on that mixer track.
  4. Go to Options > Audio Settings and set the buffer length to 128 samples with ASIO drivers selected.
  5. Use the Edison audio recorder plugin on the mixer track to capture the take, or use the playlist recorder for continuous recording.

Tone Techniques and Double Tracking

Double Tracking for Stereo Width

Record electric guitar in mono. Stereo guitar sounds are created through double tracking or stereo effects in post-production, not by recording a stereo signal. Recording in mono gives maximum flexibility to create stereo width at the mixing stage without committing to a specific stereo image during tracking.

Double tracking — recording the same guitar part twice and panning the two takes left and right — is the most effective way to create a wide, powerful guitar sound. The slight natural differences between two performances create an organic stereo image that no plugin can fully replicate. This technique is responsible for the guitar sounds on virtually every major rock and metal record from the past 60 years.

How to double track effectively:

  1. Record the first take and pan it to approximately 70–80% left.
  2. Listen back and play the part again from scratch — do not copy and paste the first take. Play along to the first take until you capture a natural performance.
  3. Pan the second take to 70–80% right.
  4. The two takes should use the same amp settings, guitar, and pickup selection. Tonal consistency between takes makes the combined stereo image feel cohesive.
  5. Minor timing and intonation differences between takes are desirable — they create the width. Performances that are too perfectly aligned produce a narrower image.

For rhythm guitar in dense arrangements, quad-tracking — recording four takes panned hard left, slightly left, slightly right, and hard right — creates an enormous, wall-of-sound effect. Be aware that quad-tracked guitars take up significant frequency space and require careful EQ to avoid drowning out vocals and other instruments.

EQ Approaches for Electric Guitar

Electric guitar occupies a broad frequency range but typically sits in the 80 Hz to 8 kHz region with its fundamental and most harmonically significant content. When mixing double-tracked guitars, apply similar EQ to both sides for a balanced stereo image, or make subtle differences between sides to add width without imbalance.

Common EQ moves for electric guitar in a full mix:

  • High-pass filter at 80–100 Hz: Removes unnecessary low-end content that competes with kick drum and bass guitar without affecting the perceived fullness of the guitar tone.
  • Cut 200–350 Hz (muddiness): This range becomes congested in dense arrangements. A 2–4 dB cut here cleans up the low mids and helps guitar sit with bass guitar without competing.
  • Boost or cut 1–3 kHz (presence): This is the midrange area where guitar's definition and aggression lives. Boost here to make rhythm guitar more cutting, or cut slightly for a smoother, warmer character.
  • Air shelf boost at 8–12 kHz: Adds sparkle and definition to clean tones and single-coil pickups. Use sparingly on high-gain tones to avoid emphasising pick noise and string buzz.

Our detailed mixing guide for beginners covers the broader EQ and processing approaches that apply once your guitar tracks are recorded and ready for the mix.

Compression on Guitar Tracks

Electric guitar often needs less compression than you might expect, particularly high-gain tracks where the amp's natural compression already controls dynamics significantly. Clean and crunch tones benefit more from compression to even out picking dynamics and sustain chords through a mix.

For clean guitar, a moderate ratio (3:1 to 4:1) with a medium attack (20–40 ms) and fast release (100–200 ms) tightens the sound without killing the pick attack. For rhythm guitar in a full arrangement, gentle parallel compression — blending a heavily compressed version of the track underneath the dry signal — adds perceived density and presence without making the guitar sound artificially squashed.

Troubleshooting Common Problems

Hum and Noise

The two most common sources of noise in home guitar recordings are ground loops and single-coil pickup hum. Ground loop hum — a low-frequency 50 or 60 Hz buzz — typically comes from multiple pieces of equipment connected to different electrical circuits. Plug everything (interface, computer, monitors, amp) into the same power strip on a single circuit to eliminate ground loop potential. If hum persists, a ground lift adapter or a DI box with ground lift can isolate the offending ground connection.

Single-coil pickup hum is inherent to the design and most noticeable in high-gain and clean settings. Face different directions while playing — single-coil hum is directional and often significantly reduces at a specific angle relative to electrical equipment like computer monitors, routers, and lighting dimmers. Playing with noise gate plugins or the gate on your amp sim set appropriately eliminates pickup hum during rests and sustain tails.

Thin or Hollow Tone

If your recorded guitar tone sounds thin or hollow compared to how the amp sounds in the room, the most common cause is phase cancellation between two microphones or between a room reflection and the close mic. Check phase on every dual-mic setup by listening to both mics combined and then toggling the polarity switch on one channel. The position that sounds fuller is correct.

For DI recordings that sound thin, check that you are genuinely using the Hi-Z input on your interface rather than a line input. Also check that your amp sim's cabinet impulse response is engaged — bypassing the cab simulation in an amp sim produces an extremely harsh, fizzy tone that many newcomers mistake for a settings problem.

Clipping and Distortion

Digital clipping in guitar recordings is almost always caused by the interface preamp gain set too high. Set gain so that hard-picked passages peak no higher than -12 dBFS on the track meter, ideally landing between -18 and -12 dBFS. If you are recording a heavily distorted guitar tone and the level is fluctuating unpredictably, the high-frequency content in distorted tones can trigger peak meters above what you expect from a visually moderate gain setting. Trust your ears and your meters over the gain knob position.

Getting Consistent Takes

Guitar performances vary naturally between takes, but maintaining consistent technique across multiple double-tracked takes is essential for a professional result. Keep your pick angle, picking force, and fretting pressure consistent between takes. Record more takes than you think you need and comp the best sections together. Even professional guitarists rarely use their first take on competitive commercial recordings.

Advanced Techniques

Re-Amping

Re-amping is the process of taking a recorded clean DI guitar signal and playing it back through a real amplifier and speaker cabinet to capture a miked amp tone after the recording session. This technique gives you the best of both worlds: the performance flexibility of DI recording during tracking, and the authentic sound of a real amp and speaker when it is time to capture the final tone.

To re-amp, you need a re-amp box — a device that converts the line-level output of your interface or audio playback to the high-impedance level expected by a guitar amplifier input. The Radial ProRMP ($99) is a widely used passive re-amp box. Connect the interface output to the re-amp box, connect the re-amp box output to the amp's instrument input, mic the cabinet, and record the result onto a new track in your DAW while playing back the clean DI recording.

Impulse Responses for Cab Simulation

Impulse responses (IRs) are audio snapshots of real speaker cabinet and microphone combinations that can be loaded into IR loader plugins to replace the cabinet simulation in amp sim software. High-quality free IRs are available from manufacturers like Celestion, and commercial IR packs provide access to rare vintage cabinet combinations not available in standard amp sim packages.

Using a third-party IR loader like the free ML Sound Lab plugin or the built-in IR loader in most modern amp sims, you can mix and match amp models from one plugin with cabinet IRs from another. This modular approach gives significantly more tonal flexibility than relying on a single amp sim's built-in cab selections.

Using an Outboard Pedal Board with DI Recording

Running your physical guitar pedals before the interface Hi-Z input is entirely compatible with DI recording. Your signal chain becomes: Guitar → Pedalboard → Interface Hi-Z input → DAW. The amp sim plugin in your DAW then acts as the amplifier section. This gives you the authentic feel and sound of your real pedals — wah, fuzz, compressor, octave — while maintaining the tone flexibility of software cab simulation. Many guitarists find this hybrid approach gives both the authentic feel of analog pedals and the flexibility of digital amp simulation.

Practical Exercises

Beginner Exercise

First DI Recording Pass

Connect your guitar to your audio interface Hi-Z input, create a mono audio track in your DAW, set the buffer size to 128 samples, and record a simple chord progression using GarageBand's Amp Designer or Guitar Rig 7 Player as your amp sim. Focus on getting a clean, noise-free signal with your input level peaking between -18 and -12 dBFS before worrying about tone.

Intermediate Exercise

Microphone Placement Comparison Session

Set up an SM57 on a guitar cabinet and record the same riff in three positions: aimed at the dust cap center (on-axis), aimed at the cone edge, and angled 30 degrees off-axis. Import all three recordings into your DAW and A/B compare them to train your ear to identify how position affects the recorded tone. Note the changes in brightness, warmth, and body between each position.

Advanced Exercise

Double Track and Mix a Full Guitar Part

Record a complete rhythm guitar part twice from scratch using the same amp settings and guitar, pan the takes 75% left and 75% right, then apply matching EQ — high-pass at 90 Hz, cut 3 dB at 280 Hz, and boost 1.5 dB at 2.5 kHz — to both sides. Add a room mic or IR-based ambience to blend underneath, then compare your stereo guitar sound against a reference track in your target genre and identify what additional processing is needed to match the professional result.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ Can you record electric guitar at home without an amp?
Yes. DI recording through an audio interface combined with amp simulator plugins like Neural DSP or Line 6 Helix Native produces professional-quality guitar tones without a physical amp. Many commercial records are recorded this way.
FAQ What microphone should I use for recording electric guitar?
The Shure SM57 is the industry standard for guitar cab recording. It handles high SPL from guitar cabinets without distortion and has a frequency response that complements electric guitar tones. For a warmer sound, combine it with a condenser mic like the AKG C414 positioned 30–60 cm from the cabinet.
FAQ Where should I position the microphone on a guitar cabinet?
Start with the SM57 aimed at the edge of the dust cap, 1 to 3 cm from the grille cloth. Moving toward the cone center adds brightness. Moving toward the cone edge adds warmth. Off-axis angling at 15–45 degrees reduces brightness further and adds a more natural character.
FAQ What audio interface do I need to record guitar?
Any interface with a Hi-Z instrument input works for DI guitar recording. The Focusrite Scarlett 2i2 Gen 4 at around $199 is the most widely used choice. For miked cab recording, any interface with phantom power and XLR inputs works.
FAQ What are the best free amp sim plugins for guitar?
GarageBand on Mac includes excellent amp simulations. Guitar Rig 7 Player is fully free on Mac and Windows. Neural DSP offers free 14-day trials of their plugins. LePou plugins are highly regarded for metal and hard rock tones.
FAQ How do I reduce latency when recording guitar?
Use your interface direct monitoring to hear yourself with zero latency. Set your DAW buffer size to 64 or 128 samples. Use ASIO drivers on Windows. Close background applications while recording.
FAQ Should I record guitar in mono or stereo?
Record in mono. Stereo guitar sounds are created through double tracking or stereo effects in post-production. Recording in mono gives maximum flexibility to create stereo width at the mixing stage.
FAQ How do I record guitar in Ableton Live?
Connect guitar to interface Hi-Z input, create an audio track, set input to the interface channel, enable monitoring, set buffer size to 64 to 128 samples, arm the track, and record. Add an amp sim plugin on the track for tone processing.
