How to Record Electric Guitar at Home: Complete Guide for 2026

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 14 min read Beginner–Intermediate

Recording electric guitar at home has never been more practical. The gap between home and professional studio guitar recordings has closed dramatically over the past decade, driven by two parallel developments: amp simulator plugins that are now genuinely indistinguishable from real amplifiers in most contexts, and audio interfaces that handle guitar DI recording at prices that make them accessible to any producer. This guide covers both approaches — real amp recording and DI with amp sims — so you can choose the right method for your setup and produce professional-quality guitar tracks from your home.

What you will learn: The two main recording methods, complete gear requirements for each, microphone placement for amp recording, how to use amp simulators for DI recording, gain staging, latency management, DAW-specific recording instructions, double tracking for stereo width, and how to get the tone you hear in your head onto a track.

The Two Methods: Real Amp vs. DI + Amp Sim

Every home guitar recording begins with a choice: record through a physical amplifier and microphone, or record direct (DI) through your audio interface and use an amp simulator plugin to create the tone in software. Both approaches produce excellent results in 2026. The right choice depends on your space, gear, neighbours, and the tonal character you are after.

Real Amp + Microphone

  • ✓ Natural amp dynamics and feel
  • ✓ Room acoustics add character
  • ✓ Zero monitoring latency
  • ✗ Requires physical space and volume
  • ✗ Neighbour and noise concerns
  • ✗ More setup and equipment required

DI + Amp Simulator

  • ✓ Silent recording — no amp noise
  • ✓ Re-amp the DI after recording
  • ✓ Minimal gear required
  • ✓ Infinitely adjustable tone in post
  • ✗ Requires low-latency monitoring setup
  • ✗ Best plugins cost $100–$400

Gear You Need

Audio Interface

The audio interface converts the analog signal from your guitar or microphone into digital audio your DAW can record. For electric guitar, you need an interface with a high-impedance instrument input — labeled Hi-Z or Inst on the front panel. This presents the correct impedance load to your guitar's pickups, preserving tone and clarity. The Focusrite Scarlett 2i2 Gen 4 ($199) covers both microphone and DI recording with two high-quality preamps and front-panel Hi-Z inputs. The Universal Audio Volt 2 ($169) adds vintage 1176-style compression on the input. Either is more than capable for professional-quality guitar recording.

Microphone — For Amp Recording

The Shure SM57 ($99) is the industry standard for electric guitar cabinet recording and has appeared on more guitar tracks than any other microphone in history. It handles the high sound pressure levels produced by guitar cabinets without distortion, has a frequency response that complements electric guitar's midrange character, and is physically robust enough for close-mic positioning over years of use. If you are adding only one microphone for guitar recording, the SM57 is the correct choice without exception.

For a more complete sound, a condenser microphone positioned 30–60 cm from the cabinet captures room ambience and adds air and dimension to the SM57's close-mic signal. The AKG C214 ($399) or a budget condenser like the Audio-Technica AT2020 ($99) blend with the SM57 to create a fuller, more three-dimensional tone. Start with the SM57 dominant in the blend and add the room mic until you get the dimension you want.

Amp Simulators — For DI Recording

Amp simulator quality in 2026 is genuinely exceptional. Neural DSP's artist-specific plugins — Archetype: Gojira, Archetype: Plini, Tone King Imperial MKII — are considered the benchmark for modern amp simulation and use neural network capture technology to model real amplifiers with audibly accurate results. Line 6 Helix Native ($399) provides the full Helix amp and effects ecosystem as a plugin with hundreds of models. For free options, GarageBand's amp simulations are excellent on Mac, and Guitar Rig 7 Player is a capable free amp simulator on both Mac and Windows.

Recording a Real Amp: Step by Step

Microphone Placement

Microphone placement is the single most significant variable in amp recording. The same amplifier and settings produce dramatically different tones based on where the microphone sits relative to the speaker cone. Understanding the tonal effect of different positions gives you an enormous range of sounds from a single microphone and amp combination.

On-axis, near the dust cap (center): Aim the SM57 directly at the center of the speaker cone, approximately 1–3 cm from the grille cloth. This gives the brightest, most present tone with maximum high-frequency content. Best for aggressive rhythm guitar that needs to cut through a dense mix.

On-axis, near the cone edge: Move the SM57 to the area where the cone meets the surround. This gives a warmer, rounder tone with more low-mid body and less brightness. Best for warm cleans, classic rock tones, or parts that need a smoother character.

Off-axis: Angle the SM57 15–45 degrees from perpendicular to the speaker cone. Off-axis placement reduces brightness and harshness and gives a more natural, room-influenced sound. Use this when the on-axis tone is too aggressive or when you want guitar to sit back in the mix rather than cut forward.

Distance: Moving the microphone farther from the cabinet — from 1 cm to 30 cm — adds progressively more room sound. Close positioning (1–3 cm) gives a tight, dry tone ideal for untreated rooms. Medium distance (10–30 cm) introduces early reflections. For most home studios with untreated rooms, close positioning minimizes unflattering room reflections.

Gain Staging

Set the preamp gain so the recording level peaks at around –12 to –6 dBFS when playing loudly. This leaves headroom above the peak and avoids digital clipping while keeping the signal well above the noise floor. Aim for green-to-yellow on the interface metering during normal playing, with brief excursions into orange on loud notes. Never hit red — digital clipping on a guitar recording is unrecoverable and requires a retake.

Managing Amp Volume at Home

Recording a real amp at home often requires running it below its ideal operating volume. Many tube amps only develop their characteristic harmonic richness when the power amp section is pushed. Solutions include: a power attenuator like the Boss Waza Tube Amp Expander or Universal Audio OX — these sit between the amp head and cabinet and absorb output power, allowing the amp to run at full power internally while the cabinet operates quietly. Alternatively, a low-wattage amp (5W, 1W, or 0.5W) runs at full power at much lower decibel levels than a 50W or 100W amp, making bedroom-volume tone genuinely achievable.

DI Recording with Amp Simulators: Step by Step

DI Setup

Connect your guitar directly to the Hi-Z instrument input on the front panel of your audio interface using a standard guitar cable. No amp or additional gear required. In your DAW, create an audio track and load your amp simulator plugin. You will hear the amp-simulated tone through your headphones or monitors as you play.

Managing Latency

The most common frustration with DI recording is latency — the delay between playing a note and hearing it through your monitoring system. The primary solution is your audio interface's direct monitoring feature. Direct monitoring routes the raw guitar signal from the interface input to your headphone output with zero latency, bypassing the computer entirely. You hear your dry guitar with zero latency, and the processed amp sim sound is also present from the DAW. Set your DAW buffer size to 64 or 128 samples while recording, use ASIO drivers on Windows, and close background applications to minimize DAW-side latency.

Re-Amping: Record Now, Perfect Tone Later

One of the key advantages of DI recording is re-amping. Recording a DI guitar track captures the raw guitar signal with no amp tone applied. This allows you to experiment with different amp simulator settings, try entirely different plugins, or send the DI signal back through a real amp after the fact — without the guitarist needing to be present again. This is the workflow used in many professional productions: capture the performance first, perfect the tone at whatever pace you want, then commit. It is the most flexible recording approach available to home studio producers.

DAW-Specific Recording Instructions

Ableton Live

Go to Preferences → Audio and set input and output device to your interface. Set buffer size to 128 samples or lower. Create an audio track. Set the track's input to the interface channel corresponding to your guitar. Enable monitoring by setting the monitoring button to In. Arm the track. Add Ableton's built-in Amp or Cabinet device from the Audio Effects browser, or load a third-party amp sim. Adjust interface gain so the input meter in Ableton does not clip. Press Record.

Logic Pro

Create a new audio track and select your interface as the input device. Assign the input to the correct channel. Enable record and monitoring on the track. Logic includes Amp Designer — add it directly to the channel strip's insert slots. Logic's Low Latency Mode (button in the Control Bar) automatically bypasses high-latency plugins during recording while keeping low-latency monitoring active. Set I/O Buffer Size to 64 or 128 samples under Preferences → Audio.

FL Studio

Go to Options → Audio Settings and select your ASIO driver. Set buffer size to 128 samples. Open the Mixer and create a new insert. Select the interface channel your guitar is connected to as the insert's input. Add a guitar amp simulator to the insert's effect chain. Use the Edison audio recorder or record directly into a playlist audio track to capture the signal. Arm the mixer insert for recording and use the transport controls to start and stop.

Double Tracking for Stereo Width

Double tracking is recording the same guitar part twice and panning the two recordings to opposite sides of the stereo field. The natural human inconsistencies between takes — slight timing differences, minor variations in picking attack — create a wide, natural stereo spread that sounds infinitely more professional than simply duplicating a mono track and panning copies. This technique is standard on virtually every produced rock, metal, and pop guitar recording.

Record take one, then record take two of the same part. Play as consistently as possible but do not try to be robotically identical — the slight variation is what creates the width. Pan take one to L 80–100 and take two to R 80–100. For a wall-of-guitars sound, record four takes total — two panned hard left and two panned hard right — the classic technique used in heavy rock and metal production.

Getting a Great Tone: Practical Tips

The tone of a recorded guitar track is determined primarily by the guitar and playing technique. A great-sounding guitar played well through modest recording equipment outperforms a mediocre guitar played poorly through expensive gear every time. Ensure your guitar is properly intonated, set up correctly, and has fresh strings before recording — these maintenance items have a larger effect on recorded tone than any microphone or plugin choice.

For amp recording, start with the amp set to a tone that sounds great in the room at low volume, then adjust mic position to capture the speaker's sweet spot. Do not try to EQ a bad amp tone into a good one — mic placement and amp settings work together to create the source tone. Mixing EQ sculpts a good recording; it does not fix a bad source.

For DI recording, load references of the sound you are going for and A/B your amp sim against the reference as you dial in tone. Experiment with different amp types before committing — a British-voiced amp cuts differently in a mix than an American-style or high-gain modern amp.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

Too much gain is the most common guitar recording error. Distorted guitar tones that sound huge in isolation often mask and compete with other mix elements when the full arrangement is playing. Record with less gain than you think you need and check the guitar in the full mix context before adding more distortion. You can always add saturation in mixing. You cannot remove it from the recorded signal.

Not checking for ground hum wastes session time. Single-coil pickups are susceptible to electromagnetic interference from lights, monitors, and power transformers. Angle the guitar in different directions while playing to find a null point that minimizes hum. Switch off any nearby fluorescent or LED dimmer lights. Move away from computer monitors. A noise gate placed before the interface eliminates persistent hum between notes.

Recording without a click track makes overdubbing and editing almost impossible later. Always record electric guitar to a metronome or drum track so the performance has a reliable rhythmic reference.

Practical Exercises

Beginner — Mic placement exploration: Set up your amp and SM57. Record 10 seconds of a chord with the mic at the dust cap center. Without changing amp settings, move to the cone edge and record the same part. Then try 30 degrees off-axis. Compare the three recordings and note the tonal character of each position.

Intermediate — DI tone matching: Find a reference guitar tone from a released song in your genre. Record your guitar DI and load an amp simulator. Try to match the reference using only the amp sim controls — no external EQ. Document which amp type, gain level, and cab choice got you closest.

Advanced — Double tracking: Record a 4-bar chord progression twice. Pan take one to L 80 and take two to R 80. Check the stereo spread on headphones. Then try L 100 / R 100 and compare. Notice how the natural inconsistency between takes creates width without artificial processing.

Frequently Asked Questions

+ FAQ What is the main difference between recording electric guitar with a real amp versus DI with amp simulators?

Real amp recording captures the natural dynamics and feel of a physical amplifier combined with room acoustics, but requires physical space and creates noise. DI with amp simulators records silently through your audio interface and allows you to adjust tone infinitely in post-production, but requires low-latency monitoring and premium plugins that can cost $100–$400.

+ FAQ Why is a Hi-Z input important when recording electric guitar directly into an audio interface?

A Hi-Z (high-impedance) instrument input presents the correct impedance load to your guitar's pickups, which preserves tone and clarity when recording DI. Without it, your tone can sound thin or compromised. Look for interfaces labeled with Hi-Z or Inst on the front panel to ensure proper guitar impedance handling.

+ FAQ Can I achieve professional-quality guitar recordings at home in 2026?

Yes, the gap between home and professional studio guitar recordings has closed dramatically. Modern amp simulator plugins are now genuinely indistinguishable from real amplifiers in most contexts, and affordable audio interfaces make professional-quality home recording accessible to any producer.

+ FAQ What are the main advantages of using DI recording with amp simulators for home recording?

DI recording allows silent, neighbour-friendly recording with minimal gear required. The biggest advantage is flexibility—you can re-amp the DI recording after tracking and adjust your tone infinitely in post-production without re-recording the original guitar performance.

+ FAQ What monitoring latency issue might I face when using amp simulators, and why does it matter?

When using amp simulators for DI recording, the plugin processing can introduce latency that makes it difficult to play in real-time with the correct feel. You need a low-latency monitoring setup to hear yourself accurately while recording, which is why real amp recording offers a zero-latency advantage in this regard.

+ FAQ Does recording electric guitar with a real amp require more equipment than DI recording?

Yes, real amp recording requires a physical amplifier, a microphone, microphone preamp, microphone stand, and proper positioning—significantly more equipment than DI recording. DI recording requires only an audio interface with a Hi-Z input, making it the more minimalist approach for home studios.

+ FAQ What is re-amping in the context of DI guitar recording?

Re-amping means taking your recorded DI guitar signal and sending it back through a physical amplifier and microphone after the initial recording session. This DI approach allows you to experiment with different amp tones and microphone placements in post-production without re-recording your original guitar performance.

+ FAQ Is the Focusrite Scarlett 2i2 Gen 4 suitable for recording both microphones and DI guitar?

Yes, the Focusrite Scarlett 2i2 Gen 4 ($199) is designed to handle both microphone and DI recording with two high-quality preamps, making it a versatile choice for home guitarists who want flexibility in their recording approach.

Can you record electric guitar at home without an amp?

Yes. DI recording through an audio interface with amp simulator plugins produces professional-quality tones without a physical amp. Many commercial records are recorded this way.

What microphone should I use for recording electric guitar?

The Shure SM57 ($99) is the industry standard. It handles high SPL without distortion and has a frequency response that complements electric guitar. For a warmer, more dimensional sound, combine it with a condenser mic like the AKG C414 positioned farther back.

Where should I position the microphone on a guitar cabinet?

Start with the SM57 at the edge of the dust cap, 1–3 cm from the grille cloth. Moving toward the cone center adds brightness. Moving toward the cone edge adds warmth. Off-axis angling at 15–45 degrees reduces harshness further.

What audio interface do I need to record guitar?

Any interface with a Hi-Z instrument input works for DI recording. The Focusrite Scarlett 2i2 Gen 4 ($199) is the most widely used choice. For miked cab recording, any interface with phantom power and XLR inputs works.

What are the best free amp sim plugins for guitar?

GarageBand on Mac includes excellent amp simulations. Guitar Rig 7 Player is fully free on Mac and Windows. Neural DSP offers free trials. LePou plugins are highly regarded for metal and hard rock tones.

How do I reduce latency when recording guitar?

Use your interface direct monitoring to hear yourself with zero latency. Set DAW buffer size to 64 or 128 samples. Use ASIO drivers on Windows. Close background applications while recording.

Should I record guitar in mono or stereo?

Record in mono. Stereo guitar is created through double tracking or stereo effects in post-production. Mono recording gives you maximum flexibility to create stereo width at the mixing stage.

How do I record guitar in Ableton Live?

Connect guitar to the interface Hi-Z input. Create an audio track in Ableton. Set input to the interface channel. Enable monitoring. Set buffer size to 64–128 samples. Arm the track and record. Add an amp sim plugin for tone processing.
