FabFilter Pro-Q 4 Review: The Industry Standard Gets Better

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 14 min read Intermediate

FabFilter Pro-Q 3 became the de facto standard parametric EQ in professional mixing and mastering over the past decade. Its combination of zero-latency minimum phase processing, true linear phase mode, per-band dynamic EQ, mid-side operation, and spectrum collision detection placed it in the plugin chains of engineers at every level — from bedroom producers to Grammy-winning mastering engineers. Pro-Q 4, released in early 2026, builds on that foundation with meaningful improvements rather than a complete reinvention.

This review covers everything new in Pro-Q 4, whether it justifies upgrading from Pro-Q 3, and where it sits in the current EQ landscape. We cover all features, real-world performance, and who should prioritise this upgrade.

Quick Verdict

Pro-Q 4 is the best general-purpose parametric EQ available in 2026. The per-band mid-side processing, improved linear phase, and updated spectrum analyser are meaningful additions for professional users. Pro-Q 3 owners who use mid-side processing heavily should upgrade immediately. Those primarily using standard parametric EQ without mid-side work can upgrade at their own pace — Pro-Q 3 remains excellent.

What's New in Pro-Q 4

FabFilter does not release major version updates frequently — Pro-Q 2 to Pro-Q 3 took years, and Pro-Q 3 to Pro-Q 4 represents a similar development cycle. Each update adds meaningful capabilities rather than superficial changes, and Pro-Q 4 follows this pattern.

Per-band mid-side processing is the headline addition. In Pro-Q 3, mid-side mode applied to the entire plugin — you either processed the mid channel, the side channel, or both, but you couldn't mix processing modes within the same EQ instance. Pro-Q 4 allows each band to be set independently to Mid, Side, Stereo (Mid+Side equally), or any specific M/S balance. This means you can cut a low-frequency buildup in the mid channel while boosting high-frequency air in the side channel in the same EQ instance without needing a second plugin. For mastering and mix bus processing, this is a substantial workflow improvement.

Improved linear phase processing addresses the pre-ringing that linear phase EQ introduces — a subtle artifact that appears before transients when linear phase is engaged, caused by the mathematical requirements of zero-phase-shift processing. Pro-Q 4's updated linear phase algorithms reduce pre-ringing without compromising phase coherence, making linear phase mode more usable on transient-heavy material like drums and acoustic instruments where the artifact was previously most noticeable.

Enhanced spectrum analyser resolution improves the display's precision at low frequencies — the area where accurate visual feedback is most difficult to provide due to the physics of frequency analysis. Pro-Q 4's analyser provides clearer, more detailed low-frequency information without increasing CPU overhead, making it more useful for precise bass management in mastering contexts.

Improved collision detection across multiple instances refines the feature introduced in Pro-Q 3. When multiple Pro-Q 4 instances are active in a session and the spectrum analyser is open, they display each other's frequency content, highlighting areas where instruments compete in the same frequency range. Pro-Q 4 improves the accuracy and responsiveness of this detection, making it more useful in dense mixes with many active tracks.

Sound Quality

The core EQ sound of Pro-Q 4 is identical to Pro-Q 3 — FabFilter did not change the processing algorithms for standard parametric EQ operation. The same minimum phase processing, the same filter slopes, the same zero-latency performance. This is a deliberate decision: Pro-Q 3's sound was already the reference standard, and changing it would disrupt existing sessions and workflows.

In minimum phase zero-latency mode, Pro-Q 4 is effectively transparent — it applies precisely the gain changes you specify without audible artifacts, coloration, or distortion. Cutting 3 dB at 400 Hz with a Q of 2.0 removes exactly that content without affecting adjacent frequencies beyond the expected filter curve. This precision and transparency is what made Pro-Q the professional standard, and Pro-Q 4 maintains it completely.

Linear phase mode's improved pre-ringing reduction is audible in direct comparison on snare drums and acoustic guitar transients. The difference is subtle but real — the same processing with less of the slight 'pre-echo' quality that linear phase could introduce on sharp attacks. For critical mastering work on percussive music, this improvement justifies the upgrade independently of the mid-side additions.

Workflow and Interface

Pro-Q's interface has been refined rather than redesigned. The core interaction model is unchanged: click anywhere on the spectrum display to create a band, drag it to adjust frequency and gain, scroll to adjust Q. This interaction model is so well-established and copied that it feels like a universal EQ interface standard rather than a product-specific design. The familiarity is a feature — anyone who has used Pro-Q 3 is immediately productive in Pro-Q 4.

The per-band mid-side control appears as a new parameter in each band's detail panel. A small M/S icon reveals the balance control — drag left toward M to increase mid processing, right toward S to increase side processing, or set to centre for equal stereo processing. The implementation is clean and doesn't clutter the main interface. Bands with non-stereo M/S settings display a small indicator, making it easy to identify which bands are doing mid-side work at a glance.

Keyboard shortcut improvements in Pro-Q 4 accelerate repetitive tasks — pressing Alt while clicking a band toggles it between active and bypassed for rapid A/B comparison; Shift-click on multiple bands selects them for simultaneous adjustment; Command/Ctrl-A selects all bands for global gain or frequency adjustments. These additions reflect FabFilter's attention to how engineers actually use the plugin in long mixing sessions.

Dynamic EQ

The dynamic EQ functionality in Pro-Q 4 is unchanged from Pro-Q 3 — any band can be set to respond dynamically to the signal level at its frequency, making it function like a frequency-specific compressor (dynamic cut) or expander (dynamic boost). The threshold, attack, release, and range controls allow precise calibration of how aggressively and quickly the band responds.

Dynamic EQ is particularly useful for controlling resonances that only become problematic at high levels — a harsh consonant in a vocal that's only noticeable on loud passages, a bass guitar note that blooms uncontrollably at high velocities, a room resonance that builds during loud sections. Static EQ cuts these issues at all times and can make the sound feel thin during quiet passages. Dynamic EQ cuts only when the problem appears.

Used subtly on the master bus, dynamic EQ can tighten the low end by cutting sub-bass frequencies that build up during dense sections while leaving the low end open during sparser arrangements. This transparent dynamic control is difficult to achieve with conventional compression and is one of the most professional-sounding applications of the tool.

CPU Performance

Pro-Q 4's CPU usage is comparable to Pro-Q 3 in standard operating modes. FabFilter's code is well-optimised — a typical Pro-Q 4 instance with 4-6 bands in zero-latency mode uses less than 0.5% CPU on modern hardware. Running 20 Pro-Q 4 instances simultaneously in a large session has no meaningful performance impact on current Apple Silicon or AMD/Intel processors.

Linear phase mode has higher CPU requirements due to the lookahead processing it requires, but the efficiency improvements in Pro-Q 4 reduce the overhead compared to Pro-Q 3's linear phase mode. Oversampling (1x, 2x, 4x) increases CPU usage proportionally but is rarely necessary for standard mixing — it's most useful in mastering contexts where the highest possible quality is the priority regardless of CPU cost.

Pros and Cons

Pros

  • Per-band mid-side processing — the single biggest workflow improvement over Pro-Q 3
  • Improved linear phase with reduced pre-ringing
  • Enhanced spectrum analyser resolution at low frequencies
  • Unchanged core EQ sound — same transparent processing as Pro-Q 3
  • Unlimited bands with no performance penalty
  • Excellent surround support including Dolby Atmos
  • Native Apple Silicon performance
  • Improved keyboard shortcuts accelerate workflow

Cons

  • Core sound identical to Pro-Q 3 — not an audio quality upgrade
  • $179 new purchase price is premium
  • No new filter types or modulation capabilities
  • Competitors like TDR Slick EQ MR offer free mid-side alternatives
  • No analogue character or saturation option for those wanting colour

Upgrade Advice: Should You Upgrade from Pro-Q 3?

If you use mid-side processing regularly in your mixes or masters — yes, upgrade immediately. The per-band mid-side control transforms what was previously a multi-plugin workflow (one Pro-Q 3 for mid, one for side) into a single plugin operation. The time savings and session clarity are worth the upgrade cost on the first complex session.

If you work in linear phase mode on transient-heavy material — yes, the reduced pre-ringing is a meaningful quality improvement. Mastering engineers working on drumline-heavy hip-hop, rock, or electronic music with sharp transients will hear the difference on careful listening.

If you use Pro-Q 3 primarily for standard parametric EQ tasks without mid-side work and without linear phase on transient material — the upgrade is less urgent. Pro-Q 3 remains fully professional and will continue receiving support from FabFilter. Upgrade when it suits your budget rather than immediately.

Alternatives

TDR Nova (free) remains the best free dynamic EQ and parametric EQ available. It lacks mid-side per-band processing and the spectrum collision detection, but the core EQ quality is excellent and it performs dynamic EQ at a level that competes with Pro-Q 4 for most mixing tasks. Every producer should have TDR Nova installed as the free benchmark.

DMG Audio Equilibrium ($179) is Pro-Q 4's most technically comparable competitor. It matches Pro-Q 4 in features, adds analogue-modelled filter types for producers who want EQ colour, and has a more flexible modulation system. The interface is more complex than Pro-Q 4's, making the learning curve steeper. For engineers who want both transparency and analogue character in one EQ, Equilibrium is the strongest alternative.

iZotope Ozone EQ (included in Ozone 12) is the strongest alternative for mastering-specific EQ work, particularly its Tonal Balance integration and match EQ capabilities. It lacks some of Pro-Q 4's workflow speed for mixing work but excels in the specific context of full-mix mastering.

Bottom line: FabFilter Pro-Q 4 is the best general-purpose EQ available. The per-band mid-side processing alone justifies the upgrade for professional users. If you don't own any version of Pro-Q, $179 buys the most professionally trusted EQ plugin in the industry.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is FabFilter Pro-Q 4 worth upgrading from Pro-Q 3?

Yes, if you use mid-side processing regularly, work with complex multi-track sessions where collision detection saves time, or want the improved linear phase processing. If you primarily use Pro-Q 3 for basic parametric EQ tasks without heavy mid-side work, the upgrade is less urgent — Pro-Q 3 remains excellent.

What is new in FabFilter Pro-Q 4?

Pro-Q 4's key additions over Pro-Q 3 include: per-band mid-side processing, an improved spectrum analyser with enhanced resolution, updated linear phase mode with reduced pre-ringing artifacts, improved collision detection across multiple instances, and workflow improvements including better keyboard shortcuts.

What is the difference between Pro-Q 4 and Pro-Q 3?

The core EQ sound and processing are identical between Pro-Q 3 and Pro-Q 4. Pro-Q 4 adds per-band mid-side control, improved linear phase with less pre-ringing, and updated spectrum analyser resolution. For producers who use mid-side processing extensively, these are meaningful improvements.

How much does FabFilter Pro-Q 4 cost?

FabFilter Pro-Q 4 costs $179 for a new license. Upgrades from Pro-Q 3 are available at a discount — typically around $49-79. FabFilter uses a perpetual license model with free updates within the same major version.

Is FabFilter Pro-Q 4 the best EQ plugin available?

Pro-Q 4 is widely considered the best general-purpose parametric EQ available for mixing and mastering. Its combination of zero-latency processing, true linear phase mode, dynamic EQ per band, mid-side operation, and spectrum collision detection makes it the most versatile single EQ plugin available.

Can I use FabFilter Pro-Q 4 on the master bus?

Yes — Pro-Q 4 is excellent on the master bus. For mastering use, engage linear phase mode on shelves where phase coherence matters most. The per-band mid-side control in Pro-Q 4 is particularly valuable on the master bus for tightening the centre image or adding width to the sides independently.

Does FabFilter Pro-Q 4 work on Apple Silicon?

Yes — FabFilter Pro-Q 4 runs natively on Apple Silicon as a Universal Binary. It is compatible with VST3, AU, and AAX formats and is fully supported in Logic Pro, Ableton Live, Pro Tools, and all major DAWs on both Mac and Windows.

What is dynamic EQ in Pro-Q 4?

Dynamic EQ in Pro-Q 4 allows any EQ band to respond to the signal level at that frequency — boosting or cutting only when the signal exceeds a threshold you set. This is useful for controlling resonances that only become problematic at loud moments, applying transparent corrections that static EQ would make too obvious.
