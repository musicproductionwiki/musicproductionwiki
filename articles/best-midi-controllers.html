Best Midi Controllers

A MIDI controller is the single piece of gear that most changes how you interact with your DAW. It transforms abstract mouse-clicking into a physical, expressive, tactile experience — and whether you are sketching chord progressions at 2 a.m. or performing a live set to a festival crowd, the right controller can fundamentally shift the quality and speed of your output. The wrong one, however, just collects dust.

This guide covers every major category of MIDI controller available in 2026 — keyboard controllers, pad controllers, hybrid controllers, and performance-focused options — at every realistic budget from under $100 to over $700. We have tested or extensively researched every product on this list, cross-referencing user feedback from working producers, live performers, and studio engineers.

Why This Matters

A MIDI controller sends no audio. It transmits note data, velocity, pitch bend, modulation, and control change (CC) messages to your DAW or hardware synthesizer. That means your controller choice is entirely about ergonomics, workflow, and expression — not about sound quality. Understanding this distinction will help you avoid overspending on specs that do not affect your music.

How to Choose the Right MIDI Controller

Before spending a single dollar, you need to answer four questions honestly:

1. What is your primary workflow? If you play piano, use software instruments melodically, or record chord progressions, you need keys. If you make hip-hop, trap, or electronic music by finger-drumming patterns, you need velocity-sensitive pads. Many producers need both, which is where hybrid controllers become compelling.

2. How much desk space do you have? A 61-key controller is wonderful for piano-style playing but it will dominate a small home studio desk. The 25-key and mini-key form factors are genuinely portable and desk-friendly, but they sacrifice octave range and hand comfort for extended sessions.

3. Which DAW do you use? Some controllers are deeply integrated with specific DAWs. The Ableton Push 3 is essentially an extension of Ableton Live. Native Instruments Komplete Kontrol keyboards are optimized for NI’s plugin ecosystem and NKS-compatible instruments. Novation controllers have tight integration with Ableton as well. If you use Logic Pro, FL Studio, or a less controller-centric DAW, you might get more value from a general-purpose controller.

4. What is your budget — realistically? The good news is that MIDI controllers have excellent value at every price point. You do not need to spend $700 to make great music. The Akai MPK Mini MK4 at $99 has launched thousands of professional careers. The bad news is that cheap faders, cheap encoders, and bad keybeds feel bad fast — and you will notice.

For a deeper dive into the technical side of what MIDI actually is and how it communicates with your software, see our guide on MIDI vs audio explained. For how to actually use a controller once you have it, how to use MIDI in your DAW walks through the full setup process.

Best Keyboard MIDI Controllers

Keyboard controllers are the most popular category by a wide margin. They range from 25 mini keys you can throw in a backpack to 88 fully weighted keys that feel indistinguishable from an acoustic piano. Here is what the specs actually mean:

Key count: 25 keys covers two octaves. 37 keys gives you a third octave. 49 is the practical minimum for playing two-handed parts without constant octave-shifting. 61 keys is the sweet spot for most producers. 88 keys is only necessary if you are a trained pianist who needs the full range.

Keybed type: Mini keys are small, light, and cheap. Synth-action keys are full-sized but light, with a short, snappy feel. Semi-weighted adds some resistance. Fully weighted (hammer action) mimics a real piano. Which you choose depends on how you play — synth action is faster for chords and sequences, weighted is better for dynamics and classical playing.

Aftertouch: This lets the controller send a second message when you press down harder after the initial note-on. It can control vibrato, filter cutoff, or any CC parameter. Polyphonic aftertouch (where each key sends its own aftertouch signal) is rare and expensive, but it is appearing on more controllers in 2026 as demand from expressive electronic music players grows.

Arturia MiniLab 3 — Best Budget Keyboard Controller

The Arturia MiniLab 3 costs $99 and packs 25 mini keys, 8 velocity-sensitive pads, 8 knobs, and pitch/mod touch strips into a compact, bus-powered USB package. The included Arturia Analog Lab Intro software gives you access to hundreds of presets from Arturia’s legendary soft synth lineup, which alone justifies the price for a new producer. The keybed is mini but surprisingly responsive, and the pads are 16-velocity sensitive with RGB color coding. For bedroom producers, students, and anyone who needs a portable first controller, the MiniLab 3 is the easiest recommendation we can make.

Pros: Great value bundle with Analog Lab Intro; compact; USB bus-powered; solid build quality for the price. Cons: Mini keys frustrate players with larger hands; only 25 keys limits melodic range without octave-shifting.

Akai MPK Mini MK4 — Best All-Rounder Under $100

The Akai MPK Mini MK4 ($99) is one of the best-selling MIDI controllers in history for a reason. It offers 25 mini keys, 8 MPC-style backlit pads, 8 assignable knobs, and a four-way thumbstick for pitch and modulation. The MK4 iteration added an OLED display, dedicated arpeggiator controls, and improved velocity sensitivity on both the keys and pads. The pads, in particular, feel punchy and responsive — better than anything else at this price. It ships with MPC Beats (a full production DAW), Hybrid 3 synthesizer, and a suite of other software. If you are choosing between the MiniLab 3 and the MPK Mini MK4, the choice comes down to whether you prefer Arturia’s synth bundle or Akai’s MPC-style pads.

See our full Akai MPK Mini MK4 review for a detailed breakdown of pad sensitivity and key response testing.

Pros: Best pads under $100; excellent software bundle; OLED display; arpeggiator. Cons: Mini keys; thumbstick not ideal for smooth pitch bends.

Arturia KeyLab Essential 49 MK3 — Best Mid-Range Keyboard

Step up to $199 and the Arturia KeyLab Essential 49 MK3 delivers a full-sized, 49-key synth-action keybed, 8 RGB pads, 9 faders, 9 knobs, transport controls, and deep integration with Arturia Analog Lab V (which includes thousands of presets spanning every Arturia soft synth). The keybed is a significant step up from mini keys — it feels comfortable for extended playing sessions, and the synth action is fast without being so light it lacks feel. The 49-key layout is the sweet spot: you get nearly four octaves, which handles most melodic and chordal parts without constant octave-shifting. The MK3 iteration added improved pad sensitivity, a new chord mode, and better integration with major DAWs including Ableton Live, Logic Pro, and FL Studio. For producers who are ready to upgrade from a mini controller but are not ready to spend $400+, this is the strongest option available.

Pros: Full-sized keys; excellent Analog Lab V bundle; faders and knobs for mixer control; great DAW integration. Cons: Pads are adequate but not MPC-level; no aftertouch on keys.

Native Instruments Komplete Kontrol S49 MK3 — Best for NI Ecosystem

At $599, the NI Komplete Kontrol S49 MK3 is a premium keyboard controller built specifically around Native Instruments’ software ecosystem. It features 49 semi-weighted keys with polyphonic aftertouch — a feature that was previously reserved for instruments costing twice as much. The NKS (Native Kontrol Standard) integration means that every NKS-compatible instrument (thousands of them) automatically maps its most important parameters to the hardware knobs, and the dual color displays above the keys show parameter names and values in real time. The light guide above the keyboard visualizes chord suggestions, scale modes, and arpeggiator patterns. If you are deeply invested in Komplete, Kontakt libraries, or the NI plugin ecosystem, the S49 MK3 is a transformative workflow tool. Outside that ecosystem, it is significantly less compelling at this price.

Pros: Polyphonic aftertouch; NKS deep integration; light guide; premium build. Cons: Expensive; best value only if you use NI software; no pads.

Novation Launchkey 49 MK4 — Best for Ableton Users

The Novation Launchkey 49 MK4 ($249) is built with Ableton Live integration at its core. The 49 full-sized, synth-action keys feel solid, and the 16 RGB pads directly control Ableton’s Session View — launching clips, triggering scenes, and recording overdubs without touching a mouse. The MK4 update added chord mode, custom modes for non-Ableton DAWs, and improved pad sensitivity. The arpeggiator and scale/chord modes are genuinely useful for producers who are not trained musicians but want to play musical parts. Nine faders with mute and solo buttons give you real mixing control. If your studio centers on Ableton Live and you want hardware that disappears into that workflow, the Launchkey 49 MK4 is probably the most efficient keyboard controller you can buy for the money.

Pros: Superb Ableton integration; 16 full-sized pads; chord and scale modes; faders with mute/solo. Cons: Less compelling outside Ableton; pads are better suited to clip launching than finger drumming.

Best Pad MIDI Controllers

Pad controllers prioritize velocity-sensitive drum pads over keys. They are the tool of choice for finger drummers, beat makers, hip-hop and trap producers, and anyone who builds music by programming rhythmic patterns rather than playing melodic parts. The key specs are pad size, pad sensitivity, pressure curve customization, and whether the pads support polyphonic aftertouch (pressure after the initial hit to add expression to sustained notes).

Akai MPD226 — Best Pad Controller Under $150

The Akai MPD226 ($149) is a pure pad controller: 16 large, MPC-style backlit pads arranged in a 4×4 grid, 6 assignable knobs, and 4 assignable faders. The pads are the same size as those on Akai’s full MPC units and feel correspondingly excellent — responsive, accurate, and able to distinguish between a light ghost note and a hard slam with satisfying precision. There are no keys, no arpeggiator, no frills. If you know you are a pad player and you want the best pads available under $200, the MPD226 is the answer. It works over USB and with DAW controller presets for Ableton, Logic, and FL Studio.

Pros: Best-in-class pads for the price; 6 knobs and 4 faders; simple, reliable. Cons: No keys at all; no standalone operation; limited onboard features.

Native Instruments Maschine MK4 — Best Premium Pad Controller

The NI Maschine MK4 ($499) is more than a pad controller — it is a complete beat production ecosystem. The hardware features 16 large, velocity-sensitive pads with pressure sensitivity, a full-color display, knobs, buttons, and a built-in audio interface (4-in/4-out). The Maschine software that ships with it is a standalone DAW-within-a-DAW that handles sampling, sequencing, mixing, and plugin hosting. The MK4 iteration brought an upgraded display, improved pad sensitivity, and tighter integration with NI’s Komplete library. For hip-hop producers who want a workflow centered on hardware beats rather than mouse-and-keyboard programming, Maschine is a genre-defining tool. For a head-to-head comparison with the Ableton Push, see our Ableton Push 3 vs Maschine MK3 comparison (MK4 differences noted).

Pros: Standalone-capable workflow; built-in audio interface; exceptional pads; powerful Maschine software. Cons: High learning curve; expensive; software-dependent for full functionality.

Novation Launchpad X — Best for Ableton Clip Launching

The Novation Launchpad X ($129) is an 8×8 grid of 64 RGB velocity-sensitive pads optimized for Ableton Live’s Session View. Each pad can launch a clip, trigger a scene, or be used as a velocity-sensitive instrument surface. The Launchpad X is not really a finger drumming tool — the pads are smaller than MPC pads — but for live performance with Ableton, improvised performance using Ableton’s note and chord modes, and DJ-style set triggering, it is one of the most efficient tools available. It ships with Ableton Live Lite and a suite of Loopmasters content.

Pros: 64 RGB pads; excellent Ableton integration; affordable; compact. Cons: Small pads; not ideal for finger drumming; limited outside Ableton.

Best Hybrid MIDI Controllers

Hybrid controllers combine keys with a substantial pad section, faders, encoders, and sometimes step sequencers. They are aimed at producers who want a single controller that covers all bases without buying separate keyboard and pad units.

Akai MPK249 — Best Full-Featured Hybrid

The Akai MPK249 ($299) is one of the most comprehensive hybrid controllers at a reasonable price. It gives you 49 semi-weighted keys with aftertouch, 16 MPC-style backlit pads across two banks (32 total), 8 assignable knobs, 8 faders, transport controls, and an arpeggiator. The semi-weighted keys feel substantially better than synth-action at this price, and the pads are the same quality you find on standalone MPC units. The MPK249 is a go-to recommendation for producers who want to do everything — play melodic parts, finger-drum beats, control their mixer, and automate parameters — from a single piece of hardware. It connects over USB and includes a solid DAW controller preset library.

Pros: Semi-weighted keys with aftertouch; 16 excellent pads; 8 faders + 8 knobs; great all-round value. Cons: Bulkier than pure keyboard controllers; faders can feel plasticky.

Arturia KeyLab 61 MK3 — Best Premium Hybrid

The Arturia KeyLab 61 MK3 ($499) is the flagship of Arturia’s hybrid lineup. It features 61 full-sized, semi-weighted keys with aftertouch, 16 RGB pads, 9 motorized faders, 9 endless encoders, transport controls, and a dedicated chord and scale mode. The motorized faders are the standout feature — they update automatically when you recall a mix preset or switch DAW channels, which is a level of professional feedback you normally only find on dedicated control surfaces at twice the price. Analog Lab V is included, giving access to thousands of Arturia soft synth presets. For a producer who wants a studio centerpiece that handles keys, beats, and DAW mixing simultaneously, the KeyLab 61 MK3 is the strongest hybrid option in 2026.

Pros: Motorized faders; 61 semi-weighted keys with aftertouch; 16 RGB pads; Analog Lab V bundle. Cons: Expensive; large desk footprint; requires power supply (not bus-powered).

Best Performance & DAW-Integration Controllers

This category covers controllers that go beyond basic MIDI input to offer deep DAW integration, standalone capabilities, or features specifically designed for live performance. These are the tools that blur the line between instrument and computer.

Ableton Push 3 — Best DAW-Integrated Controller

The Ableton Push 3 is available in two configurations: the standard version ($699, tethered to a computer) and the standalone version ($1,999, with its own processor and can run Ableton Live without a computer). Both feature a 64-pad grid, an 8×8 RGB pad layout, 8 touch-sensitive encoders, a color display, built-in stereo inputs and outputs, and the deepest integration with Ableton Live of any controller on the market. The Push 3 is not just a controller — it is essentially a hardware version of Ableton’s Session View, allowing you to create, arrange, mix, and perform without ever touching a mouse. For Ableton-centric producers, especially those who perform live, the Push 3 represents the state of the art. Read our full Ableton Push 3 review for a deep dive into both configurations.

Pros: Unmatched Ableton integration; excellent pads; standalone option; built-in audio I/O. Cons: Very expensive; deeply Ableton-specific; steep learning curve.

Novation Circuit Tracks — Best Standalone Groovebox/Controller

The Novation Circuit Tracks ($349) occupies a unique space: it is a standalone groovebox with two synthesizer tracks, four drum tracks, and a step sequencer, but it also functions as a MIDI controller and audio interface when connected to a computer. It has 32 RGB velocity-sensitive pads and 8 macro knobs. You can use it as a standalone sketch pad on the go — no laptop required — then connect it to your DAW when you are back in the studio. For producers who want to capture ideas anywhere and then develop them in software, the Circuit Tracks is a uniquely versatile tool.

Pros: Standalone groovebox; works as MIDI controller and audio interface; 32 pads; portable. Cons: Limited polyphony in standalone mode; small pads; learning curve for standalone operation.

What Specs Actually Matter — And What Doesn’t

The marketing around MIDI controllers is full of impressive-sounding specifications that, in practice, have little impact on your workflow. Here is a straightforward breakdown of what to pay attention to and what to ignore.

Velocity sensitivity: Important. Every decent controller in 2026 has velocity-sensitive keys and pads — the harder you hit, the louder the note. What varies significantly is the quality and feel of that response curve. Budget controllers often have a narrow usable range, feeling either too sensitive (every soft touch triggers at full velocity) or too dead (you have to hit hard to get any response). Good pad controllers let you customize the velocity curve in software.

Aftertouch: Matters for expression, not required. Channel aftertouch (pressing harder after a note is playing) is available on many mid-range controllers and enables real-time modulation of vibrato, filter, or any other CC-mapped parameter. Polyphonic aftertouch (each key sends independent pressure) is significantly more expressive but currently limited to premium controllers like the NI Komplete Kontrol S49 MK3 and some boutique options. If you make heavily expressive electronic music, polyphonic aftertouch is worth seeking out.

Keybed weight and action: Highly subjective, very important. There is no objectively correct keybed for all producers. Trained pianists often want fully weighted hammer action. Synth players prefer light, fast synth action. Most hybrid producers find semi-weighted to be the best compromise. Try before you buy if at all possible, or check trusted reviews that specifically discuss keybed feel.

Number of keys: Practical, not aspirational. It is tempting to buy a 61-key controller because “more is better,” but if your desk is small or you are making beats rather than playing classical piano, 49 keys will serve you just as well and cost significantly less. Be honest about how many octaves you actually use in your music.

Faders and encoders: Useful if you use them. Hardware faders and knobs are genuinely useful for mixing, automation, and real-time parameter control. But they add cost and size. If you do all your mixing with a mouse, do not pay for faders you will never touch. If you record automation curves by hand, hardware faders are a workflow revelation.

Build quality: More important than it looks. Cheap plastic encoders that wobble, faders that skip, and keys that stick are not just annoying — they introduce errors into your performances and make the controller feel unreliable. At sub-$100 prices, build quality compromises are acceptable. At $300+, they are not.

Standalone operation: Only matters if you need it. Standalone grooveboxes like the Novation Circuit Tracks and the Ableton Push 3 Standalone can function without a computer, which is transformative for mobile production and live performance. If you only ever produce in your studio, this feature is irrelevant and adds significant cost.

Buying Advice by Genre and Workflow

Different production styles have legitimately different controller requirements. Here is targeted advice for the most common scenarios.

Hip-hop and trap producers: Your priority is pads. The MPC workflow — programming 808s, hi-hats, and snares by finger-drumming into pads — is the DNA of hip-hop production. The Akai MPK Mini MK4 is the entry point. The NI Maschine MK4 is the premium destination. If you want to use your existing DAW (FL Studio, Ableton, Logic) rather than Maschine’s software, the Akai MPD226 or the Akai MPK249 are more DAW-agnostic options. For more on genre-specific workflows, see our guide on how to make trap beats.

Electronic, EDM, and synth-based producers: You need keys, probably 49 or more, with a keybed that feels good for extended playing. Aftertouch is a significant advantage for adding expression to pad and synth sounds. The Arturia KeyLab Essential 49 MK3 is the best value. If you use a lot of NI instruments, the Komplete Kontrol S49 MK3’s polyphonic aftertouch and NKS integration will fundamentally change how you interact with software synths. The Ableton Push 3 is the choice for live performance-focused electronic producers.

Lo-fi, ambient, and bedroom producers: Portability and simplicity often matter more than features here. The Arturia MiniLab 3 or Akai MPK Mini MK4 at $99 are genuinely sufficient for most lo-fi production. If you are making textural, pad-based music and you want to go deeper into sound design and automation, consider upgrading to the KeyLab Essential 49 MK3 for the extra keys and better key feel. See our guide on how to make lo-fi beats for workflow ideas that translate well to compact controller setups.

Trained pianists entering production: You will likely be frustrated by synth-action and mini keys quickly. Invest in a fully weighted or semi-weighted 61-key or 88-key controller. The Arturia KeyLab 61 MK3 with its semi-weighted action and motorized faders is an excellent studio choice. For pure piano feel, Roland and Kawai make excellent 88-key MIDI controllers, though these are more specialized and beyond the scope of this roundup.

Live performers: Your primary concern is reliability. Hardware that fails on stage is catastrophic. Prioritize controllers with solid build quality, minimal menu-diving in performance, and good integration with your performance software. The Ableton Push 3 Standalone ($1,999) is the ultimate live tool for Ableton users. The Novation Circuit Tracks is an excellent standalone option for hardware-based live acts. If you use Ableton but cannot afford the Push 3, the Novation Launchkey 49 MK4 or a Launchpad X combined with a small keyboard controller is a cost-effective live rig.

Home studio producers on a tight budget: Do not overthink it. Buy the Akai MPK Mini MK4 or Arturia MiniLab 3 for $99 and start making music. The gear gap between a $99 controller and a $499 controller is real, but it is far smaller than the gap between having a controller and not having one. You can always upgrade later when you have a clearer sense of what your workflow actually demands. Pair it with a solid DAW — see our best DAW for beginners guide to find the right software companion.

Setup, Connectivity, and DAW Integration

Most modern MIDI controllers connect via USB and are class-compliant, meaning they work without installing any drivers on Mac or PC. Simply plug in and your DAW will recognize the device as a MIDI input. Some controllers — particularly those with deep DAW integration like the Ableton Push 3, NI Maschine, or Komplete Kontrol keyboards — require dedicated software or companion apps to unlock their full feature sets.

USB vs. MIDI DIN: Almost every controller in 2026 connects via USB. Traditional 5-pin DIN MIDI ports (the round, five-pin connectors) are still present on some mid-range and premium controllers, primarily for connecting to hardware synthesizers, drum machines, and other standalone gear that does not have USB. If you run a hardware-heavy studio with vintage synthesizers, DIN MIDI connectivity matters. If you are purely in the box, USB is all you need.

Bus-powered vs. power supply: Budget and mid-range controllers are almost universally bus-powered via USB, meaning they draw power directly from your computer’s USB port. Premium controllers with motorized faders (like the Arturia KeyLab 61 MK3) or with their own processors (like the Push 3 Standalone) require a dedicated power supply. This is a minor consideration for desktop studio use but relevant for touring and mobile setups.

DAW-specific setup: Even “universal” controllers usually benefit from DAW-specific configuration. In Ableton Live, go to Preferences > MIDI and enable Track and Remote for your controller. In FL Studio, use the MIDI Settings panel to assign your controller to a controller script. In Logic Pro, use the Controller Assignments window (Command+L) to map hardware controls. Most major controllers ship with DAW-specific scripts that handle this automatically when the companion software is installed.

Latency: MIDI latency is rarely an issue with USB controllers and modern computers. Your audio latency (the delay between playing a note and hearing sound from your software instrument) is determined by your audio interface buffer size, not by the MIDI controller. Keeping your audio buffer low (128 samples or below during recording) will eliminate any perceptible latency. Practical Exercises

Beginner Exercise

First Session: Play a Chord Progression

Connect your new MIDI controller to your DAW and load a piano or electric piano software instrument. Using only white keys, play a simple I-IV-V-I chord progression (C major, F major, G major, C major) and record it as a MIDI clip. This establishes the basic workflow of controller-to-DAW-to-sound and builds muscle memory for your new keyboard layout.

Intermediate Exercise

Finger Drumming: Program a Four-Bar Hip-Hop Loop

Using the pads on your controller, program a four-bar drum loop entirely in real time: first a kick pattern, then a snare, then hi-hats, then a percussion layer. Use quantize to tighten the timing but keep slight velocity variation by varying how hard you hit each pad. This exercise develops pad sensitivity awareness and rhythmic feel on hardware rather than in the piano roll.

Advanced Exercise

Live Set: Perform a Three-Section Arrangement Without Touching the Mouse

Map your controller's pads to Ableton Session View clips and use faders for channel volume and knobs for filter cutoff on three different tracks. Build a short arrangement with a clear intro, drop, and outro section, performing the entire piece live using only the controller. This forces you to understand CC mapping, clip launching, and real-time automation simultaneously — the core skill set of hardware-based performance.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ Do I need a MIDI controller to produce music?
No — you can produce entirely with a mouse and keyboard. But a MIDI controller dramatically speeds up melodic input, gives you tactile control over your DAW, and makes the process more musical and enjoyable. Most producers find it an essential workflow upgrade.
FAQ What is the best MIDI controller for beginners?
The Akai MPK Mini MK4 and Arturia MiniLab 3 are both excellent at $99. The MPK Mini has better pads; the MiniLab 3 has a better synth software bundle. Either will serve a beginner well for at least two or three years.
FAQ How many keys do I need on a MIDI controller?
49 keys is the practical sweet spot for most producers — it covers nearly four octaves without requiring constant octave-shifting. If you are a trained pianist, consider 61 or 88. If portability is your priority, 25 mini keys is workable.
FAQ Is the Ableton Push 3 worth the price?
For dedicated Ableton Live users, especially those who perform live, yes — it is the deepest hardware-DAW integration available. For producers who primarily work in the box and never perform live, the $699 price is hard to justify versus a $249 Launchkey 49 MK4.
FAQ What is the difference between a pad controller and a keyboard controller?
Keyboard controllers have piano-style keys for playing melodic and harmonic parts. Pad controllers have velocity-sensitive drum pads for programming beats by finger-drumming. Many hybrid controllers include both. Your choice should reflect your primary production workflow.
FAQ Do MIDI controllers affect sound quality?
No. MIDI controllers send only control data — note numbers, velocity values, and CC messages. They do not carry any audio signal. Sound quality is determined entirely by your software instruments, audio interface, and monitoring setup.
FAQ Can I use a MIDI controller with any DAW?
Yes — any class-compliant USB MIDI controller will work as a basic MIDI input with any DAW. However, some controllers (Push 3, Maschine, Komplete Kontrol) have deep integration features that only work with specific software. General-purpose controllers like the Arturia KeyLab series offer broader DAW compatibility.
FAQ What is aftertouch and do I need it?
Aftertouch is a secondary pressure message sent when you press down harder on a key after the initial note-on. It can control vibrato, filter modulation, or any CC-mapped parameter in real time, adding significant expressiveness. It is not essential but is a meaningful upgrade for expressive electronic and synth-based playing.
