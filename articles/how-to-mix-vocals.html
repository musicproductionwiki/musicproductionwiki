How to Mix Vocals: Complete Step-by-Step Guide

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2025 12 min read

Vocals are the most exposed element in most mixes — listeners focus on them first, and problems are immediately apparent. A well-mixed vocal feels effortlessly present, clear, and emotionally connected to the listener without drawing attention to the technical processing behind it. Getting there requires a systematic approach that addresses each problem in the right order with the right tools.

What we'll cover: The complete vocal mixing chain in the correct order — cleaning and preparation, EQ, compression, de-essing, saturation, reverb and delay, parallel processing, automation, and how to evaluate whether the vocal is sitting correctly in context.

Step 1: Preparation and Cleaning

Before processing begins, the raw vocal recording needs preparation. This step happens before any plugins are inserted and catches problems that processing will make worse rather than better.

Comping: If multiple takes were recorded, assemble the best performance from each take using your DAW's comping feature. Select the best phrase from take 1, the best chorus from take 2, and so on. The comp should be the single strongest performance possible — pitching and timing correction work better on already-good performances than on performances with fundamental problems.

Noise reduction: Listen for background noise in the recording — room noise, air conditioning hum, computer fan noise — and reduce it if present. iZotope RX's De-noise or Dialogue Contour handles this cleanly. Alternatively, use a gate with a threshold set just above the noise floor to silence gaps between phrases.

Pitch correction: Apply pitch correction before EQ and compression so that processing acts on the corrected pitch rather than the pre-corrected pitch. Light Auto-Tune in Auto mode or Melodyne for detailed note-by-note correction. Correct tuning issues without removing the natural pitch variation and vibrato that makes the performance feel human — over-correction produces the robotic quality associated with poor Auto-Tune use.

Timing correction: Minor timing inconsistencies — syllables slightly ahead of or behind the beat — can be corrected in Melodyne or through your DAW's flex/warp tools. Address genuine problems, not character — some performance timing variation is musical.

Breath editing: Decide whether to keep or reduce breaths. Natural breaths add intimacy and realism. Excessive, loud breaths distract from the lyrical content. Reduce (not eliminate) breaths that are louder than feels natural, either with volume automation or by trimming the breath's start point.

Step 2: Equalisation

Vocal EQ removes problem frequencies and shapes the vocal's character to sit correctly in the mix. The order of moves: remove problems first, then add character if needed.

High-pass filter: Apply a high-pass filter below 80–120 Hz. There is no useful vocal information below this range — only low-frequency rumble from microphone handling, HVAC noise, and room modes. A high-pass at 100 Hz rolls off cleanly without affecting the vocal's body.

Low-mid mud (200–400 Hz): This range is where most vocal recordings accumulate boxiness — a thick, congested quality that makes the vocal feel indistinct. Cut 2–4 dB with a medium Q around 250–350 Hz. The amount varies by vocalist and microphone — listen for the frequency where a cut produces an immediate sense of clarity.

Presence (2–5 kHz): The intelligibility zone. Boosting 2–4 kHz adds presence and forward clarity — the vocal cuts through the mix. Cutting in this range reduces harshness on vocals that are naturally bright or aggressive. Medium Q, conservative amount (1–3 dB).

Air (8–16 kHz): A gentle high-shelf boost (0.5–1.5 dB above 10 kHz) adds air and openness — the quality of expensive microphones that extends the high-frequency detail. Don't overdo it — too much air creates harshness.

Dynamic EQ (FabFilter Pro-Q3's dynamic band feature, or iZotope Neutron's dynamic EQ) handles frequency issues that only occur on certain phrases. If the low-mids build up only when the vocalist gets louder, a dynamic cut that engages based on level is more transparent than a static cut that affects every moment.

Step 3: Compression

Vocal compression is covered in depth in the dedicated compression guide. In the context of the full vocal chain, use two stages: a first compressor (optical emulation like the LA-2A or Renaissance Compressor in Opto mode) for smooth, transparent levelling at a 2:1–3:1 ratio taking 3–5 dB of gain reduction, and a second compressor (VCA type) for character and transient control at 3:1–4:1 taking another 2–4 dB. Together they produce a controlled, professional-sounding vocal without the squashed quality of a single compressor doing all the work.

Attack timing is critical: 15–25 ms to preserve consonant clarity. Release on auto or adapted to the tempo. Listen for the compressor breathing — the gain reduction audibly cycling between compressed and uncompressed states — and slow the release if it's too fast.

Step 4: De-Essing

Sibilance — harsh S, T, and SH sounds — is one of the most common vocal mixing problems. Microphones capture sibilance at higher levels than human hearing perceives it in real space, and compression often makes sibilance more prominent by raising the overall level of softer passages relative to the peaks. A de-esser addresses this by compressing specifically in the sibilance frequency range (5–10 kHz for most vocalists) when sibilance occurs.

Place the de-esser after compression — compression tends to worsen sibilance, so de-essing after compression addresses the problem in its final state. Set the de-esser's frequency to the specific range where the sibilance is most prominent (listen to the vocal while soloing the de-esser's detection range to identify it). Set the threshold so it engages on genuine sibilance without affecting the rest of the vocal. Good de-essers include FabFilter Pro-DS, Waves Renaissance DeEsser, and iZotope Neutron's built-in de-esser.

Step 5: Saturation and Harmonic Enhancement

Saturation adds harmonic content to the vocal that makes it feel warmer, more present, and more three-dimensional in the mix. A subtle amount — just enough to add body without audible distortion — transforms a clean but thin digital vocal into something that feels more like a professionally recorded, analogue-processed vocal.

Soundtoys Decapitator in A mode (warm, even harmonics) at very low drive settings is a go-to for vocal saturation. FabFilter Saturn 2's Warm Tube mode. iZotope Neutron's Exciter targeting the upper midrange (2–5 kHz) for presence enhancement rather than warmth. Saturation on the parallel bus (50% wet, 50% dry) allows adding more without it becoming obvious.

Step 6: Reverb and Delay

Reverb and delay on vocals serve different purposes and are most effectively used together rather than choosing one over the other.

Short reverb or room: A short plate or room reverb (0.5–1.2 seconds) gives the vocal a sense of acoustic space without pushing it backward in the mix. 15–25 ms pre-delay ensures the dry signal arrives first, maintaining the vocal's presence. High-pass the reverb return above 200 Hz to prevent low-frequency buildup.

Long reverb for effect: A longer hall or ambient reverb (1.5–3+ seconds) for slow sections, breakdowns, or deliberate spatial effect. Used at lower levels so it creates atmosphere rather than washing out the vocal. Automating the reverb send level — increasing it in the chorus, reducing it in the verse — varies the spatial depth of the vocal dynamically.

Slap delay: A very short delay (60–120 ms, single repeat) thickens the vocal without creating a distinct echo. Often used in rock and pop production to add width and depth without obviousness. Pan the delay slightly opposite the dry vocal for stereo width.

Rhythmic delay: An eighth-note or dotted eighth-note delay (synchronized to tempo) adds rhythmic energy and feels intentional rather than artificial. The dotted eighth-note delay (popularised by U2 and Edge's guitar work) is widely used on lead vocals in pop and rock. Set the delay feedback to 2–3 repeats maximum.

Step 7: Parallel Processing

Parallel compression on the vocal — sending the vocal to a heavily compressed return and blending it at low level with the dry signal — adds density and sustain without the squashed quality of heavy compression on the main signal. Compress the parallel signal aggressively (ratio 8:1+, threshold set for 10–15 dB of gain reduction), then bring the return up slowly until the vocal gains presence and sustain without the compression becoming audible.

Parallel saturation works similarly: a saturated vocal on a parallel bus blended subtly with the dry signal adds warmth and harmonic content without making the processing audible. This is why heavily processed professional vocals often feel rich and present without sounding obviously processed.

Step 8: Automation

Volume automation is the final, most important step in vocal mixing. Even with excellent compression, some phrases will be too loud or too quiet. Automation addresses these moments surgically — phrase by phrase, sometimes word by word.

Draw volume automation on the vocal track after all processing is applied. Start by listening to the entire vocal in context of the full mix and noting moments where the vocal disappears or jumps out. Target: consistent presence throughout, with natural dynamic variation preserved (choruses slightly more intense than verses, emotional peaks slightly more present) but eliminating the unintentional dynamic jumps that pull attention.

Automate reverb and delay send levels to vary spatial depth — less reverb in intimate verse sections, more in the expansive chorus. Automate the de-esser threshold if specific sections have more sibilance than others. Automation is the most time-consuming but highest-return step in vocal mixing.

Evaluating the Vocal in Context

Every vocal mixing decision should be made with the full mix playing, not the vocal in solo. The goal is not a vocal that sounds perfect in isolation — it's a vocal that sits correctly in the mix. A vocal that sounds slightly thin soloed may have exactly the right amount of low-mid content to sit clearly among guitars and piano. A vocal that sounds bright soloed may have exactly the right edge to cut through.

Reference checks: listen on headphones (different from monitor speakers, reveals stereo placement and detail differently), on laptop speakers (reveals how the vocal sits at normal consumer playback levels), and at low volume (if the vocal disappears when you turn down, it needs more presence). The vocal should remain intelligible and present across all of these listening conditions.
