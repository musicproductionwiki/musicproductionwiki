FabFilter Pro-Q 4 Review: Is It Still the Best EQ Plugin?

By MusicProductionWiki Staff • Updated May 2026 • 13 min read

⚡ Quick Verdict

Score: 9.5/10. Pro-Q 4 raised the bar on what an EQ plugin can do. Spectral Dynamics turns it into a resonance suppressor and surgical dynamic processor, eliminating the need for separate Soothe-style plugins for most applications. Instance List gives you a project-wide EQ overview no other plugin offers. EQ Sketch and Character Modes round out a package that genuinely earns the upgrade from Pro-Q 3. FabFilter Pro-Q 4 is still the best EQ plugin available. Get the free trial before purchasing.

FabFilter released Pro-Q 3 in 2018 and it immediately became the reference EQ plugin for professional mixing and mastering. Six years of industry dominance later, Pro-Q 4 arrived in December 2024 with over 50 changes and additions. The question every Pro-Q 3 user immediately asked: is this feature bloat, or did FabFilter actually find room to improve a near-perfect plugin?

The answer, after extensive use across mixing and mastering sessions in 2025, is that Pro-Q 4 is a genuine advancement. Spectral Dynamics is the headline feature, and it's the kind of addition that immediately changes how you work. The Instance List is the workflow innovation nobody knew they needed. Character Modes expand Pro-Q's versatility into coloring territory it previously couldn't cover. And the improvements to Dynamic EQ distortion and linear phase processing are meaningful even if invisible.

What's New in FabFilter Pro-Q 4

1. Spectral Dynamics — The Game-Changer

Pro-Q 3 introduced dynamic EQ — the ability to make a band's gain respond to signal level, compressing or expanding specific frequencies dynamically rather than statically. Pro-Q 4 takes this substantially further with Spectral Dynamics mode, which applies threshold-based compression or expansion to specific frequencies within a band using spectral analysis.

The distinction matters. Traditional dynamic EQ adjusts the gain of an entire band based on whether the overall signal exceeds a threshold. Spectral Dynamics targets the precise frequencies within that band that are problematic — not the whole band. This is the same technology behind dedicated resonance suppression plugins like iZotope Soothe, Smooth Operator, and similar tools. The difference in Pro-Q 4 is that it's integrated directly into the EQ you're already using on every track.

The practical applications are substantial. Vocal recordings with resonant box coloration from a home studio environment — apply a Spectral Dynamics band in the problematic 300–600Hz range and the resonance gets dynamically suppressed only when it appears, leaving the natural tonal balance intact when the resonance isn't active. Sibilant high-frequencies — a Spectral Dynamics band above 6kHz acts as a transparent de-esser that adapts to the specific sibilant frequencies rather than a fixed threshold. Muddy midrange buildup when multiple instruments compete — targeted dynamic suppression of the overlapping frequencies reduces masking without static EQ cuts that thin out the sound when those frequencies aren't clashing.

Attack and release controls allow musical tuning of the dynamic behavior. Low attack and release for snapping transient control; slower values for smoother, less perceptible processing. The Production Expert review noted that the feature excels in the low-mid and low range but can take away too much in the top end on broader settings — a fair limitation worth knowing for high-frequency applications where the processing can be too heavy-handed at high resolution settings.

2. Instance List — Project-Wide EQ Overview

Open a single Pro-Q 4 instance and click Instance List. You see every instance of Pro-Q 4 in your session displayed in a scrollable stack — track names, miniaturized spectrum analyzers, and EQ curve overlays for each track, all in one view. Click any track in the list to edit its EQ directly without switching windows in your DAW.

More significantly, Instance List shows frequency collisions — areas where multiple tracks are boosting the same frequencies — highlighted in a red haze around affected nodes. This visual representation of frequency masking between tracks is something that previously required either careful listening and comparison or third-party analysis tools. Having it directly inside the EQ plugin changes how you approach sessions that have been mixed with competing midrange elements.

Mixonline's real-world review documented using the feature across a 77-track session with no performance issues on an M2 Ultra Mac Studio — a positive sign for scalability. The alphabetical ordering rather than track order can require workarounds (adding numbers to track names) in Pro Tools, though FabFilter noted that track order display is coming in a subsequent update for Pro Tools, with Logic Pro following.

3. EQ Sketch — Draw Your EQ Shape

EQ Sketch lets you draw a freehand curve directly on the EQ display. Pro-Q 4 automatically creates bands to match the shape you drew, interpreting your freehand input into specific band types and gain amounts. It's a rapid prototyping tool for complex EQ shapes — instead of placing individual bands one at a time and adjusting each, you sketch the general treatment you have in mind and let the plugin work out the implementation.

For experienced engineers, EQ Sketch is a workflow accelerator for specific scenarios: when you have a clear mental picture of a complex multi-band treatment and want to get there quickly, or when working on a reference match where you need to approximate a particular frequency shape rapidly. It's not the primary workflow for methodical mixing, but it's genuinely useful when it fits the task.

4. Character Modes — Transparent to Warm

Pro-Q 3 was renowned for its transparent, characterless sound — a feature and a limitation depending on context. Some mixing scenarios require pristine, accurate EQ that adds no color. Others benefit from the harmonic character associated with analog hardware. Pro-Q 3 served the former exclusively; Pro-Q 4 serves both.

Three modes are available. Clean is identical to Pro-Q 3's transparent character. Gentle adds subtle harmonic content. Warm adds more pronounced harmonic enrichment — what WaveInformer described as sounding "brilliant" in their assessment. The character is applied as part of the EQ processing rather than as a separate effect, meaning it responds to the EQ's gain structure. More boost through a warm band means more character; less boost means less.

This eliminates a common workflow step of routing signal through a coloring plugin after the EQ to add analog character. For engineers who prefer all-in-one processing, Character Modes are a welcome addition. For engineers who want separate coloring plugins for more precise control, Clean mode preserves the Pro-Q 3 behavior unchanged.

5. Improved Dynamic EQ and Linear Phase Processing

FabFilter's confirmation in their forum that Pro-Q 4's Dynamic EQ has less distortion than Pro-Q 3, and that linear phase and natural phase processing have been improved, is significant even though it's not a visible new feature. For mastering applications where transparency is paramount, improved Dynamic EQ distortion means you can push the processing harder before artifacts become audible. The improved linear phase processing means less pre-ringing at equivalent settings.

6. Continuous Slope — From 12 to 96 dB/Octave

Pro-Q 3 offered a range of fixed slope options for filters. Pro-Q 4 adds continuous slope control, letting you set any slope value between 12 and 96 dB/octave rather than choosing from preset options. Combined with Q-Gain Interaction controls, this gives engineers unprecedented precision in filter shape design — particularly useful for mastering where the difference between adjacent slopes can be audible on full-range material.

7. Frequency Collision Detection in Instance List

Beyond the Instance List overview, Pro-Q 4 highlights frequency collisions in real time across all instances visible in the list. When two or more tracks are boosting overlapping frequencies, the collision appears as a red haze in the display. This makes the common problem of frequency masking between instruments — guitars and keyboards fighting in the midrange, kick and bass competing for low-end space — visually immediate and easier to address.

Who Should Upgrade — and Who Can Wait

✅ Upgrade Now

  • You currently use Soothe, Smooth Operator, or similar resonance suppressors — Spectral Dynamics covers most of those use cases inside Pro-Q
  • You work on large sessions with many tracks and Instance List's project-wide EQ view would speed up your workflow
  • You want harmonic character options within your EQ rather than a separate coloring plugin (Warm mode)
  • You do mastering and want improved linear phase and lower Dynamic EQ distortion
  • You can catch FabFilter's loyalty upgrade discount — historically generous for existing users

⏸️ No Urgency If...

  • You're happy with Pro-Q 3 and use separate resonance plugins for their additional control options
  • Your existing sessions all use Pro-Q 3 and you prefer to keep them consistent without coexisting plugin versions
  • Budget is constrained — Pro-Q 3 remains one of the finest EQs available and doesn't become a worse plugin when Pro-Q 4 exists
  • You've never owned any version of Pro-Q — at $179 for new users, both versions are the same price, so buy Pro-Q 4

Alternatives to Pro-Q 4

Ozone EQ (within iZotope Ozone): Integrated mastering EQ with tonal balance metering and reference tracks. Works within the Ozone ecosystem but lacks Pro-Q 4's surgical precision, Spectral Dynamics, and Instance List.

Waves SSL E-Channel EQ: Analog character, fast workflow, excellent for tracking and mixing bus treatment. Not a precision tool; no dynamic EQ or spectral processing. Different tool for different applications.

Sonnox Oxford EQ: Highly regarded surgical EQ with strong analog model options. Competes with Pro-Q 4 on sound quality but lacks Spectral Dynamics and Instance List workflow advantages.

iZotope Soothe 2: Dedicated resonance suppressor with more granular control than Pro-Q 4's Spectral Dynamics. For complex, heavily resonant material where surgical spectral shaping is the primary task, Soothe 2 still offers advantages. For general use, Pro-Q 4's integration is more convenient.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is FabFilter Pro-Q 4 worth upgrading from Pro-Q 3?

Yes for most mixing and mastering engineers. Spectral Dynamics alone justifies the upgrade — it replaces a separate resonance suppressor for most applications. Instance List, Character Modes, and improved Dynamic EQ processing add further value. FabFilter offers loyalty upgrade discounts. Try the free trial first.

What is Spectral Dynamics in FabFilter Pro-Q 4?

Spectral Dynamics applies threshold-based compression or expansion to specific frequencies within an EQ band — not the whole band. This targets problem resonances precisely, acting as a built-in resonance suppressor (similar to iZotope Soothe) directly inside the EQ. Attack and release controls allow tuning the dynamic behavior.

What is the Instance List in FabFilter Pro-Q 4?

Instance List shows every Pro-Q 4 instance in your session from a single plugin window. You can edit any track's EQ directly without switching focus, see all EQ treatments simultaneously, and identify frequency collisions between tracks visualized as a red haze — making masking between instruments immediately visible.

What are Character Modes in FabFilter Pro-Q 4?

Character Modes add analog coloring to Pro-Q 4: Clean (transparent, identical to Pro-Q 3), Gentle (subtle harmonics), and Warm (more pronounced harmonic enrichment). This allows Pro-Q 4 to serve both transparent surgical EQ and analog-warm coloring applications without a separate plugin.

How much does FabFilter Pro-Q 4 cost?

$179 for new users. Upgrade discounts are available for existing Pro-Q owners — FabFilter historically offers generous loyalty pricing. Check FabFilter's website or Plugin Boutique for current upgrade pricing. A free trial is available.

Does Pro-Q 4 replace Pro-Q 3 in existing sessions?

No. Pro-Q 4 and Pro-Q 3 coexist on your system. Existing sessions keep their Pro-Q 3 instances intact — FabFilter confirmed this is intentional, as some processing (Dynamic EQ, linear phase) has been updated and sounds different. Pro-Q 3 presets are readable in Pro-Q 4 using FabFilter's preset format.

What is EQ Sketch in FabFilter Pro-Q 4?

EQ Sketch lets you draw a freehand curve on the EQ display. Pro-Q 4 automatically creates bands to match the shape. It's a rapid prototyping tool for visualizing and implementing complex EQ shapes without placing individual bands manually.

Is FabFilter Pro-Q 4 good for mastering?

Yes — one of the best mastering EQs available. Linear phase processing, Clean mode transparency, Spectral Dynamics for resonance control, Warm Character mode for subtle harmonic enrichment, and Instance List for checking EQ consistency across album tracks make it a complete mastering toolkit.

What formats does FabFilter Pro-Q 4 come in?

VST, VST3, AU, AAX Native, AudioSuite, and CLAP. Mac requires OS 10.13+ (64-bit). Windows requires Windows 11/10/8/7/Vista with a VST 2 or 3 host.
