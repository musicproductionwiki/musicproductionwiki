Overview

FabFilter Pro-Q has been the benchmark mixing EQ for over a decade. Pro-Q 3 was so good that the conversation around it had settled: it was simply the plug-in you used. Pro-Q 4 had the unenviable task of meaningfully improving something that most engineers considered complete.

It succeeds. Spectral Dynamics is not a marketing feature — it solves a genuine mixing problem in a way that no previous EQ has managed cleanly. The improved spectrum analyzer is immediately apparent. The addition of CLAP format support is a welcome technical update. And the core parametric EQ engine, already the finest available, remains untouched in ways that matter.

This review focuses on what is new and how the new features integrate into real mixing and mastering workflows.

Spectral Dynamics — Deep Dive

Spectral Dynamics is the headline feature of Pro-Q 4 and deserves extended examination because it represents a genuine conceptual advance in EQ design.

The Problem It Solves

Consider a mix where a string ensemble occasionally develops a harsh buildup around 3–4 kHz during louder passages. Standard static EQ would permanently cut that range, dulling the strings even when they're playing quietly and beautifully. Dynamic EQ is better — it only cuts when the level exceeds a threshold — but it responds to the overall level of the entire signal, not specifically to the energy at 3–4 kHz. If the strings play a loud passage that is actually smooth and beautiful in that range, the dynamic EQ may still trigger.

Spectral Dynamics solves this by analyzing the spectral energy specifically at the frequency band being processed. The band only activates when the energy at that specific frequency region crosses the threshold — not when the overall signal is loud, and not when unrelated frequencies are loud.

How to Use Spectral Dynamics

Enable Spectral Dynamics mode on any band by clicking the band and selecting "Spectral Dynamics" from the mode dropdown (or double-clicking the band dot to enter edit mode). The band then shows a threshold line in the spectrum analyzer display. Set threshold, attack, release, and gain range exactly as you would for dynamic EQ.

The key difference you will notice immediately: the band activates much more surgically. It is ideal for:

  • Acoustic instruments with resonant peaks — piano, guitar, strings, brass. The resonance only needs addressing when it actually rings; Spectral Dynamics ignores everything else.
  • Vocal sibilance control — more transparent than a broadband de-esser because it responds only to actual sibilance energy.
  • Controlling room modes in drum recordings — a 200 Hz buildup that appears only on hard snare hits, not on ghost notes.
  • Mastering — taming frequency imbalances that only appear in specific arrangements in a mix, without affecting the passages where the balance is correct.

Dynamic EQ Workflow

Pro-Q 4's dynamic EQ — carried over from Pro-Q 3 with improvements — remains one of the cleanest implementations available. Each band can be set to Dynamic mode independently, with a threshold, attack, release, and selectable direction (cut or boost when signal exceeds threshold).

The most practical dynamic EQ workflow in Pro-Q 4 uses the built-in external sidechain. Route the kick drum as a sidechain input to Pro-Q 4 on the bass track, then set a dynamic band at 80–120 Hz to cut when the kick hits. This creates a transparent frequency-domain ducking that keeps kick and bass separated without touching a compressor.

The ability to mix static and dynamic bands in the same instance of Pro-Q 4 is a significant workflow advantage. A typical professional mix might use five static correction bands and two or three dynamic bands — all in one instance with a single spectrum display.

Mid-Side Processing

Pro-Q 4 offers the most granular MS control of any EQ plug-in. The global M/S mode processes the mid and side signals together. Per-band M/S goes further: each of the 24 bands can be independently assigned to process the Left channel only, Right channel only, Mid only, or Side only.

Common per-band MS applications:

  • High-pass the Side channel at 80–100 Hz to clean up low-end mono coherence issues without touching the Mid signal
  • Boost the Side channel at 8–12 kHz to open up stereo width in the high frequencies
  • Cut a specific resonance that only appears in the Left channel (from a physical instrument placement asymmetry)
  • Apply a gentle air boost only to the Mid signal on a vocal to add presence without widening

Linear Phase Modes

Most EQ plug-ins use minimum phase processing, which introduces phase shift as a side effect of EQ. For mixing on individual tracks this is generally inaudible and acceptable. For mastering, parallel processing, and mid-side workflows where phase coherence matters, linear phase mode is essential.

Pro-Q 4 offers five linear phase resolutions: Low, Medium, High, Very High, and Maximum. Higher resolution means greater accuracy and lower pre-ringing artifacts but more CPU usage and longer processing latency. For most mastering work, Medium or High resolution is the right balance. Maximum is available for the most demanding situations.

The "Natural Phase" mode is a middle ground introduced in Pro-Q 3 and retained in Pro-Q 4: it minimizes phase shift without the full latency overhead of true linear phase. Many engineers use Natural Phase as their default and switch to full linear phase only when mastering or when mixing in parallel with another instance.

Spectrum Analyzer

The Pro-Q 4 spectrum analyzer is the best-in-class real-time spectral display available in a mixing EQ. Its resolution is noticeably higher than Pro-Q 3, with better distinction between closely spaced spectral events. The controls available are extensive:

  • Pre-EQ vs Post-EQ display — toggle to see what the EQ is doing before and after processing
  • Freeze — snapshot the spectrum at a moment in time for analysis
  • External input — display the spectrum of a different track alongside the current one (for masking analysis without Collision Detection)
  • Range and speed controls — adjust how much history the analyzer displays and how fast it responds
  • Scale — dB range, frequency range, and reference level all adjustable

For mastering engineers, the spectrum analyzer alone justifies the upgrade from Pro-Q 3. The increased resolution makes it substantially easier to spot narrow resonances and frequency buildup.

Collision Detection

Collision Detection is a new feature in Pro-Q 4 that identifies frequency masking between two tracks. Load Pro-Q 4 on a vocal, set the external sidechain to the lead guitar, and enable Collision Detection. Pro-Q 4 will highlight frequency ranges where the guitar and vocal are masking each other and suggest EQ moves to separate them.

In practice, Collision Detection is a useful starting point rather than a final solution. It surfaces masking issues you might spend time hunting manually. The suggested EQ moves are rarely perfect, but they are a good diagnostic tool, especially for less experienced engineers who are still developing their ears.

Workflow and Interface

The Pro-Q 4 interface is the same as Pro-Q 3 in all the ways that matter: bands are created by double-clicking anywhere on the spectrum display, dragged to adjust frequency and gain, and scrolled to adjust Q. The interaction is faster and more intuitive than any hardware EQ and most competing plug-ins.

New in Pro-Q 4: the band info panel has been expanded to show more detail at a glance, including the current dynamic gain reduction in real-time for dynamic and Spectral Dynamics bands. The spectrum comparison view is more legible. The overall visual density has been reduced slightly, which makes the UI feel more spacious on high-resolution displays.

Verdict

Who Should Buy Pro-Q 4

  • Buy Pro-Q 4 if you mix acoustic instruments, orchestral music, complex polyphonic material, or do mastering work. Spectral Dynamics will become a daily tool.
  • Buy Pro-Q 4 if you are starting fresh — it is the best mixing EQ available and will remain so for years.
  • Upgrade from Pro-Q 3 if you frequently fight resonant peaks or do mastering — the Spectral Dynamics and improved analyzer justify the cost.
  • Stay on Pro-Q 3 if your work is primarily straightforward pop/hip-hop mixing where static EQ covers everything and you are satisfied with your current results.

FabFilter Pro-Q 4 earns a 9.5/10. Spectral Dynamics is a meaningful addition that changes how you approach problem frequencies. The spectrum analyzer upgrade alone would have been worth a point release. The core engine remains untouchable. The only reason it is not a 10 is that the upgrade pricing from Pro-Q 3 could be more generous given the relatively focused scope of new features.

Practice Exercises

🟢 Beginner: Learn the Filter Types

Load Pro-Q 4 on a full mix bus or a vocal. Create one of each filter type: Bell, Low Shelf, High Shelf, Low-pass, High-pass, and Notch. On each band, set a dramatic boost or cut of ±12 dB and sweep the frequency slowly from 20 Hz to 20 kHz while listening. You are learning to identify what each filter type sounds like and where different frequency ranges live perceptually. A low shelf boosted at 100 Hz adds warmth; a bell boosted at 3 kHz adds presence; a high-pass at 80 Hz removes rumble. Document your observations. This single exercise builds more EQ instinct than months of reading.

🟡 Intermediate: Spectral Dynamics for Vocal De-Essing

Load Pro-Q 4 on a vocal track with noticeable sibilance ("s" and "sh" sounds that are harsh or sharp). Enable external Spectrum Analyzer to identify where the sibilance frequency lives — typically 5–8 kHz but varies by voice. Create a new band in that range. Set mode to Spectral Dynamics. Set the band to a cut of -6 to -8 dB. Lower the threshold until the band activates only on the harshest sibilant moments (watch the gain reduction meter on the band). Set attack to 1–2ms and release to 80–150ms. Compare to a traditional de-esser plugin on the same signal. Spectral Dynamics should sound more transparent because it is only activating on the actual sibilance energy, not on overall level.

🔴 Advanced: Full Mastering Chain with Pro-Q 4

Set up a mastering chain using two instances of Pro-Q 4 in series. Instance 1: Zero Latency mode, minimum phase. Use this for broad tonal shaping — gentle shelves and wide bell curves to balance the overall frequency response. Instance 2: High-resolution Linear Phase mode, M/S processing. Use this for corrective work — narrow cuts for resonances, Spectral Dynamics for frequency-specific level management, and per-band Side processing to control stereo width at specific frequencies (high-pass the Side channel at 100 Hz; gentle Side boost at 10 kHz). Compare the result to your starting point using the spectrum analyzer. This is a professional mastering topology used in top-tier commercial releases.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is new in FabFilter Pro-Q 4?

Pro-Q 4 introduced Spectral Dynamics — per-band dynamic EQ that triggers based on spectral energy at that frequency rather than overall level — along with Collision Detection for identifying frequency masking between tracks and an improved high-resolution spectrum analyzer.

Is FabFilter Pro-Q 4 worth the upgrade from Pro-Q 3?

Yes, especially if you mix acoustic instruments, orchestral music, or do mastering. Spectral Dynamics is a genuinely useful new workflow tool. If you primarily do static EQ on straightforward mixes, the upgrade is less urgent.

What is Spectral Dynamics in Pro-Q 4?

Spectral Dynamics applies dynamic EQ behavior based on the spectral energy at each frequency band specifically — not just overall level. It triggers only when energy is detected at that precise frequency region, making it ideal for controlling resonances in acoustic instruments and complex sibilance.

What is the difference between Dynamic EQ and Spectral Dynamics?

Dynamic EQ triggers based on the overall signal level or a sidechain input. Spectral Dynamics triggers based on the spectral energy specifically at the frequency band being processed, making it more targeted and less prone to false triggering.

Does FabFilter Pro-Q 4 have mid-side processing?

Yes. Each EQ band can be independently set to Left, Right, Mid, or Side processing, allowing extremely precise stereo field control within a single plug-in instance.

What is linear phase mode in Pro-Q 4?

Linear phase mode processes audio without introducing phase shift. Important for mastering and parallel processing. Pro-Q 4 offers five linear phase resolutions from Low to Maximum, plus a Natural Phase option that minimizes phase shift with less latency overhead.

How much does FabFilter Pro-Q 4 cost?

FabFilter Pro-Q 4 costs $179 USD for a new license. Upgrades from Pro-Q 3 are available at a reduced price through FabFilter's website and Plugin Boutique.

Is FabFilter Pro-Q 4 good for mastering?

Yes — it is one of the most widely used mastering EQs. Its linear phase mode, high-resolution spectrum analyzer, Spectral Dynamics, and per-band M/S processing make it ideal at every stage of the mastering process.

What are the best alternatives to FabFilter Pro-Q 4?

iZotope Neutron 5 (AI-assisted), TDR Nova (free dynamic EQ), DMG Audio Equilibrium (mastering depth), and Waves SSL G-Equalizer (analog character) are the main alternatives. For surgical mixing EQ, Pro-Q 4 remains the benchmark.
