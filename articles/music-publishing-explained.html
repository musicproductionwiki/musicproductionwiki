Music Publishing Explained: What Every Producer Needs to Know

Publishing is the half of the music business that most independent artists do not understand — which is exactly why it is the half where the most money goes uncollected. Understanding it completely is worth significant money over the course of a career.

MP The Music Production Wiki Team Updated April 2026 13 min read Beginner

Music publishing is one of the most valuable and most misunderstood areas of the music industry. Most artists understand intuitively that their recordings generate royalties when streamed or sold. Far fewer understand that their songs — the compositions, independent of any recording — generate a separate and equally valuable set of royalties through a completely different collection infrastructure. Publishing is the business of owning, administering, and exploiting the rights in musical compositions. For independent artists who write their own music, understanding publishing means understanding where half of their royalty income comes from — and taking the steps required to collect it.

What Music Publishing Actually Is

When most people think about music rights, they think about the recording — the master. But underlying every recording is a composition — the melody, chord progression, and lyrics that make the song what it is. The composition has its own separate copyright, its own separate royalty streams, and its own separate infrastructure for collection and administration. Publishing is the business of the composition copyright.

In the traditional music industry structure, a music publisher holds the copyright in compositions — or holds an exclusive license to administer compositions — and is responsible for collecting the royalties those compositions generate, exploiting the compositions commercially (pitching them for covers, sync licensing, and other uses), and accounting to the songwriter for their share of the income generated. In exchange for these services, publishers historically took 50% of publishing income — leaving the songwriter with the remaining 50%, called the writer's share.

For independent artists in 2026, the picture is more nuanced. The rise of publishing administrators — companies that collect publishing royalties on behalf of songwriters without taking an ownership stake in the copyright — means that independent artists can retain full ownership of their publishing while still accessing professional collection infrastructure. Understanding the options available — from traditional publishing deals to self-publishing with an administrator — is essential for making informed decisions about how to handle your publishing rights.

What a Traditional Music Publisher Does

A traditional music publisher provides several services to songwriters in exchange for an ownership share in the songwriters' compositions:

Copyright registration and administration. The publisher registers compositions with PROs, collection societies, and copyright offices, ensuring that royalties generated by the composition have a verified owner to be paid to. This administrative infrastructure — connecting the composition to the correct rights holder across every market where the music is used — is the technical foundation of publishing income collection.

Royalty collection. Publishers collect performance royalties from PROs worldwide, mechanical royalties from streaming platforms and download services, and sync licensing fees for the use of compositions in visual media. For internationally active songwriters, the complexity of collecting royalties from dozens of different collection societies in different countries makes publisher relationships genuinely valuable — the collection infrastructure they provide would be difficult and expensive for individual songwriters to replicate independently.

Sync licensing pitching. Publishers actively pitch their songwriters' compositions to music supervisors, sync licensing agencies, and brands seeking music for commercial use. A major publisher with established relationships in the sync licensing market can generate placement opportunities that would be difficult for an independent songwriter to access through direct outreach. For songwriters whose compositions are genuinely competitive in the sync market, publisher relationships can generate significant sync income that would otherwise be inaccessible.

Creative development. Larger publishers arrange co-writing sessions between their signed songwriters, facilitate connections with producers, and in some cases provide advances against future royalties that allow songwriters to focus on writing without immediate financial pressure. These creative development services are more characteristic of major publishing deals than boutique or administration agreements.

Types of Publishing Deals

Traditional publishing deal (co-publishing or full publishing): The publisher acquires an ownership share in the songwriter's compositions — anywhere from 50% (co-publishing) to 100% (full publishing) — in exchange for royalty collection, sync pitching, and sometimes an advance. The songwriter retains the writer's share (typically 50% of total publishing income) in most arrangements. Traditional publishing deals typically have a term of several years during which the publisher controls the exploited compositions, sometimes permanently (on catalog deals) or for a specified period.

Administration deal: The publisher administers the songwriter's compositions — collecting royalties and registering copyrights — without acquiring any ownership interest. The songwriter retains 100% of their copyright and receives the majority of royalties collected (typically 80-90%, with 10-20% going to the administrator as a fee). Administration deals are typically shorter term than traditional deals and are increasingly the preferred structure for established independent songwriters who do not need a publisher's creative or financial support but do need collection infrastructure.

Publishing administrator (self-publishing): Services like Songtrust, DistroKid's Songwriter portal, and CD Baby Pro provide publishing administration without the exclusive relationship of a traditional deal. They register compositions with PROs and collection societies worldwide and collect royalties on behalf of songwriters for a flat annual fee or a percentage of collected royalties. For independent artists releasing music at modest scale, publishing administrators provide the collection infrastructure of a traditional publisher at a fraction of the cost and without the ownership implications of a publishing deal.

The Writer's Share vs The Publisher's Share

Publishing royalties are divided into two portions — the writer's share and the publisher's share — that reflect the historical structure of the publishing industry. Understanding this division is essential for understanding how royalties are calculated and collected.

In the United States, performance royalties from PROs are typically split 50% to the songwriter (writer's share) and 50% to the publisher (publisher's share). Mechanical royalties from streaming are similarly divided. Sync licensing fees are negotiated as a total and divided according to the terms of the agreement between songwriter and publisher.

For independent artists who own their own publishing — who have set up their own publishing entity or who are self-published — both the writer's share and the publisher's share belong to them. An independent songwriter registered with ASCAP who has established their own publishing entity with ASCAP receives 100% of the royalties generated by their compositions — both shares. This is one of the most significant financial advantages of retaining publishing rights rather than signing them to a third-party publisher.

Setting up a publishing entity is straightforward: join ASCAP or BMI as a publisher (a separate registration from your songwriter registration, typically at a nominal cost) and register compositions under both your songwriter affiliation and your publishing entity. From that point, all royalties generated by your compositions flow to both your songwriter registration (writer's share) and your publishing registration (publisher's share) — both of which belong to you.

Should You Sign a Publishing Deal?

The question of whether to sign a publishing deal depends on where you are in your career, what the deal offers, and what you are giving up in exchange.

The argument for signing a publishing deal: if a reputable publisher offers a meaningful advance, has established sync relationships that can generate placement income you could not access independently, and has a track record of actively working their catalog rather than just collecting passive royalties, a publishing deal can accelerate income and career development at a rate that self-publishing cannot match. For songwriters whose compositions are genuinely competitive in the commercial market and who need financial support to continue writing, a good publishing deal with a reputable partner can be transformative.

The argument against signing a publishing deal prematurely: signing away copyright ownership in compositions — even for a term — is one of the most significant and least reversible decisions a songwriter can make. A composition that becomes commercially valuable after being signed to a publisher generates royalties for the publisher for the duration of the deal, potentially for decades. The loss of control over sync licensing decisions — a publisher can license compositions for uses you would not choose — can result in your music appearing in contexts that do not serve your artistic or commercial interests. And the advance, however appealing at signing, may not represent fair value for the compositions being assigned if those compositions subsequently generate significant income.

The honest recommendation for most independent artists at the beginning and middle of their careers: retain your publishing, set up your own publishing entity, use a publishing administrator to collect royalties internationally, and build your catalog's value independently. When your catalog reaches a level of commercial relevance that attracts genuine interest from publishers with specific, valuable relationships to offer, evaluate deals on their specific terms rather than signing out of urgency or flattery.

The Publishing Action You Should Take This Week

If you have not yet registered as a publisher with your PRO — ASCAP or BMI in the United States — do it this week. The registration costs approximately $150 for ASCAP and is free for BMI. Once registered as a publisher, register every composition you have released or plan to release under both your songwriter affiliation and your publishing entity. From that point forward, every royalty your compositions generate flows to both registrations — both of which belong to you. The difference between collecting 50% of your publishing royalties and 100% of them is, over the course of a career, one of the most significant financial decisions an independent artist makes. Make it intentionally.
