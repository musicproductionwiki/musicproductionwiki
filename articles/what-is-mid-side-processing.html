What Is Mid Side Processing

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 · 8 min read · Verified May 7, 2026
Quick Answer — Updated May 2026

Mid-side (M/S) processing is a technique that splits a stereo audio signal into two components — the Mid (everything shared between left and right channels) and the Side (everything that differs between them) — so you can process each independently. It gives engineers surgical control over the center versus the width of a mix, making it a staple tool in both mixing and mastering workflows.

Mid-side processing is one of the most powerful and underused tools available to mixing and mastering engineers. Instead of working with conventional left and right channels, M/S processing lets you isolate what sits in the center of your stereo field from what creates the width around it — and treat each zone completely independently. Once you understand how it works, you will find applications for it at almost every stage of a production. Updated May 2026.

How Mid-Side Encoding Works

The math behind M/S is elegantly simple. A standard stereo signal has a Left channel and a Right channel. To create the Mid and Side signals, your processor applies two formulas:

  • Mid (M) = (Left + Right) / 2 — the mono sum of both channels, capturing everything panned center.
  • Side (S) = (Left − Right) / 2 — the difference signal, capturing everything that separates left from right.

After processing, an inverse matrix converts M and S back to Left and Right before the signal hits your speakers or the next device in the chain. This encode-process-decode loop is called an M/S matrix. Most modern DAWs and many quality plugins handle this encoding and decoding automatically, so you never have to do the arithmetic yourself.

Figure 1 — M/S signal flow: L+R encoded to Mid and Side, processed independently, then decoded back to L/R.

Mid-Side EQ: The Most Common Application

M/S EQ is where most producers first encounter this technique. Rather than applying the same EQ curve to both channels, you can, for example, apply a high-pass filter only to the Side channel to clean up low-frequency mud in the stereo field — while leaving the Mid channel's bass completely intact. Or you can boost the high-frequency air on the Sides to open up cymbals and room without making a centered vocal brighter. Plugins like the FabFilter Pro-Q 3 and its successor make switching into M/S mode per band as simple as right-clicking a node, which is a huge reason M/S EQ has become standard practice in mastering. You will find a full breakdown of EQ strategies in our mixing EQ guide.

Mid-Side Compression

Applying compression in M/S mode lets you control the dynamic behavior of the center and sides independently. Compressing the Mid channel more aggressively can glue a busy, loud center mix — kick, bass, lead vocal — without squashing the spatial information in the reverbs and pads living in the Side channel. Conversely, gentle compression on the Side can tame overly wide elements that pump or breathe when the track gets loud. This approach pairs naturally with broader bus compression strategies used on a mix bus or stereo master.

M/S in Mastering

Mid-side processing is arguably most impactful at the mastering stage, where you are working with a full stereo mixdown and have limited room to make changes without affecting everything simultaneously. M/S gives you a way to:

  • Cut boxy or muddy frequencies from the Mid without narrowing the mix.
  • Roll off rumble and low-end buildup from the Side, tightening the low end for mono compatibility.
  • Add high-frequency shimmer to the Sides to increase perceived width without touching the vocal.
  • Apply subtle saturation to the Mid to add harmonic density to the center.

Tools like iZotope Ozone include dedicated M/S modes across their EQ, dynamics, and imager modules, making them a go-to for this workflow. Our guide on how to master a song at home covers where to place M/S processing in a mastering chain.

Key Rule: Always check your mix in mono after using M/S processing — especially if you have boosted the Side channel significantly. Heavy Side boosts can cause phase issues and thinning when the signal is summed to mono on phones, earbuds, or Bluetooth speakers.

Common M/S Processing Settings at a Glance

High-shelf boost Side Add air and openness Harshness on cymbals
Mid-cut (200–500 Hz) Mid Reduce boxiness in center Thinning vocals or snare

When to Use M/S vs. Standard L/R Processing

M/S processing is not always the right tool. For individual instrument tracks, standard left/right EQ and compression is usually more transparent and easier to control. M/S comes into its own on stereo bus processing — mix bus, parallel buses, stem masters, and full masters — where it gives you precision that L/R processing simply cannot offer. If you are making broad tonal decisions that need to affect the whole mix equally, standard L/R processing is simpler. If you need to treat the center differently from the edges without changing the overall stereo balance, reach for M/S. Understanding this distinction is part of building a smart plugin chain at the bus and master stage. For producers working in headphones, M/S EQ on a mix bus can also help compensate for the unnatural stereo imaging that headphone listening creates — see our guide on how to mix in headphones for more context.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ What does mid-side processing do?
It splits a stereo signal into a Mid channel (the mono sum of L+R) and a Side channel (the difference between L and R), letting you process the center and edges of your stereo image independently.
FAQ Is M/S processing only for mastering?
No — while it is most commonly associated with mastering, M/S processing is also useful on mix bus inserts, stereo instrument buses, and even individual stereo tracks like drum overheads or room mics.
FAQ What is the Mid channel in M/S processing?
The Mid channel contains everything that is identical between the left and right channels — typically kick, bass, lead vocal, snare, and any other elements panned to the center.
FAQ What is the Side channel in M/S processing?
The Side channel contains everything that differs between left and right — reverb tails, wide synth pads, stereo chorus effects, and room ambience. Boosting this channel increases perceived width.
FAQ Can M/S processing cause phase problems?
Yes. Aggressively boosting the Side channel or applying heavy processing to it can introduce phase cancellation, which becomes audible when the mix is played back in mono. Always check mono compatibility after M/S processing.
FAQ Which plugins support mid-side processing?
FabFilter Pro-Q 3 and Pro-Q 4, iZotope Ozone, Waves S1, DMG Audio EQuilibrium, and many other professional EQ and dynamics plugins offer built-in M/S modes.
FAQ What is the difference between mid-side EQ and multiband compression?
Mid-side EQ adjusts frequency content separately in the center and sides, while multiband compression controls dynamics across frequency ranges. They solve different problems, though both can be used together at the mastering stage.
FAQ How do I check if my M/S processing is mono-compatible?
Sum your stereo output to mono using a mono button in your DAW, a plugin, or your monitor controller. Listen for thinning, phase cancellation, or a loss of low end — any of these signal that your Side processing has gone too far.
