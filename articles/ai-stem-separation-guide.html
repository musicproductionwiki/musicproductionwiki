AI Stem Separation: Complete Guide to Moises, Lalal.ai, and Spleeter

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2025 12 min read
Quick Answer

For professional-quality stem separation, iZotope RX 11 leads on audio quality but requires a paid license. For free or low-cost web-based separation, Lalal.ai produces the cleanest results at competitive pricing. Moises is the strongest choice for musicians who want an app with additional features like key detection, tempo tracking, and pitch shifting. Spleeter is the best option for developers and technically capable users who need a free, open-source solution they can run locally and customize.

AI stem separation — the ability to extract individual elements (vocals, drums, bass, guitars, other instruments) from a fully mixed audio file — was considered technically impossible as recently as 2018. The tools available now would have seemed like science fiction a decade ago. A two-minute pop track that took hours of studio time to record and mix can be separated into its component stems in under thirty seconds.

This capability matters enormously across music production: remixing and sampling, acapella creation, karaoke, music education, post-production audio repair, and creative reinterpretation of existing recordings. Understanding which tool to use for which task requires understanding how they differ in quality, price, speed, and capability.

What we'll cover: How AI stem separation works technically, detailed assessment of Moises, Lalal.ai, Spleeter, and iZotope RX, quality comparison by stem type, use case matching, limitations of current technology, and pricing breakdown.

How AI Stem Separation Works

Modern AI stem separation uses deep learning models — specifically, neural networks trained on large datasets of isolated stems paired with their mixed equivalents. The model learns the spectral and temporal patterns that correspond to each instrument type: the frequency distribution of vocals, the transient character of drums, the harmonic content of bass lines. When presented with a new mixed audio file, the model makes predictions about which portions of the frequency spectrum belong to which source.

The fundamental technical challenge is that audio sources are not simply layered on top of each other — they interact. A bass guitar and a kick drum share low frequency energy. A vocal reverb tail sits in the same frequency range as acoustic guitar harmonics. When two sources share the same frequency at the same time, any separation decision involves a compromise. No current AI system separates perfectly without some artifact introduction.

The quality of separation depends on three factors: the training data (more diverse, higher quality data produces better models), the model architecture (deeper networks with better attention mechanisms separate more cleanly), and the separation task itself (more instrument types means more potential for confusion and crosstalk).

Moises — Best for Musicians

Moises positions itself as a musician's tool rather than a pure stem separator. Its core separation engine handles the standard four stems (vocals, drums, bass, other) plus additional categories in its more advanced models. What distinguishes Moises is the surrounding functionality: after separation, you can slow tracks down without changing pitch, change the key of any stem independently, loop sections for practice, and mute or solo any combination of stems for backing track creation.

The Moises app — available on iOS, Android, and web — makes these features accessible without any audio engineering knowledge. A guitarist who wants to learn a song can upload it, separate the guitar stem, reduce the tempo to 70%, and loop the bridge section for practice in under two minutes. This is genuinely useful for music education and instrument practice.

Separation quality is competitive with Lalal.ai on most material. Vocal separation is clean on modern pop and electronic music. It degrades more noticeably on complex orchestral material or heavily layered productions where many instruments share frequency content. Drum separation is good on acoustic drumkits; less reliable on programmed drums with heavy processing.

Pricing: Free tier available with limitations. Moises Pro starts at $3.99/month billed annually for unlimited uploads and higher quality models.

Lalal.ai — Best Audio Quality

Lalal.ai consistently produces some of the cleanest separation results available in web-based tools. Its Phoenix separation engine (the current generation at time of writing) handles vocal, drum, bass, guitar, piano, and synthesizer separation with fewer artifacts than most competitors on typical pop and electronic music. The processing approach produces notably less metallic artifacting on vocal stems than older generation tools.

The interface is straightforward: upload audio, select what you want to separate, wait for processing, download results. No app, no account required for limited free use. Paid plans range from $15 for 90 minutes of audio processing to $500 for unlimited annual processing.

Where Lalal.ai excels specifically is vocal isolation from dense mixes. The vocal stem quality is the primary reason producers and remixers choose it over alternatives — cleaner acapellas with less bleed-through from backing instruments. For sampling workflows where a clean vocal is the deliverable, Lalal.ai is the strongest web-based option.

Spleeter — Best Free/Open Source

Spleeter, developed by Deezer and released as open source in 2019, was the tool that made AI stem separation mainstream. Running locally on your machine (not cloud-based), Spleeter processes audio through pre-trained models that handle 2-stem (vocals/accompaniment), 4-stem (vocals/drums/bass/other), and 5-stem (vocals/drums/bass/piano/other) separation.

Quality compared to current commercial tools: Spleeter's separation quality is meaningful but dated. Modern cloud services have surpassed it on most material. The metallic artifacting that Spleeter introduced on vocals — a characteristic warbling quality on the vocal stem — is more pronounced than in current Lalal.ai or Moises output.

Where Spleeter remains valuable is for developers, technically capable users, and anyone processing large volumes of audio who needs a free, locally-running, customizable solution. Spleeter can be integrated into custom workflows, automated with scripts, and run without usage limits. For batch processing hundreds of audio files, Spleeter's local processing is dramatically more economical than cloud-based alternatives.

Installation requires Python and basic command-line familiarity. Not appropriate for non-technical users.

iZotope RX — Professional Standard

iZotope RX's Music Rebalance module is the professional standard for stem separation in post-production, mastering, and broadcast contexts. Running locally as part of the RX suite, Music Rebalance separates vocals, drums, bass, and other instruments with the highest consistent quality of any available tool, particularly on challenging material like live recordings, classical music, and densely layered productions.

RX also handles tasks the other tools don't approach: dialogue isolation from film soundtracks, music bed reduction in broadcast recordings, voice-over cleanup from mixed content. The breadth of its audio repair capabilities beyond stem separation makes it a different category of tool from cloud-based separators.

The price reflects the professional positioning: RX Standard starts at $399, RX Advanced at $999. This puts it out of range for most hobbyist producers but well within reach for professional studios where audio repair is billable work. iZotope frequently runs 40-50% sales, making entry-level RX more accessible.

Honest Limitations

No AI stem separation tool produces perfect results. The artifacts introduced during separation — frequency smearing, metallic vocal quality, bleed-through from adjacent instruments — range from barely noticeable on clean modern productions to very significant on complex material. Setting realistic expectations prevents disappointment.

AI stem separation works best on: modern pop, electronic music, hip-hop, and other genres where the elements are clearly separated in frequency and time. It works least well on: orchestral music, jazz with complex harmonic overlap, live concert recordings with heavy room acoustics, and heavily processed productions where many elements occupy the same frequency space.

For professional remixing, official stems from the original session remain vastly superior to AI-separated stems. AI separation is a practical tool for situations where official stems don't exist or can't be obtained — not a replacement for proper session files.
