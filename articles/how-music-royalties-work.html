How Music Royalties Work

First published May 2026 · Updated May 2026
MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 Beginner → Advanced
Legal disclaimer: This article provides general educational information about music royalties. It is not legal advice. Consult a music attorney for advice specific to your situation.

Most producers don't understand how they get paid — and the music industry benefits from that confusion. The royalty system is genuinely complex, with multiple organizations collecting different types of royalties for different uses through different distribution channels, often with delays of six months to two years between when your music generates money and when it arrives in your account. But the underlying structure is logical once you understand the two fundamental rights that underpin everything: the composition right (who owns the song) and the master right (who owns the recording).

Quick Answer

Music royalties are payments made to rights holders when music is used or reproduced. There are four main types: mechanical royalties (paid when music is reproduced digitally or physically), performance royalties (paid when music is publicly performed or streamed), sync royalties (paid when music is used in film, TV, or ads), and master royalties (paid to whoever owns the recording, typically through streaming). Each type is collected by different organizations and paid through different channels — understanding which type applies to your situation determines who you need to register with to collect it.

What you'll learn: The difference between composition rights and master rights, all four royalty types explained with real numbers, which organizations collect which royalties (PROs, the MLC, SoundExchange, distributors), how streaming royalties are calculated, what a music publisher actually does, and the step-by-step checklist for making sure you collect every dollar you're owed.

The Two Copyrights That Drive Everything Beginner

Every piece of music that generates royalties involves two separate copyrights, and understanding the distinction between them is the foundation of understanding how you get paid.

The composition copyright covers the song — the melody, lyrics, chord progression, and arrangement. When Taylor Swift writes a song, the composition is the work she created. The composition copyright belongs to the songwriter(s) who created it, or to a publisher who administers it on their behalf. Every time the composition is used — streamed, downloaded, played on radio, performed live, licensed for a film — royalties are generated for the composition copyright holder.

The master copyright (also called the sound recording copyright) covers the specific recording of the song — the audio file that was produced, mixed, and mastered. When Taylor Swift records a song for her album, that specific recording is the master. The master is typically owned by whoever financed the recording — usually a record label for signed artists, or the artist themselves for independent releases. Whenever that specific recording is used, royalties are generated for the master copyright holder.

Why does this distinction matter? Because the same music generates two separate streams of royalties that go to potentially different people. When a song streams on Spotify: the composition generates mechanical royalties (paid to the songwriter/publisher) and performance royalties (also paid to the songwriter/publisher via PROs). The master generates master recording royalties (paid to whoever owns the master — the label or the independent artist). If you wrote a song that another artist recorded and released on a major label, you would receive composition royalties but not master royalties. The label would receive master royalties. The artist might receive a percentage of master royalties per their deal with the label.

Mechanical Royalties Beginner

Mechanical royalties are paid to songwriters and publishers every time a composition is "mechanically reproduced" — originally meaning physical manufacturing (CD pressing, vinyl cutting), now extended to include digital downloads and, most importantly, interactive streaming.

In the United States, mechanical royalties for streaming are collected by the Mechanical Licensing Collective (the MLC), created by the Music Modernization Act of 2018. Every interactive streaming platform in the US — Spotify, Apple Music, Amazon Music, Tidal — pays mechanical royalties directly to the MLC, which then distributes them to registered songwriters and publishers. The MLC is free to join and the sign-up process takes approximately 30 minutes. If you write songs that are streamed in the US and you're not registered with the MLC, your mechanical royalties are being collected but not distributed to you. The MLC is currently holding hundreds of millions of dollars in unclaimed mechanical royalties that belong to unregistered songwriters.

For physical formats and downloads, mechanical royalties have historically been collected by the Harry Fox Agency (HFA) in the US, or by publishing administrators like Songtrust, CD Baby Publishing, or TuneCore Publishing internationally.

The US mechanical royalty rate for downloads is currently 9.1 cents per download (or 1.75 cents per minute for songs over 5 minutes). Streaming mechanical rates are a small fraction of a cent per stream and vary depending on the platform's revenue and licensing agreements — typically between $0.001 and $0.003 per stream at the composition level.

Performance Royalties Beginner

Performance royalties are paid to songwriters and publishers when music is "publicly performed" — which is a much broader definition than live performance. Radio airplay, streaming platforms, TV broadcast, bars and restaurants, live venues, shopping malls — all of these are public performances that generate royalties.

These royalties are collected by Performing Rights Organizations (PROs). In the United States, the three main PROs are ASCAP, BMI, and SESAC. ASCAP and BMI are non-profit and open to any songwriter. SESAC is for-profit and invitation-only. In the UK, PRS collects performance royalties. In Canada, SOCAN. Every country has its own PRO, and most have reciprocal agreements so your PRO collects on your behalf internationally.

Performance royalties are split 50/50 between the songwriter share (paid directly to the songwriter) and the publisher share (paid to the publisher, which could be you if you've registered your own publishing entity). This split is non-negotiable — it's how every PRO operates. If you're registered as a songwriter only and haven't also registered as a publisher, you leave the publisher 50% on the table. ASCAP and BMI allow you to register a "vanity publishing company" — essentially just a business name — so you can collect both halves. This is a simple step that doubles your performance royalty income at no cost.

ASCAP charges a one-time $50 membership fee. BMI is free for songwriters. Both distribute royalties quarterly. The amounts are calculated based on complex weighting formulas involving the number of plays, the size of the audience, the type of performance (radio vs streaming vs live), and the time of day.

Sync Royalties Intermediate

Sync royalties (synchronization royalties) are paid when music is synchronized with moving images — placed in a film, TV show, commercial, video game, YouTube video, or any other visual media. Unlike mechanical and performance royalties, sync fees are negotiated directly between the rights holder (or their representative) and the production company, advertiser, or media company that wants to use the music. There's no set rate or government-mandated structure — it's a free market negotiation.

Sync licensing involves two separate licenses: a sync license (for the composition) and a master use license (for the recording). Both licenses are typically required. If you write and produce a song that you own entirely — both the composition and the master — you control both licenses and can negotiate both fees yourself. If you wrote a song for another artist whose label owns the master, two separate negotiations are required.

Sync fees vary enormously based on the prestige of the placement, the size of the audience, whether the placement is in a trailer versus the film itself, and the term and territory of the license. A song in a national television commercial might earn $10,000–$100,000 for a single placement. A song in an independent film might earn $500–$2,000. Sync placement in a major streaming series (Netflix, HBO) might earn $5,000–$30,000 per placement. The music that gets licensed typically needs to be cleared — meaning all ownership questions resolved and all rights holders accessible and willing to license.

Master Recording Royalties Intermediate

Master recording royalties (also called neighboring rights or sound recording royalties in some contexts) are paid to whoever owns the master recording whenever that recording is used commercially. For independent artists who distribute their music through DistroKid, TuneCore, CD Baby, or similar services, master royalties from streaming are paid to the distributor and then passed through to the artist. The streaming platform pays the distributor, who takes a distribution fee and pays the remainder to the artist.

Digital performance royalties on masters from digital radio services (Pandora, SiriusXM) and non-interactive streaming are collected separately by SoundExchange, a non-profit digital rights organization. SoundExchange collects these royalties directly from platforms and distributes 45% to the featured artist, 5% to non-featured artists (session musicians, backup vocalists), and 50% to the master rights holder. Importantly, recording artists receive SoundExchange payments directly, not through labels — which is one reason artists have an incentive to register with SoundExchange regardless of their record deal. SoundExchange registration is free.

What Most Producers Miss

The most common reason producers leave royalty money uncollected isn't ignorance of the existence of royalties — it's the friction of setting up multiple registrations across multiple organizations. Here's the minimum required to collect all your royalties:

  • Register as a songwriter AND publisher with one PRO (ASCAP or BMI) to collect performance royalties — both halves
  • Register with the MLC to collect US mechanical royalties from streaming
  • Register with SoundExchange to collect digital performance royalties on your master recordings
  • Use a music distributor that passes through streaming master royalties (DistroKid, TuneCore, CD Baby)
  • Consider a publishing administrator (Songtrust, CD Baby Publishing) for international mechanical and performance collection if you're getting international streams
Critical Action Item

The MLC is holding unclaimed royalties that belong to unregistered songwriters. If your music has been streamed in the US and you're not registered with the MLC, you're leaving money on the table that has already been collected on your behalf. Registration is free at themlc.com. There's no good reason to delay this.

How Streaming Royalties Are Actually Calculated Advanced

The "per stream" rate often cited in music industry discussions — roughly $0.003–$0.005 per stream on Spotify — is a simplification that obscures how streaming royalties actually work. Understanding the real calculation explains why the number varies so much between artists and between pay periods.

Spotify and other streaming platforms use a "streamshare" or "pro-rata" model. Each month, Spotify calculates its total revenue (subscriptions plus ad revenue). It allocates a percentage of that revenue to the royalty pool (currently approximately 70%). It then divides that pool among all rights holders in proportion to their share of total streams for that month. If a record gets 0.001% of all streams on Spotify in a given month, it receives 0.001% of the royalty pool for that month.

This means the per-stream rate is not fixed — it fluctuates based on total platform revenue and total stream count. As Spotify's subscriber base grows, the royalty pool grows. As total stream count grows, each individual stream's share of the pool shrinks. The practical result is that per-stream rates have slowly declined as streaming has grown.

From the total payout to a song, approximately 50–70% goes to the master rights holder (label or independent artist), and 30–50% goes to the composition rights holders (songwriter and publisher through mechanical and performance royalty channels). These percentages vary by territory and by the specific agreements platforms have negotiated with labels and PROs.

The Royalty Collection Checklist

  1. Register as a songwriter and publisher with ASCAP (ascap.com) or BMI (bmi.com). Choose one — you can only belong to one PRO at a time.
  2. Register every song you write with your PRO immediately upon creation — not just upon release. Include ISRC codes for recordings and ISWC codes for compositions where available.
  3. Register with the Mechanical Licensing Collective (themlc.com). Free. Collects US streaming mechanical royalties.
  4. Register with SoundExchange (soundexchange.com). Free. Collects digital radio performance royalties on your master recordings.
  5. Use a distribution service that passes through 100% of master streaming royalties (DistroKid, TuneCore, CD Baby). Keep records of every release's ISRC codes — these are how platforms track and report your streams.
  6. For international royalty collection, consider a publishing administrator like Songtrust ($100 one-time fee plus 15% commission) that has relationships with collection societies in 215 countries.
  7. If you co-write songs, document ownership splits in writing before release. Verbal agreements become disputes when money arrives. A simple co-writing agreement signed by all parties prevents problems later.
