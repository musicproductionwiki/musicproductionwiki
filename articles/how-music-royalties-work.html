How Music Royalties Work: Every Revenue Stream Explained

The music royalty system is genuinely complex — built over a century of legislation, court decisions, and industry agreements that few people outside the business fully understand. Here is the complete picture, explained clearly.

MP The Music Production Wiki Team Updated April 2026 14 min read Beginner

Every time a song is streamed, played on the radio, performed live, used in a film, or downloaded, royalties are generated. Those royalties flow through a system of rights, licenses, and collecting organizations that most musicians never fully understand — which means most musicians never fully collect what they are owed. The music industry generates billions of dollars in royalties annually. A meaningful portion of that money sits uncollected because the artists who earned it did not know it existed or did not take the steps required to claim it. This is the guide that changes that.

The Foundation: Two Types of Rights, Two Types of Royalties

Every piece of recorded music involves two separate copyrights and two separate streams of royalties. Understanding this fundamental distinction is the key to understanding the entire royalty system.

The master recording copyright belongs to whoever owns the recording — the specific audio file of the finished song. For independent artists who record themselves, this is typically the artist. For artists signed to record labels, this is typically the label. Master royalties are generated when the recording is used in ways that require a master license: streaming, downloads, sync licensing (use in film, TV, advertising, games), and in some countries, public performance of the recording.

The musical composition copyright belongs to whoever owns the underlying song — the melody and lyrics, independent of any specific recording of them. The same composition can be recorded by many different artists, and each recording generates separate master royalties while all share the same publishing royalties. Publishing royalties are generated when the composition is used: streamed or downloaded (mechanical royalties), performed publicly on radio or in venues (performance royalties), or used in sync (sync licensing fees for the composition).

For independent artists who write and record their own music, both copyrights belong to them — which means they are entitled to both master royalties and publishing royalties from every use of their music. The tragedy is that many independent artists collect only master royalties — through their distributor — while publishing royalties sit uncollected because they have not taken the separate steps required to claim them.

Master Royalties: What Your Distributor Collects

Master royalties from streaming are the most familiar royalty stream for most independent artists — the money that arrives from Spotify, Apple Music, Tidal, and other platforms via your distributor. Every stream of your recording generates a master royalty, calculated by each platform according to its own formula based on the proportion of total streams your recording represents in a given period.

The per-stream rates vary by platform and fluctuate over time as platforms renegotiate their licensing agreements with rights holders. As a general reference: Spotify pays approximately $0.003 to $0.005 per stream. Apple Music pays approximately $0.007 to $0.01 per stream. Tidal pays approximately $0.009 to $0.013 per stream. YouTube Music pays significantly less — approximately $0.001 to $0.002 per stream — reflecting YouTube's position as an advertising-supported service rather than a subscription service.

These rates apply to the total master royalty — which is then split between all rights holders in the recording. For an independent artist who owns their master recording entirely, 100% of the master royalty flows to them through their distributor. For artists who have assigned a percentage of their master to a label, manager, or other party, the royalty is divided according to the terms of those agreements before reaching the artist.

Your distributor collects master royalties from every platform you are distributed to and pays them into your account on a schedule that varies by distributor — typically monthly or quarterly, with a payment threshold that must be reached before a payout is triggered. Monitor your streaming dashboard regularly to verify that royalties are being collected and that the amounts align with your streaming counts.

Mechanical Royalties: The Stream Nobody Explains

Mechanical royalties are publishing royalties generated when a composition is reproduced — copied — in a fixed form. The term "mechanical" originates from the era of player piano rolls and early recording technology, when reproducing a composition mechanically required a license. In the streaming era, mechanical royalties are generated every time a song is streamed or downloaded — each stream technically creates a temporary reproduction of the composition in the listener's device, triggering a mechanical royalty obligation.

In the United States, streaming platforms pay mechanical royalties to a single licensing body — the Mechanical Licensing Collective (MLC) — which distributes those royalties to rights holders. The MLC was established by the Music Modernization Act of 2018 specifically to address the problem of uncollected mechanical royalties in the streaming era — a problem that had resulted in billions of dollars in unmatched royalties sitting in limbo because the platforms could not identify which compositions corresponded to which recordings.

To collect mechanical royalties from the MLC, you must register your compositions with the MLC directly at themlc.com. Registration is free. Once registered, the MLC will match your composition registrations against streaming usage data and distribute royalties accordingly. Many independent artists have significant mechanical royalties waiting at the MLC that they have never claimed because they were not aware the MLC existed or did not know that registration was required. Check themlc.com now if you have released music in the United States and have not registered your compositions.

Performance Royalties: Radio, TV, and Public Venues

Performance royalties are generated when a composition is publicly performed — played on the radio, broadcast on television, streamed on internet radio services like Pandora or iHeartRadio, or performed live in a venue that holds a performance license. Performance royalties are collected by Performing Rights Organizations — ASCAP, BMI, and SESAC in the United States, PRS in the UK, SOCAN in Canada, APRA AMCOS in Australia and New Zealand, and equivalent organizations in most other countries.

To collect performance royalties, you must register with a PRO. In the United States, the major PROs are ASCAP and BMI — both collect performance royalties on behalf of songwriters and publishers, both are free to join for songwriters, and both require exclusive membership (you can only be a member of one). SESAC is invitation-only and focuses on established songwriters with significant royalty streams. GMR (Global Music Rights) is another PRO that represents some high-profile songwriters.

Once you join a PRO, register every composition you release. The PRO monitors radio broadcasts, TV broadcasts, live venue performances (through blanket licenses held by venues), and streaming services that are subject to performance royalty obligations, then distributes royalties to rights holders based on reported usage. The tracking is not perfect — particularly for smaller market radio play and live venue performances — but registered compositions are far more likely to receive royalties than unregistered ones.

Performance royalties from streaming are a separate stream from mechanical royalties — they are collected by PROs rather than the MLC, and they apply to streaming services like Pandora that operate under public performance licenses rather than interactive streaming licenses. The distinction is technical and the amounts may be small for independent artists, but they are real money that flows to registered rights holders and is lost forever for unregistered compositions.

Sync Licensing: The Highest-Value Single Transaction

Sync licensing is the use of music in synchronization with visual media — film, television, advertising, video games, social media, and any other audiovisual content. A sync license grants permission to use a specific composition and/or recording in a specific piece of visual media for a specific territory and time period. Sync fees are negotiated directly between the rights holder and the content creator or their music supervisor, and they represent some of the highest per-use values in the music industry.

A sync placement in a national television commercial can pay $10,000 to $100,000 or more for both the master and publishing rights. A placement in a major film can pay similar amounts. Even smaller placements — in YouTube videos, independent films, or corporate content — regularly pay hundreds to thousands of dollars. For independent artists with unique sounds, strong catalog, and the time to pursue placements actively, sync licensing can represent a significant revenue stream that dwarfs streaming income.

Sync licensing requires both a master license (from whoever owns the recording) and a sync license (from whoever owns the composition). For independent artists who own both rights, this means you can negotiate and grant both licenses yourself — or work with a sync licensing agent who identifies placement opportunities, negotiates deals on your behalf, and takes a percentage (typically 25-50%) of the sync fee as commission.

Sync libraries — companies that license music to content creators non-exclusively, often at lower fees but higher volume — offer independent artists a lower-effort path to sync income. Musicbed, Artlist, Epidemic Sound, and similar services license music from independent artists and pay royalties each time a content creator licenses a track. The per-placement fees are lower than custom sync deals, but the volume of placements through a major sync library can generate meaningful income passively.

Neighboring Rights: The International Royalty Stream Most US Artists Miss

Neighboring rights — also called related rights in some territories — are performance royalties paid to the owner of a master recording (rather than the composition) when that recording is played on the radio, broadcast on television, or performed in public. In the United States, neighboring rights do not apply to terrestrial radio broadcast — US law does not require radio stations to pay master royalties for over-the-air broadcasts, only publishing royalties. This is a significant exception that the US music industry has debated for decades.

However, in most other countries — the UK, Canada, Australia, Germany, Japan, and the European Union — neighboring rights apply to radio and television broadcast, and master owners receive royalties each time their recordings are played on broadcast media. For independent artists whose music receives international radio play, neighboring rights royalties can represent a meaningful income stream.

To collect neighboring rights royalties internationally, you can work with a neighboring rights collection agency — SoundExchange in the United States (for digital radio and streaming), PPL in the UK, Re:Sound in Canada, or a global neighboring rights agency like AURA or Kobalt Neighbouring Rights that collects on your behalf across multiple territories. Registration is required and varies by organization.

The Complete Royalty Collection Checklist

Here is every registration you should complete to ensure you are collecting every royalty stream available to you as an independent artist and songwriter.

Register with a US PRO (ASCAP or BMI) and register every composition you release. Register your compositions with the MLC at themlc.com. Sign up with SoundExchange at soundexchange.com to collect digital performance royalties from streaming radio services like Pandora and SiriusXM. Register with your country's neighboring rights organization if you are outside the US or if your music receives international radio play. Consider a publishing administrator like Songtrust or DistroKid's Songwriter portal to collect mechanical royalties from territories and services beyond the MLC's reach. Ensure your distributor is collecting master royalties from all platforms and that your metadata is complete and accurate on every release.

This checklist takes a few hours to complete the first time. After that, registering new releases with each organization becomes a fifteen-minute process per release. The royalties that result from this registration — particularly from mechanical royalties that would otherwise sit uncollected at the MLC and neighboring rights from international radio — often exceed what independent artists expect and can materially improve the economics of independent music careers.

The Money That Is Already Waiting for You

If you have released music in the United States without registering your compositions with the MLC, there is a meaningful probability that mechanical royalties are sitting in the MLC's unmatched royalties pool waiting for you to claim them. The MLC holds these funds for a period before redistributing them to registered rights holders. Go to themlc.com now, create an account, and register your compositions. The process takes less than an hour. The royalties you find may surprise you.
