Music Production on Ipad 2026

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 · 19 min read · Verified May 7, 2026
Quick Answer — Updated May 2026

iPad music production in 2026 delivers genuinely professional results. The best DAW apps are GarageBand (free, beginners), Cubasis 3 (professional), BeatMaker 3 (beat-making), AUM (modular/live), and Koala Sampler (sampling). USB-C iPads connect directly to audio interfaces, and projects transfer to desktop via audio stems or ALS export. The main limitations are AUv3 plugin selection (not VST) and screen real estate — not processing power.

Updated May 2026

BandLab reported 100 million users in 2025. A significant and growing portion of them are producing music entirely on mobile devices. The M4 iPad Pro delivers CPU performance that exceeds many desktop computers from just three years ago. Apps like Cubasis 3 and BeatMaker 3 have matured into genuinely professional tools with full AUv3 plugin hosting, advanced automation, and reliable desktop export workflows. And for a growing number of producers — particularly those who travel, work in non-traditional spaces, or simply prefer touch-based interaction — the iPad has become a primary production environment rather than a mere sketching tool.

This guide covers everything you need to set up and use an iPad as a serious music production platform in 2026: the best DAW apps and how they compare, audio interface and MIDI controller options, workflow strategies, the limitations you need to understand before committing, and exactly how to move projects between iPad and your desktop DAW. Whether you are a beginner picking up GarageBand for the first time or a working producer looking to go fully mobile, this is the reference you need.

Which iPad Should You Buy for Music Production?

The first question most producers ask is whether they need the most expensive iPad or whether a cheaper model will do the job. The honest answer depends on the complexity of your sessions and your budget. Here is the full breakdown for 2026:

iPad Model Chip Best For Price (approx.)
iPad Pro 13" (M4) Apple M4 Professional production, large projects, high plugin count From $1,299
iPad Pro 11" (M4) Apple M4 Professional production, portable From $999
iPad Air 13" (M2) Apple M2 Serious production, great value for performance From $799
iPad Air 11" (M2) Apple M2 Most producers' sweet spot — capable and portable From $599
iPad (A16) Apple A16 Beginners, GarageBand, smaller projects From $329
iPad mini (A17 Pro) Apple A17 Pro Ultra-portable, great for sampling and sketching From $499

For most music producers, the iPad Air 11" with M2 chip is the sweet spot — enough processing power for complex sessions with multiple AUv3 plugins running simultaneously, USB-C for direct audio interface connection without adapters, and a price that avoids the iPad Pro premium. The M2 chip handles 20–30 simultaneous AUv3 plugin instances without breaking a sweat in typical production scenarios.

The standard iPad with A16 chip (2026) handles GarageBand and basic Cubasis sessions well but shows strain with larger projects or heavy AUv3 plugin use — particularly when running multiple instances of CPU-intensive synths like Moog Minimoog Model D alongside a full drum machine and effects chain. If your work will involve complex layered productions, budget for at least the iPad Air.

The iPad Pro with M4 chip is the unambiguous performance leader — it processes audio with headroom that rivals Mac Studio configurations from two years ago. If you are running a full live performance rig on iPad, recording large sessions with many audio inputs, or doing heavy sound design with processor-intensive AUv3 instruments, the Pro is worth the premium. For producers who primarily sketch beats and transfer to desktop, it is overkill.

All USB-C iPads connect directly to audio interfaces without adapters. The only iPads still using Lightning (which require a USB-to-Lightning adapter for interface connection) are legacy models from before 2022. If you are buying new in 2026, every current iPad model uses USB-C.

Pro Tip: RAM Matters More Than Chip Generation
When choosing between iPad models, pay attention to RAM alongside the chip. The base iPad Pro 11" M4 ships with 8GB RAM, while the higher storage tiers come with 16GB. For large sessions with many AUv3 plugins loaded simultaneously, 16GB makes a meaningful difference — apps are less likely to be unloaded from memory when switching between them, which is critical in a live performance context where switching to a browser and back should not kill your synth state.

Best DAW Apps for iPad in 2026

The iPad DAW ecosystem has matured considerably. There are now several genuinely professional options, each with a distinct philosophy and workflow. Here is a detailed breakdown of every app worth considering:

iPad DAW apps mapped by complexity vs. touch optimization. Bubble size indicates relative feature depth.

GarageBand — Best Free Option

GarageBand for iPad is free, deeply capable, and used by professional producers for idea sketching and even finished tracks. It includes a full complement of Apple instruments — Drummer (Apple's algorithmic drummer), Smart Instruments for chord-based playing, the Alchemy synth engine (the same one found in Logic Pro on Mac), and Logic's collection of vintage synthesiser models. The audio recorder handles multi-track recording with solid latency performance, and the MIDI piano roll is intuitive for touch-based note entry.

The limitations are real and worth knowing upfront: GarageBand does not support third-party AUv3 plugins beyond a limited approved selection, track count caps at 32 on older iPads (though this is rarely a practical constraint for sketching), and there is no native export to Ableton, Pro Tools, or any non-Apple DAW. What GarageBand does offer is seamless iCloud integration with Logic Pro on Mac — projects opened in Logic appear with all their tracks, regions, and Apple instrument settings intact. For any producer who uses Logic as their primary desktop DAW, GarageBand on iPad is an outstanding companion tool for capturing ideas on the go.

If you are completely new to music production and deciding where to start, GarageBand is the correct answer — it is genuinely excellent for its price (free), and the skills transfer directly to Logic Pro when you are ready to upgrade. See our best DAW for beginners guide for a broader comparison including desktop options.

Cubasis 3 — Best Professional iPad DAW

Cubasis 3 by Steinberg is the most fully-featured professional DAW available on iPad. It supports unlimited audio and MIDI tracks (hardware permitting), full AUv3 plugin hosting with instance-level parameter lock, MIDI effects including arpeggiators and chord pads, advanced automation with curve editing, time-stretching via the built-in AudioWarp engine, and — critically — ALS export, allowing you to open Cubasis sessions directly in Ableton Live on desktop with audio regions and MIDI preserved.

This ALS export makes Cubasis the best choice for producers who split production time between iPad and Ableton Live. You can sketch a track on Cubasis while traveling, export the ALS file via AirDrop or Files, open it in Ableton on your laptop or desktop, and continue working with full MIDI data and audio regions intact. No stems-only limitation. This workflow alone justifies the price premium over other iPad DAWs for Ableton users.

The Cubasis 3 interface has been adapted thoughtfully for touch — it does not feel like a compromised desktop port. Track headers are sized for finger interaction, automation lanes are easy to draw with a fingertip or Apple Pencil, and the mixer is laid out in a way that makes sense on a touch screen. At ~$49.99 it is the most expensive iPad DAW, but for serious producers the value is strong. Steinberg also offers in-app expansion packs for additional instruments and samples.

BeatMaker 3 — Best for Beat Production

BeatMaker 3 by Intua is the iPad's answer to MPC-style production. It combines a sampler, drum machine, piano roll, audio recorder, and full mixer in one tightly integrated app with a workflow designed around beat-making and pattern-based composition rather than linear recording. The pad-based interface is highly intuitive for hip-hop, trap, and electronic beat production — you can chop samples, build drum patterns, layer synth chords, and mix the full session without ever leaving the app.

Full AUv3 plugin support means you can load third-party synths and effects directly inside BeatMaker sessions — run Moog Animoog Z as a pad instrument, load FabFilter Pro-Q 3 on a channel strip, and layer everything through BeatMaker's mixer. At ~$24.99 it is excellent value. Export is via audio stems — there is no direct Ableton or Logic session format export, but the stem export is clean, labeled, and reliable for import into any desktop DAW. For producers focused on making trap beats or hip-hop production, BeatMaker 3 is the natural iPad home.

AUM — Best for Live Performance and Modular Routing

AUM (Audio Mixer) by Kymatica is not a traditional DAW in the linear sense — it is an audio routing and mixing environment that connects AUv3 apps, instruments, effects, and hardware audio inputs into custom signal chains. Think of it as a patchable mixing console for iPad apps, similar in concept to a modular mixer but for software. You can route the output of a synth app through multiple effects apps, merge it with an audio input from a hardware instrument, and record the entire combined output as audio.

AUM is the preferred environment for live performance on iPad. A typical live rig might involve Moog Animoog Z running as a lead synth AUv3, a drum machine app providing rhythm, an effects chain including reverb and delay AUv3s, all connected inside AUM and controlled via MIDI from a hardware controller. AUM records the output of your entire signal chain in real-time. For producers building hybrid hardware-software rigs for live use or for ambient and experimental music production, AUM is essentially a required tool.

Koala Sampler — Best for On-the-Go Sampling

Koala Sampler is a remarkably capable sampling tool for its $3.99 price. It records samples from the iPad microphone, from audio inputs connected via a USB-C interface, or from other apps running on the iPad via Inter-App Audio or AUv3. The interface is built around a 16-pad sampler grid with loop points, pitch, and basic effects on each pad — it is designed to be immediately playable without reading a manual. For lo-fi production, hip-hop sampling, field recording-based music, and beat sketching on the go, Koala is indispensable. Export is via audio stems or direct WAV export per pad.

Drambo — Best for Modular Synthesis and Electronic Music

Drambo by Beepstreet is a modular synthesizer and sequencer environment for iPad that occupies a unique space in the ecosystem. It is simultaneously a step sequencer, a modular signal flow environment, and a full AUv3 host. Drambo is genuinely deep — the learning curve is steeper than BeatMaker or Cubasis, but producers focused on synthesis, generative music, and electronic composition find it extraordinarily powerful. At ~$19.99 it represents exceptional value for the depth on offer.

Audio Interfaces for iPad: What Works and What to Buy

Connecting a professional audio interface to an iPad transforms it from a self-contained recorder into a full recording studio input — enabling you to record vocals with a condenser microphone, track electric guitar, and monitor through studio speakers with professional latency performance.

All current iPad models use USB-C, which means any USB-C class-compliant audio interface connects directly without adapters. Class-compliant means the interface works without driver installation — it is plug-and-play. Nearly all modern consumer and prosumer audio interfaces are class-compliant.

Recommended audio interfaces for iPad in 2026:

  • Focusrite Scarlett Solo (4th Gen): ~$119. Single mic/instrument input, bus-powered via USB-C, connects directly to iPad. Excellent preamp quality for the price. Ideal for solo producers recording vocals or guitar. See our Focusrite Scarlett Solo review for full specs and verdict.
  • Focusrite Scarlett 2i2 (4th Gen): ~$169. Two mic/instrument inputs, enabling simultaneous dual-input recording. The most popular audio interface in the world, and works flawlessly with iPad via USB-C. Reviewed in our Scarlett 2i2 Gen 4 review.
  • IK Multimedia iRig Pro I/O: ~$149. Specifically designed with iOS/iPadOS in mind, includes a headphone output with blend control. Works via USB-C and includes a dedicated iPad-compatible app ecosystem.
  • Apogee ONE: ~$249. High-end single-channel interface with Apogee's renowned preamp quality. Made specifically for Apple devices and optimised for iPad use.
  • MOTU M2: ~$179. Two inputs, excellent converter quality, bus-powered, and class-compliant for iPad. The MOTU M2 is a favourite among producers who want step up from Scarlett quality without a large price jump.

For a broader comparison of interface options at different budgets, our best audio interfaces under $200 guide covers desktop and iPad-compatible options side by side.

Power considerations: Most bus-powered interfaces draw power from the iPad via USB-C. This works but does drain the iPad battery faster during sessions. For extended recording sessions, consider using a USB-C hub with a dedicated power pass-through port, which allows simultaneous charging and interface connection.

USB hubs for expanded connectivity: A USB-C hub with multiple USB-A/USB-C ports, HDMI output, and SD card reader dramatically expands the iPad's connectivity — allowing simultaneous connection of an audio interface, MIDI controller, external storage, and display. Anker and CalDigit make reliable options. Ensure the hub supports USB Audio Class 2.0 for reliable audio interface operation at sample rates above 48kHz.

MIDI Controllers for iPad

The iPad's touch screen is itself a capable MIDI input device — every iPad DAW app supports on-screen keyboard and pad playing. But dedicated MIDI controllers dramatically improve playability, expressiveness, and workflow efficiency.

USB MIDI controllers: Most class-compliant USB MIDI controllers connect directly to USB-C iPads via USB-C cable or via a USB-C hub. This covers the vast majority of pad controllers, keyboard controllers, and drum machines on the market. Akai MPK mini, Native Instruments Komplete Kontrol, Arturia MiniLab, and virtually every modern MIDI controller from major manufacturers will work without driver installation.

Bluetooth MIDI controllers: Bluetooth MIDI (BLE-MIDI) allows completely wireless controller connection to iPad. The Arturia MiniLab 3 connects via Bluetooth MIDI. The Roli Seaboard Block connects wirelessly and its polyphonic expression (MPE) data is fully supported in MPE-compatible iPad apps. Latency on Bluetooth MIDI has improved significantly — modern BLE-MIDI implementations typically deliver 5–10ms latency, which is acceptable for most production workflows though not ideal for live performance.

Apple Pencil: In apps that support it (Cubasis 3, GoodNotes used alongside DAW apps, some AUv3 instruments), the Apple Pencil functions as a pressure-sensitive drawing and modulation input. Drawing automation curves with Apple Pencil in Cubasis 3 is one of the genuinely superior iPad production experiences compared to a mouse-based desktop workflow. The precision of the Pencil for fine automation editing is a legitimate iPad advantage.

For a comprehensive overview of controller options for both iPad and desktop, see our best MIDI controllers guide.

AUv3 Plugins: The iPad Plugin Ecosystem

iPad does not support VST or VST3 plugins — the standard format used by desktop DAWs. Instead, iPadOS uses AUv3 (Audio Unit version 3), which is Apple's mobile plugin standard. AUv3 plugins are sandboxed, run inside host apps (like Cubasis 3, AUM, or BeatMaker 3), and are installed through the App Store like any other app.

The AUv3 ecosystem in 2026 is substantially richer than it was just three years ago. Here are the standout categories and instruments worth knowing:

Synthesizers:

  • Moog Minimoog Model D (AUv3): The legendary monophonic synthesiser ported faithfully to iPad. Excellent for bass and lead sounds.
  • Moog Animoog Z: Moog's MPE-capable polyphonic synth with a unique anisotropic synthesis engine. One of the best synth apps on any platform.
  • Korg iM1 / Korg Module: Classic Korg synthesis and sample playback, fully AUv3.
  • Zeeon (BeepStreet): Analogue-modelled polyphonic synth with a superb sound engine.
  • FAC Bandit: Multi-band saturation and distortion effect with precise AUv3 parameter control.

Effects:

  • FabFilter Pro-Q 3 (AUv3): The same parametric EQ used in professional studios worldwide, now available as a full AUv3 plugin for iPad DAWs.
  • Eventide H9 Harmonizer: The legendary Eventide algorithms available as AUv3 effects.
  • AudioKit Reverb: Algorithmic reverb with flexible control.
  • AUFX series (Kymatica): Space (reverb), Dub (echo), and other high-quality effects in AUv3 format.

Sample-based instruments:

  • Samples from Logic (Apple): Apple provides a large library of sampled instruments via GarageBand and Logic Pro that are accessible in compatible apps.
  • BS-16i: A capable SoundFont and SF2 sample player AUv3, allowing use of any SoundFont library.

The AUv3 ecosystem does not match VST in raw breadth — you will not find every obscure boutique plugin from a small developer. But the most important tools are present, and the quality of the best AUv3 instruments is genuinely world-class. The FabFilter Pro-Q 3 AUv3, for example, is identical in functionality to the desktop VST version.

iPad Music Production Workflow: How to Work Effectively

The technical capability of an iPad for music production is well established. The more practical question is: how do you structure an efficient workflow on a device with a smaller screen, touch-based interface, and a different app paradigm than a desktop computer? Here are the workflow strategies that experienced iPad producers consistently use.

Use Split View for multitasking between apps: iPadOS Split View allows two apps to run simultaneously side by side. A common workflow is running Cubasis 3 in the left panel and a synth app or sample browser in the right panel — you can play the synth, hear it through AUM or directly, and immediately drag the recorded audio into your Cubasis session. Split View dramatically reduces the cognitive and physical cost of switching between apps during a session.

Commit to an app as your session hub: The biggest workflow mistake new iPad producers make is trying to juggle too many open apps simultaneously without a clear routing plan. Decide which app is your session hub — Cubasis 3, BeatMaker 3, or AUM — and route everything else to it. Treat the hub app as you would treat your DAW on desktop: it is where final arrangement, mixing, and export happens.

Use Inter-App Audio and AUv3 consistently: For instruments, always prefer AUv3 plugins loaded inside your host DAW over standalone app playback where possible. AUv3 instruments are session-saved with your project — when you close and reopen the project, the instrument state is restored. Standalone app state is not automatically saved to your DAW session.

Apple Pencil for automation: If you have an Apple Pencil, use it for all automation editing. The precision of pencil-drawn automation curves in Cubasis 3 is genuinely superior to mouse-based automation on desktop in terms of the physical feel and immediacy. This is one of the areas where iPad production has a legitimate advantage over desktop.

External storage and project management: iPadOS supports USB-C external drives and SD cards (via adapter or hub). Store your sample libraries on an external drive or in iCloud to avoid filling internal storage. For project management, use the Files app — it integrates with iCloud, Dropbox, and Google Drive, making cross-device access to your sessions straightforward.

Low-latency monitoring: For recording audio (vocals, guitar), buffer size and latency are critical. Set your DAW's buffer size to the lowest stable setting your iPad and interface can support — typically 64 or 128 samples. At 44.1kHz with a 64-sample buffer, you get approximately 1.5ms processing latency, which is imperceptible during live performance and recording. Most current iPads with a quality interface handle 64-sample buffers without dropouts.

Transferring iPad Projects to Desktop DAW

One of the most practical questions iPad producers ask is: how do I get my project off the iPad and into Ableton, Logic, or Pro Tools on my computer? The answer depends on which iPad DAW you are using.

Cubasis 3 → Ableton Live (ALS Export): Cubasis 3 supports direct ALS (Ableton Live Set) export. In Cubasis, select File > Export > Export as Ableton Live Set. The exported ALS file contains all audio regions placed on their correct timeline positions and all MIDI regions with note data preserved. Open the ALS file in Ableton Live on your desktop and the session structure is intact. This is the cleanest iPad-to-Ableton workflow available. Note that AUv3 plugins do not transfer — you will need to reapply effects using desktop VSTs, but audio and MIDI data carries over completely.

GarageBand → Logic Pro (iCloud): GarageBand projects saved to iCloud appear automatically in Logic Pro on Mac under the GarageBand section of the project browser. Open the project in Logic and all tracks, Apple instrument settings, and audio regions are present. Apple instrument patches (Alchemy presets, Drummer settings) transfer correctly because Logic Pro uses the same instrument engines. Third-party AUv3 plugins used in the GarageBand session will not transfer.

BeatMaker 3 / Koala Sampler / Drambo → Any DAW (Audio Stems): These apps export individual track stems as WAV files. In BeatMaker 3, use the Multitrack Export function to export each pad/instrument track as a separate WAV file with identical start points. Import these stems into any desktop DAW and they will line up on the timeline correctly. This is the universal transfer method and works with every desktop DAW including Pro Tools, FL Studio, and Ableton.

AUM → Any DAW (Rendered Audio): AUM records its master output and individual channel outputs as audio. Export the rendered audio files via the Files app or AirDrop to your desktop.

AirDrop is the fastest transfer method for moving files between iPad and a Mac. For Windows users, the Files app with iCloud Drive or a USB-C cable connection (iPad appears as a file drive in Windows Explorer) is the most reliable approach.

Honest Assessment: iPad Music Production Limitations in 2026

iPad music production is more capable than many desktop-focused producers expect, but there are real limitations that matter depending on your workflow. Being clear-eyed about these limitations helps you decide whether iPad works as a primary or secondary production environment for your specific needs.

Plugin ecosystem breadth: The AUv3 ecosystem, while rich in highlights, does not match the breadth of VST/VST3. Specific plugins that exist as VST3 do not have AUv3 equivalents — niche synthesisers, specialized mastering tools, vintage console emulations from smaller developers. If your desktop workflow depends on specific plugins, check whether AUv3 versions exist before committing to iPad as a primary environment.

Screen real estate: Even on the iPad Pro 13", a DAW timeline with many tracks is more compressed than on a 27" desktop monitor. Detailed MIDI editing, particularly for complex orchestral arrangements with many simultaneous instruments, becomes genuinely harder on a smaller screen. Connecting the iPad to an external monitor via USB-C HDMI helps significantly for static editing sessions.

File management complexity: iPadOS's sandboxed file system, while improved significantly in recent versions, is still more complex to navigate than a Mac or PC file system when managing large sample libraries, multiple project folders, and external storage. The Files app is capable but less powerful than Finder or Windows Explorer for complex file operations.

Third-party software integration: Certain desktop production tools — like Splice sample sync, some VST-only instruments, and DAW-specific hardware controllers — do not have iPad equivalents or integrations. If your workflow is built around Splice for sample management, for example, the iPad browser version is functional but not equivalent to the desktop app.

What iPad does better than desktop: Touch-based interaction is genuinely superior for certain tasks — drawing automation curves, finger-drumming into a pad grid, pinch-zooming in a piano roll, and recording live parameter changes with fingertip control. The iPad is also completely silent (no fan noise), more portable than any laptop, starts instantly, and the battery life of current M-series iPads (up to 10 hours under production load) exceeds most laptops. These are real production advantages, not just lifestyle benefits.

Practical Exercises

Beginner Exercise

Build Your First Beat in GarageBand for iPad

Open GarageBand on your iPad and create a new project using the Drummer instrument. Add a second track using one of GarageBand's built-in keyboard instruments and record a simple 8-bar chord progression using the on-screen Smart Keyboard. Export the finished loop as an audio file via the Share menu and listen back through headphones to evaluate your mix balance.

Intermediate Exercise

Record and Transfer a Cubasis 3 Session to Ableton

In Cubasis 3, build a 16-bar track using at least one AUv3 instrument, one recorded audio track through a USB-C audio interface, and basic automation on the volume fader. Use the ALS Export function to export the session, transfer the file via AirDrop to a Mac, and open it in Ableton Live — verify that all audio regions and MIDI data are correctly placed on the timeline.

Advanced Exercise

Build a Live Performance Rig in AUM

Configure AUM with three signal chains: a polyphonic AUv3 synth (such as Moog Animoog Z) routed through a reverb and delay AUv3 effects chain, a drum machine app sending MIDI-triggered audio into a separate AUM channel, and a live audio input from an instrument connected via USB-C interface. Map MIDI CC parameters from a hardware controller to AUM channel faders and key plugin parameters, then perform and record a five-minute live set, exporting the stereo output for review.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ Can you make professional music on an iPad?
Yes. Producers have released commercially successful music made entirely or primarily on iPad using apps like Cubasis 3, BeatMaker 3, and GarageBand. The M-series iPad Pro has comparable CPU performance to desktop computers. The main limitations are plugin selection and screen real estate, not processing power.
FAQ What is the best DAW app for iPad in 2026?
For beginners, GarageBand is free and excellent. For professional production, Cubasis 3 is the top choice — especially for Ableton users due to its ALS export. For beat-making, BeatMaker 3 is the strongest option. For live performance and modular routing, AUM. The best app depends on your workflow and genre.
FAQ Can I use a USB audio interface with iPad?
Yes. All current USB-C iPads connect directly to USB-C class-compliant audio interfaces without adapters. The Focusrite Scarlett Solo, Scarlett 2i2, MOTU M2, and IK Multimedia iRig Pro all work reliably with iPad in 2026.
FAQ How do I transfer a project from iPad to Ableton or Logic?
Cubasis 3 supports direct ALS (Ableton Live Set) export, preserving audio and MIDI timeline positions. GarageBand projects open directly in Logic Pro on Mac via iCloud. For all other apps, export individual track stems as WAV files and import them into your desktop DAW at matching timeline positions.
FAQ What MIDI controllers work with iPad?
Most class-compliant USB MIDI controllers connect directly via USB-C. Bluetooth MIDI controllers including the Arturia MiniLab 3 and Roli Seaboard Block connect wirelessly. Apple Pencil also functions as a pressure-sensitive input in compatible apps like Cubasis 3.
FAQ Is GarageBand on iPad worth using for music production?
GarageBand on iPad is genuinely excellent for its price (free) and is used by professional producers for idea sketching. Its main limitations are the lack of third-party AUv3 plugin support and no export to non-Apple DAWs. GarageBand projects open seamlessly in Logic Pro on Mac via iCloud.
FAQ What iPad is best for music production in 2026?
The iPad Pro with M4 chip is the most capable option, with processing power exceeding some desktop computers. For most producers, the iPad Air 11-inch with M2 chip is the sweet spot — excellent performance, direct USB-C interface connection, and a significantly lower price than the iPad Pro.
FAQ Can you use VST plugins on iPad?
No. iPad does not support VST or VST3 plugins. Instead, iPadOS uses AUv3 (Audio Unit version 3), the iOS/iPadOS plugin standard. Hundreds of AUv3 instruments and effects are available including Moog Minimoog Model D, Moog Animoog Z, FabFilter Pro-Q 3, and many others.
