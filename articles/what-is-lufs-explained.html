What Is Lufs Explained

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 · 9 min read · Verified May 7, 2026
Quick Answer — Updated May 2026

LUFS (Loudness Units relative to Full Scale) is the international standard for measuring perceived loudness in audio over time, defined by the ITU-R BS.1770 specification. Every major streaming platform uses LUFS to normalize playback — Spotify and YouTube target -14 LUFS integrated, Apple Music targets -16 LUFS. For most releases in 2026, mastering to -14 LUFS integrated with true peaks at -1 dBTP is the safest and most universally compatible target.

Updated May 2026

If you have ever uploaded a track to Spotify and noticed it sounds quieter than other songs on the same playlist — or louder, then gets pulled down — the answer almost certainly involves LUFS. It is one of the most important concepts in modern music production and one of the most misunderstood. This reference covers what LUFS means technically, how streaming normalization works, exact platform targets, what true peak limiting is, and how to measure LUFS inside your DAW.

What LUFS Actually Means

LUFS stands for Loudness Units relative to Full Scale. It is a standardized measurement of perceived loudness defined by the ITU-R BS.1770 specification — an international broadcasting standard adopted by the European Broadcasting Union (EBU) and now used by virtually every streaming platform, broadcaster, and mastering engineer in the world.

The key word is perceived. Regular peak meters show you the highest instantaneous level your audio signal reaches — the literal amplitude of the waveform at any given moment. But that number tells you almost nothing about how loud the music actually sounds to a human listener.

LUFS fixes this by measuring loudness the way ears actually experience it. Human hearing is not flat — we are most sensitive to frequencies in the midrange (roughly 1–4 kHz, the range of the human voice) and less sensitive to deep bass and high treble at equal volume. A sub-bass sine wave at -10 dBFS will sound much quieter than a midrange synth at -10 dBFS, even at the same peak level. LUFS accounts for this by applying frequency weighting that mirrors how human ears perceive different parts of the spectrum.

Three Types of LUFS You Need to Know

LUFS measurements come in three forms, each useful for different stages of production.

Integrated LUFS is the average loudness of the entire audio file from start to finish, with gating applied to exclude silence and very quiet sections. This is the number streaming platforms use for normalization. When someone says "master to -14 LUFS," they mean integrated LUFS. It reflects the true average loudness of the musical content after silence is gated out.

Short-term LUFS measures average loudness over the last 3 seconds, updated continuously in real time. This is useful during mixing to monitor how individual sections — a verse, a chorus, a drop — compare in loudness. A chorus that hits significantly harder than the verse will show clearly on a short-term meter.

Momentary LUFS measures loudness over the last 400 milliseconds, giving a near-instant reading. It is useful for catching loudness spikes and monitoring the energy of specific transients. For mastering and streaming submission, integrated LUFS is what matters. Short-term and momentary readings are tools for the mix stage. Understanding these distinctions is foundational to mastering a song at home without professional metering.

True Peak: The Number Nobody Talks About Enough

Alongside integrated LUFS, every streaming platform specifies a true peak limit — typically -1 dBTP or -2 dBTP. This is separate from your regular peak meter, and ignoring it is one of the most common mastering mistakes.

When your audio gets converted from your master WAV to a lossy streaming format (Spotify uses Ogg Vorbis; Apple Music uses AAC; YouTube uses AAC), the encoding process can create inter-sample peaks — moments where the reconstructed audio exceeds 0 dBFS even if the original file showed clean levels. Standard peak meters do not detect these. A true peak meter does.

If your master hits -1 dBTP, the platform has enough headroom to encode without clipping. A ceiling of -2 dBTP provides extra safety margin, especially for platforms like Amazon Music that enforce stricter limits. Every good mastering limiter — FabFilter Pro-L 2, iZotope Ozone's Maximizer, Sonnox Oxford Limiter — has true peak limiting built in. Confirm it is enabled before you export. For a deeper look at limiting techniques, see how to use a limiter.

Note that Apple Music's Sound Check normalization is optional — listeners can disable it — so your master also needs to hold up when played at full level without gain adjustment.

The Loudness War Is Over — Here Is Why It Matters

Key takeaway: Streaming normalization has effectively ended the loudness war. A track mastered at -8 LUFS and one at -14 LUFS both play back at the same perceived volume on Spotify. The -8 LUFS master sounds worse — the heavy limiting used to achieve that loudness destroys dynamic range, transients, and depth. A dynamic, well-balanced master always wins at the same playback volume.

This changes how you should think about mixing headroom and loudness decisions throughout your session. Preserving dynamic range is no longer a compromise — it is a competitive advantage.

How to Measure LUFS in Your DAW

Popular free LUFS metering plugins include Youlean Loudness Meter, dpMeter 5 by TB Audio, and MLoudnessAnalyzer by MeldaProduction. Most modern DAWs also include built-in loudness meters. For mastering work, paid options like iZotope Insight, FabFilter Pro-L 2, and Nugen Audio VisLM offer more detailed integrated and true peak analysis.

Place your LUFS meter on the master bus output, export a full-length render, and let the meter calculate integrated loudness across the entire file. Do not target a number mechanically — aim for your mix to sound its best, then check where it lands. A well-mixed track in most genres will fall naturally between -14 and -10 LUFS before heavy limiting. If you are consistently hitting -6 LUFS, your compression chain needs a review. Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ What does LUFS stand for?
LUFS stands for Loudness Units relative to Full Scale. It is the international standard for measuring perceived loudness in audio, defined by the ITU-R BS.1770 specification and used by every major streaming platform.
FAQ What LUFS should I master to for Spotify?
Spotify normalizes playback to -14 LUFS integrated. Master your track to sound as good as possible — a well-balanced master around -14 LUFS with true peaks at -1 dBTP will play back on Spotify without any gain adjustment.
FAQ What is the difference between LUFS and dB?
Regular dB measures the peak amplitude of an audio signal — the highest instantaneous level. LUFS measures perceived loudness over time, accounting for how human ears respond to different frequencies. LUFS is a far more accurate representation of how loud a track actually sounds.
FAQ What is integrated LUFS?
Integrated LUFS is the average loudness of an entire audio file from start to finish, gated to exclude silence. This is the primary measurement used by streaming platforms for normalization, and differs from momentary LUFS (400ms window) and short-term LUFS (3-second window).
FAQ What is true peak limiting and why does it matter?
True peak limiting caps the absolute highest level your audio reaches between samples, including inter-sample peaks that occur during lossy encoding like AAC or Ogg Vorbis. Setting a true peak ceiling of -1 dBTP prevents distortion when platforms encode your master to their streaming format.
FAQ Should I master louder than -14 LUFS to be competitive?
No. Streaming platforms normalize playback, so a track at -8 LUFS and one at -14 LUFS play back at the same perceived volume. The -8 LUFS master will sound worse because the heavy limiting required destroys dynamic range, transients, and depth.
FAQ What LUFS does Apple Music use?
Apple Music normalizes to -16 LUFS integrated via its Sound Check feature. However, Sound Check is optional and users can disable it, so your master needs to hold up when played at full level without normalization.
FAQ What free tools can I use to measure LUFS in my DAW?
Popular free options include Youlean Loudness Meter, dpMeter 5 by TB Audio, and MLoudnessAnalyzer by MeldaProduction. Paid options like iZotope Insight, FabFilter Pro-L 2, and Nugen Audio VisLM provide more detailed integrated and true peak analysis for mastering.
