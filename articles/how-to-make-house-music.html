How to Make House Music: The Complete Production Guide

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 14 min read Beginner–Intermediate

House music has been one of the dominant forces in global music since it emerged from Chicago in the early 1980s. The genre's signature elements — four-on-the-floor kick drums, soulful chord stabs, pumping bass, and repetitive hypnotic structure — are simultaneously simple in concept and infinitely nuanced in execution. Understanding what makes house music work is the first step to producing it convincingly.

This guide covers the complete house music production process: tempo and feel, drum programming, basslines, chord stabs, melodic elements, the sidechain compression technique central to the genre, arrangement for DJ sets, and the subgenres that have extended the original sound into the 21st century.

What you'll learn:

The four-on-the-floor drum pattern, house basslines, chord stabs and pads, sidechain compression for the pumping effect, house arrangement structure, mixing for the club, and the key differences between deep, tech, classic, and afro house.

Tempo, Key, and the Four-on-the-Floor Foundation

House music lives at 120–130 BPM. The most common range for contemporary house is 124–128 BPM — fast enough to drive energy on a dance floor, slow enough to allow the harmonic and melodic elements room to breathe. Start at 125 BPM as a baseline and adjust to match the feel of your track once the drums and bass are in place.

House music is largely built in minor keys with jazz-influenced harmony — Am, Dm, Cm, and Fm are common roots. The harmonic language borrows from soul, disco, and gospel: minor 7th chords, dominant 7th tensions, major 7th resolutions. This is the emotional heart of house — the tension between groove and harmony that gives classic tracks their spiritual, euphoric character.

The Four-on-the-Floor Pattern

The non-negotiable foundation of house music: a kick drum on every quarter note beat — beats 1, 2, 3, and 4 of every bar, without exception. This relentless pulse is what defines house as a genre and separates it from hip-hop (where the kick is syncopated) and drum and bass (where it's broken).

In your DAW's step sequencer or piano roll:

  • Kick: Steps 1, 5, 9, 13 of a 16-step pattern (every quarter note)
  • Snare/clap: Steps 5 and 13 (beats 2 and 4 — the backbeat)
  • Closed hi-hat: Every step (16th notes) or every other step (8th notes)
  • Open hi-hat: Steps 3, 7, 11, 15 (the off-beat 8th notes — "the and" of each beat)

This four-element pattern is complete house music. Everything else — bass, chords, melody, vocals — builds on this rhythmic foundation.

House Drum Sound Design

The character of house drums matters as much as the pattern. Classic house uses Roland TR-909 and TR-808 drum machine sounds — punchy, analog-modeled, with specific frequency characters that mix well together and translate powerfully on large speaker systems.

The kick drum is the most critical sound in house production. A classic TR-909 kick has a punchy transient, a tonal body that sits between 50–80Hz, and a short tail that doesn't linger. For modern house, the kick needs to cut through at loud volume — tight, punchy, with a defined transient and a sub component that locks with the bassline.

The snare and clap in house are often layered — a TR-909 snare with a TR-909 clap, tuned to complement each other. The snare handles the low-mid crack (around 200Hz); the clap adds the high-frequency snap (3–8kHz). Together they create a fuller backbeat than either alone.

Hi-hats define the groove. Classic house uses TR-909 hi-hats — metallic, with a specific "tsss" character. Velocity variation across hi-hat hits is essential. Identical velocity on every hi-hat step sounds mechanical; variation of ±20–30% velocity creates a human swing without actually adjusting the timing.

House Drum Processing Chain

On the drum bus, after the individual channel processing:

  1. Transient shaper — add attack punch to the kick and snare transients
  2. Glue compressor — SSL G-Bus style, 2:1 ratio, slow attack, 1–2dB GR, to lock the kit together
  3. Saturation — gentle even-harmonic saturation adds warmth and density to the drum bus
  4. High-pass at 30–40Hz — remove sub-infrasonic content that wastes headroom without audible benefit

The House Bassline

House basslines are rhythmic, melodic, and functional — they lock with the kick, drive the groove, and carry harmonic information. A great house bassline doesn't just hold the root note; it moves, creates anticipation, and connects the kick pulse with the chord harmony above it.

The classic house bass structure has two layers:

Sub bass (below 80Hz): A pure sine wave or sub-heavy synth patch locked to the root note of each chord. This sits beneath everything and provides the physical floor-rattling bass energy that house music requires at club volume. The sub must duck when the kick hits — sidechain compression from the kick to the sub channel is mandatory to prevent low-frequency conflicts and maintain headroom.

Mid bass (80–250Hz): The melodic, rhythmic bass element. This is where the bassline groove lives. Classic house uses the Roland TB-303 (acid bass), the Juno-106, or a sampled funk bass as the mid-bass source. The pattern often emphasizes off-beat 16th notes, octave jumps, and short note values that create rhythmic tension against the four-on-the-floor kick.

TB-303 acid bass: The Roland TB-303 bassline synthesizer was a foundational instrument in Chicago house. Its characteristic squelching, resonant filter sweeps created the "acid" subgenre. TAL-BassLine-101 (free) and Roland Cloud's official TB-303 Software Synthesizer emulate this sound for modern producers. Even in non-acid house, the TB-303's ability to create rhythmically active, filter-modulated basslines is unmatched.

Sidechain Compression: The Pump

The pumping, breathing effect that characterizes house music is created by sidechain compression — the kick drum triggers a compressor on the bassline, pads, and other sustained elements, ducking them momentarily every time the kick hits and releasing between beats. This rhythmic ducking creates the sense of the music "pumping" with the kick drum.

Sidechain settings for house:

ParameterSettingWhy
Attack0–5msInstant response — duck immediately when kick hits
Release80–200msControls pump character — faster = tighter pump, slower = more sustained breathe
Ratio4:1 to 10:1High ratio for obvious pumping effect; lower for subtle ducking
ThresholdSet so 4–8dB GR on kick hitsEnough ducking to create the effect without destroying the element
KneeHardImmediate response on kick transient

Apply sidechain compression to the sub bass, mid bass, chord pads, and any other sustained elements. The kick drum itself should not be compressed by the sidechain — it drives the sidechain, it does not respond to it. The result is a mix where every sustained element pulses with the kick, creating the characteristic house music energy.

Chord Stabs and Pads

House music's emotional depth comes from its harmonic elements. Two distinct layers:

Chord stabs: Short, percussive chord hits with fast attack and fast decay — the note triggers and cuts off within a 16th note. Classic chord stab sounds: Juno-106 with a short amp envelope, electric piano (Rhodes or Wurlitzer) with staccato playing, or any synth with an instant attack and fast release. Chord stabs are typically played on the off-beats (the "and" of each beat) or in syncopated 16th-note patterns. They carry the rhythmic chordal identity of house — the punchy, percussive harmonic hits that drive alongside the drums.

Sustained pads: Slower-attack, longer-sustain chords that provide the harmonic atmosphere underneath the stabs and drums. These can be strings, saw-wave synth pads, or organ sounds. In deep house, the pad is often the emotional center of the track — a slowly evolving chord progression played on a warm synthesizer pad. Apply gentle sidechain compression from the kick to the pad to make it pulse with the groove.

Common chord progressions in house:

  • i-VII-VI-VII loop: Am7 → Gmaj7 → Fmaj7 → Gmaj7 (circular, hypnotic — works for entire track)
  • i-iv: Am7 → Dm7 (minimal, atmospheric — classic deep house)
  • ii-V-i: Bm7b5 → E7 → Am7 (jazz-influenced tension and resolution)
  • I-IV-vi-V: Fmaj7 → Bbmaj7 → Dm7 → C7 (uplifting, more major-key feel)

Melodic and Vocal Elements

House music's melodic vocabulary draws from gospel, soul, and disco. Lead elements typically include: sampled soul vocal phrases (chops from records, or original recordings in the soul tradition), gospel-influenced piano runs, organ phrases, and synthesizer leads with moderate modulation and expression.

Vocal samples in house are typically short phrases rather than full verses — a hook fragment, a repeated word or phrase, a gospel shout — looped and arranged to create hypnotic repetition. The sample interacts with the chord progression rather than sitting above it: pitching and timing the vocal to align with the harmony beneath it is the production craft that separates functional house from emotionally resonant house.

House Music Arrangement

House tracks are built for DJ mixing, which means the arrangement structure serves the DJ as much as the listener. A standard house track arrangement:

  • Intro (32–64 bars): Drums only, or drums and minimal bass. No chords, no melody. Allows the DJ to mix in over the outgoing track without a harmonic clash.
  • Build (16–32 bars): Progressive addition of elements — bass enters, then chords, then atmosphere. Energy rises steadily.
  • Main arrangement (32–64 bars): Full production with all elements. This is the "up" section of the track.
  • Breakdown (16–32 bars): Drums removed or reduced to minimal elements. Harmony and atmosphere continue. Emotional breathing room before the drop.
  • Drop (32–64 bars): Full arrangement returns, often with additional energy — a filter opening, a new percussion element, a vocal coming in. The peak of the track.
  • Outro (32–64 bars): Gradual removal of elements. Ends with drums alone for the outgoing DJ to mix out over.

Total track length: 6–8 minutes. This is longer than most other genres because the DJ needs mixing time at both ends. Tracks under 5 minutes are difficult to DJ with and are less likely to be played in clubs.

House Subgenres

Deep House

120–124 BPM. Jazzy chords, warm sounds, soulful vocals. More atmospheric than energetic. Larry Heard (Mr. Fingers) as the founding producer.

Tech House

125–130 BPM. Minimal harmonic elements, complex percussion, heavy bass. Between techno's austerity and house's groove. Fisher, Chris Lake dominant in 2020s.

Classic / Chicago House

120–126 BPM. TR-909 drums, gospel piano, soul vocals. Frankie Knuckles, Larry Heard, Marshall Jefferson. The original form.

Afro House

120–126 BPM. African percussion elements, tribal rhythms, chants, and melodic patterns layered over four-on-the-floor. Black Coffee leading producer.

Acid House

120–128 BPM. TB-303 squelching bassline as the primary character. Filter sweeps, resonant bass, often more abrasive than standard house.

Melodic House

122–126 BPM. Emphasis on emotive melodic leads, complex chord progressions. Often longer atmospheric breakdowns. Emerging from progressive house roots.

Mixing House Music

House music mixing priorities differ from pop or hip-hop because the end destination is a large club sound system. The kick and bass must be powerful, controlled, and mono-compatible — club systems are often mono below 200Hz. The stereo field should be wide and immersive in the mids and highs but tight in the lows.

Key mixing approaches for house:

  • Kick and bass relationship: High-pass the bass above 30–40Hz and ensure the kick's fundamental (typically 50–80Hz) has space. Sidechain the bass to duck on kick hits.
  • Check in mono: Club systems are mono at low frequencies. Everything below 250Hz should hold up in mono without phase cancellation or mud.
  • Master loudness: House music for streaming targets -9 to -12 LUFS — louder than pop because the genre is designed for high-energy playback. DJ masters intended for Beatport or digital DJing are typically louder at -8 to -10 LUFS.
  • Headroom for DJ mixers: Leave 1–2dB of headroom at the master. DJ mixers and club systems add gain, and an already-limited master will distort in the DJ booth.

Frequently Asked Questions

What BPM is house music?

120–130 BPM, with 124–128 BPM the most common range for contemporary house. Deep house typically sits at 120–124 BPM; tech house at 126–130 BPM.

What makes house music sound like house?

The four-on-the-floor kick pattern (kick on every beat), sidechain compression creating a pumping effect, chord stabs or pads, a rhythmic bassline, and the soulful or melodic lead elements drawn from soul, gospel, and disco.

How do I make the pumping effect in house music?

Sidechain compression: route the kick drum to the sidechain input of a compressor on the bass, pad, and synth channels. Fast attack (0–5ms), medium release (80–200ms), 4:1 to 10:1 ratio. The kick triggers the compressor, ducking all sustained elements on every beat.

What synthesizers are used in house music?

Classic house: Roland Juno-106 (chords), Roland TB-303 (acid bass), Roland TR-909 (drums). Modern equivalents: TAL-U-NO-LX (free Juno emulation), TAL-BassLine-101 (free 303 emulation), Serum, Vital (free), and Native Instruments Komplete for sample-based sounds.

How long should a house track be?

6–8 minutes for DJ-friendly tracks, with 32–64 bar intros and outros for mixing. Streaming-only releases can be 4–5 minutes, but anything intended for club play needs the full length for DJ usability.

What DAW is best for house music?

Ableton Live is most popular for house — its session view suits loop-based production and its Push integration suits the DJ-producer workflow. FL Studio and Logic Pro are both widely used. DAW choice matters less than familiarity with the tools.
