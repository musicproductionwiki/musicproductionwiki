How to Make House Music — Complete Production Guide

Updated May 2026  ·  17 min read  ·  Techniques

Quick answer: House music is built on a four-on-the-floor kick pattern (kick on every beat at 120–130 BPM), a snare or clap on beats 2 and 4, open hi-hats on the offbeats, a walking or bouncing bassline, and chord stabs (short piano or synth chord hits placed on syncopated offbeats for groove). The chord progressions lean toward minor keys with seventh chords for soul, or major keys for uplifting energy. The arrangement follows a DJ-friendly structure with extended intros, builds, drops, and breakdowns.

House music was born in Chicago's Warehouse club in the early 1980s, pioneered by DJs and producers like Frankie Knuckles, Larry Heard, and Jesse Saunders. They took the repetitive, four-on-the-floor rhythms of disco, stripped them back to their essentials, added synthesised basslines and chord progressions influenced by gospel and soul, and created something entirely new.

Today house music is a global genre with dozens of sub-genres — deep house, tech house, afrohouse, progressive house, minimal, electro house — all sharing the same foundational DNA but taking it in different sonic directions. Whether you're making warm deep house influenced by Larry Heard's classic sound or tightly grooved tech house, the production principles are the same.

The House Music Template — Core Elements

Four-on-the-Floor Kick

The single most defining element of house music is the four-on-the-floor kick pattern: the kick drum lands on every beat — beats 1, 2, 3, and 4 — at 120–130 BPM. This unrelenting pulse is what gives house music its driving, locomotive energy and what makes it work on a dance floor. The kick needs to be punchy with a defined transient attack and a clean, deep thump in the 60–80Hz range. Classic house kick sounds come from the Roland TR-909 (brighter, snappier), the TR-808 (deeper, sub-heavy), and sample libraries that recreate both. The kick should cut through the mix clearly — it should be felt physically at club volumes.

Tuning the kick matters. The fundamental pitch of the kick drum should complement the key of the track. A kick tuned to D when the track is in D minor will integrate more naturally with the bassline and chord progressions. Most DAWs allow pitch shifting of drum samples — use it.

Snare and Clap

The snare or clap lands on beats 2 and 4 (steps 5 and 13 in a 16-step grid). In classic house, a rimshot or snare from the TR-909 is layered with a handclap from the same machine. The clap adds brightness and snap; the snare adds weight. Modern house often uses a layered combination of a crisp electronic snare and a processed clap or finger snap, with reverb to add room character. The snare should not compete with the kick — it lives primarily in the upper-mid frequency range (1–5kHz), where the kick has minimal energy.

Hi-Hat Patterns

House hi-hat patterns create the groove and rhythmic feel that makes one house track sound different from another. The basic pattern: closed hi-hats on every eighth note (every other 16th step), with an open hi-hat on the offbeats — typically the "and" of beat 2 and the "and" of beat 4 (steps 7 and 15). This gives the pattern a swinging, open quality.

The subtle secret of house groove is velocity variation on the hi-hats. The accented hits (on the beats) sit at 100% velocity. The ghost hits (between beats) sit at 40–70%. Small timing variations — pushing or pulling individual hi-hat hits by a few ticks — create the human feel that distinguishes a groovy house beat from a mechanical one. In Ableton, use the Groove Pool with an 8A or 8B swing template to add this automatically.

Percussion and Shakers

Beyond the core kick/snare/hat, house music makes heavy use of auxiliary percussion. A shaker or tambourine playing a 16th-note pattern adds high-frequency movement and ties the groove together. Congas and bongos in a syncopated pattern add organic texture — deep house especially leans on this Latin percussion influence from disco. Keep auxiliary percussion at lower volumes than the main kit (typically -6 to -10 dB relative to the kick). Automate the percussion level through the arrangement to add and remove it at transitions.

Chord Progressions and Stabs

The harmony in house music is one of its most emotionally powerful elements. House draws directly from the gospel and soul music that Chicago's Black church community brought to the city, and those influences are everywhere in the chord voicings and progressions.

Common House Chord Progressions

Deep House — Am7 progression (soulful, melancholic)
Am7 — Dm7 — G7 — Cmaj7
Notes: A C E G / D F A C / G B D F / C E G B
4 bars, one chord per bar. Play as sustained pads or broken arpeggios.

Classic Chicago House (uplifting, gospel-influenced)
Am — F — C — G
Notes: A C E / F A C / C E G / G B D
2 bars per chord or 1 bar per chord for a faster harmonic rhythm. Add the 7th on each chord for more warmth.

Tech House (minimal, hypnotic)
Dm — Dm — Am — Am
Notes: D F A / D F A / A C E / A C E
2 bars per chord. Often played as a one- or two-note motif rather than full chords. Repetitive by design.

Afrohouse (warm, rhythmic)
Fm — Db — Ab — Eb
Notes: F Ab C / Db F Ab / Ab C Eb / Eb G Bb
1 bar per chord. Often played with a percussion-forward rhythmic feel, chords hitting on offbeats.

Chord Stabs — The Defining Rhythm Element

A chord stab is a short, rhythmically percussive chord hit. Rather than holding a chord for its full duration, you place it as a 16th note or 8th note hit at a syncopated position — on the "and" of beat 1, the "and" of beat 3, or in three-against-four patterns across the bar. The stab creates rhythmic momentum and groove; it's what gives house chords their propulsive energy rather than a static, held-chord quality.

The classic house stab sound: a piano or Rhodes sample with a fast attack and medium decay, or a synth with a fast filter envelope that opens briefly then closes. The note is short — it hits and decays quickly. In a 4-bar loop, the stab pattern repeats but with occasional variation: an extra stab here, a missed stab there, to create interest and avoid mechanical repetition.

Common stab placements in a 16-step grid: steps 3, 7, 10, 15 (syncopated, creates a forward-driving feel); or steps 1, 5, 9, 11, 13 (more driving and aggressive, common in tech house).

The Bassline

House basslines range from simple root-note pulses (common in minimal and early tech house) to complex, melodic walking bass lines (deep house, Chicago house). The bass in house sits in the 40–200Hz range and must coexist with the kick drum.

Deep House Bassline

Deep house basslines often walk through the chord changes melodically — moving from the root to the 5th, then the 3rd, then back to the root over 2–4 bars. The sound is typically a warm, round synth bass with a slow filter envelope opening — sine or slightly sawtooth oscillator, low-pass filter, moderate resonance. Famous examples: Larry Heard's "Can You Feel It" uses a simple, meditative bass line that breathes with the chord changes above it.

Tech House and Minimal Bassline

Tech house baselines are often a single repeated root note that pulses with the kick, with occasional melodic movement (a fifth up, or a chromatic passing note) at bar transitions. The sound is tighter and more rhythmic than deep house — shorter note length, higher attack, often with slight distortion or saturation for presence in club systems. The bass should be tuned precisely to the key of the track.

Bassline EQ and Sidechain

The bassline and kick drum share the same low-frequency space. Use sidechain compression to duck the bass briefly when the kick hits — this prevents frequency clash and keeps both elements audible at club volumes. Apply a high-pass filter to the bass below 30–40Hz to remove inaudible sub-frequencies that waste headroom. A gentle low-pass filter (around 8–12kHz) on the bass removes unnecessary high-frequency content and keeps it sitting correctly in the frequency spectrum.

Arrangement — DJ-Friendly Structure

House music arrangement follows a DJ-friendly structure designed to allow seamless mixing. The extended intro and outro give the DJ time to mix the track in and out of their set. The main body builds, peaks, and resolves in a way that creates emotional narrative on the dance floor.

Intro (bars 1–16 / 0:00–0:30): Kick and percussion only, or kick plus a minimal melodic element. No full chord drops yet. This gives the DJ time to mix the previous track out. Keep energy building slowly.

Build (bars 17–32 / 0:30–1:00): Add bassline. Begin introducing chord stabs at low velocity. Open hi-hat starts. Tension is building but the main drop hasn't hit yet.

Drop / Main Section (bars 33–64 / 1:00–2:00): Full arrangement — drums, bass, chords, stabs, vocal hook or melodic lead. Maximum energy. In club tracks this section repeats for 2–4 minutes to allow the DJ to mix and the dance floor to lock in.

Breakdown (bars 65–80 / 2:00–2:30): Strip back to minimal elements — kick only, or reverb wash, or a vocal pad. Create space and anticipation for the second drop.

Second Drop (bars 81–112 / 2:30–3:30): Full arrangement returns, often with a slight variation — a new percussion layer, a counter-melody, or a vocal hook variation. This is usually the emotional peak of the track.

Outro (bars 113–128 / 3:30–4:00): Gradually remove elements — stabs out, then melody, leaving kick and percussion for the final bars. DJ can mix out.

DAW-Specific Notes

Ableton Live: The Session view clip launcher is the natural environment for house production — build your elements as clips, trigger them live, record the arrangement. The Groove Pool provides 8th-note and 16th-note swing templates that immediately give house patterns the right feel. The Drum Rack allows individual compression and EQ on each drum sound within a single instrument.

Logic Pro: Logic's Ultrabeat drum machine and the Drummer track provide excellent starting points for house drum programming. The built-in Retro Synth and ES2 are well-suited for house basslines and stabs. Logic's MIDI Transform functions allow quick quantise-to-swing operations for groove.

FL Studio: The Step Sequencer is fast for programming house patterns. FL Studio's Patcher environment allows complex routing for sidechain setups. The Gross Beat plugin is popular for house and electronic music fills and effects.

Practical Exercises

🟢 Beginner — Build a House Loop in 20 Minutes

Open your DAW. Set BPM to 124. Program the four-on-the-floor kick: steps 1, 5, 9, 13 in a 16-step grid. Add a snare on steps 5 and 13. Add closed hi-hats on every step at alternating velocities (100% on odd steps, 60% on even steps). Add an open hi-hat on steps 3, 7, 11, 15. Play the loop back — this is the house drum foundation. Now create a new instrument track. Load a piano or Rhodes. Write an Am7 chord (notes A, C, E, G) and place it as a short 16th-note hit on step 3 of bar 1. Copy it to step 7 and step 11. Adjust the velocity of each hit — some louder, some softer. Add a simple bass note (just the A below the chord) timed to punch with the kick. Loop 4 bars and listen. You have a house groove.

🟡 Intermediate — Full Deep House Loop with Chord Progression

Build an 8-bar chord progression: bars 1–2 on Am7, bars 3–4 on Dm7, bars 5–6 on Fmaj7, bars 7–8 on Em7. Program chord stabs — short 16th-note hits placed on the "and" of beat 1 (step 3) and the "and" of beat 3 (step 11) in each bar. Vary the velocity and occasionally add or remove a stab hit to avoid mechanical repetition. Write a walking bass line that moves from the root to the 5th on each chord change. Add a shaker playing every 16th note at about -8dB relative to the kick. Apply sidechain compression from the kick to the bass. Add reverb to the chord stabs (medium room, about 20% wet). Export the 8-bar loop and listen back on headphones. The chord movement and bass interaction should create a clear sense of harmonic progression and groove.

🔴 Advanced — Full 4-Minute House Track with Arrangement

Using the loop you built in the intermediate exercise, build a complete DJ-format arrangement: 16-bar intro (kick and bass only), 16-bar build (add stabs at low velocity, open hats), 32-bar main drop (full arrangement), 16-bar breakdown (strip to pad and atmospheric reverb tail), 32-bar second drop (full arrangement + a new counter-melody or vocal chop layer), 16-bar outro (kick and percussion only). Apply automation throughout: filter cutoff sweep building through the intro, reverb wet amount swell at the breakdown, stab velocity ride through the main drop. Mix using a bus compressor on the full drum group, EQ on the bass, and a limiter at -1dBTP on the master. Export as a 24-bit WAV and listen back in full — the arrangement should feel like a complete club track with natural energy movement throughout.

Frequently Asked Questions

What BPM is house music?

House typically runs between 120–130 BPM. Deep house sits at 120–124 BPM. Tech house runs 125–130 BPM. Afrohouse can be slower at 115–125 BPM. Classic Chicago house was typically 120–125 BPM.

What makes a house beat?

The defining element is the four-on-the-floor kick pattern — a kick on every beat at 120–130 BPM. Combined with an open hi-hat on the offbeats and a snare or clap on beats 2 and 4, this creates the driving rhythmic foundation of all house sub-genres.

What chords are used in house music?

House music uses minor and major key progressions with gospel and soul influences. Seventh chords (Am7, Dm7, Fmaj7) add the warmth characteristic of classic house. Common progressions: Am7–Dm7–G7–Cmaj7 for deep house; Am–F–C–G for a more uplifting feel.

What is a chord stab in house music?

A chord stab is a short, rhythmically percussive chord hit — typically a 16th or 8th note — placed on syncopated offbeat positions to create rhythmic momentum and groove. Stabs are the key to making house chords feel energetic rather than static.

What key is house music in?

House is commonly in minor keys for depth (A minor, D minor, F minor) or major keys for uplifting energy (C major, G major, F major). The key choice heavily influences the emotional character of the track.

What DAW do house music producers use?

Ableton Live is the most popular for house, particularly for its Session view and groove tools. Logic Pro is widely used for its MIDI tools and included instruments. FL Studio is also common, especially among producers who work across multiple genres.

What samples are used in house music?

Classic house samples soul, disco, and gospel records. Modern house uses sample packs or original recordings. Sample clearance is legally required for any sampled material in commercial releases.

How long should a house track be?

Club-format tracks run 6–8 minutes with extended intros and outros for DJ mixing. Streaming versions are typically 3–5 minutes. The arrangement should build energy, peak, and resolve with clear structural movements.
