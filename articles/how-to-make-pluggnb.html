How To Make Pluggnb

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 · 24 min read · Verified May 7, 2026
Quick Answer — Updated May 2026

PluggnB combines slow-tempo plugg production (60–85 BPM, sparse 808 bass, atmospheric pads, minimal percussion) with R&B melodic singing and emotional songwriting. Build it with long-sustain tuned 808s, warped pitch-shifted melodic samples, minor key chord progressions with 7th and 9th extensions, lush reverb on every element, and an intimate vocal mix that sits inside the music rather than sitting exposed on top of it.

Updated May 2026

PluggnB sits at one of the most interesting intersections in contemporary music — the cold, hazy minimalism of Atlanta plugg rap colliding with the warmth and melody of R&B songwriting. It shouldn't work on paper. In practice, it produces some of the most emotionally resonant music being made right now.

The genre grew from the SoundCloud underground — producers experimenting with making beats so slow and sparse they barely qualified as rap instrumentals, then pairing them with singers who understood that the space in the beat was the point. Bryson Tiller's early SoundCloud output is the clearest early blueprint. Partynextdoor, 645AR, and a wave of Audiomack artists pushed it further. Now it's an aesthetic that runs through mainstream R&B production whether producers label it pluggnb or not.

This guide breaks down every production element so you can build the sound from the ground up — DAW setup, 808 programming, melodic design, chord theory, percussion, vocal mixing, and final mastering considerations.

PluggnB Sound Architecture at a Glance
  • 808 Bass — Tuned, long sustain, portamento glide (sub foundation)
  • Atmospheric Pads — Minor 7th chords, reverb-heavy, barely audible movement
  • Warped Melodics — Pitch-shifted samples, pluck synth, electric piano or Rhodes
  • Percussion — Sparse: 808 kick, occasional snare, minimal hi-hats
  • Vocals — R&B melodic singing, Auto-Tune, doubling, long reverb

Power comes from subtraction, not addition — every layer serves the space around it.

What Is PluggnB? The Genre DNA

To produce pluggnb well, you first need to understand where it came from. Plugg rap emerged from Atlanta around 2012–2016 through the GBC (Gravity Boys Collective) and DSM underground scenes. It's characterized by extremely slow tempos (60–85 BPM), minimalist production built around heavy 808 bass, warped or pitch-shifted melodic samples, and a monotone, drowsy vocal delivery from rappers. The production aesthetic was about negative space — what you left out mattered more than what you put in.

PluggnB takes that production blueprint and replaces the rap vocal with full R&B melodic singing. The lyrical content shifts from street narratives to love, heartbreak, emotional dependency, and vulnerability. Artists don't just rap softly over plugg beats — they build actual melodic hooks, use conventional song structures (verse, pre-hook, chorus, bridge), and write with the harmonic intent of R&B songwriters who've studied the genre's traditions. The chord vocabulary expands: where a plugg rapper might lock into a two-bar loop, a pluggnb singer demands a progression that breathes and moves.

The result is music that feels simultaneously heavy and intimate, cold and romantic — a contradiction that's precisely the point. The icy production temperature created by those slow BPMs and minimal percussion creates a kind of emotional vulnerability chamber where the vocal can say things that wouldn't land the same way over a busier beat.

Key reference artists: Bryson Tiller (especially early SoundCloud tracks like "Exchange"), Partynextdoor, 645AR, Kali Uchis, SZA (whose production often touches pluggnb territory), and Summer Walker. On the production side: OG Parker, Cardiak, Wheezy, and a generation of anonymous SoundCloud beatmakers who never received mainstream credit for establishing the aesthetic but whose influence is audible everywhere in contemporary R&B.

Understanding this lineage helps you make decisions that feel authentic rather than imitative. When you know why the 808 sustains for four beats instead of two, why the hi-hat pattern is nearly absent, and why the melodic sample sounds slightly wrong on purpose, you can make those choices with intention rather than accident.

Step 1 — DAW Setup and Project Configuration

PluggnB is DAW-agnostic but has a natural home in FL Studio — the piano roll workflow suits the genre's precise 808 bass programming and detailed melodic sequencing. The step sequencer and pattern-based arrangement view encourage the kind of loop-centric thinking that drives pluggnb structure. Ableton Live is popular among producers working in the West Coast and New York R&B spaces, where the session view's improvisational flow helps composers experiment with harmonic movements quickly. Logic Pro appears in more polished, commercially-oriented pluggnb, especially in sessions where live musicians and orchestral samples need to be integrated.

If you're newer to production and deciding which platform to commit to, check out our comparison of FL Studio vs Ableton Live — both are legitimate choices for this genre.

Project settings:

  • BPM: Start at 75 BPM. Valid range: 60–85 BPM. Slower feels heavier and more hypnotic. Faster within the range feels slightly more accessible for streaming. Most successful pluggnb tracks sit between 70–80 BPM.
  • Time signature: 4/4 standard. Don't get experimental here — the genre's emotional power comes from its melodic and harmonic choices, not metric complexity.
  • Sample rate / bit depth: 44.1 kHz or 48 kHz, 24-bit. Use 48 kHz if your vocalist or collaborators work in video environments where 48 kHz is standard.
  • Grid resolution: 1/16 note. At 75 BPM, 16th notes feel slow and deliberate — exactly the right granularity for the genre's syncopated bass and percussion patterns.

Signal routing: Before you place a single note, set up your track routing. Create a group bus for drums/percussion, a group bus for melodics (pads, samples, plucks), a group bus for bass (the 808), and a dedicated vocal chain. You'll be running long reverb tails on nearly everything — busses let you manage this without CPU chaos and keep your mix organized when things get complex in arrangement.

Monitoring: PluggnB's sub-bass 808 content is brutal on headphones and can completely mislead your low-end decisions. If possible, monitor on full-range studio monitors at moderate levels. If you primarily mix in headphones, reference your 808 decisions on multiple playback systems before committing. The 808 that sounds perfect in Sony MDR-7506 headphones may be inaudible on a phone speaker and distorting on a car stereo.

Step 2 — The 808 Bass: The Emotional Core

The 808 is the most important element of any plugg-family production. In pluggnb, it does double duty: it anchors the rhythm and carries the melodic bassline. Every note matters — a wrong 808 note ruins the emotional character of a section instantly. This is not background texture. The 808 is the emotional spine of the track.

Sound Selection

Use a Roland TR-808 bass drum sample (long-sustain version) or synthesize one using a sine wave oscillator in Serum, Vital, or FL Studio's 3xOsc. The 808 should have a punchy transient at the very start of the note — this is the "click" that registers on small speakers where the sub frequency won't reach — and a long, smooth sine wave sustain. No upper harmonics, no distortion at the source. Saturation and distortion get added deliberately in processing when needed, but start clean.

For a synthesized 808 from scratch: set a single sine wave oscillator, pitch it to C1 (or your root note), add a short pitch envelope that drops the pitch about a major third over approximately 20–40ms (this creates the "punch"), then set a long amplitude envelope with fast attack (0ms), zero sustain, and long decay (2–4 seconds). This is the fundamental structure of every 808 you hear in this genre.

For a deeper technical breakdown of building 808 sounds from scratch, see our guide on how to make trap 808s from scratch — the same principles apply directly to pluggnb with longer decay settings.

Tuning — The Non-Negotiable

Tune every 808 note to the musical key of your track. An out-of-tune 808 is the single most common mistake in plugg-family production, and it's immediately obvious to any listener with musical sensitivity — even listeners who can't articulate what's wrong will feel that something is off.

Method: Use a spectrum analyzer or tuner plugin (Waves Tune Real-Time works well in-line; the free MAutoPitch works fine for identification) to identify the fundamental frequency of your 808 sample. Then use the pitch controls in your sampler to transpose it to match your key. In FL Studio's Sampler, you do this in the pitch/time section. In Ableton, use a Simpler or Sampler device.

For melodic basslines that move between notes, you need to pitch each individual 808 hit to match the note it's playing — or use a plugin like the 808 Flex in FL Studio or a MIDI-triggered Simpler patch that automatically transposes the sample to the MIDI note you play. The latter approach is faster and more accurate for complex melodic lines.

Portamento and Slides

Portamento — pitch glide between notes — is essential to the pluggnb 808 sound. When the bassline moves from one note to the next, you want the pitch to slide smoothly rather than jump. The slide time is usually 50–150ms for a natural feel; shorter slides (20–50ms) sound punchier; longer slides (200ms+) sound more dramatic and drawn out.

In FL Studio: use the "slide" note type in the piano roll — right-click a note and select slide. This triggers the pitch glide automatically between consecutive notes. In Ableton: route 808 hits through a synthesizer with portamento enabled and a mono (legato) voice setting, then overlap MIDI notes to trigger the glide.

808 Processing Chain

Plugin Type Purpose Suggested Settings
High-pass filter Remove sub-sub energy below 30–40 Hz HPF at 30–40 Hz, 12 dB/oct
Saturation / harmonic exciter Add upper harmonics so the 808 translates on small speakers Tape or tube saturation, light drive, blend 30–50%
Compressor Tighten the sustain and add punch Ratio 4:1, attack 10–20ms, release 200ms, GR 3–6 dB
EQ Shape the fundamental and remove mud Boost 60–80 Hz (fundamental), cut 200–300 Hz (mud)
Limiter (optional) Control peak transients Ceiling -1 dBTP, only if needed

Layer a short kick drum sample — a tight 808 kick or a punchy acoustic kick sample — on the same hit as your 808 to reinforce the attack transient. This layered transient is what makes the 808 feel powerful even on earbuds and phone speakers where the sub frequency physically cannot reproduce. The kick layer provides the "body" click; the 808 provides the emotional resonance.

808 Rhythm Patterns

In pluggnb, the 808 bassline is melodic — it moves between notes across the bar rather than sitting on a single root. Common approaches:

  • Root-movement pattern: Land on beat 1 with the root note, move to the 5th or b7th on beat 3, return to root. This creates motion without leaving the harmonic comfort zone.
  • Melodic counter-line: Program the 808 as a slow melodic line that partially mirrors or complements the vocal melody. This is the most sophisticated approach — it requires harmonic awareness but produces the most immersive results.
  • Syncopated stab pattern: Hit the 808 on the upbeats and off-beats rather than the downbeats. The space between hits is part of the rhythm.

At 75 BPM, even simple patterns feel dramatic because each note lasts long enough to be perceived as a musical event with a beginning, middle, and end.

Step 3 — Melodics, Pads, and Warped Samples

The harmonic layer of a pluggnb beat is what separates a great track from a mediocre one. The 808 provides the emotional weight; the melodic layer provides the emotional character — the specific shade of longing, melancholy, warmth, or ache that makes a track feel like it's about something specific rather than generically sad.

Chord Progressions

PluggnB uses minor key progressions almost exclusively. The three most common frameworks:

  • i – bVII – bVI: In A minor: Am – G – F. This is the backbone of the genre. It's descending, inevitable-feeling, and provides just enough harmonic movement to support a vocal without distracting from it. Extend the chords: Am7 – Gmaj7 – Fmaj7 for R&B richness.
  • i – iv: Am – Dm. A dark, minimal two-chord vamp. At slow pluggnb tempos, two chords over eight bars feel like a complete harmonic statement. This works best in sections where the vocal melody needs maximum space.
  • i – bVI – bVII – i: Am – F – G – Am. Circular, slightly hopeful on the bVII before returning to the tonic. Good for chorus sections where you want slight emotional lift without leaving the minor tonality.

Voicing is critical. Avoid plain triads — they sound thin at these tempos and clash with the atmospheric production aesthetic. Minimum: add the 7th to every chord. Better: add the 9th. Even better: voice the chord with intervals spread across multiple octaves so it fills the frequency spectrum without sounding cluttered. Stack a minor 7th chord in the low-mid range (root, minor 3rd, 5th, minor 7th) and layer a high, bright inversion on top.

Pad Sound Design

Pads in pluggnb serve an atmospheric function — they're the emotional weather of the track rather than a foreground element. They sit just below the threshold of conscious attention: the listener feels them more than hears them distinctly.

Sound design approach: use a synth with multiple detuned oscillators (slight detune creates natural chorus movement without adding a separate effect). Set a slow attack (200–500ms) and a very long release (1–2 seconds). Add a gentle LFO on filter cutoff at a very slow rate (0.1–0.5 Hz) — this creates organic, breathing movement without obvious wobble. Apply heavy reverb (plate or hall, pre-delay 20–40ms, decay 2.5–4 seconds). The pad should bloom slowly when you press a key and linger long after you release it.

Popular choices: Serum's pad presets as a starting point (heavily modified), Vital's wavetable oscillators, Omnisphere for its more complex atmospheric textures, and Nexus 3 for genre-specific R&B pad presets. The tool matters less than the principle: slow, smooth, reverb-saturated, slightly detuned.

Warped Melodic Samples

The warped sample is the most distinctive sonic signature of plugg-family production. It creates a sense of familiar-but-disorienting familiarity — you recognize the source sound (a vocal snippet, a flute, a harp, a piano chord) but it's been transformed into something dreamlike and slightly uncanny.

Process:

  1. Find a short sample — a vocal phrase, a flute run, a single piano chord, a harp pluck. 1–3 seconds of audio.
  2. Pitch-shift it down 3–7 semitones. This changes the timbre quality, not just the pitch — things sound darker, thicker, more melancholic.
  3. Time-stretch it to 150–300% of its original length. Use a degrading algorithm rather than a transparent one. In FL Studio, use the Pitch Shifter and the "resample" stretch mode rather than the high-quality mode — the artifacts are desirable. In Ableton, use the Complex Pro warp mode, which creates distinctive texture in extreme stretching. The goal is not clean pitch-shifting — it's controlled degradation.
  4. Layer reverb with a long pre-delay (40–80ms) and long decay (3–5 seconds). This pushes the sample further back in the three-dimensional space of the mix.
  5. Add chorus or ensemble modulation — subtle, not obvious. This smears the pitch slightly, creating the "shimmer" characteristic of pluggnb melodics.
  6. Automate the sample's playback starting position or use random offset to create slight timing variations between repetitions. This prevents the loop from feeling mechanical.

You can also use creative pitch shifting techniques beyond simple semitone transposition — try micro-tuning, formant shifting, or layering multiple pitch-shifted versions of the same source at different intervals to create chordal textures from a single sample.

Pluck Synths and Electric Piano

Beyond the warped sample, pluggnb melodics often include a pluck synth — a synth with a fast attack that sounds like a plucked string or pizzicato, but with a sustained, reverb-heavy tail. Settings: fast attack (0–5ms), medium decay (200–500ms), low sustain (20–40%), medium release (300ms). Add a gentle low-pass filter with slight envelope modulation so the brightness "blooms" on the attack and dims during the sustain. Serum and Vital both handle this beautifully with wavetable oscillators.

Electric piano (Rhodes or Wurlitzer-style) adds organic warmth that pure synthesis can't replicate. Layer it under the synth pluck or use it as the primary harmonic element in certain sections. Plugins: Arturia's Wurli V and Piano V, Native Instruments Noire or Una Corda (for a more muted, intimate piano character), Keyscape's Rhodes patches. Keep the Rhodes reverb-heavy and slightly back in the mix — it's a textural element, not a feature.

Step 4 — Percussion: The Art of Restraint

Pluggnb percussion is defined by what it doesn't do. Every instinct you have from working in hip-hop, trap, or R&B production toward adding more — more hi-hat velocity variation, more snare fills, more percussion layers — needs to be consciously resisted here. The slow tempo means every percussion hit is a significant rhythmic event. The space between hits is the groove.

Kick Pattern

Use an 808 kick drum or a similar deep, round kick sample. The kick hits on beat 1 almost always. Beat 3 may or may not have a kick — in many pluggnb tracks, beat 3 is empty kick-wise, leaving the 808 sustain from beat 1 or 2 to carry the low-end through the third beat. This creates the characteristic "floating" sensation of the genre.

The kick should be tuned (yes, even the kick) — tune it to approximately the same root note as your track, or to the root's octave. An 808 kick and an 808 bass that share a tuning create a subharmonic relationship that sounds intentional and musical.

Snare / Clap

Snare hits on beat 2 and beat 4 — standard backbeat placement. But the character matters: use a snare that has weight without being sharp. A snappy, bright trap snare doesn't fit; a deeper, slightly muted snare or a sampled clap with heavy reverb fits well. Some pluggnb tracks remove the snare almost entirely and let the hi-hats imply the backbeat loosely.

Layer a room reverb or hall reverb on the snare — pre-delay 10–20ms, decay 600ms–1s. Don't use a short, tight room. The reverb tail should be audible and should blend into the atmospheric reverb of the rest of the track, creating cohesion.

Hi-Hats

This is where pluggnb differs most dramatically from trap. Trap hi-hats are constant, often 32nd-note rolls and triplet patterns — they drive the energy and create momentum. PluggnB hi-hats are sparse: typically 8th-note or 16th-note closed hi-hats on selected beats, with occasional open hi-hat accents. Long stretches with no hi-hat at all are common.

When hi-hats do appear, keep velocity variation subtle (±10–20%) and avoid rolls or triplet bursts. The hi-hat in pluggnb marks time gently rather than driving it aggressively. Think of the hi-hat as the second hand of a slow clock — present, minimal, functional.

Additional Percussion

Occasional shaker or tambourine hits, lightly reverb'd, add texture without rhythm. Some producers add a very quiet sub-kick or floor tom sample on beat 3 to provide low-mid presence without an audible kick hit. Organic percussion samples (hand drum, bongo) can add warmth and humanity when used extremely sparingly — one or two hits per bar at low volume.

For more on sculpting drum sounds in a mix context, see our guide on how to mix drums.

Step 5 — Song Structure and Arrangement

PluggnB is not a beat genre — it's a song genre. Unlike pure plugg rap where a two-bar loop can carry an entire track, pluggnb demands songwriting structure because the vocal performance requires it. A singer needs a verse to establish context, a pre-hook to build tension, and a chorus where the emotional climax arrives. The beat must support that arc.

Standard PluggnB Song Structure

  • Intro (8–16 bars): Minimal — often just the pad and 808 bass, no percussion. Let the atmosphere establish itself before the rhythm enters. Some intros feature a distant vocal sample or reversed atmospheric sound.
  • Verse (16 bars): Add percussion. Keep melodics sparse — don't stack all your layers here. Leave room for the vocal to breathe. The verse should feel slightly sparse so the chorus feels like an arrival.
  • Pre-Hook (8 bars): Subtle build — add a melodic element you've been holding back. Strip the percussion down to just kick and 808 for a momentary "breakdown" feeling before the chorus hits.
  • Chorus (16 bars): Your fullest arrangement — all melodic layers, full percussion, 808 at its most prominent. Add slight reverb automation to increase the depth of field on key vocal lines.
  • Bridge / Outro (8–16 bars): Strip back to intro-level sparseness. Let the track "breathe out." Some pluggnb tracks end abruptly; others fade out slowly over a sustained pad chord.

Arrangement Automation

Use automation to create movement within static sections rather than changing elements in and out dramatically. Automate: reverb send amount (increase in chorus for depth), pad filter cutoff (slowly open during verse build), 808 volume (slight swell into chorus), and hi-hat velocity (gradual increase approaching the chorus). These micro-movements prevent the track from feeling frozen at slow tempos.

Understanding how to arrange a song for emotional impact is a skill that directly transfers to pluggnb — the slow tempo makes every structural decision more audible and consequential.

Step 6 — Vocal Production and Mixing

The vocal is the defining element of pluggnb — it's what distinguishes the genre from instrumental plugg. Getting the vocal right requires both technical and aesthetic decisions that work together to create the characteristic "inside the music" vocal feel.

Recording Setup

Record in as acoustically treated a space as possible. PluggnB vocals have long reverb tails — any room reflections in the dry recording will compound with the artificial reverb in your processing chain and create a muddy, uncontrolled sound. Record as dry as possible so that every element of the final spatial character is deliberate.

Microphone: A large-diaphragm condenser captures the vocal intimacy the genre requires. Suitable options include the Audio-Technica AT2020 at entry level, the Rode NT1 for a step up, or higher-end options like the AKG C414 for professional sessions. The microphone character matters: PluggnB vocals generally benefit from a slightly warm, smooth top end rather than a hyped, bright sound. For more on microphone selection, see our guide to the best microphones for home studio recording.

Auto-Tune Usage

Auto-Tune in pluggnb is not optional — it's part of the aesthetic. But it's used differently from trap rap's hard-tuned, robotic effect. In pluggnb, Auto-Tune runs at a medium retune speed (15–25 in Auto-Tune Pro, or a similar mid-range setting) that corrects pitch while preserving the natural slur and slide of a singing voice. The result is a slightly "warbled" tuned quality that sounds dreamlike rather than mechanical.

Settings: Set the scale to match your track's key (or chromatic if the vocal melody uses passing tones outside the scale). Retune speed: 15–25 (not 0 for hard tune, not 100 for invisible). Humanize: 20–30. Flex Tune: on at 40–60% if available. The Auto-Tune should feel like a subtle haze on the vocal, not an obvious effect.

Alternatively, Melodyne can be used for pitch correction with a different character — more organic, less "tuned" in the electronic sense. Some producers run both: Melodyne for detailed correction, then a light Auto-Tune for the aesthetic warble character. For a full comparison of these tools, see Auto-Tune vs Melodyne.

Vocal Processing Chain

The pluggnb vocal chain is built around creating intimacy and depth simultaneously — a paradox that requires careful balancing:

  1. High-pass filter: HPF at 80–100 Hz to remove sub-frequency rumble and handling noise without thinning the vocal character.
  2. De-esser: Control harsh sibilance before compression so the compressor doesn't pump on S and T consonants. Set the frequency to where your vocalist's sibilance peaks (typically 5–9 kHz), threshold gentle.
  3. Compressor: Gentle compression — ratio 2.5:1 to 4:1, attack 5–10ms, release 60–100ms, gain reduction 3–6 dB. You want the vocal to feel controlled but not squashed. The natural dynamics of the performance should remain audible. A VCA-style compressor (SSL channel, FabFilter Pro-C 2 in Clean mode) works well here.
  4. EQ (corrective): Cut problem frequencies — nasal buildup around 400–600 Hz, proximity effect buildup below 200 Hz if using a condenser close-mic'd. Use a high-quality EQ like FabFilter Pro-Q series for surgical work.
  5. Auto-Tune / Melodyne: Applied post-corrective EQ as described above.
  6. EQ (creative): A gentle high shelf boost at 10–12 kHz adds air and presence without harshness. A subtle boost around 2–3 kHz adds definition and cut-through.
  7. Short room reverb (pre-send): A very short room reverb (0.3–0.5s decay) applied directly on the channel at low mix percentage (10–15%) adds the sense that the vocal was recorded in a physical space rather than being completely dry.

Reverb and Spatial Processing

This is where pluggnb vocal production diverges most from conventional R&B mixing. The reverb is generous, visible, and integral — not something you apologize for or hide. The vocal sits inside a reverb space rather than sitting dry in front of the speakers.

Set up a reverb send: route the vocal channel to a reverb aux/return track. Reverb type: plate or hall (not room — room is too close and natural for this aesthetic). Settings: pre-delay 25–45ms (this keeps the direct vocal slightly separated from the reverb so it doesn't wash out), decay 2–4 seconds, high-frequency damping applied moderately (so the reverb tail isn't too bright). Send level: approximately -6 to -3 dB — this is a lot of reverb by conventional standards. That's the point.

Add a second, smaller reverb with a shorter decay (0.8–1.2s) for a closer ambience that glues the voice into the mix. Layer both reverbs for a complex, three-dimensional spatial quality.

For detailed reverb technique applicable across this genre, see our guide on how to use reverb on vocals.

Vocal Doubling and Harmonies

Doubling in pluggnb is subtle — not the wide, thick double-tracking of pop production. Record the vocal line twice and pan one slightly left (-15 to -25) and one slightly right (+15 to +25). Keep both passes Auto-Tuned so they sit in pitch together. The width this creates is gentle rather than dramatic, adding presence without creating the wide stereo image that would feel too polished for the genre's intimate character.

Harmonies — typically a minor third or a 5th above the lead — appear selectively on chorus hook lines. Treat harmonies with the same reverb depth as the lead vocal. In some pluggnb productions, harmonies are pitch-shifted from the lead rather than separately recorded, contributing to the blurred, ethereal quality of the sound.

Vocal Arrangement — Placement and Space

At 75 BPM, the natural breath between vocal phrases is longer than in faster genres. Don't fill every silence. The silences between phrases carry emotional weight — they're part of the performance, not dead air to cover with additional musical elements. Let the reverb tails from each phrase ring out naturally before the next line begins. This creates the sense of a performer singing with emotional weight, choosing each word carefully.

Step 7 — Mixing, Referencing, and Mastering Preparation

PluggnB's minimalism creates a deceptive mixing challenge. Fewer elements means every element is audible — there's no busy arrangement to hide poor decisions. The 808 bass, the pad, the warped sample, and the vocal must work together perfectly because none of them can hide behind the others.

Low-End Management

Low-end is the most critical area. The 808, the pad's lower voicings, and any kick drum all occupy frequencies below 200 Hz. Without management, this region becomes muddy and undefined. Approach: side-chain the pad to duck slightly when the 808 hits (a subtle 3–6 dB duck, not the pumping effect of EDM side-chain). High-pass the pad at 80–120 Hz to give the 808 exclusive ownership of the sub frequencies. The 808 should be the only element with significant energy below 80 Hz.

Check your low-end decisions in mono — collapse the stereo field and verify that the 808 fundamentals remain clear and the low-end doesn't become a single indistinct rumble. For comprehensive low-end mixing strategies, see our guide on how to mix bass.

Mid-Range Balance

The vocal, warped melodic, pluck synth, and pad all compete in the mid-range (300 Hz – 3 kHz). Use narrow cuts (rather than broad boosts) to carve space. Find the frequency where each element is most dominant and cut that same frequency gently from every other competing element. This technique — sometimes called "frequency parking" — keeps elements from masking each other without dramatically changing their character.

Stereo Field

PluggnB uses a wide stereo field in the atmospheric elements — pads and reverb returns spread wide. The 808 bass and kick should be mono or near-mono (mono below 100 Hz is standard practice). The vocal lead sits in center. Doubled vocals spread slightly left and right. Warped melodic samples can be spread wide in the stereo field to create the sense of being surrounded by the atmosphere of the track.

Loudness and Dynamics

PluggnB mixes should be dynamic — not compressed into the loudness war territory of pop and EDM. The genre's emotional impact depends on the quiet sections sounding genuinely quiet and the chorus sections having genuine weight and volume. Aim for an integrated loudness around -14 to -12 LUFS for streaming platforms. The 808 will create significant true peak values — use a true peak limiter at -1 dBTP on the master bus to protect against clipping on encode.

Mastering approach: minimal processing. A gentle low shelf boost (+1–2 dB at 60 Hz) to enhance the 808's presence, a subtle air boost (+1 dB at 14–16 kHz) for openness, and a transparent limiter. PluggnB doesn't want a heavily processed master — the atmospheric character comes from the mix, not the master.

Reference Tracks

Reference against known pluggnb and plugg-adjacent tracks throughout your mixing process: Bryson Tiller's "Exchange" and "Don't" (early SoundCloud mixes as well as the official releases), Partynextdoor's self-titled project, Summer Walker's early EPs, and 645AR's catalog. Import a reference track into your session and A/B your mix against it at matched loudness levels every 20–30 minutes of mixing. The reference will immediately reveal whether your low-end is too heavy, your vocal is too far back, or your reverb is too dense.

Practical Exercises

Beginner Exercise

Build Your First PluggnB Loop

Set your DAW to 75 BPM, place an 808 bass hit on beats 1 and 2.5 of a single bar, and tune the 808 to C. Add a simple Am7 pad chord held across the entire bar with heavy reverb (3-second decay). Listen to this two-element loop for five minutes and pay attention to how much music those two elements create — this is the foundation of pluggnb minimalism.

Intermediate Exercise

Warp a Sample into a PluggnB Melodic

Find any short vocal or instrument sample (1–2 seconds), pitch-shift it down 5 semitones using a degrading algorithm, then time-stretch it to 200% of its original length. Apply a reverb with a 4-second decay and a 50ms pre-delay, then loop it over an Am – G – F chord progression at 75 BPM. Adjust the pitch-shift amount and stretch ratio until the sample sounds familiar but disorienting — that uncanny quality is the target.

Record or obtain a vocal performance over a pluggnb instrumental (minimum 16-bar verse and 8-bar chorus). Build a complete vocal chain — HPF, de-esser, 4:1 compression, corrective EQ, Auto-Tune at retune speed 20, creative EQ, short room reverb on-channel, and a long plate reverb send with 3-second decay. Then arrange the instrumental so the verse has one fewer melodic layer than the chorus, and automate the reverb send to increase by 3 dB entering the chorus. Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ What is pluggnb?
PluggnB is a hybrid genre fusing plugg rap production — minimal, atmospheric, bass-heavy beats with sparse percussion — with R&B melodic singing and emotional songwriting. Key characteristics include slow tempos (60–85 BPM), long-sustain 808 bass, warped melodic samples, lush reverb-heavy pads, and intimate vocal production.
FAQ What BPM is pluggnb?
PluggnB typically runs at 60–85 BPM, with most tracks sitting at 70–80 BPM. This very slow tempo creates the heavy, drifting atmosphere the genre is known for. Some pluggnb crosses into 85–95 BPM mid-tempo territory, but the core sound is distinctly slow.
FAQ What is the difference between plugg and pluggnb?
Plugg (plug rap) is an Atlanta-born rap subgenre with minimalist, bass-heavy beats and laid-back rap vocal delivery. PluggnB takes the same production template — slow BPM, 808 bass, warped melodics — but pairs it with full R&B melodic singing instead of rapping. The production is similar; the vocal approach and songwriting intent are completely different.
FAQ What synths are used in pluggnb?
PluggnB uses warped pitch-shifted samples (vocal chops, flute, harp, or piano processed heavily), pluck synths (Serum or Vital with short attack and slow release), lush pads (atmospheric chords held long), electric piano or Rhodes for harmonic texture, and subtle ambient samples layered underneath. Nexus 3 and Omnisphere are popular choices.
FAQ How do you make pluggnb 808s?
PluggnB 808s are long-sustain sine wave bass notes from a TR-808 sample or synthesized with a sine wave in Serum or FL Studio. Key settings: long decay (1–4 seconds), pitch tuned to the track's key, and portamento (pitch glide) for smooth slides between notes. Layer a short kick drum sample on the attack for definition on small speakers.
FAQ What chord progressions work in pluggnb?
PluggnB uses minor key progressions almost exclusively. Most common: i – bVII – bVI (Am – G – F), i – iv (Am – Dm for dark vamps), and i – bVI – bVII – i. Chord voicings should include minor 7ths and 9ths for R&B richness. Avoid plain triads — they sound thin at these tempos.
FAQ Who are the key artists in pluggnb?
Key pluggnb-influencing artists include Bryson Tiller (widely credited as an early pioneer of the fusion), Partynextdoor, 645AR, Kali Uchis, SZA, and Summer Walker. Producers like OG Parker, Cardiak, and Wheezy have created pluggnb-adjacent production.
FAQ How do you mix vocals for pluggnb?
PluggnB vocal mixing uses generous reverb (long plate, 2–4s decay), deliberate Auto-Tune at medium retune speed for the dreamy warble aesthetic, subtle vocal doubling for width, and gentle compression. The vocal sits inside the mix rather than on top — it blends with the atmospheric elements rather than sitting exposed above them.
