How to Make PluggnB: Complete Production Guide

The genre fusing plugg rap's atmospheric beats with R&B's emotional songwriting — every element broken down so you can produce it from scratch.

Quick Answer: PluggnB combines slow-tempo plugg production (60–85 BPM, sparse 808 bass, atmospheric pads, minimal percussion) with R&B melodic singing and emotional songwriting. Key elements: long-sustain tuned 808s, warped pitch-shifted melodic samples, minor key chord progressions with 7th extensions, lush reverb throughout, and an intimate vocal mix that sits inside the music rather than on top of it.

PluggnB sits at one of the most interesting intersections in contemporary music — the cold, hazy minimalism of Atlanta plugg rap colliding with the warmth and melody of R&B songwriting. It shouldn't work on paper. In practice, it produces some of the most emotionally resonant music being made right now.

The genre grew from the SoundCloud underground — producers experimenting with making beats so slow and sparse they barely qualified as rap instrumentals, then pairing them with singers who understood that the space in the beat was the point. Bryson Tiller's early SoundCloud output is the clearest early blueprint. Partynextdoor, 645AR, and a wave of Audiomack artists pushed it further. Now it's an aesthetic that runs through mainstream R&B production whether producers label it pluggnb or not.

This guide breaks down every production element so you can build the sound from the ground up.

What Is PluggnB? The Genre DNA

To produce pluggnb, you first need to understand plugg. Plugg rap emerged from Atlanta around 2012–2016 through the GBC (Gravity Boys Collective) and DSM underground scenes. It's characterized by extremely slow tempos (60–85 BPM), minimalist production with heavy 808 bass, warped or pitch-shifted melodic samples, and a monotone, drowsy vocal delivery from rappers.

PluggnB takes that production blueprint and replaces the rap vocal with full R&B melodic singing. The lyrical content shifts from street narratives to love, heartbreak, emotional dependency, and vulnerability. Artists don't just rap softly over plugg beats — they build actual melodic hooks, use conventional song structures (verse, pre-hook, chorus), and write with the harmonic intent of R&B songwriters.

The result is music that feels simultaneously heavy and intimate, cold and romantic — a contradiction that's exactly the point.

Key reference artists: Bryson Tiller (especially early SoundCloud tracks like "Exchange"), Partynextdoor, 645AR, Kali Uchis, SZA (production often touches pluggnb territory), and Summer Walker. Producers: OG Parker, Cardiak, Wheezy, and a generation of anonymous SoundCloud beatmakers who never got mainstream credit for establishing the sound.

Step 1 — DAW Setup

PluggnB is DAW-agnostic but has a natural home in FL Studio — the piano roll workflow suits the genre's precise 808 bass programming and detailed melodic sequencing. Ableton Live is popular among producers working in the West Coast and New York R&B spaces. Logic Pro appears in more polished, commercially-oriented pluggnb.

  • BPM: Start at 75 BPM. Valid range: 60–85 BPM. Slower feels heavier and more hypnotic. Faster within the range feels slightly more accessible for streaming.
  • Time signature: 4/4 standard.
  • Sample rate / bit depth: 44.1 kHz or 48 kHz, 24-bit.
  • Grid resolution: 1/16 note. At 75 BPM, 16th notes feel slow and deliberate — exactly the right granularity for the genre's syncopated bass and percussion patterns.

Step 2 — The 808 Bass: The Emotional Core

The 808 is the most important element of any plugg-family production. In pluggnb, it does double duty: it anchors the rhythm and carries the melodic bassline. Every note matters — a wrong 808 note ruins the emotional character of a section instantly.

Sound Selection

Use a Roland TR-808 bass drum sample (long-sustain version) or synthesize one using a sine wave oscillator in Serum, Vital, or FL Studio's 3xOsc. The 808 should have a punchy transient at the very start of the note and a long, smooth sine wave sustain. No upper harmonics, no distortion at the source — that gets added in processing.

Tuning

Tune every 808 note to the musical key of your track. An out-of-tune 808 is the single most common mistake in plugg-family production. Use a tuner plugin or spectrum analyzer to identify the fundamental frequency of your 808 sample, then use the pitch controls to match it to the root note of your key. At 75 BPM in A minor, your root 808 note should be A — every other note in the bassline should also be a note from the A natural minor or A Dorian scale.

Decay and Portamento

Set a long decay: 1.5–3 seconds at 75 BPM. The sustained tail is what creates the floating, heavy low-end that defines the genre — don't cut it short. Enable portamento (pitch glide) at 60–120ms so that when the 808 moves between notes it slides smoothly rather than jumping. That slide is the signature 808 technique of the plugg sound.

Layering the Kick

Place a short, punchy kick drum sample on top of every 808 hit. The kick provides the transient "click" that makes the 808 audible on systems without strong sub-bass. Without this layer the 808 disappears entirely on laptop speakers and earbuds. The kick sits at 60–100Hz; the 808 sustain sits below 80Hz. They share space harmonically but serve different perceptual functions.

Step 3 — Warped Melodics: The Signature Texture

The melodic elements of pluggnb are what separate it from standard R&B production. Rather than clean piano or standard synth pads, pluggnb uses heavily processed samples and synths that sound familiar but disorienting — instruments processed beyond their natural state into something new.

How to Warp a Sample

  1. Choose source material: Short recordings of flute, harp, choir, acoustic piano, or even vocal phrases. Vinyl records and Splice samples both work. The more "organic" the source, the more interesting the warp result.
  2. Pitch-shift down: Drop it 3–7 semitones. Lower pitch creates a darker, heavier character. Extreme shifts (an octave or more) produce alien textures for background use.
  3. Time-stretch aggressively: Stretch it to 150–300% of its original length. The stretching artifacts — the smearing and wobbling of the transients — create the characteristic "warped" texture of pluggnb melodics. In FL Studio use the Time Stretch tool in the channel sampler. In Ableton, use Complex Pro warp mode. In Logic, use Flex Pitch or the Speed Fade.
  4. Apply long reverb: Add a large hall reverb (2–4s decay). The reverb should be so prominent that the sample "bleeds" into the surrounding silence — the reverb tail is as important as the note itself.
  5. Add modulation: Chorus (subtle, ±8–12 cents, slow LFO) or a slow pitch vibrato makes the warped sample feel alive and slightly unstable — which is exactly the emotional quality the genre needs.

Pluck Synths

Short-attack pluck synths provide punctuated melodic movement without the heaviness of pads. In Serum or Vital: single oscillator (sine or triangle wave), fast attack (0ms), decay 200–600ms, zero sustain, zero release. Add a second oscillator slightly detuned (±5 cents) for warmth. Modulate the filter cutoff via envelope — closes quickly after the attack, producing a muted but present pluck. Layer reverb and a short ping-pong delay. Program the pluck melody in the minor scale of your key with syncopated rhythms that complement rather than double the 808 pattern. [Serum by Xfer Records]

Electric Piano / Rhodes

A warm, slightly overdriven Rhodes-style electric piano adds jazz-influenced harmonic character. Use Arturia Rhodes V, Spectrasonics Keyscape, or Logic's built-in electric piano. Apply light drive, chorus, and a 1.5–2s reverb. Play minor 7th chord voicings with the sustain pedal held — let the notes blur together slightly. This creates a lush, sustained harmonic bed that fills the space between 808 hits. [Spectrasonics Keyscape]

Step 4 — Chord Progressions and Harmony

PluggnB is almost exclusively minor key. The harmonic language is R&B-informed — lush chord extensions, slow movement — but simplified to fit the sparse, repetitive structure of plugg production. You're not writing jazz changes. You're choosing 2–4 chords and letting them breathe for 4–8 bars each.

Most Common PluggnB Progressions

  • i – bVII – bVI (e.g. Am – G – F) — the most used; deeply familiar emotional pull, works in almost any context
  • i – iv (Am – Dm) — dark, hypnotic two-chord vamp; used for extended sections and breakdowns
  • i – bVI – bVII – i (Am – F – G – Am) — creates gentle forward motion and resolution
  • i – bIII – bVII – iv — darker, less obvious movement; works well for bridges and emotional climaxes

Always voice your chords with 7th and 9th extensions. Am7 instead of Am. Fmaj9 instead of F. Dm9 instead of Dm. Plain triads sound thin and unfinished at 75 BPM — the extensions fill the harmonic space and add R&B sophistication.

Key Selection

Work in minor keys only for core pluggnb: A minor, D minor, E minor, G minor, C minor are all common. Choose based on the vocalist's comfortable range — the melody needs room to move without straining. If writing for a male artist: D minor or E minor usually sits well. For a female artist: A minor or C minor work well as starting points.

Step 5 — Percussion: Sparse and Deliberate

PluggnB percussion is minimal by design. The goal is not a wall of drums — it's a few precise elements that anchor the 808 groove without cluttering the space. Every hit you add should earn its place. Every hit you remove should leave the track feeling more powerful, not less complete.

Core Elements

  • 808 kick — already covered above. This is the primary rhythmic anchor. Usually on beats 1 and 3, sometimes just beat 1.
  • Snare / clap — on beat 3 in many pluggnb tracks (not the standard 2 and 4). Sometimes absent entirely for full sections. When present: punchy, short, not dominant. A clap sample often works better than a snare — more intimate, less aggressive.
  • Hi-hats — extremely sparse. Try placing hi-hats only on specific off-beats rather than every 16th note. An open hi-hat on the "and" of beat 2 creates forward momentum without density. Rolling hi-hat patterns (32nd notes) are used in modern, more commercial-leaning pluggnb but feel out of place in the purist sound.
  • Finger snap — a single-velocity finger snap placed at specific beats adds intimacy and handmade character. It sounds like the producer recorded it in the room, which is exactly the feeling you want.
  • Vinyl crackle — a low-level continuous vinyl noise loop underneath everything adds warmth and lo-fi texture. Mix it at -25 to -30dB — barely audible, but its absence would feel sterile.

The golden rule of pluggnb percussion: if removing a hit makes the track feel more open and heavy without losing the groove, remove it. The genre's power comes from what it withholds.

Step 6 — Atmospheric Pads and Background Texture

Below the melodics and above the 808, pluggnb has a deep atmospheric layer that creates the "misty" feeling the genre is known for. These elements should be practically inaudible on their own — but the track sounds hollow and wrong without them.

  • String pads — sustained strings playing the chord progression very slowly and quietly. Spitfire LABS Strings or any orchestral strings library works. Apply 3–4s reverb. Mix at -18 to -22dB relative to the lead vocal.
  • Ambient vocal samples — a softly layered "ah" or "ooh" vocal pad sample beneath everything. Not identifiable as a vocal, just a warm human-sounding texture. Many pluggnb producers use Omnisphere's vocal pad presets for this.
  • Sub-bass drone — a very quiet, continuous sine wave on the root note of the key. Below 50Hz, mixed at -30dB. Creates subliminal weight and harmonic anchoring.
  • Filtered noise — narrow-band white noise filtered down to 200–500Hz at extremely low levels. Adds a room-like presence without actual reverb artifacts.

Step 7 — Vocal Production

The vocal is what makes pluggnb different from plugg rap — and how you handle it determines whether the track succeeds or fails. The pluggnb vocal aesthetic is intimate, slightly processed, and intentionally imperfect. It sits inside the music rather than on top of it.

Recording

Record in a dry, treated space — closet, reflection filter, or treated room. You will add reverb intentionally in the mix. A dry recording gives you full control over the spatial character. Mic at 6–8 inches, slightly off-axis to reduce plosives. Capture breathy, quiet takes rather than pushing the voice. The genre rewards vulnerability over power. Record 3–5 takes of each section and comp from the most emotionally honest moments — not the most technically perfect ones.

Pitch Correction

Auto-Tune is used in pluggnb as an aesthetic tool, not just a correction tool. A medium retune speed (around 20–30 in Auto-Tune, or equivalent in Melodyne's polyphonic mode) creates a slightly artificial, dreamy quality where the voice bends toward pitches rather than snapping to them instantly. More extreme retune speeds (5–10) produce the warbling robot sound used in plugg rap — usable sparingly in pluggnb for effect. Don't use "invisible" correction (retune speed 127 or Melodyne's most natural setting) — some visible Auto-Tune character is part of the genre's identity. [Auto-Tune by Antares]

Doubling and Width

Layer the lead vocal with a "dub" — a second vocal recording or a pitch-shifted copy at ±5–10 cents, panned 20–30% left and right. This adds width and the characteristic "layered" sound of pluggnb without sounding explicitly doubled. Blend the dub at -6 to -10dB below the lead. Some producers also add a whisper vocal double — re-recording the same melody at a much quieter, more intimate level — and blending it beneath the main take at -12 to -15dB.

Reverb

PluggnB uses significantly more reverb on vocals than standard pop. Use a large plate or hall reverb with 2–4s decay and set it at a higher wet level than you're used to — the vocal should breathe in a reverberant space, not sit dry and exposed. The reverb pre-delay (30–60ms) prevents the reverb from blurring the initial consonants. Use a high-pass filter on the reverb return at 200Hz to keep the low-mids from getting muddy. [Valhalla Room Reverb]

Compression

Keep compression gentle on the vocal. A 2:1 ratio with a slow attack (15–30ms) preserves the natural dynamic shape of the performance — the quiet parts stay quiet, the emotional peaks breathe through. PluggnB vocals are more dynamic than pop vocals. Respect that dynamic range. An over-compressed vocal kills the intimacy that defines the genre.

EQ

High-pass at 80–100Hz. Add warmth at 200–250Hz if the vocal sounds thin. Cut any harsh resonances in the 2–4kHz range — pluggnb vocals should be warm and smooth, not aggressive. A gentle high-shelf boost at 10–12kHz adds air without brittleness.

Placing the Vocal in the Mix

This is the most important conceptual difference between pluggnb and pop vocal mixing. In pop, the vocal sits clearly on top of the production — everything else is background. In pluggnb, the vocal sits inside the music. It's still the most prominent element, but it blends with the reverb, the warped melodics, and the atmospheric pads into a unified sonic cloud. Think of the vocal as one part of a texture, not a separate performance placed over a backing track.

Step 8 — Mixing PluggnB

Low End Management

High-pass everything except the 808 and kick at 100–200Hz. The 808 owns the low end completely — no competition allowed. Bass guitars, piano chord voicings, and pad instruments all need their low frequencies removed to give the 808 room to breathe and sustain cleanly.

Shared Reverb Sends

Rather than individual reverbs on each channel, create a shared reverb send bus — a large hall or plate at 3–4s decay — and route all melodic instruments and the vocal into it. This creates a unified sense of space where everything exists in the same room. PluggnB's coherence comes from this shared spatial glue. If every element has its own reverb, the mix sounds chaotic and disconnected at slow tempos.

Stereo Field

Keep the 808 and vocal center-mono. Spread the pads and warped melodics wide. Keep hi-hats and percussion narrow-to-center. This produces the genre's characteristic feeling: a heavy, centered low end and vocal, surrounded by a lush wide atmospheric halo.

Master Bus

Light glue compression (SSL G-Bus style, 2:1, slow attack, 100–200ms release). Subtle tape saturation (IK Tape, Slate Virtual Tape Machines, or UAD Ampex) for warmth without hardness. No brick-wall limiting — aim for -11 to -10 LUFS integrated. PluggnB's dynamics and the weight of the 808 depend on headroom. An over-limited pluggnb mix loses the physical impact that makes the genre feel the way it does on good speakers.

Practical Exercises

🟢 Beginner — Deep Listening Analysis

Choose one pluggnb reference track (Bryson Tiller's "Exchange," PartyNextDoor's "Not Nice," or any 645AR release). Listen five times with a specific focus each pass: (1) Count the BPM using a tap tempo tool. (2) Identify every instrument you can hear. (3) Notice what's absent — what instruments would you expect from R&B that aren't here? (4) Track when the 808 moves to different pitches. (5) Count how many hi-hat hits occur in a single bar. Write down your full analysis. This exercise builds genre vocabulary faster than any tutorial can.

🟡 Intermediate — Build a Full 8-Bar Instrumental

Set your DAW to 75 BPM in A minor. Build in this order: (1) Program a tuned 808 bassline — 5–6 notes with portamento, all in A natural minor. (2) Layer a short kick sample on every 808 hit. (3) Create a warped pluck melody — take any short sample, pitch it down 4 semitones, stretch it to 200%, add reverb. (4) Add an atmospheric pad playing Am7 – G – Fmaj9 in slow sustained hits. (5) Place 3–4 sparse hi-hat hits per bar (not on every 16th note). (6) Add vinyl crackle at -28dB underneath everything. Let it loop for 2 minutes. The test: does it feel like it's moving even while being highly repetitive? If yes, the groove is working correctly.

🔴 Advanced — Produce a Complete PluggnB Song

Collaborate with a vocalist or record yourself. Choose A minor, 72 BPM. Write a complete song with verse, pre-hook, hook, verse 2, hook, bridge, and final hook. Record the vocal dry and process it with deliberate Auto-Tune (retune speed ~25 in Auto-Tune Pro), 3s plate reverb, and subtle doubling. Build the full production around the vocal — let the singer's melody guide your 808 movement and chord choices, not the other way around. Mix with the vocal inside the music. Master to -11 LUFS. Compare your final mix to two commercial pluggnb references. Write a 150-word honest analysis of the gaps. This comparison — done honestly — will teach you more than any other exercise in this guide.

Common Mistakes When Making PluggnB

  • 808 out of tune — the most common and most damaging mistake. Every 808 note must be tuned to your key. Check with a tuner plugin on every new 808 sample you use.
  • Too fast — above 90 BPM it stops feeling like pluggnb and becomes regular R&B or trap. Resist the urge to speed it up to "make it more energetic."
  • Too much percussion — adding hi-hats on every 16th note removes the atmospheric weight the genre depends on. Less is more — always.
  • Vocal too exposed — mixing the vocal like a pop record (loud, dry, on top of everything) removes the intimate blend that makes pluggnb feel the way it does. Ease it back into the reverb field.
  • Not enough reverb globally — the genre lives in reverberant space. A dry mix sounds clinical and wrong. If your reverb sends feel like "too much," you're probably close to right.
  • Generic chord voicings — plain major or minor triads sound thin at these tempos. Always add 7ths and 9ths. Voicing matters enormously when chords sustain for 4–8 bars.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is pluggnb?

PluggnB is a hybrid genre fusing plugg rap's atmospheric, bass-heavy production with R&B melodic singing. Key characteristics: 60–85 BPM, long-sustain tuned 808 bass, warped melodic samples, minimal percussion, lush reverb, and intimate vocal production. Think of it as R&B songwriting over plugg instrumentals.

What BPM is pluggnb?

60–85 BPM, with most tracks at 70–80 BPM. This extremely slow tempo creates the heavy, drifting atmosphere that defines the genre. Above 90 BPM it starts to lose its pluggnb character.

What chord progressions work in pluggnb?

Minor key progressions with 7th and 9th extensions. Most common: i – bVII – bVI (Am – G – F), i – iv (Am – Dm), and i – bVI – bVII – i. Plain triads sound thin — always add extensions.

How do you make pluggnb 808s?

Long-sustain sine wave bass notes tuned to your key, with portamento glide between notes (60–120ms). Layer a short kick on every 808 hit for definition on small speakers. Apply compression to sustain consistency and light saturation for harmonic presence.

What is the difference between plugg and pluggnb?

Plugg uses rap vocal delivery over atmospheric, slow-tempo beats. PluggnB uses the same production template but with full R&B melodic singing, conventional song structure, and romantic/emotional lyrical content. The production is similar; the vocal approach and intent are completely different.

How do you mix pluggnb vocals?

Deliberate Auto-Tune at medium retune speed (~20–30), generous long-plate reverb (2–4s), subtle vocal doubling for width, gentle compression (2:1 slow attack), and placement inside the mix rather than on top of it. The vocal blends with the atmospheric elements — it doesn't sit exposed above them.

Who are the key artists in pluggnb?

Bryson Tiller (early SoundCloud work), Partynextdoor, 645AR, Kali Uchis, SZA, and Summer Walker have all worked in pluggnb territory. The genre has a large underground contingent on SoundCloud and Audiomack that influenced these mainstream artists.

What DAW do pluggnb producers use?

FL Studio is most common, especially among Atlanta-connected producers. Ableton Live is used by many producers working in New York and West Coast R&B spaces. Logic Pro appears in more polished, commercial-leaning pluggnb. The genre works in any DAW.

How do you make warped melodics for pluggnb?

Take any short sample, pitch-shift it down 3–7 semitones, time-stretch it to 150–300% of its original length, then layer long reverb and chorus modulation. The stretching artifacts create the characteristic warped texture. In FL Studio use the Time Stretch tool. In Ableton use Complex Pro warp mode.

What is the target LUFS for mastering pluggnb?

Aim for -11 to -10 LUFS integrated for streaming. PluggnB's dynamics and 808 weight depend on headroom. Over-limiting the master removes the physical impact that defines the genre on good speakers. Don't chase loudness at the expense of dynamics.

Frequently Asked Questions

+ FAQ What is the ideal BPM range for PluggnB production?

PluggnB typically sits between 60–85 BPM, maintaining the slow-tempo foundation of plugg rap while allowing room for R&B melodic expression. This slower tempo creates the spacious, intimate atmosphere that defines the genre and gives 808 bass movement time to breathe.

+ FAQ How should I tune and shape the 808 bass in PluggnB?

Use long-sustain tuned 808s with portamento glide to create smooth bass movement rather than sharp attacks. The 808 should be melodic and pitch-controlled, serving as both the harmonic foundation and a melodic element that complements the vocal melody.

+ FAQ What chord progressions work best for PluggnB tracks?

Minor key progressions with 7th extensions create the emotional, melancholic foundation essential to PluggnB. These extensions add harmonic richness and emotional depth that support both the warped samples and R&B vocal melody.

+ FAQ How do I create the atmospheric pad sound characteristic of PluggnB?

Layer reverb-heavy pads using minor 7th chords, keeping them barely audible in the mix so they provide harmonic support without cluttering the space. The pads should enhance the atmosphere rather than dominate, allowing the sparse arrangement to remain the focal point.

+ FAQ What's the difference between PluggnB vocals and traditional R&B vocals?

PluggnB vocals are mixed intimately inside the music rather than sitting on top of it, using techniques like Auto-Tune, doubling, and long reverb to create a hazy, integrated sound. The vocal sits within the sparse beat architecture rather than commanding attention like mainstream R&B.

+ FAQ How should I approach percussion in a PluggnB beat?

Keep percussion minimal and strategic—typically just an 808 kick, occasional snare hits, and minimal hi-hats. The power of PluggnB comes from subtraction and space, so every percussion element should serve the overall atmosphere rather than fill the mix.

+ FAQ What production techniques create the 'warped melodic' sound in PluggnB?

Use pitch-shifted samples, pluck synths, and electric pianos that are subtly warped or time-stretched to create the signature disorienting, atmospheric quality. These warped elements provide melodic interest without adding clarity or definition to the sparse mix.

+ FAQ How does PluggnB differ from standard plugg rap production?

PluggnB replaces plugg's monotone, drowsy rap vocals with full R&B melodic singing, actual song hooks, and harmonic songwriting intent. While plugg focuses on instrumental atmosphere with minimal vocals, PluggnB builds complete emotional narratives through melody and conventional song structures like verses, pre-hooks, and choruses.
