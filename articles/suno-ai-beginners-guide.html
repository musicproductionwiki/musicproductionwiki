Suno AI Beginner's Guide: How to Use Suno to Make Music in 2026

Everything you need to go from zero to generating your first professional-sounding track — prompts, Custom Mode, stems, extensions, and the settings that actually matter.

By MusicProductionWiki · Updated May 2026 · 12 min read
Quick Answer: Suno AI is a browser-based AI music generator that creates full songs — vocals, lyrics, instrumentation — from a text prompt. Free accounts get 50 credits per day (10 songs). The fastest path to quality results: write specific style prompts combining genre + mood + tempo + instruments, use Custom Mode to control your own lyrics, and use Extend to build full-length tracks from your best clips.

What Is Suno AI?

Suno AI is a generative music platform that uses large language models and audio diffusion technology to create full songs from text prompts. Unlike older AI music tools that generated loops or backing tracks, Suno produces complete compositions: verse, chorus, bridge, outro — with vocals, harmonies, and instrumentation all generated simultaneously.

You type a description. Suno returns two song variations, each roughly 30–120 seconds long. You pick the better one, extend it, add more sections, and eventually download a full-length track. The entire process — from blank page to finished song — can take under ten minutes.

Suno launched publicly in late 2023 and rapidly became one of the most used AI music tools in the world, alongside Udio. By 2026 it processes tens of millions of song generations per month. The tool is web-based, requires no download, and works in any modern browser.

What makes Suno distinctive is the quality of its vocals. Earlier AI tools generated music that sounded robotic or obviously synthetic. Suno's vocal output — while still identifiably AI-generated to trained ears — is coherent, melodic, and stylistically convincing across genres from pop to metal to jazz to country.

Suno Plans and Pricing (2026)

Suno uses a credit system. Every song generation costs 5 credits. Extending a clip costs 5 credits. Cover art generation costs 1 credit.

The free tier is genuinely useful for learning. You get roughly 10 generations per day, which is enough to experiment with prompts, understand Suno's creative tendencies, and produce non-commercial content. The key limitation is commercial rights — free-tier output cannot legally be used in YouTube monetized videos, released on Spotify, or sold.

Pro at $8/month is the sweet spot for most users. You get stem separation (vocals and instrumentals as separate files), priority generation during peak hours, and full commercial rights. If you're serious about using Suno for actual music release or content creation, Pro is the minimum viable plan.

Suno Interface Walkthrough

When you open Suno and click Create, you see a single text field labelled "Song Description" and a large Create button. This minimalist interface hides considerable depth — let's map out everything.

The Main Creation Panel

The Song Description field is where you write your style prompt. Keep it under 200 characters for best results — Suno processes the whole prompt as a single context, and very long prompts sometimes cause it to lose focus on individual elements.

Below the description field is the Custom toggle. When off, Suno writes its own lyrics. When on (Custom Mode), you see two additional fields: Lyrics and Song Style. This is the main creative control lever and we'll cover it in depth below.

The Instrumental toggle removes vocals entirely, generating a pure instrumental track. Useful for background music, sync licensing, and beat-making workflows.

Your Library

Every generation you make appears in your Library on the left sidebar. Generations are stored indefinitely on paid plans. Free accounts may see older generations expire after 30 days. You can rename, favorite, and organize tracks into collections from the library view.

Song Actions Menu

Clicking the three-dot menu on any generation reveals the full action set: Extend, Remix, Get Stems, Download, Share, and Delete. Extend and Remix are the two most powerful creative tools in Suno — we'll cover both in detail.

How to Write Suno Prompts That Work

The prompt is everything in Suno. A weak prompt produces generic, forgettable output. A precise, well-structured prompt produces output that sounds intentional and stylistically coherent. Here's the framework that consistently produces better results.

The GMTIV Formula

Structure every style prompt with these five elements in order: Genre → Mood → Tempo → Instrumentation → Vocal Style.

Element What to Include Example
Genre Be specific. Not just "hip-hop" but "trap" or "boom bap" or "drill" UK drill
Mood Emotional character. One or two words max menacing, paranoid
Tempo BPM range or energy descriptor 140 BPM, urgent
Instrumentation 2–3 key sounds or instruments dark piano, 808s, hi-hat rolls
Vocal Style Delivery, texture, register deep male rap, monotone flow

Weak prompt: "hip-hop song about the streets"

Strong prompt: "UK drill, menacing and paranoid, 140 BPM, dark piano melody, 808 bass, hi-hat rolls, deep male rapper with monotone delivery, street lyrics"

The difference in output quality between these two prompts is dramatic. The strong version gives Suno enough information to make creative decisions consistently. The weak version produces something generic and often inconsistent between the two variations.

Genre Descriptors That Work Well

Suno has been trained on enormous volumes of music and responds well to specific genre terminology. Terms that reliably produce strong results include: lo-fi hip-hop, chill wave, dark ambient, progressive house, melodic dubstep, neo-soul, bedroom pop, indie folk, Afrobeats, synthwave, hyperpop, bossa nova, delta blues, shoegaze, and post-punk. Vague genre terms like "pop" or "rock" produce less distinctive results unless you add strong mood and instrumentation modifiers.

What to Avoid in Prompts

Avoid quality descriptors like "professional," "amazing," "high quality," or "radio-ready." Suno ignores these — they have no musical meaning. Avoid artist names when possible; Suno will attempt to capture the style but results are inconsistent and legally grey. Avoid contradictory instructions ("fast and slow," "heavy but delicate") — pick one direction and commit to it. Avoid overly long prompts exceeding 200 characters; they reduce coherence.

Custom Mode: Writing Your Own Lyrics

Custom Mode is the most important creative tool in Suno for serious users. It separates the style prompt (how it sounds) from the lyrical content (what it says), giving you full control over both dimensions independently.

How Custom Mode Works

Toggle Custom in the creation panel. You now see two fields: Song Style and Lyrics. The Song Style field takes the same prompt format as the standard description field — genre, mood, tempo, instrumentation, vocal style. The Lyrics field accepts free-form text that Suno will use verbatim as the lyrical content of the song.

Suno uses structural tags to understand song arrangement from your lyrics. The core tags are: [Verse], [Chorus], [Bridge], [Pre-Chorus], [Outro], and [Intro]. Placing these tags before each lyric section tells Suno exactly where you want the song structure to change.

Custom Mode Lyric Format

[Intro]
(atmospheric pad, building)

[Verse 1]
City lights reflecting on the rain
Every night the same routine, the same old pain
But there's a feeling in the air tonight
Something's shifting, I can feel it's right

[Pre-Chorus]
Hold on, hold on

[Chorus]
We're breaking through the static
Something almost automatic
Finding clarity in chaos
Hold on

[Verse 2]
...continue structure

Parenthetical notes like (atmospheric pad, building) or (guitar solo) inside sections are processed as production directions — Suno will attempt to follow them musically. This is a powerful way to shape the energy and arrangement even within Custom Mode.

Instrumental Breaks and Sections

To create an instrumental section, use [Instrumental Break] or [Guitar Solo] or [Drum Break] as standalone tags without any lyrics beneath them. Suno will generate that section as purely instrumental, which is excellent for creating dynamic arrangements with real breathing room between vocal sections.

Extending Tracks: Building Full Songs

Every Suno generation produces a clip of roughly 30–120 seconds. That's not a full song — it's a starting point. The Extend feature is how you turn a strong clip into a complete track.

How to Extend

Click the three-dot menu on any generation and select Extend. Suno opens an extension panel showing a timeline of your clip. You can choose where the extension begins: from the current ending (continue forward), or from a point within the clip (branch mid-song). You can also write a new style prompt or lyrics specifically for the extended section, which lets you evolve the song's direction.

Most users extend 3–4 times to build a complete 3–4 minute track. Each extension costs 5 credits. The key is extending from your strongest clip — if you generated two variations, always pick the better one before extending.

Controlling Song Structure Through Extensions

The most effective approach to building full songs is to plan the structure before you start extending. A typical pop or hip-hop structure might be: Intro → Verse 1 → Chorus → Verse 2 → Chorus → Bridge → Final Chorus → Outro. Map this out, then generate and extend section by section, using Custom Mode to feed the appropriate lyrics into each extension.

When extending, write a brief style note for what you want the new section to do: "Chorus — higher energy, bigger drums, soaring vocals" or "Bridge — stripped back, just piano and vocals." This production context in the extension prompt significantly improves how Suno handles musical transitions.

Remix: Changing a Track's Direction

Remix is distinct from Extend. Where Extend continues a song forward in time, Remix regenerates an existing clip with modifications to the style or lyrics while attempting to preserve the core musical identity of the original. Think of it as "keep the vibe, change the details."

Remix is useful when you have a strong melodic or harmonic idea in a generation but want to change the genre, swap the vocal style, remove vocals entirely, or shift the energy. A lo-fi hip-hop clip can be remixed into a jazz version. A pop song can be remixed into an acoustic version. The results are not always predictable but when they work, they're spectacular.

Practically, Remix works best when you make moderate changes — not radical ones. Remixing a death metal track into ambient electronic will usually produce incoherent results. Remixing a pop track into indie pop, or shifting a hip-hop beat from trap to boom bap, produces much more useful output.

Getting Stems from Suno

Stems — separate audio files for vocals and instrumentals — are available on Pro and Premier plans. To get stems from any generation, click the three-dot menu and select Get Stems. Suno processes the track and delivers two files: a vocals stem and an instrumental stem.

Stems unlock several powerful workflows. You can take the instrumental stem into a DAW and add your own vocal performance on top. You can take the vocal stem and build a completely different beat underneath it. You can use the instrumental stem as a backing track for live performance. You can run the instrumental through audio analysis tools to understand what Suno built harmonically and structurally.

Stem quality is not perfect — there is often some bleed between stems (vocal artifacts in the instrumental, instrumental frequencies in the vocal stem). But for creative purposes, the stems are typically clean enough to work with in production environments. Running the stems through iZotope RX's Music Rebalance tool after downloading significantly improves separation quality.

Downloading and Exporting

All Suno output downloads as MP3. The bit rate is 192kbps on free accounts and 320kbps on paid plans. For most use cases including streaming release, 320kbps MP3 is more than adequate. For professional mixing and mastering workflows, you may want to use a tool like Eleven Labs or a dedicated AI audio upscaler to convert to WAV before working in your DAW.

To download, click the three-dot menu on any track and select Download. Your browser will download the MP3 immediately. Stem files download as a ZIP archive containing the two separate stems.

Pro Tips: Getting Better Results from Suno

Generate in Batches

Don't stop at one prompt. Generate 3–5 variations of the same concept with slightly different prompts, then cherry-pick the strongest clip across all of them. Suno is nondeterministic — the same prompt produces different results every time. Treating generation as a selection process rather than a single creation act dramatically improves your output quality.

Use Instrumental Mode for Beats

If you want to use Suno output as background music, content music, or a beat to rap or sing over yourself, always use Instrumental Mode. The vocal-free output is cleaner, mixes more easily, and avoids copyright complications around the AI-generated lyrics and vocal performance.

Iterate on Prompts Systematically

When a prompt isn't working, change one element at a time. Swap the mood descriptor. Change the tempo. Add or remove an instrument. This systematic approach tells you exactly which element is causing the problem, rather than rewriting the whole prompt and losing what was working.

Use the Cover Regeneration Feature

Suno auto-generates cover art for every song. You can regenerate cover art unlimited times for 1 credit each. Spend a credit or two regenerating covers until you have art that matches your track's energy — this matters if you're sharing on social media or releasing the track.

Listen on Headphones Before Extending

Always evaluate a clip on headphones before committing credits to extend it. What sounds good on phone speakers may reveal pitch problems, timing issues, or unwanted artifacts at higher quality playback. Catching problems early saves credits.

Copyright and Ownership — What You Need to Know

This is the most frequently misunderstood aspect of Suno. Here's the practical reality as of 2026.

Suno's Terms of Service grant paid subscribers full commercial rights to output they generate. You can release it on Spotify, monetize it on YouTube, sync it to video, and sell it. However, this is a contractual right granted by Suno — not a copyright registration with the US Copyright Office or any national authority.

The US Copyright Office has taken the position that AI-generated works with no human creative input are not eligible for copyright protection. Works where a human provides substantial creative contribution — through detailed prompts, custom lyrics, significant editing, and arrangement decisions — occupy a legally contested grey area. This is actively being litigated and legislated in multiple jurisdictions.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is Suno AI free to use?

Suno offers a free tier with 50 credits per day (roughly 10 songs). Paid plans start at $8/month for Pro, which gives 2,500 credits monthly and commercial usage rights.

How long does it take Suno to generate a song?

Suno generates a full clip in approximately 30–90 seconds depending on server load. Most generations complete within a minute.

Can I use Suno music commercially?

Commercial use requires a paid Suno plan (Pro or Premier). Free tier output is for personal, non-commercial use only under Suno's Terms of Service.

What is the best way to write Suno prompts?

The best Suno prompts combine genre, mood, tempo, instrumentation, and vocal style in that order. Be specific about energy level and avoid vague terms like "good" or "professional" which have no musical meaning.

Does Suno generate lyrics automatically?

Yes. Suno generates lyrics automatically based on your style prompt. You can also write and paste your own lyrics using Custom Mode for full lyrical control.

What file formats does Suno export?

Suno exports MP3 audio files. Stem separation (vocals and instrumentals) is available on Pro and Premier plans, delivered as a ZIP archive.

Can I extend a Suno song?

Yes. Use the Extend feature to add more sections to any generated clip. Each extension costs 5 credits. Most full-length tracks require 3–4 extensions from the initial clip.

What is Suno's credit system?

Each song generation costs 5 credits. Extending a clip costs 5 credits. Cover art generation costs 1 credit. Free accounts get 50 credits per day; Pro accounts get 2,500 per month; Premier accounts get 10,000 per month.

Practical Exercises

Beginner Exercise: Your First Suno Track

Create a free Suno account and use the GMTIV formula to write your first prompt. Choose a genre you love and are familiar with. Generate two variations, listen to both on headphones, and pick the stronger one. Then use Extend once to add another section. Download the final track. The goal is to complete the full workflow end-to-end — prompt → generate → evaluate → extend → download — in a single session.

Intermediate Exercise: Custom Mode Song

Write a complete song structure using Custom Mode. Write the lyrics first — a verse, pre-chorus, chorus, and bridge — before opening Suno. Use the structural tags ([Verse], [Chorus], etc.) correctly. Generate in Custom Mode with your lyrics, then extend the track three times to build a full 3-minute song. The goal is to understand the relationship between the lyrics you write and the musical output Suno produces.

Advanced Exercise: Stems-Based Production

Upgrade to Pro for a month. Generate a track in Instrumental Mode using a highly specific style prompt targeting a particular era and genre (e.g., "1990s boom bap hip-hop, dusty samples, hard-hitting snares, jazz piano chops, 92 BPM"). Get the stems. Import the instrumental stem into your DAW. Record or import your own vocal performance on top. Mix and master the combined track. Compare the result to a purely AI-generated output. The goal is to understand where AI generation ends and human creative contribution begins.
