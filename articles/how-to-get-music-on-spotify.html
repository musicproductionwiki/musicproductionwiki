How to Get Your Music on Spotify in 2026: Step-by-Step

Getting on Spotify is not the hard part. Getting your metadata right, pitching for playlists, and building a profile that actually grows — that is where most independent artists leave money and opportunity on the table.

MP The Music Production Wiki Team Updated April 2026 12 min read Beginner

Spotify has over 600 million active users and pays out billions of dollars in royalties every year. Getting your music onto the platform is genuinely straightforward — the process takes less than an hour from start to submission. But most independent artists stop at getting their music uploaded and miss everything that happens after — the metadata optimization, the Spotify for Artists setup, the playlist pitching window, and the profile building that determines whether a release grows or disappears. This guide covers all of it.

Step One: Finish and Master Your Music

This sounds obvious but it bears saying clearly: do not rush your music onto Spotify before it is ready. Spotify's algorithm surfaces music based on listener behavior — saves, playlist adds, completion rate, and return listens. A track that isn't finished, isn't mixed well, or isn't mastered to competitive loudness will underperform regardless of how good the underlying song is. The platform does not give you a second chance to make a first impression on the algorithm.

Your master file should be a WAV or FLAC file — lossless audio that your distributor will compress into the streaming format used by each platform. Spotify streams at 320kbps for premium users — high quality, but still a lossy format. Starting with a lossless master ensures that the compression process begins with the best possible source material.

Your master should hit approximately -14 LUFS integrated loudness — Spotify's normalization target. Tracks louder than this are turned down by Spotify's loudness normalization. Tracks quieter are left at their original level, which means they play back quieter than surrounding tracks. Target -14 LUFS and your music will play at the level Spotify intends — no louder, no quieter than everything around it.

Step Two: Prepare Your Metadata

Metadata is the information attached to your release — and getting it right before submission is one of the most important things you will do in your entire release process. Metadata errors are difficult to fix after a release goes live, can affect royalty collection for years, and create fragmented artist profiles that make it harder for listeners to find your catalog.

Before submitting to your distributor, confirm and prepare the following information:

Artist name: Exactly as you want it to appear on Spotify. Check the spelling three times. If you have existing releases on Spotify, confirm your new release uses the exact same artist name spelling — including capitalization and punctuation — to ensure it appears on your existing artist profile rather than creating a new one.

Song title: Exactly as you want it to appear. Do not use ALL CAPS unless that is your intentional stylistic choice. Do not include featured artist information in the song title field — use the featured artist field your distributor provides for this.

Featured artists: If your track includes a featured artist, enter them in the designated featured artist field — not in the song title. The correct format is "Song Title (feat. Artist Name)" and most distributors generate this automatically when you use the featured artist field correctly.

Primary genre and subgenre: Choose the most accurate genre available. Spotify uses genre metadata as one signal for algorithmic playlist placement — an incorrectly categorized track may be surfaced to the wrong audience.

Release date: Plan your release date at least two weeks in advance of when you want the music live — and ideally four to seven weeks in advance if you want to pitch for editorial playlists. More on this below.

ISRC code: International Standard Recording Code — a unique identifier for your recording. Your distributor generates this automatically if you don't already have one. If you're re-releasing a track that previously had an ISRC, use the same ISRC to maintain streaming history and data continuity.

Songwriter and producer credits: Enter the legal names of every songwriter and producer on the track. This information is used for publishing royalty collection — incorrect or missing songwriter credits mean publishing royalties go uncollected.

Explicit content flag: Mark explicitly if your track contains explicit language. Unmarked explicit content can result in your track being removed from family-friendly playlists and may violate platform terms of service.

Step Three: Choose Your Distributor and Submit

You cannot submit music to Spotify directly as an independent artist. You need a distributor — a licensed intermediary that delivers your music to Spotify and other platforms on your behalf. We cover distributors in detail in our complete distribution guide, but for Spotify specifically, here is what matters.

DistroKid is the fastest distributor for Spotify delivery — releases typically appear within two to five business days of submission. For artists who release frequently, its unlimited release model at a flat annual fee is the most economical option. TuneCore offers comparable delivery speeds with more comprehensive publishing royalty collection. CD Baby is slower but more comprehensive for artists who also sell physical media.

When you submit through your distributor, you will upload your master audio file, your album artwork, and all your metadata. Your artwork must be at least 3000 x 3000 pixels, in JPEG or PNG format, with no website URLs, social media handles, or pricing information embedded in the image — Spotify rejects artwork that violates these guidelines.

After submission, your distributor delivers the release to Spotify. Spotify reviews and approves it — a process that typically takes two to five business days but can take longer during high-volume periods like the weeks before major holidays. Plan your submission timeline accordingly.

Step Four: Claim Your Spotify for Artists Profile

Spotify for Artists is the free dashboard that gives you access to streaming data, profile customization, and — critically — the ability to pitch your music for editorial playlist consideration. Claiming your profile is not optional if you are serious about your Spotify presence. It takes fifteen minutes and unlocks capabilities that make a measurable difference to how your releases perform.

Go to artists.spotify.com and claim your profile. You will need to verify your identity as the artist or their representative. Once verified, you can customize your artist bio, select your artist pick — the release or playlist you want featured at the top of your profile — upload a header image, and add your social media links.

The streaming analytics available through Spotify for Artists are detailed and actionable: listener demographics by age and gender, geographic distribution of listeners, which playlists are driving streams, save rates, playlist add rates, and how your listeners discover your music. These metrics are not vanity numbers — they are signals that tell you where your audience is, what they respond to, and where to invest your promotional energy.

Step Five: Pitch for Editorial Playlists

Spotify's editorial playlists — curated by Spotify's in-house editorial team — are among the most powerful discovery tools on the platform. A placement on a major editorial playlist can add tens of thousands of streams to a release and introduce your music to listeners who would never have found it organically. Pitching for editorial consideration is free, takes fifteen minutes, and is available to every artist with a Spotify for Artists account.

The critical timing requirement: you must pitch your release at least seven days before its release date, while it is still unreleased. Once a track is live on Spotify, the editorial pitching window closes. This is the single most important reason to plan your releases at least two weeks in advance — ideally four to six weeks — rather than submitting and releasing simultaneously.

To pitch, go to your Spotify for Artists dashboard, navigate to the upcoming release, and click the pitch button. You will be asked to describe your track — genre, mood, instrumentation, the story behind the song — and to identify the Spotify playlist categories you believe it fits. Write this description with care. The editorial team reads pitches and uses them to understand your music in context. A pitch that clearly articulates what makes your track distinct, who it is for, and what mood or activity it serves gives the editorial team more to work with than a generic description.

You can pitch one track per release — choose the track you believe has the strongest editorial playlist potential, not necessarily your personal favorite. Singles perform better in editorial pitching than album tracks, because Spotify's editorial team prioritizes music that listeners will engage with immediately rather than in the context of a longer project.

Step Six: Build Your Release Around a Window, Not a Date

Most independent artists treat a release as a single moment — the day the music goes live. Professional release strategy treats a release as a window of three to six weeks during which promotional energy compounds. Understanding this difference changes how you plan every release.

The week before release: announce the release date, share a preview clip, build anticipation with your audience on social media and through your newsletter. The goal is to prime your existing audience to listen, save, and add to playlists the moment the music drops — early engagement signals quality to Spotify's algorithm.

Release day: post across every platform. Send your newsletter. Share the Spotify link directly — not a link to your website, not a link to a landing page, the direct Spotify link. Every extra click between a potential listener and your music reduces the percentage who make it there. Make it effortless.

The week after release: respond to every comment and message. Share listener reactions. Post the music in communities where it is genuinely relevant — not spam, but authentic sharing in spaces where your music belongs. The algorithm watches engagement in the first seven days particularly closely.

Weeks two through six: keep the music in conversation. Share streaming milestone posts when you hit them. Create content around the story of the track. Submit to independent playlist curators — tools like SubmitHub, Groover, and Playlist Push connect artists with independent playlist curators who accept submissions for consideration. Independent playlist placement builds the stream count and listener data that makes algorithmic playlist placement — Discover Weekly, Release Radar, Radio — more likely.

The Spotify Algorithm: What Actually Drives It

Spotify's recommendation algorithm — the system behind Discover Weekly, Release Radar, Spotify Radio, and the Daily Mix playlists — is the most powerful music discovery tool ever created for independent artists. Understanding what signals it uses helps you make decisions that work with it rather than against it.

Save rate is the most important metric on Spotify. When a listener saves your track to their library, it is the strongest signal available to the algorithm that your music is genuinely valued — not just streamed passively but actively claimed. A save rate above 10% is exceptional. Above 5% is good. Below 2% suggests the wrong audience is finding your music or the music is not connecting with the audience that is finding it.

Completion rate — the percentage of listeners who hear the track all the way through rather than skipping — is the second most important signal. Tracks that listeners skip in the first thirty seconds are penalized in algorithmic recommendations. Tracks that listeners complete consistently are rewarded with more placement. The first thirty seconds of your track are not an introduction — they are an audition for the algorithm.

Playlist adds, follows generated per stream, and listener return rate — whether someone who discovers your music comes back to listen again — all contribute to the algorithmic picture of how valuable your music is to listeners. These metrics cannot be gamed sustainably. The only durable strategy is music that genuinely connects with its audience — which is both the most obvious and the most honest conclusion available.

The Spotify Reality Check

Spotify pays approximately $0.003 to $0.005 per stream. A million streams generates roughly $3,000 to $5,000 in master royalties. This is not a sustainable income for most independent artists at current streaming volumes — which is why the artists who build sustainable careers from streaming treat Spotify as a discovery and audience-building tool rather than a primary revenue source. The goal of Spotify is not streaming income. The goal is building an audience that buys tickets, merchandise, sync licenses, and direct support. Keep that perspective and every streaming decision becomes clearer.
