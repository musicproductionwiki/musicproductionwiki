How to Get Your Music on Spotify in 2026

First published May 2026 · Updated May 2026
MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 Beginner → Advanced

Getting music on Spotify is now the easy part. Any producer with a finished track and $10–$23 can have their music live on Spotify within 24–48 hours through a distributor. The hard part — the thing nobody tells you when they explain how to get on Spotify — is everything that comes after. Distribution is the starting line, not the finish line. Getting streams requires understanding how Spotify actually works as a discovery and promotion platform, and making decisions that align with how the algorithm finds and surfaces new music.

Quick Answer

To get music on Spotify: (1) finish and master your track, (2) sign up with a music distributor (DistroKid, TuneCore, or CD Baby), (3) upload your track with correct metadata at least 7 days before your release date, (4) claim your Spotify for Artists profile, and (5) submit your unreleased track for editorial playlist consideration through Spotify for Artists before release. That's the process. Getting significant streams after that requires a promotion strategy beyond the technical distribution steps.

Step-by-Step: Getting Your Music on Spotify Beginner

  1. Prepare your audio file. Spotify requires WAV or FLAC files (most distributors accept both). At minimum: 44.1kHz, 16-bit. Better: 44.1kHz or 48kHz, 24-bit. Make sure your track is properly mastered with integrated LUFS targeting around -14 LUFS and true peak below -1.0 dBTP. A professionally mastered track sounds better and meets Spotify's technical requirements without normalization artifacts.
  2. Prepare your artwork. Spotify requires square artwork at minimum 3000x3000 pixels, JPG or PNG format. Text in artwork must be legible at small sizes — check your artwork at 56x56 pixels (the size it appears in notification screens). No Spotify logos, streaming service logos, or website URLs are allowed in artwork.
  3. Prepare your metadata. Accurate metadata prevents royalty collection problems. Required: track title (exactly as you want it to appear — no "feat." tags in main title unless it's the official title), artist name, release date, genre (primary and secondary), language, ISRC code (your distributor typically generates this). Optional but important: BPM, key, mood/theme tags. Co-writer information should be recorded in your publishing registration, not in Spotify metadata.
  4. Choose a distributor and upload. DistroKid, TuneCore, or CD Baby are all appropriate. Upload at least 7 days before your release date — this gives time for Spotify's ingestion process and is required for editorial playlist pitching eligibility.
  5. Claim your Spotify for Artists profile. Go to artists.spotify.com and claim your artist profile once your first release is live. Verification is typically instant for DistroKid users, 24–48 hours for others. Spotify for Artists gives you access to streaming analytics, the ability to customize your artist profile, and most critically — the editorial playlist submission tool.
  6. Submit for editorial playlists. In Spotify for Artists, you'll see any unreleased tracks eligible for submission. Submit your track with a complete pitch: genre, mood, instrumentation, and a brief description of the song and its context. You can only submit one track per release and it must be submitted before the release date. Editorial submissions are free and available to any artist regardless of prior streaming numbers.

How Spotify's Algorithm Works Intermediate

Spotify's recommendation algorithm — called Bandits for Recommendations Across Neighborhoods, or BaRT internally — uses behavioral signals to determine which music to surface to which listeners. Understanding what signals it responds to helps you make decisions that work with the algorithm rather than against it.

The most important signal: save rate. When a listener adds your song to their library (saves it), Spotify interprets this as a strong positive signal that the music is worth recommending more broadly. A track with a high save rate gets expanded algorithmic distribution — it appears in more Discover Weekly playlists, more Radio stations, and more Autoplay recommendations. Save rate matters more than raw stream count.

The second most important signal: stream-to-skip ratio. If listeners consistently skip your song before it reaches the 30-second mark, the algorithm deprioritizes it. If listeners consistently play it through to completion and beyond, the algorithm promotes it. This is why the first 30 seconds of a song are critically important for streaming performance — you need to hold attention before the algorithm counts a "skip."

Playlist adds by real listeners (as opposed to bot plays) create another positive signal. Every time a real person adds your track to a personal playlist, Spotify registers it as organic engagement that suggests the track fits a real use case for real listeners.

Playlist Strategy Beyond Editorial Intermediate

Editorial playlists (Spotify-curated, like "Rap Caviar" or "New Music Friday") are the highest-profile placement but by no means the only path to meaningful streams. Independent playlist curators — real people who manage popular genre or mood-specific playlists with thousands to millions of followers — are often more accessible and more genre-specific than editorial playlists.

Playlist pitching services (SubmitHub, Groover) connect artists with independent curators for paid or free submission. The conversion rates are low — typically 5–15% — but placement in even a mid-sized independent playlist (10,000–100,000 followers) can generate thousands of streams and meaningful algorithmic data. Don't buy streams or artificial playlist placements — Spotify's fraud detection is sophisticated, and accounts caught using stream manipulation face removal from the platform.

Why Metadata Matters for Royalties Intermediate

Correct metadata isn't just about searchability — it's directly connected to royalty collection. The Mechanical Licensing Collective (MLC) matches streams to compositions using metadata to distribute mechanical royalties. If your song's ISRC code, songwriter names, or title are inconsistent between your distributor, your PRO registration, and your MLC registration, the match process fails and royalties go unmatched. The MLC's unmatched royalty pool — funds collected but not yet distributed because songs can't be matched to registered rights holders — runs into hundreds of millions of dollars. Correct metadata from the start prevents your royalties from ending up in that pool.

❌ Most Common Spotify Release Mistake

Uploading the day before release and missing the editorial submission window. Spotify for Artists only allows editorial playlist pitching for tracks submitted at least 7 days before the release date. Artists who upload last-minute lose their only opportunity to be considered by Spotify's editorial team for their release cycle. Set your release date at least 2–3 weeks out, upload immediately, and submit for editorial consideration as soon as the option appears in your Spotify for Artists dashboard.
