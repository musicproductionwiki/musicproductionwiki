How to Make Drill Music — Complete Production Guide

Updated May 2026  ·  15 min read  ·  Techniques

Quick answer: Drill music is built on a foundation of dark minor-key melodies, sliding 808 bass, tight hi-hat rolls, and a heavy snare on beats 2 and 4. Chicago drill runs at 140–145 BPM with aggressive trap percussion; UK drill is slightly slower, darker, and more cinematic. Both styles require careful 808 tuning, intentional melody writing in minor keys, and layered hi-hat patterns for authenticity.

Drill is one of the most sonically distinctive sub-genres in modern hip-hop. It started on the south side of Chicago around 2012 — producers like Young Chop and DJ L defined a sound built on dark melodies, relentless hi-hat patterns, and bass so heavy it physically moves people. By 2015, London producers had adapted the template into UK drill — slower, darker, more cinematic, with orchestral elements and a grimier texture.

Today, drill influences everything from mainstream pop to underground rap worldwide. If you want to produce drill — from scratch, in any DAW — this guide covers the full template: drum programming, 808 design, melody writing, arrangement, mixing, and the specific differences between UK and Chicago drill.

Understanding Drill Sub-Genres

Before opening your DAW, you need to understand which flavour of drill you're making — because the production approach differs meaningfully between Chicago drill and UK drill, and modern Brooklyn/Australian/Afro-drill all have their own variations on top.

Chicago Drill (2012–present)

Chicago drill was codified by producers Young Chop, DJ L, and Childish Major. The defining characteristics: BPM between 140–145, hard trap snares, faster hi-hat rolls with frequent triplet runs, aggressive 808 patterns, and melodies built from dark piano, strings, or simple synth lines. Chief Keef's early mixtapes and albums are the textbook reference — "I Don't Like," "Love Sosa," and "Faneto" define the template.

Chicago drill is more aggressive sounding. The snare is loud and often sampled or layered. The kick hits hard on beat 1 and occasionally beat 3. The 808 is forward in the mix and often slides between notes aggressively.

UK Drill (2015–present)

UK drill emerged from south London, pioneered by producers like Izzar, M1OnTheBeat, and AXL. It adapted the Chicago template but slowed slightly, darkened the palette, and added a cinematic quality. UK drill beats frequently sample classical strings or horror-score sounds. The hi-hat patterns are less aggressive, with more rolling triplets. The 808 slides are longer and more melodic — the bass in UK drill often functions like a second melody line.

Headie One, Unknown T, Central Cee, and Digga D represent the canonical UK drill sound. UK drill has arguably overtaken Chicago drill in global influence — its aesthetic now shapes pop, grime, and commercial music worldwide.

Setting Up Your DAW

Drill works in any DAW. Set your project tempo to 140 BPM — you can adjust up or down by 2–3 BPM to taste. Set the time signature to 4/4. Use a 16-step grid (sixteenth notes) for drum programming. Most drill is written in 4-bar or 8-bar loops that repeat through a verse with minimal variation.

FL Studio Setup

FL Studio is the most popular DAW for drill production, used by the majority of Chicago and UK drill producers. Set your BPM in the tempo box at the top. Open the Step Sequencer (F6) and load your drum sounds. FL Studio's Step Sequencer is ideal for drill drum patterns — 16 steps per row, drag-to-resize for velocity. For 808s, load your 808 sample into a Channel Rack slot, then use the Piano Roll to draw pitched notes. Enable portamento on the 808 channel for authentic slides.

Ableton Live Setup

In Ableton, create a new MIDI track for drums. Drag a Drum Rack onto it. Load your kick, snare, clap, and hi-hat samples into the appropriate pads. Create a MIDI clip in the Session or Arrangement view, set it to 1 bar or 2 bars, and program your pattern in the piano roll with the grid set to 1/16. For 808s, create a separate MIDI track, load a sampler (Simpler or Sampler), and load your 808 sample with glide/portamento enabled on the instrument.

Logic Pro Setup

In Logic, open a new project at 140 BPM. Create a Drummer track or, better, create a Software Instrument track and load the Ultrabeat drum machine for precise step programming. For 808s, load the EXS24 (now Quick Sampler) or Alchemy, load your 808 sample, and enable glide in the instrument settings. Logic's Arpeggiator or Step Sequencer plugin can help generate hi-hat roll patterns quickly.

Programming the Drums

The drum pattern is the foundation of every drill beat. Get this wrong and nothing else will save the track. Get it right and even a simple melody sounds authentic.

Kick Pattern

Drill kicks are relatively sparse compared to trap. The kick lands on beat 1 (step 1 of 16) in almost every variation. A common additional kick placement is step 11 or step 13. The kick should be punchy and deep — tuned to complement the 808 so they don't clash in the low end. In FL Studio, load a 808-style kick (Roland 808 kick or a TR-808 sample) into the step sequencer. Turn the volume up and cut the high end slightly with a low-pass filter.

Snare Pattern

The snare hits on beats 2 and 4 — steps 5 and 13 in a 16-step grid. This is non-negotiable in drill. Some UK drill tracks delay the snare slightly (pushing it just after the beat) for a more rolling, laidback feel. Layer your snare with a clap for more crack. Add subtle reverb — a short room reverb with high pre-delay adds space without muddying the mix.

Hi-Hat Pattern — The Defining Element

The hi-hat pattern is what separates good drill from generic trap. The basic drill hi-hat plays on every 8th note (steps 1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11, 13, 15) with ghost hits on every other 16th note at lower velocity. The key is the triplet roll — a burst of 3 rapid hi-hat hits inserted at specific points in the bar, usually in the second half. In FL Studio, right-click your hi-hat row and select "Make grooves from template" or manually draw in the roll by subdividing steps to 1/32 or 1/64 resolution.

Velocity variation is critical. Real drill hi-hats are not robotic. Pull the velocity on alternate hits down to around 60–80% to get a natural human feel. The accented hits (every 8th note) sit at 100% velocity. Ghost hits sit at 40–60%.

Hi-hat roll technique: For the signature drill roll, select 3–4 consecutive 32nd note slots at the end of a bar and fill them with hi-hat hits at descending velocity (100%, 75%, 50%). Pitch-automate the hi-hat up slightly during the roll for added movement. This is the core hi-hat trick used on virtually every UK drill record.

Open Hi-Hat and Ride

Open hi-hats add breathing room. Place one or two open hi-hat hits per 2 bars — typically on the "and" of beat 4 (step 15). Set a short choke group so the closed hi-hat cuts the open one. Some producers use a ride cymbal instead of an open hat for a different texture, particularly in UK drill where a more cinematic percussive layer suits the aesthetic.

Building the 808 Bassline

The 808 is the heart of drill music. It's not just a bass — it's a melodic instrument that carries as much emotional weight as the top melody. Getting the 808 right takes time and ear training, but the process is clear.

Choosing Your 808 Sample

Start with a clean TR-808 bass drum sample — you want the long decay version, not the short punchy kick. The most commonly used 808 samples in drill are sourced from Roland's original TR-808 drum machine or high-quality sample packs that recreate it. In FL Studio, load your 808 into a Channel Rack slot using the Fruity Kick or, more commonly, load it into a Sampler channel set to pitch-following mode.

Tuning the 808

Every 808 hit must be tuned to the key of the beat. An out-of-tune 808 is the single most common mistake in amateur drill production — it makes the beat sound cheap and clashing. Set the root note of your 808 sample in the sampler settings, then use the Piano Roll to write pitched notes. If your beat is in D minor, your 808 notes should primarily be D, A, F, and C — the notes of the D natural minor scale.

Use a tuner plugin (or FL Studio's built-in note display) to verify your 808 is hitting the right pitch. Some producers detune the 808 by a few cents sharp or flat for a raw feel — but the foundational pitch must be correct first.

808 Slides and Portamento

The sliding 808 glide from one note to another is the defining sound of drill bass. In FL Studio, you achieve this by drawing a pitch slide note (the orange triangle) in the Piano Roll between two 808 notes. Set the portamento time to between 100–250ms for a natural slide. In Ableton, enable glide on the Sampler instrument and draw MIDI notes that overlap — the overlap time determines slide length. In Logic, use Alchemy's glide setting and draw notes with overlapping durations in the Piano Roll.

The most effective 808 patterns use a long root note as the anchor, then slide up or down to a chord tone (third or fifth) before dropping back. UK drill tends to use longer, more sustained slides — sometimes a single note holds for two full bars with a slow pitch drop.

808 Compression and Saturation

808s need compression and saturation to sit in a mix correctly. Apply a fast-attack, medium-release compressor to even out the transient (try 5ms attack, 100ms release, 4:1 ratio). Add gentle saturation — a tube or tape saturation plugin adds harmonic content that makes the 808 audible on smaller speakers that can't reproduce the sub frequencies. In FL Studio, use the Fruity Blood Overdrive or Maximus for this. In Ableton, use Saturator. In Logic, use the built-in Bitcrusher lightly or Amp Designer.

Writing the Melody

Drill melodies are intentionally simple and dark. The mood is everything. A 4-bar loop with 6 notes can define an entire song if those notes are right. Complexity is not the goal — atmosphere is.

Choosing the Key and Scale

Write in a minor key. D minor, G minor, A minor, and F minor are the most used. For UK drill especially, the Phrygian mode (natural minor with a flattened second degree) adds a distinctly dark, Arabic quality that defines the sub-genre's sound. In practice: if you're in D Phrygian, your scale is D, Eb, F, G, A, Bb, C. That flat 2 (Eb) is the note that creates the signature unsettling tension.

Sound Selection for Melodies

Chicago drill melodies: dark piano (Electric Piano or acoustic piano with reverb), simple sub-bass synth lines, or horn stabs. Keep it minimal. UK drill melodies: orchestral strings, cellos, dark choir pads, plucked string samples, or detuned synths. The cinematic quality comes from layering a string pad with a plucked lead melody on top. Many UK drill producers sample classical music — if you do this, be aware of clearance requirements before any commercial release.

Melody Structure

Write a 2-bar or 4-bar loop. The melody should breathe — don't fill every beat. Leave space for the 808 to occupy the low-mid range. A typical drill melody uses 3–6 notes per bar with rests between phrases. Write your main melodic hook in the first 2 bars, then either repeat it or add a minimal response phrase in bars 3–4.

Use note repetition. Drill melodies often land on the same note two or three times in a row before moving. This gives the pattern a hypnotic, locked-in quality rather than sounding like a traditional melody line. The repetition combined with the sliding 808 creates movement and tension simultaneously.

Layering and Texture

Drill melodies are almost always layered. A common approach: one melody line (lead piano or pluck), one pad underneath (strings or choir), and a third sub-layer an octave below the lead. Pan the pad slightly left, the lead slightly right, and keep the sub-layer centred. This creates width without muddying the arrangement. Use reverb on the melody layers — a large hall or plate reverb with the wet signal pulled back to around 20–30% adds space and atmosphere without losing clarity.

Arrangement

Drill arrangements are simple and repetitive by design. The loop plays almost continuously — that's intentional. The rapper's delivery carries the arrangement. Your job as a producer is to create enough variation within the loop to keep the track from feeling static.

Basic Arrangement Structure

A standard drill arrangement: 4-bar intro (melody only, no drums), then drums enter for the first verse. Chorus or hook section has the full loop playing loudly. Bridge or break: drop the drums for 2–4 bars, let the melody breathe, then bring everything back for impact. Outro: fade or cut the drums, leaving the melody loop to close.

Variation Techniques

To stop the loop from feeling static, use automation and filtering. Drop a high-pass filter on the melody at the end of a section to create a "drop" effect. Automate the reverb wet signal up briefly during a break for an ethereal moment before the drums return. In the chorus, bring the 808 up 2–3 dB in volume for more impact. Use a one-shot hi-hat roll fill to signal a transition between sections.

Mixing a Drill Beat

Drill mixing is about low-end management, clarity in the mids, and a loud, punchy overall output. The 808 and kick must coexist in the same frequency space without cancelling each other.

Low-End Management

Sidechain the 808 to the kick. When the kick hits, the 808 briefly ducks — this prevents frequency clash and keeps both elements audible. In FL Studio, use Peak Controller to sidechain. In Ableton, use a chain with a Compressor triggered by the kick. Set the attack fast (1–5ms), release medium (80–120ms), and ratio at 4:1 to 6:1.

Apply a high-pass filter to every element except the kick and 808. Even the snare should have its sub frequencies rolled off below 100Hz. This keeps the low end clean and focussed.

Drum Bus Processing

Group your drums onto a bus. Apply light compression to glue them together (2:1 ratio, medium attack, medium release). Add a touch of saturation or tape emulation for warmth and punch. A final limiter on the drum bus at -0.1 dBTP ensures headroom before the master.

Melody and Atmosphere

Eq the melody to cut around 300–400Hz where muddiness builds up. Boost slightly at 3–5kHz for presence and clarity. Reverb on melody elements should be pre-delayed by 20–40ms so the dry signal reaches the listener first — this preserves clarity while still adding space. Use a stereo imager on the melody bus to widen the strings or pads without touching the mono-summed low end.

Practical Exercises

🟢 Beginner — Build a Basic Drill Loop in 30 Minutes

Open your DAW and set the BPM to 140. In your drum machine or step sequencer, program: kick on steps 1 and 11, snare on steps 5 and 13, hi-hat on every even step (1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11, 13, 15), and add ghost hi-hats at 50% velocity on steps 2, 4, 10, and 14. Load an 808 sample and write a simple 4-note pattern in D minor: D (2 beats), A (1 beat), F (1 beat), return to D. Enable portamento so the notes slide. Play the loop back. This is your foundation — every drill beat starts here.

🟡 Intermediate — UK vs Chicago Drill Comparison

Build two versions of the same melodic idea — one in Chicago style, one in UK style. For Chicago: use a dark piano, set BPM to 143, make the hi-hat pattern more aggressive with triplet rolls on every other bar. For UK: slow to 140, replace the piano with an orchestral string layer, extend the 808 slides to 2–3 beats, and pull the hi-hat density back by 30%. A/B the two versions. Notice how the percussion density and 808 slide length are the biggest sonic differentiators. Export both and listen on headphones.

🔴 Advanced — Full Gold Standard Drill Beat with Arrangement

Write a complete 2-minute drill beat with full arrangement: 4-bar intro (melody only), 16-bar verse (full loop), 8-bar chorus (melody + drums + 808 pushed up 2dB), 4-bar break (drums drop, reverb automation on melody), 16-bar second verse, 8-bar chorus, 8-bar outro. Mix using a drum sidechain, high-pass on all non-low elements, and a limiter at -1dB on the master. Use a spectrum analyser to verify the 808 is not clashing with the kick. Export as a WAV at 24-bit/44.1kHz.

Frequently Asked Questions

What BPM is drill music?

Chicago drill sits between 140–145 BPM. UK drill typically runs at 140–142 BPM. Both genres are often written at half the perceived tempo — a 140 BPM project feels like a heavy 70 BPM groove when you're working in it.

What key is drill music in?

Drill is almost exclusively written in minor keys. D minor, G minor, A minor, and F minor are the most common. The dark, unsettling sound comes from heavy use of the natural minor and Phrygian modes, particularly in UK drill.

What 808 plugin is used in drill?

The most used 808 samples come from the Roland TR-808 drum machine. In modern production, producers load sampled 808s into FL Studio's sampler, Ableton's Simpler, or Logic's Quick Sampler — pitching and tuning them to the key of the beat.

What is the difference between UK drill and Chicago drill?

Chicago drill (originated ~2012) uses faster hi-hat rolls, prominent trap snares, and aggressive 808 slides. UK drill (originated ~2015–2016) is darker, slower, uses more sustained sliding 808s, orchestral samples, and has a cinematic quality. The percussion density and melodic palette are the biggest sonic differences.

What snare is used in drill music?

Drill uses tight, punchy snares — often layered with a clap. The snare sits on beats 2 and 4. UK drill sometimes delays the snare slightly after the beat for a more rolling, syncopated feel.

How do I make a drill melody?

Write a short 2–4 bar loop in a minor key using dark, atmospheric sounds — strings, piano, or choir. Use space. Drill melodies rarely need more than 6–8 notes in a bar. Dark synths and Phrygian or Arabic scales work especially well for UK drill.

What samples are used in drill music?

Drill uses orchestral strings, dark piano, distorted synths, and cinematic sound design. UK drill frequently incorporates classical or film-score samples. Always clear samples legally before any commercial release — uncleared samples will block distribution.

Can I make drill music in GarageBand?

Yes, GarageBand has drum machines and samplers capable of basic drill beats. For serious production work, FL Studio or Ableton Live offer better control over 808 tuning, hi-hat velocity patterns, and overall workflow efficiency.
