How to Make Drill Music: The Complete Production Guide

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 13 min read Beginner–Intermediate

Drill music emerged from Chicago around 2011 and has since evolved into a global production language with distinct regional variants — UK drill from South London, Brooklyn drill from New York, and Pop Drill that has crossed into mainstream chart territory. What unites them: sliding 808 bass notes that glide between pitches, a specific triplet hi-hat pattern with irregular accents, dark minor key melodies often built on Phrygian or harmonic minor scales, and a sparse, space-heavy arrangement that lets the 808 dominate.

This guide covers the complete drill production process — drums, 808s, melodies, arrangement, and the specific settings that separate drill from trap or any other 808-based genre.

What you'll learn:

Tempo, drum patterns with triplet hi-hats, sliding 808 technique, dark minor melody construction, UK vs Chicago vs Brooklyn drill differences, arrangement structure, and mixing priorities for drill production.

Tempo and Feel

Drill runs between 130 and 145 BPM, with most tracks sitting at 138–142 BPM. This is faster than trap (typically 120–140 BPM) but the feel is heavier and more deliberate than the speed suggests, due to the half-time rhythmic emphasis on beat 3 rather than beats 2 and 4.

Set your project to 140 BPM as a starting point. The tempo affects how the 808 slides sound — at higher tempos the slides feel sharper and more staccato; at lower tempos they feel longer and more dramatic. Adjust from 140 based on how the 808 glide time interacts with the groove.

The Drill Drum Pattern

The drill drum pattern is built around three core elements: a heavy kick, sparse snare, and the characteristic triplet hi-hat.

Kick Pattern

The kick in drill hits hard on beat 1 and has syncopated additional hits that vary by track. A common foundation: kick on beat 1, the "and" of beat 2, beat 3, and occasionally the last 16th of beat 4 (creating anticipation into beat 1). Drill kicks are heavy, punchy, and tuned — often the kick fundamental is tuned to match or complement the 808's key. Kick should sit at 60–80Hz with a sharp transient above 150Hz for cut.

Snare and Clap

Drill uses a minimal snare approach — often only on beat 3 for a half-time feel. Some tracks use no snare at all and let the kick carry the rhythm alone. When present, the snare is dry and punchy with minimal reverb. A clap layered on the snare (or in place of it) is common in UK drill, giving a sharper, more modern texture than a pure snare.

The Triplet Hi-Hat Pattern — The Defining Element

This is what separates drill from trap. Standard trap hi-hats use 16th notes with rolls. Drill uses triplet 16th notes (also called sextuplets — 6 notes per beat rather than 4). Within this triplet grid, specific positions are accented or removed to create the characteristic loping, asymmetric feel.

A basic drill hi-hat pattern (in a 6-note triplet grid per beat, numbered 1–6): positions 1, 3, 4, 6 hit; positions 2 and 5 are often rests. This creates a "tut-tut-ta-tut" feel rather than the even "tik-tik-tik" of straight 16th hats. Add velocity variation: the "4" position is typically the loudest accent, giving the pattern its forward push.

Hi-hat rolls in drill: Rapid 32nd or 64th note hi-hat rolls appear at the end of bars or phrases — 4–8 hit bursts that transition into the next section. Place these on the last 16th note position of bar 4 in a 4-bar phrase. Vary the roll length (4 notes, 6 notes, 8 notes) for variation across the track. This is one of the most recognizable textures in drill production.

The Sliding 808 Bass

The sliding 808 is the signature of drill. Unlike trap's often static 808 notes, drill 808s glide between pitches — the bass slides from one note to the next rather than jumping. This creates a melodic, almost vocal quality in the bass that is inseparable from the genre's character.

How to Program Sliding 808s

FL Studio: In the Piano Roll, draw 808 notes that overlap (the next note starts before the previous ends). Enable "Legato" mode in the channel settings and add a "Portamento" (slide) note between the notes you want to glide. FL's native step sequencer handles this via the "SLIDE" note type. Set portamento time to 100–300ms in the channel properties.

Ableton Live: Use a sampler or synth with portamento/glide enabled. Draw overlapping notes in the piano roll. The sampler's portamento setting controls the glide speed. Set portamento time to 80–250ms. With Ableton's Sampler, enable "Glide" in the pitch settings.

Logic Pro / Kontakt / general: Any instrument with portamento or glide enabled will slide when MIDI notes overlap in the piano roll. Enable mono mode (monophonic) on the instrument and set the portamento/glide time. In mono mode, overlapping notes trigger the slide rather than creating chords.

808 tuning is critical in drill. An 808 out of tune with the melody sounds chaotic rather than dark. Tune the 808 sample by ear or with a spectrum analyzer — find the sample's fundamental pitch and transpose it to match the root note of your melody's key. Most DAWs show the detected pitch of a sample; adjust the fine tune until it matches your target note.

Dark Drill Melodies

Drill melody is built on darkness. The harmonic vocabulary: natural minor (Aeolian), Phrygian mode (with its characteristic flat 2nd creating maximum tension), harmonic minor (with its raised 7th), and diminished intervals (tritones, flat 5ths) that create unease.

The Phrygian mode is particularly characteristic of drill — it is the natural minor scale with the 2nd degree lowered by a semitone. In D Phrygian: D, Eb, F, G, A, Bb, C. The Eb note (flat 2nd) is the defining interval — the half-step above the root creates immediate tension and the dark, menacing quality associated with the genre.

Melody instruments in drill:

  • Dark piano or electric piano: Often played staccato in the mid-high register with minimal sustain. The melody is percussive rather than flowing.
  • String sections: Long, sustained string notes hold harmony while the 808 moves melodically. Dark orchestral strings (cellos, violas) add weight in the low-mids.
  • Mallets (xylophone, vibraphone): Particularly common in UK drill — metallic, percussive melodic elements that accent specific beats without sustaining.
  • Synth pads: Dark, detuned synthesizer pads in the background — Serum or Vital with moderate detuning and a slow attack create the atmospheric gloom of many UK drill instrumentals.
  • Flute: A specific texture in UK drill — a minor key flute melody provides contrast against the dark low-end without softening the overall character.

Arrangement

Drill arrangements are built around the hook/verse structure of rap, but the beat often runs as a nearly continuous loop with minimal variation. The typical structure for a drill instrumental submission to a rapper:

  • Intro (8–16 bars): Full beat from bar 1 — unlike house music, drill doesn't require a long drums-only intro. Some producers do 4 bars of melody before the drums hit for impact.
  • Verse section (16 bars): Main beat pattern. Consistent loop with subtle variation — occasional hi-hat roll, 808 accent, additional percussion hit.
  • Hook/Chorus section: Often the same beat with additional elements layered — a wider synth, a string section swell, a different percussion hit pattern. The hook should feel slightly bigger than the verse.
  • Bridge/breakdown: Some drill tracks include a 4–8 bar section with the 808 alone or melody alone, stripping the drums for contrast. Creates tension before the final verse or outro.
  • Outro: Beat continues, often with elements dropping out gradually over 8–16 bars.

Mixing Drill Beats

The mix hierarchy in drill: 808 and kick at the top, melody second, drums and hi-hats third, atmosphere last. The 808 should be the loudest element — it is the emotional and rhythmic core of the production.

Key mixing priorities:

  • 808 clarity: The 808's fundamentals (40–80Hz) must be present without mud. High-pass everything except the kick and 808 above 80Hz. Sidechain the 808 to the kick with fast attack and 100ms release.
  • Hi-hat control: High-pass hi-hats above 5kHz. Cut any low-mid content in hi-hat samples that bleeds into the mix. Gentle compression (2:1, slow attack) evens out velocity variation without destroying the pattern's dynamic feel.
  • Melody placement: The melody lives in the 300Hz–4kHz range. High-pass below 200Hz (bass and kick occupy below this). Cut any harshness in the 2–4kHz range if it conflicts with where vocals will sit.
  • Atmosphere: Pads and strings in the background — reduce below -12dB in the mix. They should be felt more than heard, creating depth without competing with the 808 and melody.

Frequently Asked Questions

What BPM is drill music?

130–145 BPM, with most tracks at 138–142 BPM. Chicago drill is on the slower end; Brooklyn drill tends toward the faster end. The half-time rhythmic emphasis makes it feel heavier than the tempo suggests.

How do I make sliding 808s for drill?

Enable portamento/glide on your 808 instrument. Draw overlapping notes in the piano roll — the next note starts before the previous ends. With glide enabled, the pitch slides between notes. Set glide time to 100–300ms. In FL Studio, use the SLIDE note in the piano roll with legato mode enabled.

What makes drill different from trap?

The triplet hi-hat pattern with irregular accents (instead of trap's straight 16th-note rolls), sliding 808 notes with portamento, darker Phrygian or harmonic minor melodies, sparser snare placement (often only beat 3 or no snare), and faster BPM (130–145 vs trap's 120–140).

What scale is used in drill music?

Natural minor (Aeolian), Phrygian mode (with its characteristic flat 2nd — the defining drill interval), harmonic minor, and diminished intervals. D minor, C minor, and A minor are common drill keys.

What is the difference between UK drill and Chicago drill?

UK drill has a more complex triplet hi-hat pattern with irregular accents, darker and more orchestral melodies (Phrygian mode, flute, strings), and often a more cinematic arrangement. Chicago drill uses a simpler hi-hat pattern, heavier 808 emphasis, and a more straightforward loop structure.

How do I make drill hi-hats?

Use a triplet 16th grid (6 notes per beat). Within that grid, hit positions 1, 3, 4, 6 and leave 2 and 5 as rests for the basic drill hi-hat feel. Add velocity variation (±20–30%) and occasional 32nd-note rolls at bar ends. The irregular accent pattern and velocity variation are what give drill hats their characteristic loping feel.
