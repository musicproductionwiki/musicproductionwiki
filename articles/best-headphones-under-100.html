Best Headphones Under 100

Updated May 2026 — You do not need to spend $150 or more on headphones to start mixing and producing music seriously. The sub-$100 bracket contains some of the most respected and enduring studio headphone models ever made. The Sony MDR-7506 has appeared on more professional recording sessions than most producers could count, and it costs less than a dinner for two. The Audio-Technica ATH-M40x sits right at the ceiling of this price bracket and offers one of the most neutral frequency responses available at any price under $200.

What separates a studio headphone from a consumer model at this price point is frequency response accuracy and build durability. Consumer headphones are tuned to sound exciting — boosted bass, emphasized treble, a "V-shaped" response that makes pop music sound punchy and bright. This coloring is fun for casual listening but catastrophic for mixing. A mix that sounds balanced through a bass-boosted consumer headphone will sound thin and trebly on flat-response monitors. Studio headphones use a flatter, more revealing frequency response so that what sounds balanced in your headphones actually translates to other playback systems.

This guide covers every serious studio headphone option under $100 in 2026 — what each sounds like, who it is best for, and which one to buy depending on your primary use case: mixing, tracking, production, or broadcast monitoring.

1. Sony MDR-7506 — Best Overall (~$80)

The Sony MDR-7506 has been a professional studio staple since its introduction in 1991. That is over 35 years of continuous production, widespread adoption in broadcast, film, and music recording, and a reference point known to audio engineers around the world. If you visit a recording studio, a radio station, a film dubbing stage, or a live broadcast truck, there is a strong chance a pair of MDR-7506 headphones is somewhere in the room. This ubiquity is not accidental — it is earned through decades of reliable, consistent performance.

Sound character: The MDR-7506 has a slightly scooped mid-range — a gentle reduction in the 1–3 kHz range — and a boosted upper-mid and treble presence peak around 6–8 kHz. This makes the headphone revealing. Problems in the high-frequency range — sibilance on vocals, cymbal harshness, high-frequency mix imbalances — are easy to hear and easy to identify. The low-end is controlled and fairly accurate without the artificial enhancement found in consumer models. Bass extends adequately into the sub-bass region without sounding inflated or exaggerated.

The slightly hyped treble character of the MDR-7506 has a counterintuitive practical advantage: because it is so widely used across professional workflows, mixing engineers have decades of accumulated reference experience knowing exactly how to interpret what they hear through it. If your mix sounds balanced on an MDR-7506, it is a reliable indicator it will translate. If something sounds harsh or thin through the 7506, it almost certainly is.

Build quality: The MDR-7506 is closed-back with a foldable design and a coiled cable terminating in a 3.5mm plug with a screw-on 6.3mm adapter included. The ear cups are circumaural (over-ear) with synthetic pleather padding. Comfort is adequate for moderate sessions — most users find the clamping force firm but manageable, and the padding can become warm after 2–3 hours of continuous use. The headband is adjustable with a classic Sony articulating design. Build quality is genuinely durable for the price: most owners report years of daily use without significant failure, and replacement ear pads are widely available and inexpensive.

Specs: Frequency response 10Hz–20kHz, impedance 63 ohms, sensitivity 106 dB/mW, weight approximately 230g without cable. The 63-ohm impedance means the MDR-7506 drives easily from any audio interface headphone output, laptop, or phone without needing an external headphone amplifier.

Best for: Mixing reference, tracking (recording while monitoring backing tracks), broadcast monitoring, field recording, podcast production. Nearly any professional audio context where a reliable, consistent, widely-understood reference headphone is needed.

Limitations: The treble emphasis means the MDR-7506 can make high-frequency problems very obvious — which is the point, but it also means mixing exclusively on these headphones can lead engineers to over-attenuate the high end to compensate, resulting in dull-sounding mixes. Always cross-reference on speakers. The synthetic pleather ear pads degrade over time (typically 2–4 years of heavy use) and will need replacement — budget ~$15 for replacement pads at some point. The coiled cable, while durable, is thicker and heavier than a straight cable and can pull on the headphones during desk-based sessions.

Verdict: The MDR-7506 is the safest, most universally recommended studio headphone under $100. Its longevity in professional environments is a genuine endorsement. If you can only buy one pair of headphones for your studio and you want confidence that you are working with a known, trusted reference, this is the pick.

2. Audio-Technica ATH-M40x — Best for Mixing Accuracy (~$99)

The Audio-Technica ATH-M40x sits right at the ceiling of this price bracket and represents the most neutral, accurate frequency response available in a studio headphone under $100. Where the MDR-7506 has a known treble emphasis, the ATH-M40x offers a flatter response that gives a more truthful picture of what is actually in your mix — particularly in the critical 2–5 kHz mid-range region where mix clarity and intelligibility live.

Sound character: The ATH-M40x is notably flatter than both the MDR-7506 and the more widely discussed ATH-M50x. It does not have the M50x's bass-forward character or the MDR-7506's upper-treble peak. Instead, you get a relatively even response across the full spectrum with a mild lift in the upper mids that adds clarity without becoming fatiguing. Bass is accurate and present without being flattering. This neutrality is exactly what you want when making mixing decisions about EQ and tonal balance.

Build quality: The ATH-M40x uses the same chassis as the M50x — Audio-Technica's professional M-series design with a robust hinged yoke, rotating ear cups (90-degree swivel for single-ear monitoring), and a detachable cable system with a proprietary locking connector. Three cables are included: a straight 1.2–3m coiled cable, a straight 3m cable, and a straight 1.2m cable. The detachable cable system is a genuine practical advantage over the MDR-7506's hardwired coiled cable — when a cable fails (and they do eventually fail), you replace the cable, not the headphone.

Specs: Frequency response 15Hz–24kHz, impedance 35 ohms, sensitivity 98 dB/mW, weight approximately 240g without cable. The 35-ohm impedance makes these extremely easy to drive from any source.

Best for: Mixing and critical listening where frequency accuracy is the priority. Producers who want to make accurate EQ and tonal decisions without the treble colorations of the MDR-7506. Long sessions where the more neutral response reduces listening fatigue compared to brighter alternatives.

Limitations: The ATH-M40x does not have the MDR-7506's industry-standard status — fewer engineers have immediate intuitive knowledge of how to interpret its response. Because it is more neutral, it is slightly less useful as a diagnostic tool for catching harshness. The ear cup padding can be firm for some users in extended sessions. At ~$99, it is the most expensive pick in this guide; producers on a tighter budget will find better value elsewhere.

MDR-7506 vs. ATH-M40x: Choose the ATH-M40x if you want the most neutral, accurate response for mixing decisions. Choose the MDR-7506 if you want the widest professional recognition and an established reference point that is understood in any studio context. Both are excellent headphones — the choice depends on whether you prioritize neutrality or universal compatibility with professional reference workflows. For more on choosing between these and other mixing-focused headphones, see our best headphones for mixing guide.

3. Audio-Technica ATH-M20x — Best Budget Pick (~$49)

For producers who are just starting out and need a capable studio headphone without spending $80 or more, the Audio-Technica ATH-M20x is the strongest recommendation in the ~$49 price range. It is the entry-level model in Audio-Technica's M-series lineup, sharing design DNA with the M40x and M50x above it while making specific cost-reduction decisions that keep the price accessible without destroying the fundamental functionality.

Sound character: The ATH-M20x has a mild V-shaped frequency response — slightly boosted bass and slightly elevated treble with a mild mid-range scoop — but it is much less exaggerated than consumer headphones at a similar price. The bass is present and useful for gauging low-end content without being the inflated, bloated bass of a Beats or Sony XB-series consumer headphone. The treble is extended without being harsh. It is not as neutral as the M40x, but it is a legitimate studio headphone rather than a consumer product in studio clothing.

Build quality: The ATH-M20x uses a fixed (non-detachable) cable, which is the most significant build downgrade from the M40x. The cable is straight rather than coiled, and the ear cups do not rotate to the same degree as the higher models. The headband padding is adequate but thinner. The overall build is noticeably lighter in weight and feel than the MDR-7506 or M40x — this is not necessarily a negative for comfort, but it suggests less durability over extended daily professional use. For home studio use at this price point, the build is more than adequate.

Specs: Frequency response 15Hz–20kHz, impedance 47 ohms, sensitivity 96 dB/mW, weight approximately 190g without cable. Very easy to drive from any source.

Best for: First studio headphone purchase. Recording vocals or instruments (closed-back prevents bleed into microphone during tracking). Learning to identify mix issues on a neutral-leaning budget headphone. Producers building their first home recording setup on a limited budget alongside an entry-level audio interface.

Limitations: The fixed cable is the main practical limitation — if the cable fails, you are replacing the headphone. The frequency response is not as accurate as the M40x above it, so mix decisions made purely on the M20x should always be cross-referenced on other systems. Not as comfortable for marathon sessions as the MDR-7506 due to thinner ear cup padding.

Verdict: At ~$49, the ATH-M20x is the best value entry-level studio headphone available. Producers building a budget home studio should consider pairing these with an entry-level interface like the Focusrite Scarlett Solo — for a combined investment under $180, you have a genuine recording and monitoring setup capable of producing professional-quality results. 4. Samson SR850 — Best Soundstage for Production (~$40)

The Samson SR850 is the most compelling semi-open option in the under-$100 category and one of the most consistently recommended budget studio headphones in online producer communities. At ~$40, it punches significantly above its price for production, arrangement, and composition work where a wider, more natural-feeling soundstage is beneficial.

What semi-open means: Semi-open ear cups have a partially perforated or vented enclosure that allows some air and sound exchange with the room. This creates a soundstage that is wider and more natural-sounding than a fully closed-back design — instruments feel more spread out and less "in your head." The tradeoff is that semi-open headphones leak sound in both directions: some outside noise enters, and some of what you are listening to leaks out. This makes the SR850 unsuitable for tracking in a recording session (it will bleed into an open microphone), but ideal for production, composition, and mixing where isolation is not required.

Sound character: The SR850 has a relatively extended and detailed high-frequency response — some users find it slightly bright but generally in a revealing rather than fatiguing way. The low-end is less prominent than the MDR-7506 but accurate and controlled. The mid-range is clear and present. The defining characteristic is the soundstage: for a $40 headphone, the sense of width and depth is genuinely impressive and makes arranging multi-instrument productions feel more natural and less claustrophobic than closed-back alternatives at this price.

Build quality: The SR850 has a self-adjusting headband design and uses a fixed cable. Build materials are predominantly plastic but the construction is solid enough for home studio use. The velour ear pads are a genuine highlight at this price — velour is more breathable and comfortable for long sessions than the synthetic pleather found on most competing headphones in this range, and it does not seal as aggressively, which contributes to the open-feeling soundstage.

Specs: Frequency response 10Hz–30kHz, impedance 32 ohms, sensitivity 98 dB/mW. The 32-ohm impedance makes these exceptionally easy to drive.

Best for: Beat production and arrangement sessions where you want a natural, spacious soundstage. Producers who work primarily ITB (in the box) and are not recording live instruments that require isolation monitoring. Composers scoring to picture who want a less fatiguing open sound for long working sessions.

Limitations: Not suitable for tracking or recording sessions — the semi-open design will leak audio into open microphones. Sound isolation from external noise is minimal, making these impractical in noisy environments. The build quality, while adequate, is the most plasticky of the six picks. The fixed cable is a long-term durability concern.

Verdict: At ~$40, the Samson SR850 is an extraordinary value for producers who work primarily in production and arrangement contexts. If you are building beats, composing, or doing most of your listening work in a reasonably quiet environment and do not need isolation, these headphones deliver a soundstage experience that costs three to four times as much to replicate in a closed-back design.

5. AKG K72 — Best for Long Sessions (~$49)

The AKG K72 is AKG's entry-level closed-back studio headphone, designed as a professional monitoring tool for studio and broadcast use. AKG is an Austrian company with a long history in professional audio — their microphones (the C414 in particular) are widely considered among the best in the world, and this professional heritage informs the K72's approach to frequency response and build design even at the budget price point.

Sound character: The K72 has a warmer, slightly bass-forward frequency response compared to the MDR-7506 and ATH-M20x. The mid-range is smooth and present, and the treble is extended without the brightness peak found in the Sony MDR-7506. This warmer character is more forgiving of sibilant recordings and makes the K72 pleasant for extended listening sessions — it is the least fatiguing headphone of the six picks. The tradeoff is that the warmth reduces some high-frequency resolution: subtle issues in the top end of a mix are less immediately obvious than they are through the MDR-7506.

Build quality: The K72 uses AKG's classic self-adjusting headband — a floating, spring-loaded headband design that automatically distributes clamping force evenly across the head without requiring manual adjustment. This design is genuinely comfortable and significantly reduces listening fatigue during long sessions. The ear pads are large and plush with adequate circumaural depth. The cable is fixed and straight. Build quality is solid plastic — not the most premium feel but reliable for home studio use.

Specs: Frequency response 16Hz–20kHz, impedance 32 ohms, sensitivity 112 dB/mW (notably high sensitivity — these get loud easily), weight approximately 140g without cable. At 140g, the K72 is the lightest headphone in this guide, contributing to its comfort advantage.

Best for: Producers who do long production and mixing sessions and prioritize comfort over the last degree of frequency accuracy. Beginners who find the brightness of the MDR-7506 or the Superlux HD 681 fatiguing. Vocal recording sessions where a warmer monitoring sound helps the vocalist feel relaxed and comfortable.

Limitations: The warm character makes it less useful as a diagnostic tool for high-frequency mix problems. Not as widely used in professional environments as the MDR-7506, so there is less accumulated community reference knowledge about how to interpret its colorations. The fixed cable limits long-term repairability.

Verdict: The AKG K72 at ~$49 is the most comfortable headphone in this guide and an excellent choice for producers who spend long hours in headphones and find brighter options fatiguing. The warmer sound signature pairs well with vocal recording and production work. It is not the ideal choice for diagnostic mixing work, but for an all-day production session, it is the most physically pleasant headphone in the bracket.

6. Superlux HD 681 — Best Extreme Budget (~$35)

The Superlux HD 681 is the most extreme value proposition in this guide — a semi-open studio headphone that sells for ~$35 and has been recommended in producer and audio engineering communities for over a decade as a legitimate budget mixing tool. Superlux is a Taiwanese manufacturer that produces OEM headphones for various brands; the HD 681 is widely understood to be acoustically modeled on the AKG K240 — a semi-open studio headphone that sells for significantly more.

Sound character: The HD 681 has an extended treble response that is immediately noticeable — these are bright headphones with a significant presence in the 6–10 kHz region. This brightness makes them revealing and useful for hearing subtle high-frequency problems in mixes, but it also means they can be fatiguing on extended sessions, and some users find the treble emphasis tiring after 1–2 hours. The bass response is present and reasonably accurate. The mid-range is generally clear. Like the Samson SR850, the semi-open design creates a wider, more natural soundstage than closed-back headphones at a similar price.

Build quality: This is where the HD 681's budget limitations are most apparent. The construction uses lighter plastics than the other picks in this guide, the headband can feel less secure, and the clamping pressure is higher than ideal. The cable is fixed and non-detachable. On the positive side, the HD 681 comes with both velour and pleather ear pad sets, allowing you to tune comfort and sound slightly (velour pads typically reduce some of the treble peak). Replacement parts are available and inexpensive.

Specs: Frequency response 10Hz–30kHz, impedance 32 ohms, sensitivity 98 dB/mW. Very easy to drive.

Best for: Producers who want a second pair of reference headphones at minimal cost. Producers on a very tight budget who cannot stretch to $40 for the Samson SR850. A cross-reference tool to check mixes on a different-flavored response after working on the MDR-7506. Students and hobbyists experimenting with music production before committing to a more significant investment.

Limitations: Treble fatigue is a real concern for long sessions. Build quality is the weakest of all six picks and will not last as long under daily professional use. Semi-open design means no isolation and not suitable for tracking. The brighter response requires more interpretive experience to mix accurately through — less ideal for beginners than the AKG K72 or ATH-M20x.

Verdict: At ~$35, the Superlux HD 681 punches well above its price for critical listening and as a secondary reference tool. It is not the ideal first studio headphone for a beginner — the treble brightness can be misleading until you learn to compensate — but as a budget-conscious producer's second pair or a spare for tracking, it offers extraordinary value.

Buying Guide: How to Choose the Right Headphone for Your Workflow

Every headphone in this guide is a legitimate studio tool, but each suits a different primary use case. Understanding your workflow before buying prevents you from ending up with a semi-open headphone when you need isolation for tracking, or a warm, comfortable pair when you need diagnostic accuracy for mix decisions. Here is a breakdown by use case.

Choosing by Use Case

Tracking and recording: You need a closed-back headphone. Semi-open and open-back designs leak audio that will bleed into any open microphone in the room, ruining your recordings. The ATH-M20x and MDR-7506 are both excellent tracking headphones — comfortable enough for the vocalist or instrumentalist to wear for the duration of a recording session, and accurate enough to give a useful monitoring mix. The MDR-7506 is preferred for its slightly louder output and wider professional recognition; the ATH-M20x is the better value if budget is tight.

Mixing and critical listening: The ATH-M40x is the most accurate option in this guide for making tonal and EQ decisions. The MDR-7506 is the most widely used reference point, giving you a known coloration that the professional world has calibrated against for decades. Either can produce professional results when you understand their characteristic signatures. For a deeper exploration of headphone-based mixing techniques, our guide on mixing headphones vs. studio monitors covers the workflow tradeoffs in detail.

Beat production and arrangement: The Samson SR850 and AKG K72 are the most comfortable long-session options. The SR850's semi-open design creates a more natural-feeling production environment. The K72's self-adjusting headband and warmer response are ideal for marathon production sessions. If you are primarily building beats and not tracking live instruments, either of these is a strong choice.

Impedance and amplification: All six headphones in this guide have impedance of 32–63 ohms and are designed to be driven directly from audio interfaces, laptops, phones, and other consumer outputs. None of them require a dedicated headphone amplifier. If you are curious about higher-impedance headphones like the Beyerdynamic DT 990 Pro (250 ohms), those can deliver excellent results but require more powerful headphone outputs to reach adequate listening levels — something to consider if you upgrade your setup later. See our audio interface buying guide for information on headphone output quality in different interface tiers.

Open-back vs. closed-back vs. semi-open explained: Closed-back headphones have fully sealed ear cups that block outside sound and prevent audio from leaking out — essential for tracking and recording. Open-back headphones have fully perforated or mesh ear cups that allow air and sound to pass freely, creating the widest, most natural soundstage — but they are not suitable for recording (significant bleed) and provide no isolation from ambient noise. Semi-open is a compromise: partially vented cups that create a wider soundstage than closed designs without the full bleed of open-back models. Of the six picks in this guide, the MDR-7506, ATH-M40x, ATH-M20x, and AKG K72 are closed-back; the Samson SR850 and Superlux HD 681 are semi-open.

Headphones vs. studio monitors: Every headphone in this guide, regardless of price, creates an artificial stereo image inside your head rather than in a real acoustic space. Mixing only on headphones can lead to unbalanced stereo width, inaccurate bass perception, and stereo-field decisions that do not translate well to speaker playback. The best practice for mixing on a budget is to use headphones as your primary tool, but check your mix on speakers and consumer earbuds before finalizing. If you are building a complete monitoring setup, our guide on best studio monitors under $300 covers the speaker side of the equation.

Consumer and gaming headphones: Consumer headphones and gaming headsets are tuned for an enjoyable listening experience — enhanced bass, boosted treble, and a colored frequency response that makes music sound exciting. This frequency coloring makes them unreliable for mixing and critical listening. A mix that sounds balanced on a bass-boosted consumer headphone will sound thin and bright on flat-response monitors. If you are serious about producing music that sounds good everywhere, the switch to a studio headphone is non-negotiable — and at $40 for the Samson SR850 or $49 for the ATH-M20x, the entry point is genuinely accessible.

Ear pad replacement and long-term ownership: Synthetic pleather ear pads on closed-back headphones — particularly the MDR-7506 — degrade over time, typically within 2–4 years of regular use. They become tacky, crack, and eventually flake. Replacement ear pads for the MDR-7506 cost ~$15 and take about five minutes to swap. Factor this into the total cost of ownership when comparing models. Velour ear pads (found on the SR850 and available as aftermarket upgrades for many headphones) do not degrade in the same way but can compress and lose padding over time. Keeping your headphones in a case or bag when not in use significantly extends the life of ear pads and headband padding.

Using studio headphones in a complete home studio context: Headphones are one component of a complete home studio monitoring toolkit. For producers building out their first studio, the order of priority is typically: audio interface first (the signal chain foundation), then studio headphones (for mixing in environments where monitor speakers are impractical), then studio monitors when the room and budget allow. The headphones covered in this guide pair well with any of the entry-level audio interfaces in our best audio interfaces under $200 guide — all recommended interfaces have headphone outputs capable of driving any of the six picks in this guide without an external amplifier.

Practical Exercises

Beginner Exercise

The Reference Calibration Listen

Pick three well-produced commercial songs in the genre you work in — songs you know well and that sound great on your phone speaker. Play each one through your new studio headphones at a comfortable volume and make mental notes: does the bass sound bigger or smaller than you expect? Does the top end sound brighter or duller? This exercise builds the foundational reference memory that makes mixing on headphones reliable — you are mapping the colorations of your specific headphones against a known standard.

Intermediate Exercise

The Three-System Mix Check

Finish a full mix using only your studio headphones, then immediately play it back on three other systems: your computer speakers, a Bluetooth speaker or phone speaker, and a pair of consumer earbuds. Note every element that sounds different — bass levels, vocal presence, stereo width, and overall loudness. Go back to your mix and make adjustments, then repeat the three-system check. After five to ten sessions of this workflow, you will develop an intuitive understanding of how your headphones' colorations translate to other playback systems, which is the core skill for mixing on headphones professionally.

Advanced Exercise

Frequency Response Compensation Mapping

Using a free spectrum analyzer plugin (such as SPAN by Voxengo) loaded on a test track with pink noise, compare what you see versus what you hear through your studio headphones. Then load a commercially released, well-mastered reference track in the same genre you produce and analyze its spectrum while listening carefully. Document the frequency regions where your headphones seem to overemphasize or underemphasize relative to the spectrum reading — this creates your personal headphone compensation map. Apply this knowledge as a corrective mental model when making EQ decisions in future mixes, counteracting your specific headphone's colorations without using additional hardware or software.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ What are the best studio headphones under $100?
The Sony MDR-7506 (~$80) is the industry-standard recommendation — it has been a professional studio staple for over 35 years and delivers accurate, revealing sound for mixing and critical listening. The Audio-Technica ATH-M20x (~$49) is the best budget option for entry-level producers, and the Samson SR850 (~$40) is a semi-open standout for production and arrangement work.
FAQ What is the difference between open-back and closed-back headphones for music production?
Closed-back headphones have sealed ear cups that block outside sound and prevent audio from leaking out — essential for tracking and recording sessions where bleed into microphones is a concern. Open-back headphones have perforated or mesh ear cups that allow air and sound to pass freely, creating a wider, more natural soundstage — better for mixing and critical listening but not suitable for recording. Semi-open designs are a compromise between the two.
FAQ Are $100 headphones good enough for mixing?
Yes, with caveats. The Sony MDR-7506 and Audio-Technica ATH-M40x have been used on professional-level productions. The key limitation of all headphones for mixing — regardless of price — is that they create an artificial stereo image inside your head rather than in a real acoustic space. Always check mixes on speakers and earbuds before finalizing.
FAQ What impedance should studio headphones be for home studio use?
For home studio use powered by a laptop, audio interface headphone output, or phone, headphones with impedance between 32–80 ohms are ideal — they reach adequate volume from consumer-level outputs without a dedicated amplifier. All six headphones recommended in this guide fall in the 32–63 ohm range and do not require extra amplification.
FAQ Can I use gaming or consumer headphones for music production?
Consumer and gaming headsets are tuned for an enjoyable listening experience with enhanced bass and boosted treble — a colored frequency response that makes music sound exciting but unreliable for mixing. A mix that sounds balanced on a bass-boosted consumer headphone will sound thin and bright on flat-response monitors. Studio headphones use a flatter, more revealing frequency response so that what sounds balanced in your headphones will translate to other playback systems.
FAQ What is the best budget headphone for recording vocals?
For recording vocals — monitoring yourself while singing into a microphone — you need closed-back headphones to prevent the backing track from bleeding into the mic. The Audio-Technica ATH-M20x (~$49) and Sony MDR-7506 (~$80) are both excellent for this: comfortable enough for long sessions, closed-back to prevent bleed, and accurate enough to provide a usable monitoring mix.
FAQ Should I get the Sony MDR-7506 or Audio-Technica ATH-M40x?
The ATH-M40x (~$99) has a flatter, more neutral frequency response than the MDR-7506, which has a slightly scooped mid-range and boosted treble. For mixing accuracy, the M40x is arguably more neutral. For tracking and the widest professional compatibility, the MDR-7506's 35-year track record makes it hard to argue against. Choose the M40x for more neutral mixing; choose the MDR-7506 for the industry-standard reference point.
FAQ Do I need a headphone amplifier for studio headphones under $100?
No. All recommended headphones in the under-$100 category have impedance of 32–63 ohms and are designed to be driven directly from audio interfaces, laptops, phones, and other consumer outputs. A headphone amplifier is only necessary for high-impedance headphones (150 ohms and above) that require more voltage to reach proper listening levels.
