How To Make Future Bass

To make future bass, start with lush supersaw chords using extended voicings (7ths, 9ths, add2), layer multiple detuned oscillators with unison spread, add rhythmic vocal chops processed through formant shifting and granular effects, use aggressive sidechain compression on every melodic element tied to your kick, and build drops around the iconic pitch-rising synth lead technique. Tempo should sit between 140-170 BPM with half-time drum patterns creating the characteristic laid-back groove.

Future bass emerged in the early 2010s as a melodic evolution of trap and dubstep, combining emotional chord progressions with aggressive sound design and heavily processed vocals. Pioneered by producers like Flume, Illenium, San Holo, and Madeon, the genre has become one of the most popular styles in modern electronic music, dominating festival stages and streaming platforms alike. This comprehensive guide covers every aspect of future bass production, from initial sound design to final mixdown techniques.

Understanding the genre's core characteristics is essential before diving into production. Future bass typically operates at 140-170 BPM but employs half-time drum patterns that create a perceived tempo of 70-85 BPM, giving tracks their characteristic laid-back yet energetic feel. The harmonic content tends toward bright, uplifting progressions with extensive use of major 7th, major 9th, and add2 chords. Rhythmically, the genre relies heavily on sidechain compression, creating the pumping motion that defines the sound. Updated May 2026.

Chord Progressions and Harmonic Foundation

The emotional core of future bass lives in its chord progressions. Unlike harder EDM genres that often use simple triads, future bass demands extended harmonic vocabulary. The most common approach involves taking major or minor triads and extending them with 7ths, 9ths, and added 2nds to create lush, complex harmonies that sound simultaneously nostalgic and futuristic.

Start by selecting your key. Major keys work best for uplifting, euphoric future bass, while minor keys suit more emotional or melancholic productions. Common key choices include C major, D major, A minor, and F# minor—keys that sit comfortably in the middle range of most synthesizers and allow for rich voicings without excessive muddiness in the low-mid frequencies.

A classic future bass progression might be: Imaj7 - Vmaj7 - VIm7 - IVmaj7. In C major, this translates to Cmaj7 - Gmaj7 - Am7 - Fmaj7. Notice how every chord includes at least one extension beyond the basic triad. These extensions create harmonic richness and the slightly ambiguous, dreamy quality characteristic of the genre. The major 7th interval in particular produces that bittersweet, nostalgic sound that future bass fans recognize immediately.

Another effective approach uses slash chords and inversions to create smooth voice leading between changes. Instead of Cmaj7 - Fmaj7 - Am7 - Gmaj7, try C/E - F/A - Am7 - G/B. This technique keeps common tones between chords and creates bass movement that walks up or down by step, making progressions feel more cohesive and professionally crafted. The bass note movement becomes its own melodic line, adding an extra layer of musical interest.

When voicing these chords in your synthesizer, spread them across multiple octaves. A typical approach places the root note in the bass (often as a separate bass synth layer), the 3rd and 5th in the lower-mid range (around C3-G3), and the extensions (7ths, 9ths, 2nds) in the upper-mid to high range (C4-G5). This spacing prevents muddiness while maintaining harmonic richness. Avoid clustering all chord tones within a single octave, which creates a dense, unclear sound that won't translate well in the mix.

Rhythm plays an equally important role as harmony. Future bass chord progressions rarely sustain static throughout each bar. Instead, they use rhythmic chops, stutters, and gating to create movement. A common pattern divides chords into eighth-note or sixteenth-note stabs with varying lengths, often syncopated against the drum pattern. This rhythmic treatment, combined with heavy sidechain compression, creates the genre's characteristic bounce and energy.

Supersaw Synth Design and Sound Selection

The supersaw synthesizer forms the backbone of future bass sound design. This rich, wide, and harmonically dense timbre comes from layering multiple slightly detuned sawtooth oscillators, creating natural chorus and phase interactions that produce the characteristic shimmer and width. While hardware synthesizers like the Roland JP-8000 popularized the supersaw in the 1990s, modern software synthesizers offer far more control and sonic possibilities.

Serum by Xfer Records remains the industry standard for future bass production, used by virtually every major artist in the genre. Its visual interface makes it easy to see exactly what's happening with your waveforms, and its wavetable engine allows for complex timbral modulation that static oscillators cannot achieve. Alternatives include Vital (a free option with similar capabilities), Massive X, Omnisphere, and Pigments. Regardless of your choice, the principles remain the same.

To create a basic supersaw in Serum, start with the sawtooth waveform on oscillator A. Increase the unison voices to 7-9 voices, then adjust the detune parameter to taste—typically between 15-30 cents. This detune amount determines how wide and chorus-like your sound becomes. Lower values (10-15 cents) create a tighter, more focused sound suitable for lead lines, while higher values (25-40 cents) produce the massive, washy texture perfect for chord stabs. Set the blend parameter slightly toward the center-weighted option to maintain some mono compatibility while still achieving width.

Layer a second oscillator (oscillator B) using a different waveform—often a square wave, triangle, or custom wavetable with brighter harmonic content. Detune this oscillator 7-12 cents from oscillator A and use fewer unison voices (3-5). This creates additional harmonic complexity and prevents your sound from feeling one-dimensional. The slight detuning between oscillators A and B creates subtle beating that adds organic movement to sustained chords.

Filtering shapes your supersaw's character dramatically. Future bass typically uses a low-pass filter with the cutoff frequency set between 3 kHz and 8 kHz, allowing plenty of high-end sparkle while removing unnecessary digital harshness above 10 kHz. Apply moderate resonance (20-35%) to emphasize the cutoff frequency and add presence. Map an envelope to the filter cutoff with a medium attack (50-150 ms) and medium-fast decay (200-500 ms), creating the characteristic pluck and brightness curve that makes chords feel dynamic rather than static.

The amplitude envelope requires careful attention. Future bass chord stabs typically use a fast attack (0-10 ms), zero sustain, and a medium-long release (500-1500 ms). This envelope shape creates percussive initial transients followed by a rich tail that fills space between chord hits. When combined with sidechain compression, this envelope shape allows the kick to punch through while the synth tail creates sustained harmonic content.

Processing extends beyond the synthesizer itself. Route your supersaw through a chain of effects: saturation for harmonic excitement, chorus for additional width, and reverb for space. Use algorithmic reverb with a 1.5-3 second decay time, pre-delay between 20-40 ms to maintain transient clarity, and high-pass the reverb return around 300-500 Hz to prevent low-mid buildup. Many producers also apply subtle pitch modulation (vibrato) at 4-6 Hz with very low depth (3-8 cents) to add organic movement to sustained sounds.

Don't rely on a single preset or sound throughout your track. Layer 2-4 different supersaw patches with slightly different characteristics—one bright and aggressive, one soft and warm, one mid-focused for punch. Pan these layers differently (center, 30% left, 30% right) and apply different modulation rates to each. This layering approach creates the professional depth and complexity that distinguishes amateur productions from professional releases.

Vocal Chops and Processing Techniques

Vocal chops represent one of future bass's most recognizable elements. These heavily processed, often unintelligible vocal fragments create melodic and rhythmic hooks that complement the instrumental arrangement. The technique involves taking a vocal sample, slicing it into small segments, rearranging these segments into new melodic patterns, and processing them with pitch shifting, formant manipulation, and time-stretching effects.

Start by selecting appropriate source material. Look for vocal samples with clear, sustained notes and interesting timbral qualities. Acapellas from pop songs work well, as do royalty-free vocal sample packs specifically designed for electronic music production. Services like Splice offer extensive vocal libraries with pre-cleared samples you can legally use in commercial releases. Aim for clean, dry vocal recordings without heavy reverb or effects—you'll add these during processing.

Slice your vocal sample into individual words, syllables, or even phonemes using your DAW's audio editing tools. Most producers work in Ableton Live, FL Studio, or Logic Pro, each offering different approaches to slicing. Ableton's Simpler and Sampler devices excel at this workflow, allowing you to map slices across MIDI notes for easy triggering. FL Studio's Fruity Slicer provides similar functionality, while Logic's Quick Sampler offers intuitive slice-to-MIDI conversion.

Once sliced, arrange your vocal chops into a melodic pattern that complements your chord progression. Future bass vocal melodies typically follow the chord tones but add melodic interest between changes. A common approach creates call-and-response patterns between vocal chops and synthesizer leads, or uses vocals to fill empty spaces in the arrangement. Keep patterns relatively simple—4-8 note sequences work better than complex runs, as heavy processing can make busy patterns sound cluttered.

Pitch shifting is fundamental to vocal chop processing. Most producers shift individual chops up or down to match their track's melody, often transposing by perfect 4ths, perfect 5ths, or octaves to maintain natural-sounding intervals. However, future bass often pushes beyond natural-sounding pitch shifts. Transpose certain chops up 12-19 semitones for that characteristic high, chipmunk-like quality, or down 7-12 semitones for deeper, more masculine tones. These extreme shifts create the otherworldly quality that defines the genre.

Formant shifting adds another layer of manipulation. Formants are the resonant frequencies that give vowel sounds their characteristic quality, independent of pitch. When you pitch-shift without adjusting formants, voices sound unnaturally high or low (like speeding up or slowing down a tape). Formant shifting allows you to change pitch while maintaining natural-sounding vowel character, or intentionally mismatch formants and pitch for creative effects. Plugins like Little AlterBoy by Soundtoys ($99), Manipulator by Polyverse Music ($149), or the free Auburn Sounds Graillon offer precise formant control.

Time-stretching and granular processing create glitchy, stuttered effects. Use your DAW's time-stretch algorithm (set to complex or polyphonic mode for best vocal results) to extend or compress individual chops without changing pitch. Stretch a single syllable across 2-4 beats to create ethereal, sustained pads. Alternatively, compress a sustained note into rapid stutter rhythms for high-energy sections. Granular synthesis plugins like Granite by New Sonic Arts or Portal by Output take this further, breaking audio into tiny grains and rearranging them in real-time for experimental textures.

Pro Tip: Create contrasting vocal chop layers by processing the same source material differently. Layer a high-pitched, heavily formant-shifted version with a darker, subtly processed version panned opposite in the stereo field. This technique adds depth and prevents vocal elements from sounding thin or one-dimensional. The high version cuts through the mix and provides excitement, while the low version adds body and harmonic support.

Effects processing completes the vocal chop sound. Apply generous reverb—often large hall or plate algorithms with 2-4 second decay times and 30-50 ms pre-delay. High-pass the reverb return around 400-600 Hz to prevent muddiness. Add delay for rhythmic interest, typically eighth-note or dotted-eighth patterns synced to your project tempo. Filter the delay feedback with a high-pass around 800 Hz and a low-pass around 8 kHz so delayed repeats sit behind the original signal without cluttering the mix.

Sidechain compression affects vocal chops just as heavily as melodic synths. Route your kick drum to trigger a compressor on your vocal chop bus with aggressive settings: fast attack (0.1-1 ms), medium release (80-150 ms), and 6-10 dB of gain reduction. This creates the pumping rhythm that locks vocals to the groove. Some producers prefer using volume automation or dedicated sidechain plugins like Kickstart by Nicky Romero or Volumeshaper for more precise control over the pumping shape.

Don't overlook subtle automation. Automate formant shift amounts, pitch bend, or filter cutoff throughout the track to maintain interest and prevent static-sounding loops. A vocal chop might start with natural formants during the verse, then shift to extreme chipmunk processing for the drop, then settle into a mid-range character for the breakdown. These changes help define arrangement sections and maintain listener engagement throughout the track.

Drum Programming and Rhythmic Foundation

Future bass drum programming balances trap-influenced patterns with house music's driving energy. The genre typically runs at 140-170 BPM with half-time snare patterns, creating a perceived tempo around 70-85 BPM that feels simultaneously energetic and laid-back. This tempo dichotomy allows for rapid hi-hat patterns and synthesizer modulation while maintaining groove-focused, head-nodding rhythms.

Kick drum selection and processing requires careful attention. Future bass kicks need punch and sub-bass weight without overwhelming the mix. Look for samples with a strong fundamental frequency between 50-60 Hz, a clear transient attack, and minimal mid-range content that would conflict with bass synths. Many producers layer two or three kick samples: a punchy top layer for attack (high-pass filtered around 100-150 Hz), a pure sine wave sub-bass layer for low-end weight (low-pass filtered around 80-100 Hz), and sometimes a mid-range layer for body (band-passed between 150-400 Hz).

The kick pattern itself follows a four-on-the-floor structure in most future bass tracks, hitting on every quarter note (beats 1, 2, 3, and 4 of each bar). However, producers often add subtle variations: ghost kicks on the "and" of beat 2 or 4, or removing the kick on beat 4 of every second or fourth bar to create anticipation before drops. These variations prevent monotony while maintaining the reliable, driving pulse that makes tracks feel energetic and danceable.

Snare and clap sounds combine trap and dubstep influences. Use layered snares with crispy top-end (3-5 kHz presence boost), fat body (200-400 Hz), and generous reverb tails. The snare typically hits on beat 3 of each bar in half-time fashion, though adding ghost snares on the "e" or "and" of surrounding beats adds bounce and interest. Layer 2-3 different snare samples: a crispy electronic snare for top-end crack, a deeper acoustic snare for body, and a clap sample for width and presence.

Hi-hats and percussion provide energy and forward motion. Future bass hi-hat patterns often run at double-time or even quad-time relative to the kick and snare, playing sixteenth-note or thirty-second-note patterns that create rapid, shuffling rhythms. Use closed hi-hats for steady patterns and open hi-hats for accents and transitions. Vary hi-hat velocity extensively—avoid static, machine-gun patterns by creating dynamic variation between hits. Many producers draw in velocity curves that gradually increase through a phrase, building tension that releases when the pattern resets.

Percussion fills and transitions distinguish professional productions. Use rapid tom rolls, snare fills, or crash cymbals to mark section changes and prepare listeners for drops. A classic future bass fill technique uses a rapid snare roll (thirty-second notes) over the last half or full bar before a drop, often with increasing pitch (via pitch automation or layering progressively higher-tuned samples) and volume. Combine this with risers, white noise sweeps, and impact sounds for maximum effect.

Processing drums requires a different approach than melodic elements. Compress drums individually with moderate ratios (3:1 to 6:1) and fast attack times to control transients without destroying punch. Use parallel compression on drum busses by sending drums to a heavily compressed auxiliary track (10:1 ratio, aggressive settings) and blending this crushed signal under the clean original at 20-40% level. This technique adds density and power while maintaining natural dynamics.

Sidechain compression shapes the entire groove. Route your kick to trigger a compressor on virtually every other element in your mix: bass, synths, pads, and even vocals. This creates the characteristic pumping that makes future bass feel alive and breathing. Settings vary by element, but typical values include fast attack (0.1-1 ms), medium-fast release (80-200 ms), and 4-10 dB of gain reduction. The release time determines how quickly elements recover after each kick hit—shorter values create more aggressive pumping, while longer values create smoother, more subtle ducking.

Drum layering extends beyond basic kicks and snares. Add sub-bass hits timed with your kick for extra weight, percussion loops for organic texture, and foley sounds (impacts, whooshes, vinyl crackle) for character. Many producers layer field recordings or found sounds beneath traditional drum samples, creating unique textures that help tracks stand out. Just ensure these additional layers serve the groove rather than cluttering it—less is often more.

Bass Design and Low-End Management

Future bass occupies an interesting space regarding low-end content. Unlike dubstep or drum and bass, which feature aggressive, modulated bass lines as the primary focus, future bass often treats bass as supportive rather than dominant. The bass sits below the melodic elements, providing warmth and foundation without overpowering the emotional chord progressions and vocal elements that define the genre.

Two primary bass types appear in future bass: sub bass and mid bass. Sub bass covers the 30-80 Hz range, providing pure low-end weight you feel rather than hear. Mid bass occupies 80-300 Hz, adding harmonic content and definition that makes bass lines audible on smaller speakers and headphones. Most productions layer both types, often as separate tracks with different processing chains optimized for each frequency range.

For sub bass, synthesis is usually straightforward. Use a sine wave or triangle wave oscillator tuned to follow your chord progression's root notes. Keep the pattern simple—often just whole notes or half notes sustaining the root while chords change above. Avoid complex basslines in this register, as harmonic information becomes indistinct below 80 Hz and can create muddiness. Process sub bass minimally: perhaps subtle saturation to generate second-order harmonics that help it translate on small speakers, and limiting to control peaks.

Mid bass requires more complexity. Layer sawtooth or square wave oscillators with slight detuning (5-10 cents) to create width and interest. This layer can follow more complex patterns: root-fifth movement, passing tones, or even counter-melodies that complement the main chord progression. Filter mid bass with a low-pass around 300-400 Hz to prevent it from interfering with pad and synth elements. Apply moderate resonance to add presence at the filter cutoff frequency, helping bass cut through the mix.

A powerful technique involves creating rhythmic bass patterns that lock to your drum groove. Rather than sustaining notes, use short, plucky bass hits that align with kick drums and add syncopated notes between kicks. This approach, borrowed from UK garage and bassline house, creates forward motion and energy. Gate or cut long release tails using volume automation or a gate plugin, ensuring each bass note has defined start and end points that create rhythmic precision.

Sidechain compression affects bass differently than melodic elements. While you want aggressive ducking on synths and pads, bass typically receives gentler treatment. Apply moderate sidechain compression (4-6 dB gain reduction) with slightly slower attack (5-10 ms) and release (150-300 ms) times. This creates space for kicks without completely removing bass energy. Alternatively, use surgical EQ automation that dips 50-80 Hz by 3-6 dB whenever the kick hits, achieving similar results with more precise frequency control.

Distortion and saturation add harmonics that help bass translate across different playback systems. Most listeners won't have subwoofers or studio monitors capable of reproducing 40 Hz cleanly, but adding harmonic overtones through distortion creates upper partials (80 Hz, 120 Hz, 160 Hz) that suggest the fundamental frequency on smaller speakers. Use multiband saturation, applying heavier processing above 100 Hz while leaving the pure sub-bass relatively clean. Decapitator by Soundtoys, FabFilter Saturn, or even stock bit-crusher plugins work well for this purpose.

Stereo width requires caution in the low-end. Keep everything below 120 Hz completely mono to ensure club system compatibility and prevent phase cancellation issues. Many streaming services and playback systems sum low frequencies to mono, and stereo bass information can disappear entirely during this process. Use a stereo imaging plugin or mid-side EQ to enforce mono below 120 Hz, then gradually widen content between 120-300 Hz using subtle stereo chorus or haas delay effects. Above 300 Hz, you can apply more aggressive widening without compatibility concerns.

Arrangement determines bass effectiveness. During busy drop sections with dense synth chords and multiple melodic layers, keep bass simple—sustained roots that provide foundation without competing for attention. During breakdowns and verses where arrangements are sparser, bass can take a more melodic, complex role, filling empty space and providing interest. This dynamic approach ensures bass serves the song rather than following a one-size-fits-all pattern.

Arrangement Structure and Energy Management

Future bass tracks follow recognizable structural conventions borrowed from mainstream EDM while incorporating unique elements that define the genre. Understanding these conventions helps you create tracks that feel professionally arranged while leaving room for creative variation that makes your music distinctive.

A typical future bass arrangement structure includes: intro (8-16 bars), verse or build (16-32 bars), first drop (16-32 bars), breakdown or second verse (16-32 bars), second drop (16-32 bars), outro or final breakdown (8-16 bars). This structure totals roughly 3-4 minutes, the sweet spot for streaming and DJ sets. However, many successful tracks deviate from this formula—some include extended intros, pre-choruses, or third drops, while others condense sections for more immediate impact.

The intro establishes mood and key elements without overwhelming listeners. Start with atmospheric pads, filtered percussion, or vocal chops playing alone or in sparse combinations. Gradually introduce additional elements every 4-8 bars: add hi-hats, then kick, then bass, then more melodic content. This layering approach creates forward momentum and teaches listeners the song's primary motifs before the main sections arrive. Many producers include the main chord progression in the intro, either as a full statement or hinted through arpeggios and fragments.

Verses or build sections develop musical ideas while creating tension that resolves in the drop. Introduce vocal elements (lead vocals, vocal chops, or vocal samples) that provide human interest and emotional connection. Keep instrumental density moderate—perhaps kick, bass, one pad sound, and percussion, leaving space for vocals to shine. Gradually add elements as the verse progresses: additional percussion, riser effects, white noise builds, increasing filter cutoff frequencies, and pitch-rising synth swells. The last 4-8 bars before the drop intensify these build elements dramatically.

The drop represents the track's emotional and energetic peak. This is where all your carefully crafted elements—supersaw chords, vocal chops, aggressive sidechain compression, and driving drums—combine into maximum impact. Introduce the drop with an impact sound (crash cymbal, synthesized impact, or down-pitched hit) exactly on beat 1, often accompanied by a brief moment of silence (1/16th or 1/8th note) immediately before for dramatic effect. This pause creates anticipation and makes the drop hit harder when it arrives.

Within the drop itself, maintain interest through variation. Change chord voicings, alter synth timbres through filter modulation, add and remove layers, or introduce call-and-response patterns between different melodic elements. A common approach divides the 16-32 bar drop into two halves: the first half states your main musical idea clearly, while the second half introduces variation—perhaps removing certain elements, changing the drum pattern, or adding a counter-melody. This AB structure within the drop prevents listener fatigue while maintaining energy.

Breakdowns provide contrast and emotional breathing room between high-energy drops. Strip the arrangement back to basics: remove the kick, reduce bass presence, simplify chord patterns, and emphasize vocals or lead melodies. This contrast makes subsequent drops feel more impactful by comparison. Many producers introduce new melodic ideas or chord progressions during breakdowns, adding musical development that prevents tracks from feeling repetitive. Gradually rebuild energy during the last 8 bars of the breakdown, reintroducing drums and increasing density to transition smoothly into the next section.

Transitions between sections require careful attention. Use riser effects (white noise, filtered synths with ascending pitch, cymbal rolls) during the last 1-2 bars before section changes. Add downlifter effects (pitch-descending synths, reversed crash cymbals) immediately after section changes to emphasize the transition. Automate filter cutoffs to sweep upward during builds and downward after drops. Remove or filter out the kick on the last 1-2 beats before drops to create anticipation. These techniques signal section changes to listeners and maintain forward momentum throughout the track.

Energy management prevents listener fatigue. Not every moment should operate at maximum intensity—variation in density, frequency content, and rhythmic complexity creates a dynamic listening experience that holds attention. Map your track's energy across its full duration: intro starts low, builds toward the first drop (peak energy), relaxes during the breakdown (medium-low energy), builds toward the second drop (peak energy again, possibly exceeding the first drop), then gradually releases during the outro. This arc creates satisfying emotional development from start to finish.

Arrangement automation adds professional polish. Automate filter cutoffs, reverb send levels, delay feedback amounts, and stereo width throughout the track. These changes, often subtle, prevent static-sounding sections and create motion that maintains interest. For example, gradually open a low-pass filter during a 16-bar verse, creating a sense of development even while the basic elements remain constant. Or increase reverb send levels during breakdowns to create spaciousness, then reduce them during drops for impact and clarity.

Mixing and Mastering Considerations

Professional-sounding future bass requires careful mixing that balances the genre's competing demands: loud, aggressive drops with powerful low-end, while maintaining clarity and emotional impact in melodic elements. The dense harmonic content from supersaws, vocal chops, and multiple synth layers creates potential for frequency masking and muddiness if not addressed systematically.

Start with gain staging. Before applying any processing, adjust the fader levels of individual tracks so your master bus peaks around -6 to -10 dB during the loudest drop sections. This headroom prevents clipping and gives your master bus processing room to work effectively. Many beginners make the mistake of mixing at 0 dB or even clipping, then wondering why their final masters sound distorted or lifeless compared to professional releases.

Frequency separation prevents masking. Use subtractive EQ to carve out space for each element. High-pass nearly everything except kick, bass, and low pad elements—most instruments have no useful information below 100-200 Hz, and removing it creates clarity rather than thinness. Cut competing frequencies between elements: if your bass has presence around 150 Hz, notch that frequency from kick drums and pads. If your lead synth occupies 2-4 kHz, reduce that range in chord stabs and vocal chops. These surgical cuts, typically 2-4 dB with medium Q values, create space without obviously changing individual sounds.

Saturation and harmonic enhancement add weight and presence. Apply subtle saturation to individual synth busses (2-3 instances of different saturation types rather than one heavy application) to generate harmonic overtones that make sounds feel fuller and more present. Tape saturation emulations add warmth and gentle compression, while tube saturation creates more aggressive even-order harmonics. Transistor or diode clipping adds odd-order harmonics for brightness and edge. Experiment with different saturation types on different elements—no single approach works for everything.

Compression requires genre-specific application. Beyond the obvious sidechain compression tied to kicks, use gentle bus compression on groups of similar elements (all synths together, all percussion together, all bass elements together) with low ratios (2:1 to 3:1) and slow attack times that preserve transients. This glue compression subtly binds elements together, making them feel like a cohesive group rather than disparate parts. Apply 2-3 dB of gain reduction maximum—you're aiming for subtle cohesion, not obvious pumping.

Reverb creates space and depth but can easily muddy a mix. Use different reverb types for different elements: short rooms (0.5-1.2 seconds) for drums to add space without washing out rhythm, medium halls (1.5-2.5 seconds) for synth chords to add dimension, and large halls or plates (2.5-4 seconds) for vocal chops and lead elements to create atmosphere. Always high-pass reverb returns around 300-600 Hz to prevent low-mid buildup, and consider using pre-delay (20-50 ms) to maintain transient clarity. Mix reverb subtly—if you can clearly hear it, you've probably used too much.

Stereo width requires balance. Too narrow and mixes sound mono and unexciting; too wide and they lose mono compatibility and can sound phasey. Keep low-end (below 120 Hz) completely mono, as discussed earlier. Use mid-side EQ or stereo widening plugins to enhance width in the upper-midrange and high frequencies (2 kHz and above) where the ear is most sensitive to stereo information. A useful technique involves duplicating a synth track, hard panning the copies left and right, pitch-shifting one copy up 5-8 cents and the other down 5-8 cents, and blending these under the centered original at low levels. This creates natural-sounding width without phase issues.

Reference tracks guide mixing decisions. Choose 3-5 professionally released future bass tracks you admire and import them into your DAW as reference tracks. Regularly A/B compare your mix against these references, matching overall loudness levels for fair comparison. Listen for frequency balance (does your track have similar bass weight, midrange presence, and high-end sparkle?), stereo width (does your mix feel as wide and immersive?), and dynamics (does your track breathe and pump similarly?). This comparison reveals deficiencies in your mix that your ears might miss when listening in isolation.

Mastering brings the final polish. While professional mastering engineers offer valuable expertise and fresh ears, many producers master their own tracks for demos and self-releases. A basic mastering chain includes: corrective EQ to address any remaining frequency imbalances, multiband compression to control dynamics independently across frequency ranges, stereo enhancement to add final width, limiting to achieve competitive loudness, and final safety limiting to prevent any unexpected peaks.

Aim for -8 to -10 LUFS integrated loudness for streaming platforms (Spotify, Apple Music, YouTube), which will automatically turn down louder masters to match their target levels. Going significantly louder than -8 LUFS often sacrifices dynamics and punch for minimal gain, as streaming normalization negates the loudness advantage. Use a meter like Youlean Loudness Meter (free) or iZotope Insight to monitor LUFS values. Listen to your master on multiple playback systems—studio monitors, headphones, car stereo, phone speakers—to ensure it translates well across different listening environments.

Practical Exercises

Beginner Exercise

Create Your First Future Bass Chord Progression

Choose a major key and create an 8-bar chord progression using only major 7th and minor 7th chords. Use the progression Imaj7 - Vmaj7 - VIm7 - IVmaj7, then voice these chords across three octaves in a synthesizer. Add eighth-note rhythmic chops with varying lengths to create movement, and apply a basic sidechain compressor triggered by a four-on-the-floor kick pattern.

Intermediate Exercise

Design and Layer Three Unique Supersaw Patches

Create three different supersaw patches with distinct characteristics: one bright and aggressive (high filter cutoff, high detune), one warm and mid-focused (moderate cutoff, lower detune), and one wide and ambient (low cutoff, high detune, long release). Layer these three patches playing the same chord progression, pan them to different stereo positions, and process each through different reverb types. Practice adjusting the balance between layers to create depth and complexity in your sound.

Advanced Exercise

Produce a Complete 16-Bar Drop Section

Create a full future bass drop incorporating all elements: layered supersaw chords with extended voicings, processed vocal chops creating a counter-melody, half-time drum programming with detailed hi-hat patterns, layered kick and bass with proper frequency separation, aggressive sidechain compression on all melodic elements, and transitional effects. Focus on arrangement variation between the first 8 bars and second 8 bars, ensuring the section maintains interest while staying cohesive. Mix the section to -6 dB peak level with clear separation between all elements.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ What BPM should I use for future bass?
Future bass typically ranges from 140-170 BPM with half-time snare patterns, creating a perceived tempo of 70-85 BPM. The most common tempos are 150 BPM and 160 BPM, which provide the energetic feel while maintaining the characteristic laid-back groove when drums are programmed in half-time.
FAQ What is the best synthesizer for making future bass?
Xfer Serum is the industry standard for future bass production, used by virtually all major artists in the genre. Its wavetable engine and visual interface make complex sound design intuitive. Alternatives include Vital (free with similar capabilities), Native Instruments Massive X, Spectrasonics Omnisphere, and Arturia Pigments. The principles of layered, detuned oscillators apply regardless of synthesizer choice.
FAQ How do I make my future bass supersaws sound wider?
Increase unison voices to 7-9 per oscillator and set detune between 20-35 cents. Layer multiple synthesizer patches panned to different stereo positions (center, 30% left, 30% right). Use stereo chorus effects and mid-side processing to enhance width above 2 kHz while keeping content below 120 Hz completely mono for compatibility. Avoid over-widening, which can cause phase cancellation and weak mono playback.
FAQ What chord progressions work best for future bass?
Future bass favors extended chords including major 7ths, major 9ths, minor 7ths, and add2 chords. Common progressions include Imaj7-Vmaj7-VIm7-IVmaj7 or variations using slash chords for smooth voice leading. The key is using chord extensions beyond basic triads to create the lush, emotionally complex harmonies characteristic of the genre. Major keys create uplifting feelings while minor keys suit more melancholic productions.
FAQ How much sidechain compression should I use?
Apply aggressive sidechain compression (6-10 dB gain reduction) to all melodic elements—synths, pads, vocal chops—with fast attack (0.1-1 ms) and medium release (80-150 ms) tied to your kick drum. Bass receives gentler treatment with 4-6 dB reduction and slightly slower timing. The pumping effect from sidechain compression is fundamental to future bass, creating the characteristic breathing rhythm that defines the genre.
FAQ How do I process vocal chops to sound professional?
Slice vocals into small segments, pitch-shift individual chops to match your melody (often with extreme shifts of +12 to +19 semitones), apply formant shifting for unnatural character, and process with generous reverb (2-4 second decay) and synced delays. Use granular effects or time-stretching for glitchy textures. Layer different processing styles of the same source material and apply heavy sidechain compression tied to your kick drum.
FAQ Should future bass be loud when mastered?
Aim for -8 to -10 LUFS integrated loudness for streaming platforms, which automatically normalize audio to their target levels. Going much louder than -8 LUFS sacrifices dynamics and punch without real benefit, as streaming services turn down hot masters. Focus on maintaining dynamics and clarity rather than achieving maximum loudness. The pumping from sidechain compression creates perceived loudness and energy more effectively than peak limiting.
FAQ What is the typical arrangement structure for future bass?
A standard structure includes: intro (8-16 bars), verse/build (16-32 bars), first drop (16-32 bars), breakdown (16-32 bars), second drop (16-32 bars), and outro (8-16 bars), totaling 3-4 minutes. However, successful tracks often deviate from this formula. The key is creating energy contrast between high-intensity drops and lower-energy verses or breakdowns, with transitions using risers, downlifters, and filter automation to signal section changes.
