How to Make Afrobeats: Complete Production Guide (2026)

Updated May 2026 12 min read Technique Guide
Quick Answer: Afrobeats runs at 95–115 BPM and is defined by layered West African percussion (shekere, talking drum, agogo), minor pentatonic chord progressions, warm mid-forward bass lines, and melodic vocal hooks with call-and-response phrasing. FL Studio and Ableton Live are the dominant DAWs. The key to authentic Afrobeats production is polyrhythmic percussion layering — multiple rhythmic patterns running simultaneously — and a warm, slightly compressed mix with the vocals front and centre.

Afrobeats has gone from a regional Nigerian genre to a global commercial force. Burna Boy, Wizkid, Davido, Tems, and Rema have all achieved major international chart placements without compromising the genre's identity. African electronic music genres — Afrobeats, Amapiano, Afro House — grew by over 700% on LANDR in 2024. Afrobeats is now a significant production opportunity for any producer willing to understand its musical language.

This guide covers Afrobeats production from the ground up: the genre's history and related styles, BPM and feel, percussion construction, chord progressions and harmony, bass design, vocal production, DAW workflow, and a clear framework for producing Afrobeats authentically rather than superficially.

BPM, Feel, and the Afrobeats Groove

Afrobeats sits at 95–115 BPM. Most mainstream commercial Afrobeats productions land at 100–112 BPM — this range gives the genre its characteristic feel: danceable without being frantic, relaxed without being sleepy. The tempo works because the rhythmic density comes from layered percussion rather than tempo speed — at 105 BPM with four or five distinct percussion patterns running simultaneously, Afrobeats feels rhythmically full and active without rushing.

The groove in Afrobeats is not strictly quantised. Professional Afrobeats productions have a slightly loose, human feel in the percussion — particularly the hi-hats and auxiliary percussion. When programming drums in a DAW, introduce micro-timing variations: push certain hi-hat hits 10–15ms behind the grid, slightly anticipate ghost notes. The rigidly quantised drum machine feel sounds immediately wrong in Afrobeats context. The feel should breathe.

Afrobeats Percussion: The Foundation

The Core Drum Pattern

The Afrobeats kick pattern is not four-on-the-floor. It typically hits on beat 1, with a second hit somewhere in the second half of the bar — often on the "and" of beat 3 or beat 4. This syncopated kick placement, combined with the snare on beats 2 and 4 (sometimes with a ghost hit before beat 3), creates the characteristic forward-moving, slightly off-centre groove that defines Afrobeats.

The hi-hat pattern is dense — typically 16th notes with strong velocity variation. Not every 16th note is the same volume. The accented hits fall on the 8th notes, with the in-between 16th notes lighter. Occasional open hi-hat accents on the off-beats add rhythmic interest.

Layering African Percussion

The shekere (a gourd covered in a bead net) provides a distinctive shuffled rhythm that sits between the kick and hi-hat patterns. Programme shekere hits on the off-beats of beats 2 and 4, with additional hits scattered across the bar. The shekere's role is to create polyrhythm — a contrasting rhythm against the main drum pattern that makes the groove feel dense and alive without adding another drum.

The agogo bell (a double metal bell struck with a stick) provides a higher-pitched, ringing counter-rhythm. Traditional agogo patterns in West African music follow specific rhythmic clave patterns. In modern Afrobeats production, the agogo is often simplified — hitting on beats 1, the "and" of 2, beat 3, and the "and" of 4 creates a basic version that works well in a DAW context.

The talking drum (dundun) is used more selectively — as a fill element or as call-and-response punctuation with the main melodic elements. It is pitched and can be automated to slide in pitch for expression. In modern Afrobeats production, talking drum samples are often processed with EQ to sit in the upper mid-range (500Hz–3kHz) where they punch through the mix without clashing with the kick or bass.

Chord Progressions and Harmony

Afrobeats harmony leans heavily on minor tonality with a characteristic warmth — not the dark, menacing minor of phonk or the melancholic minor of sad R&B, but an energetic, melodic minor that feels simultaneously joyful and emotional. The most common harmonic approaches:

  • Two-chord vamp: Many Afrobeats tracks use just two chords — typically Im and IV (e.g., Am and Dm, or Gm and Cm). The two chords alternate throughout the track, and the vocal melody provides all the harmonic movement. This simplicity is intentional — it keeps the groove uncluttered.
  • Minor pentatonic progression: Im – bVII – bVI – bVII (e.g., Am – G – F – G). This loop is a staple of Afropop and Afrobeats. The flat 7th chord gives it warmth without being melancholic.
  • Four-chord minor cycle: Im – bVII – IV – V (e.g., Am – G – Dm – E). The V chord here creates forward momentum and a sense of resolution when returning to Im.
  • Modal harmony: Some Afrobeats producers use Dorian mode (a minor scale with a raised 6th) for its brighter quality. The Dorian Im – II (e.g., Am – B) movement is characteristic of certain Nigerian Afropop productions.

Bass Lines in Afrobeats

Afrobeats bass is warm, mid-present, and rhythmically active. Unlike Western pop where the bass often sustains on root notes, Afrobeats bass plays melodic patterns that interact with the percussion. Key characteristics:

  • Mid-presence: Afrobeats bass sits in the 80–250Hz range more prominently than Western bass. It is less about sub-bass rumble and more about warm, present mid-bass that you feel in the chest.
  • Rhythmic activity: The bass plays different notes on each beat rather than simply sustaining. It often mirrors or responds to the kick drum pattern — hitting with the kick, then playing a short melodic fill between kicks.
  • Portamento: Bass notes often slide between pitches rather than jumping — portamento at 50–100ms creates the organic, vocal quality of traditional bass playing.
  • Compression: Heavy compression (ratio 8:1, fast attack, slow release) keeps the bass consistent across its melodic movement without pump or inconsistency.
  • Saturation: Light saturation in the mid-range (150–800Hz) adds harmonic content that helps the bass cut through speakers without excessive volume.

Melodic Elements: Guitar, Synth, and Keys

The melodic layers in Afrobeats — guitars, keyboards, and synths — have a characteristic texture: warm, slightly compressed, with reverb that creates a sense of physical space rather than a digital wash. The most important melodic elements:

Guitar: Acoustic or electric guitar is central to many Afrobeats productions. The characteristic Afrobeats guitar plays short, rhythmic phrases in the upper-mid range — not extended solos, but percussive strumming patterns and single-note melodic hooks that sit between and around the vocal. Guitar parts often use a plucked, staccato style with the low strings muted. In production, compress the guitar heavily to even out picking dynamics, apply a warm EQ (boost slightly at 2–5kHz for string definition), and add a medium plate reverb.

Piano and Rhodes: Piano chord stabs — often played with a slightly funky rhythm, landing slightly ahead of or behind the beat — are a staple of Afropop production. Rhodes electric piano provides a warmer alternative. Both instruments typically play in the mid-to-upper range (C4 and above), leaving room for the bass below.

Synth leads and pads: Modern Afrobeats productions layer synth sounds beneath and around the acoustic elements. Soft analogue-style pads provide harmonic richness without cluttering the mix. Synth lead lines often double the vocal hook an octave up, adding brightness and catchiness to the chorus.

Vocal Production in Afrobeats

The vocal is the centrepiece of Afrobeats. Production choices should serve the vocal above everything else. Key vocal production approaches in Afrobeats:

  • Double tracking: Main vocal doubled with a slightly different performance layered underneath. The double is panned slightly off-centre (L or R) and mixed lower than the main vocal (typically -3 to -6dB below the lead).
  • Call and response: Backing vocal phrases respond to the lead vocal — either answering the lead's phrases or repeating a hook. This call-and-response structure comes from West African musical tradition and is characteristic of the genre.
  • Ad libs: Short, expressive vocal interjections between main phrases — "hey," "yeah," "aye" — add energy and character. These should be prominent enough to be heard but not compete with the main vocal.
  • Compression: Moderate compression on the lead vocal (ratio 3:1 to 4:1, medium attack to preserve consonants, fast release). Afrobeats vocals are typically more dynamically controlled than hip-hop but less compressed than pop.
  • Reverb: Medium hall or plate reverb with a pre-delay of 20–30ms. The pre-delay keeps the vocal present and immediate while the reverb adds space. Afrobeats vocals should feel in the room, not swimming in reverb.
  • Melodic pitch: Vocal melodies in Afrobeats are highly melodic — almost sung throughout rather than rapped or spoken. Even the more speech-like verses have strong melodic contour. Pitch correction should be used transparently rather than heavily processed.

Amapiano: The Related Subgenre

Amapiano deserves specific attention because it is the fastest-growing African genre globally as of 2026 and shares significant production DNA with Afrobeats while having its own distinct identity.

The defining elements of Amapiano production:

  • Log drum bass: The signature Amapiano element — a deep, resonant bass drum sound derived from a log drum sample, tuned to pitch and played melodically to create the bass line. It sits in the 60–150Hz range and is both rhythmic and harmonic simultaneously.
  • Shuffled percussion: Amapiano has a characteristic shuffle feel — the 16th notes between beats are pushed slightly late, creating a laid-back, rolling groove. This is different from straight 16th notes and is essential to the authentic Amapiano feel.
  • Jazz piano: Jazzy chord voicings on piano or Rhodes — extended chords (9ths, 11ths, 13ths) played with a loose, improvisatory feel. The piano in Amapiano is more harmonically complex than in mainstream Afrobeats.
  • BPM: 110–116 BPM — slightly faster than Afrobeats proper.
  • Atmosphere: Warm, hypnotic, with a sense of space. Amapiano mixes tend to have more reverb and air than Afrobeats, creating a floating quality.

DAW Workflow for Afrobeats

FL Studio: Set BPM to 105. Use the Step Sequencer for percussion programming — add kick, snare, hi-hat, and open hat channels first. Import shekere and agogo one-shot samples from your sample library into additional channels. Introduce velocity variation using FL's velocity rack (right-click on step sequencer). Build the chord progression in the Piano Roll using a piano VST (Keyscape, Spitfire LABS Piano, or FL's stock Steinway). Build bass in Piano Roll with portamento enabled on the Sampler or a bass VST. Import guitar samples or record live guitar through an interface.

Ableton Live: Set BPM to 105. Build drums in Drum Rack — programme kick and snare, then add auxiliary percussion channels for shekere, agogo, and talking drum. Use Ableton's groove pool to add subtle timing humanisation (try the MPC 60 or LM-1 groove templates at 40–60% intensity). Construct chord progressions in Piano Roll using the Wavetable synth for pad chords, and a sample-based instrument for guitar or piano. Use return tracks for shared plate reverb across melodic elements.

Producing Afrobeats Authentically: Key Principles

Afrobeats is a culturally specific genre with deep roots. Producing it authentically — rather than producing a shallow imitation — requires understanding its musical DNA. Three principles matter most:

Study the source material deeply. Listen to Burna Boy, Wizkid, Davido, Tems, Rema, Fireboy DML, and Asake with production ears. Identify how the percussion layers work, where the bass sits, how the vocals are treated, what the mix feels like. Listen to Nigerian Afropop from the early 2000s alongside 2024 productions to understand how the sound has evolved. The more you understand from the inside, the more authentic your productions will be.

Use real instruments where possible. Afrobeats produced with live guitar, live bass, and live percussion recordings has a quality that programmed versions struggle to match. Even one live element — a shekere recording, a guitar riff played by hand — adds authenticity that samples cannot fully replicate. Consider collaborating with musicians who have physical relationship with these instruments.

Serve the vocal. Every production decision in Afrobeats should serve the vocal performance. If a bass line or guitar riff competes with the vocal, it is wrong regardless of how good it sounds in isolation. Mix decisions should give the vocal absolute priority in frequency space, volume, and emotional clarity.

Practical Exercises

Beginner: Build Your First Afrobeats Groove

Set your DAW to 105 BPM. Programme a kick on beat 1 and the "and" of beat 3. Programme snare on beats 2 and 4. Add closed hi-hats on every 16th note with strong velocity variation (accent 8th notes at 100 velocity, 16th notes between at 60–70 velocity). Download free shekere samples from Spitfire LABS or Splice. Programme shekere hits on the off-beats between beats 1 and 2, and between beats 3 and 4. Loop this two-bar pattern and listen. Add slight timing randomisation to the shekere — push each hit 10–20ms behind the grid. This is the foundation of Afrobeats rhythm. Spend 30 minutes adjusting individual hit velocities until the pattern has a living, breathing feel rather than a mechanical grid.

Intermediate: Build an Afrobeats Chord Progression and Bass Line

Open your piano VST. Set a two-chord vamp: Am (A-C-E) for two bars, then Dm (D-F-A) for two bars. Loop this four-bar chord loop. Now build a bass line that moves between the roots: A for the Am section, D for the Dm section. But do not just hold the note — play a rhythmic pattern: hit the root, rest, hit the 5th, hit the root again. Enable portamento at 75ms for smooth slides between notes. Saturate the bass lightly (30% drive on a mild overdrive). Now add a simple agogo pattern above the groove: hit on beat 1, the "and" of 2, beat 3, the "and" of 4. Listen to how the three layers (percussion, bass, chords) interact rhythmically. Adjust each element until all three breathe together rather than fighting for attention.

Advanced: Full Afrobeats Production with Vocal Processing

Produce a complete Afrobeats beat including all percussion layers (kick, snare, hi-hat, shekere, agogo, optional talking drum), a melodic bass line, guitar or piano chord stabs, a synth pad layer, and an atmospheric element. Then either record a vocal melody or import a royalty-free vocal sample from Splice. Process the vocal: high-pass at 100Hz, gentle EQ boost at 3kHz for presence, compression at 4:1 with medium attack, plate reverb at 20ms pre-delay, and a doubling track panned at ±15ms for width. Mix the full arrangement and reference against a Wizkid or Burna Boy production from 2024. Identify the three biggest differences — most likely in the vocal treatment, bass warmth, and percussion feel — and specifically address each one before considering the exercise complete.

Frequently Asked Questions

What BPM is Afrobeats?

Afrobeats typically runs at 95–115 BPM, with most mainstream productions sitting around 100–112 BPM. Amapiano runs slightly faster at 108–116 BPM.

What is the difference between Afrobeats and Afrobeat?

Afrobeat (singular) is Fela Kuti's pioneering 1970s genre — jazz, funk, and West African music with political messaging. Afrobeats (plural) is the modern commercial genre from Nigeria encompassing Afropop, Afrofusion, and related contemporary styles.

What is Amapiano?

Amapiano is a South African genre from Johannesburg's townships characterised by log drum bass lines, jazzy piano chords, shuffled percussion, and a warm hypnotic atmosphere. It has become one of the fastest-growing genres globally alongside Afrobeats.

What DAW do Afrobeats producers use?

FL Studio and Ableton Live are the most widely used DAWs in Afrobeats production. FL Studio is particularly popular among West African producers. Logic Pro is common among producers on Mac.

What percussion instruments are used in Afrobeats?

Modern Afrobeats draws from West African traditional instruments: shekere (gourd rattle), agogo bell, talking drum, djembe, and conga — typically sampled or synthesised and combined with trap-influenced kicks, snares, and hi-hats.

What key is Afrobeats in?

Afrobeats frequently uses minor pentatonic and natural minor scales. D minor, G minor, and A minor are common. The harmonic language often blends Western chord progressions with African melodic sensibility. Two-chord vamps are very common.

What sample packs are best for Afrobeats production?

Loopmasters has dedicated Afrobeats packs. Splice has a growing Afrobeats collection. Native Instruments Komplete includes African percussion. Spitfire Audio LABS has free percussion samples that work well in Afrobeats contexts.

How do I add Afrobeats feel to a standard drum pattern?

Push hi-hat off-beats 10–15ms behind the grid. Layer shekere hits on off-beats against the main drum pattern. Add ghost snare hits at low velocity between main snare hits. The feel is polyrhythmic — multiple rhythms happening simultaneously, none dominating completely.
