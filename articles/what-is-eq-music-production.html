What Is Eq Music Production

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 · 9 min read · Verified May 7, 2026
Quick Answer — Updated May 2026

EQ (equalization) is the tool that shapes the tonal balance of audio by adjusting specific frequencies — cutting problematic ones, boosting desirable ones, and creating space so instruments don't clash. Every sound contains multiple frequencies from sub-bass to air (20Hz–20kHz), and EQ lets you control each range independently. It is one of the two most fundamental mixing tools alongside compression, and you cannot produce a professional mix without understanding it.

Updated May 2026 — EQ — equalization — is the tool you use to shape the tonal balance of audio. If compression controls how loud things are over time, EQ controls which frequencies are present and at what level. Together, they are the two most fundamental mixing tools in existence.

How EQ Works: Frequencies and the Spectrum

Sound is vibration. Every vibration has a frequency — the number of times per second it oscillates, measured in Hertz (Hz). Human hearing spans roughly 20Hz to 20,000Hz (20kHz). Every sound in music occupies some portion of this range, often multiple portions simultaneously.

An EQ is a filter — or a collection of filters — that changes the amplitude (volume) of specific frequency ranges. Boost a frequency and it gets louder relative to everything else. Cut a frequency and it gets quieter. The skill of EQ is knowing which frequencies to target and by how much.

Every EQ Filter Type Explained

Parametric EQs — the standard type in professional mixing — offer several filter shapes, each suited to different tasks. Understanding each one is essential before you read any EQ cheat sheet or plugin manual.

Filter TypeWhat It DoesTypical Use
High-Pass Filter (HPF)Removes frequencies below the cutoffCut rumble from vocals, guitars; almost every track in a mix
Low-Pass Filter (LPF)Removes frequencies above the cutoffTame harshness on room mics, remove digital artifacts from bass DI
Bell / PeakBoosts or cuts a band around a center frequencySurgical cuts, presence boosts, tone shaping
Shelf (High/Low)Boosts or cuts all frequencies above/below a set pointAdding air at 12kHz, warming up low end at 100Hz
Notch / Band-RejectDeep narrow cut at a specific frequencyRemoving 50/60Hz hum, resonances, feedback

The Q parameter (also called bandwidth) controls how wide or narrow the affected frequency range is around your chosen center frequency. A low Q (wide) affects a broad range for gentle, musical changes. A high Q (narrow) targets a very small range — ideal for surgical cuts to remove specific problem frequencies. Steep HPF/LPF slopes (18–24 dB/octave) cut more aggressively than shallow ones (6–12 dB/octave).

Subtractive vs. Additive EQ

Subtractive EQ means cutting frequencies rather than boosting them. Cuts are generally more transparent and musical than boosts — removing problematic frequencies is almost always better than boosting adjacent ones to compensate. The professional approach is to cut what doesn't belong rather than boost what's missing. This is the single most important EQ philosophy for beginners to internalize.

Additive EQ has its place — gentle high-shelf boosts for air, subtle presence bumps at 3–4kHz for vocal clarity — but large boosts tend to sound unnatural and can introduce phase issues. Keep boosts under +3 dB for most situations, and always sweep for resonances before boosting broad areas.

Pro Rule: EQ before compression in your signal chain. EQ first shapes the tone; the compressor then reacts to that shaped signal. If you compress first, the compressor may react heavily to frequencies you later EQ away. A second EQ after compression is valid for addressing any tonal changes the compressor introduces — a technique called EQ sandwich.

EQ Settings for Key Instruments

These are starting points — always trust your ears over fixed numbers. For a full deep-dive, see the guide on how to EQ vocals and the complete mixing EQ guide.

  • Vocals: HPF at 80–100Hz; cut 200–350Hz by 2–3 dB to reduce boxiness; cut 500–800Hz if nasal or honky; boost 2–5kHz for presence and intelligibility; gentle high-shelf boost at 10–14kHz for air.
  • Kick drum: Boost 50–80Hz for sub weight; cut 200–400Hz to remove mud; boost 3–5kHz for click and attack.
  • Bass guitar: HPF around 40Hz to remove sub rumble; boost 80–120Hz for fundamental body; cut 200–400Hz if muddy; boost 700Hz–1kHz for string definition.
  • Electric guitar: HPF at 100Hz; cut 200–400Hz for mud; boost 2–4kHz for presence and cut through the mix.
  • Acoustic guitar: HPF at 80Hz; cut 200–300Hz for boxiness; boost 5–8kHz for sparkle and string detail.
  • Snare: Cut 300–500Hz for ring; boost 200Hz for fatness; boost 5–8kHz for crack and snap.

For more drum-specific EQ techniques, the guide on how to use EQ on drums covers every drum element in detail.

Mid-Side EQ

Mid-Side (M/S) EQ processes the mid channel (mono center of the stereo image) and the side channels (the differences between left and right) independently. This lets you cut low end from the sides for a tighter, mono-compatible mix, boost presence in the mid channel for vocal clarity, or add air to the sides for stereo width — without affecting the other channel. It is an advanced technique most commonly applied on bus and master processing. Tools like FabFilter Pro-Q 4 make M/S EQ accessible and visual, which is one reason it has become an industry standard plugin.

Most Common EQ Mistakes

Beginners consistently make the same errors. Avoid these to immediately improve your mixes: boosting before cutting (always find and reduce problem frequencies first); using too narrow a Q for broad tonal moves; not high-pass filtering every track that doesn't need low end; EQing in solo rather than in context of the full mix; and making large boosts instead of gentle cuts. The best EQ plugins won't fix a bad approach — technique comes first.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ What does EQ do in music production?
EQ (equalization) adjusts the balance of different frequencies within an audio signal. It can boost or cut specific frequency ranges to shape the tonal character of sounds, fix problems like muddiness or harshness, and create space for different instruments to coexist in a mix without clashing.
FAQ What is the difference between parametric and graphic EQ?
A parametric EQ lets you control frequency, gain (boost/cut amount), and Q (bandwidth) for each band independently — making it the most flexible and precise type for mixing. A graphic EQ uses fixed frequency bands with only gain adjustment available — simpler but less flexible. Parametric EQ is standard in professional mixing.
FAQ What is a high-pass filter in EQ?
A high-pass filter (HPF) removes frequencies below a set point while allowing higher frequencies to pass through. It's used to eliminate low-end rumble, hum, and unwanted sub frequencies from sources that don't need them — like cutting everything below 80Hz on a guitar or below 100Hz on a vocal.
FAQ What is Q in EQ?
Q (also called bandwidth) controls how wide or narrow the affected frequency range is around your chosen center frequency. A low Q (wide) affects a broad range of frequencies for gentle, musical changes. A high Q (narrow) affects a very small range — ideal for surgical cuts to remove specific problem frequencies.
FAQ Should you EQ before or after compression?
The standard order is EQ before compression. EQ first shapes the tone of the signal, then the compressor reacts to that shaped tone. If you compress first, the compressor may react heavily to frequencies you later EQ away. However, a second EQ after compression can address tone changes introduced by the compression itself.
FAQ What frequencies should I cut on vocals?
Common vocal EQ moves: high-pass filter at 80–100Hz to remove rumble; cut 200–350Hz by 2–3 dB to reduce boxiness; cut 500–800Hz if the vocal sounds nasal or honky; boost 2–5kHz for presence and intelligibility; boost 10–14kHz gently for air and sparkle. Always make cuts first before adding any boosts.
FAQ What is subtractive EQ?
Subtractive EQ means cutting frequencies rather than boosting them. Cuts are generally more transparent and musical than boosts — removing problematic frequencies is almost always better than boosting adjacent ones to compensate. The professional approach is to cut what doesn't belong rather than boost what's missing.
FAQ What is mid-side EQ?
Mid-side (M/S) EQ processes the mid channel (mono center of the stereo image) and the side channels (the differences between left and right) independently. This lets you cut low end from the sides for a tighter mix, boost presence in the mid for vocal clarity, or add air to the sides for width — without affecting the other channel.
