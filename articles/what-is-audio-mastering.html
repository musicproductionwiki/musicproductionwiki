What Is Audio Mastering? Complete Guide

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2025 10 min read

Mastering is the final step in music production before distribution — the process of optimising a finished mix for playback across all listening systems and preparing it for release. It's also one of the most mystified stages of music production, often described in vague terms that make it sound like magic. It isn't. Understanding what mastering actually does makes you a better producer and helps you get better results whether you're mastering yourself or working with a professional.

What we'll cover: What mastering is and what it isn't, what happens during the mastering process step by step, loudness standards and integrated loudness targets, the difference between mix and master, stem mastering, AI mastering services, and when you actually need professional mastering.

What Mastering Is — and Isn't

Mastering is the process of taking a finished stereo mix and processing it to achieve three things: tonal consistency with commercial releases in the same genre, appropriate loudness for the target distribution platform, and technical correctness for the delivery format (streaming, vinyl, CD, broadcast).

What mastering is not: a fix for a bad mix. Mastering can make a good mix sound great. It cannot rescue a mix with fundamental problems — frequency imbalances, poor gain staging, clashing elements, or a poorly balanced arrangement. If a mix has these issues, mastering will make them louder and more apparent, not correct them. The phrase "we'll fix it in mastering" is a myth that costs producers time and money when they discover that mastering revealed problems rather than solving them.

Mastering also isn't magic. A good mastering engineer brings fresh ears, a calibrated listening environment, high-quality processing, and experience with the sonic characteristics of successful releases in your genre. These things produce measurably better results than mastering in the same room you mixed in, on the same speakers, immediately after finishing the mix. But the improvements are refinements, not transformations.

The Mastering Process Step by Step

Critical listening and assessment: A mastering engineer listens to the mix multiple times before touching anything. They identify frequency imbalances, dynamic inconsistencies, any technical issues (clicks, dropouts, clipping), and how the mix compares to reference tracks in the same genre. This listening happens on calibrated monitors in an acoustically treated room — the engineer's ability to hear accurately is the foundation of everything else.

Equalisation: Mastering EQ addresses broad tonal balance issues — too much low-mid buildup making the mix sound muddy, insufficient high-frequency content making it sound dull, excessive brightness making it fatiguing. Mastering EQ moves are typically subtle — fractions of a dB to a couple of dB across broad frequency ranges — because they affect the entire mix simultaneously. A 2 dB boost at 10 kHz lifts everything in that range: cymbals, vocals, guitars, synths, ambience. The impact of mastering EQ is therefore different in kind from mixing EQ on individual tracks.

Compression and dynamics processing: Mastering compression serves two purposes: controlling the overall dynamic range of the master for loudness optimisation, and adding cohesion — the glue that makes a well-mixed track feel like a single unified piece of audio rather than a collection of individual elements. Mastering compression ratios are low (1.5:1 to 3:1) with gentle attack and release settings that preserve the mix's dynamics while subtly controlling its peaks. Heavy mastering compression flattens dynamics and produces the loud, crushed sound associated with the "loudness war" releases of the late 1990s and 2000s.

Stereo imaging: Mastering tools can adjust the width and balance of the stereo image. Mid-side processing allows independent control of the center (mono) and sides of the stereo field. Widening the sides can make a mix feel more spacious; narrowing them increases mono compatibility. Checking mono compatibility — how the mix sounds when the stereo channels are summed to mono — is a standard part of mastering because many listening contexts (phone speakers, certain club systems, mono Bluetooth speakers) reproduce mono only.

Limiting: The limiter is the final processor in the mastering chain. It sets the ceiling — the maximum output level — and catches any peaks that would cause clipping on the delivery format. For streaming, the ceiling is typically -1 dBTP (true peak) or -0.3 dBTP to avoid inter-sample peaks that can cause distortion when decoded by streaming platform codecs. The amount of gain applied before the limiter — the loudness of the master — is calibrated to the integrated loudness target for the target platform.

Quality control and sequencing: Before delivery, mastering includes checking for any technical issues in the processed audio, setting the spacing between tracks on an album, ensuring track order is correct, and confirming metadata (track names, ISRC codes, UPC) is properly embedded. For digital delivery, the master is exported in the required format (WAV at 24-bit/44.1 kHz or 48 kHz for streaming, DDP image for CD, specific format for vinyl cutting).

Loudness Standards and LUFS

Streaming platforms normalise audio to a target integrated loudness level, measured in LUFS (Loudness Units relative to Full Scale). Tracks louder than the platform's target are turned down; tracks quieter are turned up (on some platforms). This normalisation makes the "louder sounds better" strategy of the loudness war counterproductive — a heavily limited, maximally loud master is turned down on Spotify to match the playback level of a more dynamic master, but sounds worse because of the dynamic compression applied to achieve that loudness.

PlatformTarget LoudnessTrue Peak Ceiling
Spotify-14 LUFS (integrated)-1 dBTP
Apple Music-16 LUFS (integrated)-1 dBTP
YouTube-14 LUFS (integrated)-1 dBTP
Tidal-14 LUFS (integrated)-1 dBTP
CDNo normalisation0 dBFS

The practical target for streaming masters: -14 LUFS integrated loudness with a -1 dBTP true peak ceiling. This target sits within the range where Spotify and YouTube won't turn the master down, preserves adequate dynamic range for the music to breathe, and is technically safe for streaming codecs. Genres with naturally high average loudness (EDM, hip-hop) often target -9 to -11 LUFS; genres where dynamics are central to the music (acoustic, classical, jazz) may target -16 to -20 LUFS. Match the reference tracks in your genre rather than an arbitrary target.

Stem Mastering

Stem mastering is an alternative to traditional full-mix mastering where the mix is delivered to the mastering engineer in stems — grouped submixes rather than a single stereo file. Typical stems: drums, bass, instruments, vocals, effects. The mastering engineer can process each stem independently before summing them to the master, giving more control over specific elements than full-mix mastering allows.

Stem mastering is more expensive than full-mix mastering (more files, more processing, more time) and requires more preparation from the producer. It's appropriate when: the mix has specific elements that would benefit from independent mastering processing (a vocal that needs separate loudness treatment, drums that need independent compression), the mix is being prepared for multiple formats with different stem requirements, or the producer wants to give the mastering engineer more flexibility without going back to the mix session.

AI Mastering Services

AI mastering services — LANDR, eMastered, Matchering, iZotope's Ozone Master Assistant — analyse uploaded audio and apply automated mastering processing. Quality has improved significantly and is competitive with budget human mastering for typical pop, electronic, and hip-hop tracks in conventional genres.

AI mastering works best when the mix is already well-balanced and just needs loudness optimisation and a final polish. It works less well on experimental music, unconventional mixes, or tracks where the mastering engineer needs to make creative decisions about the music's character rather than just optimising technical parameters. The $9–29/month pricing of AI mastering services makes them financially accessible for independent artists who release frequently.

DIY Mastering vs. Professional Mastering

DIY mastering — mastering your own music in your own studio — is viable and produces professional results when: you have calibrated studio monitors in an acoustically treated room, you're using quality mastering-grade tools (Ozone, FabFilter Pro-L2, Sonnox), you're referencing against commercial tracks in your genre, and you have enough distance from the mix to hear it objectively.

The primary limitations of DIY mastering are psychological (hard to hear a mix objectively after working on it for weeks) and acoustic (most home studios don't have the room treatment and monitor calibration of a professional mastering studio). Fresh ears from a professional mastering engineer who hasn't heard the mix before often catch things the producer's ears have become blind to.

Professional mastering is most justified for: commercially important releases where the budget exists, albums requiring consistent sequencing and tonal matching across many tracks, and vinyl masters (which have specific technical requirements including mono bass below 200 Hz and strict peak levels that require specialist knowledge).
