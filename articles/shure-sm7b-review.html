Shure SM7B Review: The Best Dynamic Mic for Vocals?

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2025 12 min read
Quick Verdict

The Shure SM7B is the most widely used microphone for home studio vocals, podcasting, and broadcast in the world, and its reputation is earned. It sounds excellent on a wide range of voices, rejects room noise better than any condenser, and is essentially indestructible. Its only real limitation is gain — it requires 60+ dB of clean preamp gain, meaning budget audio interfaces may introduce noise. At $399, it's an investment, but one that lasts decades.

The Shure SM7B has been in continuous production since 1973 in various iterations. It was used on Michael Jackson's Thriller, on countless broadcast studios, and is now the dominant microphone in home studio vocal recording and podcasting. Understanding why it became this ubiquitous — and what its actual limitations are — helps you decide whether it's the right microphone for your specific situation.

What we'll cover: The SM7B's frequency response and sound character, preamp gain requirements and solutions, studio vocal vs. broadcast use, the built-in controls, the SM7dB comparison, who the SM7B is best for, and who should consider alternatives.

Sound Character

The SM7B has a warm, smooth frequency response with a gentle presence boost in the upper midrange (around 5–8 kHz) when the presence switch is engaged. The low end is full and controlled — the proximity effect (bass boost from close-miking) adds weight to the fundamental of the voice without becoming boomy. The high frequencies are extended but smooth, without the airy brightness of large-diaphragm condenser microphones.

The character is often described as "radio ready" — the SM7B imparts a specific quality to voices that sounds immediately broadcast-appropriate. This quality comes from the frequency response shaping the voice toward the intelligibility and warmth characteristics valued in broadcast applications. On certain voices, the SM7B is extraordinarily flattering from the first take. On others — particularly very bright, high-frequency voices — the warmth can make them sound slightly dull.

The cardioid polar pattern provides excellent rear rejection — sound coming from behind the microphone is attenuated by 20+ dB. Combined with the dynamic capsule's inherently lower sensitivity to room acoustics compared to condenser microphones, the SM7B is significantly more forgiving of untreated recording environments than condenser alternatives. This is one of the primary reasons it dominates home studio use — it produces professional-quality vocal recordings in rooms that would expose a condenser's room sensitivity immediately.

The Gain Requirement

The SM7B's most frequently cited limitation is its low sensitivity — it produces a much lower output signal than condenser microphones, requiring 60+ dB of clean preamp gain to record at a healthy level without introducing preamp noise. This is not unique to the SM7B — it's a characteristic of passive dynamic microphones generally — but the SM7B is sensitive enough to the gain question that it needs to be addressed directly before purchase.

Budget audio interfaces (Focusrite Scarlett Solo, Behringer UM2) typically provide 50–55 dB of maximum gain, which is borderline insufficient for the SM7B. At maximum gain, these interfaces introduce noise that is audible on quiet passages and in the gaps between phrases. The resulting recording sounds like it's been recorded in a room with ambient hiss — tolerable but not professional.

Solutions: use an audio interface with 60+ dB of clean gain (the Focusrite Scarlett 2i2 4th Gen at 56 dB is borderline, the SSL 2+ at 62 dB is sufficient, the Universal Audio Apollo at 65+ dB is excellent). Alternatively, use an inline preamp booster — the Cloudlifter CL-1 ($149) adds 25 dB of clean gain before the signal reaches the interface, solving the gain problem without requiring a new interface. The SM7dB (see below) addresses this problem with a built-in preamp.

Built-In Controls

The SM7B includes two switches on the rear: a high-pass filter (rolls off below approximately 100 Hz) and a presence boost (adds approximately 5 dB of boost centred around 5–8 kHz). Both switches have three positions: flat, gradual rolloff/boost, and steeper rolloff/boost.

The high-pass filter is useful for reducing rumble from handling noise, HVAC systems, and proximity effect when recording close to the microphone. The presence boost adds clarity and forward projection — useful for voices that sound slightly dull or recessed on the SM7B. Many engineers prefer to EQ these adjustments in the DAW rather than using the hardware switches, since DAW EQ is adjustable after the fact. The switches are convenient for live use or when you want to commit to the sound at the source.

SM7B vs. SM7dB

Shure released the SM7dB in 2023 — an updated version with a built-in active preamp that provides 18 or 28 dB of additional gain before the signal leaves the microphone. This built-in gain solves the SM7B's primary limitation: the SM7dB works with any interface regardless of preamp gain capability.

The SM7dB sounds essentially identical to the SM7B with the internal preamp at minimum gain — the capsule and polar pattern are the same. At higher gain settings, there is a small amount of additional coloration from the internal active electronics, which most users find pleasant. The SM7dB requires USB power (via a Y-cable or separate power supply) to run the internal preamp.

At approximately $499 vs. $399 for the SM7B, the SM7dB costs $100 more than the SM7B but eliminates the need for a Cloudlifter ($149) or a high-gain interface upgrade. For anyone buying the SM7B alongside a budget interface, the SM7dB is frequently the more economical total solution.

Who the SM7B Is For

The SM7B is ideal for: home studio vocal recording in untreated or lightly treated rooms (the dynamic capsule's room rejection is a significant advantage), podcasting and broadcast voice-over (the warmth and broadcast character are genre-appropriate), voices that sound harsh or overly bright on condenser microphones (the SM7B's warmth often complements these voices better), and any application where durability and reliability matter (the SM7B is effectively indestructible under normal studio use).

The SM7B is less ideal for: recording acoustic instruments (the low sensitivity and limited high-frequency extension make it less versatile for instruments than condensers), recording in treated studios where condenser microphones can reach their full potential, and voices that already have a warm, low-frequency bias (the SM7B may make these sound muddier).

Verdict

The Shure SM7B is genuinely one of the best microphones for home studio vocal recording and podcasting available at any price. Its sound quality, build quality, and room rejection make it the practical first choice for the majority of home studio applications. The gain requirement is the only serious caveat — budget for a sufficient preamp or the SM7dB at purchase, and you have a microphone that will be in service for decades.

Score: 9.2/10
