What Is MIDI? The Complete Beginner's Explanation

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated April 2026 10 min read Beginner

MIDI — Musical Instrument Digital Interface — is a communication protocol that allows electronic musical instruments, computers, and software to talk to each other. It was standardized in 1983 and has remained essentially unchanged since, which is a testament to how elegantly its designers solved the problem of making different musical devices work together. Every synthesizer you program in your DAW, every drum pattern you sequence, every virtual instrument you play from a keyboard controller uses MIDI. Understanding what MIDI actually carries — and what it doesn't — changes how you use every tool in your studio.

What MIDI Is — and What It Isn't

The most important thing to understand about MIDI is what it does not carry: audio. MIDI is not sound. MIDI does not transmit audio signals. A MIDI cable carries no sound whatsoever — it carries only performance instructions, and those instructions tell a connected device or software instrument what notes to play, when to play them, how hard to play them, and a range of other performance parameters.

Think of MIDI as sheet music — a set of instructions that describe a musical performance without being the performance itself. The sheet music tells a pianist which notes to play, how long to hold them, and how loudly to play them. The sound you hear depends on the pianist interpreting those instructions on a specific piano. MIDI works the same way: a MIDI sequence tells a synthesizer which notes to play, when, and with what velocity. The sound you hear depends on the synthesizer interpreting those instructions — which is why the same MIDI sequence can produce completely different sounds when played through different instruments.

This distinction has profound creative implications. Because MIDI is instructions rather than audio, you can change the sound of a recorded MIDI performance at any time — simply by changing the instrument the MIDI is routed through — without re-recording anything. A piano melody recorded as MIDI can become a string melody, a synthesizer lead, or a marimba part by changing the instrument plugin receiving the MIDI data, with the original performance preserved exactly.

What MIDI Messages Actually Contain

MIDI communication consists of messages — small packets of digital data that describe specific events. Understanding the types of messages helps you understand what MIDI can and cannot express.

Note On messages tell a device to start playing a note. They contain three pieces of information: which note (a number from 0 to 127 corresponding to specific pitches on the musical scale), the velocity (how hard the key was struck, from 0 to 127 — 127 being maximum), and the MIDI channel (which of sixteen possible channels the message is on, allowing multiple instruments to receive MIDI simultaneously on the same connection without interfering with each other).

Note Off messages tell a device to stop playing a note. They contain the same note number and channel as the Note On message that started the note, signaling the device to release that specific pitch.

Control Change (CC) messages transmit continuous controller data — information about knob positions, slider movements, pedal positions, and other real-time performance controls. The modulation wheel on a keyboard controller sends CC1 messages. A sustain pedal sends CC64 messages. Volume control sends CC7 messages. CC messages allow expressive, real-time control of instruments and effects beyond simple note triggering.

Program Change messages tell a device to switch to a different preset or program — changing from one synthesizer patch to another, for example. Used primarily in live performance contexts where instruments need to switch sounds at specific points in a set.

Pitch Bend messages transmit pitch wheel data — the continuous pitch modulation typically generated by the pitch wheel on a keyboard controller. Pitch bend has a higher resolution than standard CC messages, allowing smooth, continuous pitch variation.

Aftertouch messages transmit pressure data — information about how hard a key is being pressed after the initial attack. Aftertouch allows expressive control of parameters like vibrato depth, filter cutoff, or volume by varying key pressure after a note is triggered, without lifting and re-striking the key.

MIDI Channels: The Routing System

MIDI supports sixteen channels — numbered 1 through 16 — on a single connection. Each channel is a separate communication lane: a device can transmit and receive on any channel, and multiple devices can share the same physical MIDI connection by operating on different channels simultaneously.

In a DAW, MIDI channels allow you to route different MIDI data to different instruments within the same session. A keyboard controller transmitting on channel 1 can trigger a piano plugin while a drum machine transmitting on channel 10 triggers a drum plugin — both connected to the same computer via the same USB cable, communicating on different channels without interfering with each other.

Channel 10 is traditionally reserved for percussion in the General MIDI specification — a standardization of instruments and their channel assignments used in early MIDI-based music and some electronic instruments. In modern DAW production, this convention is rarely followed rigidly, but it explains why some older hardware automatically routes percussion to channel 10.

MIDI Controllers: Translating Physical Performance into MIDI Data

A MIDI controller is any hardware device that generates MIDI data from physical interaction — keyboard keys, pads, knobs, sliders, breath, or other physical inputs. A MIDI controller produces no sound itself — it generates MIDI messages that are sent to a software instrument or hardware synthesizer, which generates the actual audio.

Keyboard controllers range from simple 25-key mini keyboards — portable, affordable, and sufficient for basic note input — to full 88-key weighted controllers that replicate the feel of an acoustic piano. The key consideration beyond size is key action: unweighted keys (synth action) are light and fast, suitable for synthesizer-style playing. Semi-weighted keys add some resistance. Fully weighted keys with hammer action replicate piano feel, which matters for pianists who rely on dynamic control through key weight.

Pad controllers — grids of pressure-sensitive pads — are the preferred input device for drum programming and beat-making. The Native Instruments Maschine, Akai MPC, and Ableton Push are the dominant pad controllers in music production, each offering different combinations of pad sensitivity, sequencing capability, and DAW integration. For producers who program drums, a pad controller dramatically improves the feel and expressiveness of drum programming compared to mouse-clicking notes into a piano roll.

MIDI controllers with knobs and sliders allow real-time control of DAW parameters, plugin controls, and mixer faders — a workflow particularly valuable for mixing and performance. Mapping physical controls to software parameters reduces mouse-heavy workflows and makes hands-on interaction with your DAW possible.

The Piano Roll: Editing MIDI in Your DAW

Every DAW represents MIDI data visually in a Piano Roll — a grid where the horizontal axis represents time and the vertical axis represents pitch, with a piano keyboard displayed along the left edge to indicate which pitches correspond to which rows. MIDI notes appear as horizontal bars — their position on the grid indicates pitch, their horizontal position indicates when they start, and their length indicates their duration.

The Piano Roll is where most MIDI editing happens: drawing new notes by clicking, moving existing notes by dragging, adjusting note length by dragging the right edge, and editing velocity by adjusting the bars below the note grid. Velocity is displayed as vertical bars beneath each note — taller bars indicate higher velocity (louder or harder-struck notes), shorter bars indicate lower velocity.

Quantization — snapping notes to a rhythmic grid — is the most commonly used Piano Roll tool for producers who program drums and sequences. Perfect quantization creates the mechanical precision of electronic music. Partial quantization — moving notes toward the grid without snapping them perfectly — preserves some of the human feel of a performance while correcting timing errors. The amount of quantization you apply is a creative decision that affects the feel of the music as much as any other production choice.

MIDI 2.0: The Future of the Protocol

After nearly forty years without significant change, MIDI received its first major update with the MIDI 2.0 specification, finalized in 2020 and gradually being implemented in hardware and software. MIDI 2.0 dramatically increases resolution — velocity, pitch bend, and controller data go from 7-bit (128 possible values) to 32-bit (over four billion possible values), allowing expressive nuance that was impossible in MIDI 1.0. It adds bidirectional communication, allowing devices to automatically configure themselves when connected. It introduces per-note expression — allowing each individual note to have its own pitch, vibrato, and timbre variation, rather than applying expression to all notes simultaneously.

MIDI 2.0 is backward compatible with MIDI 1.0 — devices support both standards and communicate at the highest level that both devices support. Its full adoption will take years, but the instruments and DAWs that implement it will offer expressive capabilities that bring digital music-making meaningfully closer to the nuance of acoustic performance.

The MIDI Realization That Changes Everything

MIDI is not a recording of a performance. It is a description of a performance. This means every MIDI performance you record is infinitely editable without quality loss — you can change any note, any velocity, any timing, any duration, at any point after recording, and the result is exactly as if you had performed it that way originally. There is no audio degradation from editing MIDI. No generation loss. No bouncing to audio required before editing. The performance exists as pure data until you need to render it as audio for export or mixing. This is the fundamental reason that MIDI-based production is so powerful — nothing is ever committed until you choose to commit it.
