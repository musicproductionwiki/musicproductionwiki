What Is MIDI

First published May 2026 · Updated May 2026
MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 Beginner → Advanced

MIDI is one of those acronyms that new producers encounter constantly — in their DAW, on their keyboard controller, in tutorials — without ever getting a genuinely clear explanation of what it actually is and why it matters. The result is producers who use MIDI every day without understanding it, which leads to confusion when something goes wrong and missed creative opportunities when they don't know what's possible.

Quick Answer

MIDI (Musical Instrument Digital Interface) is a communication protocol — a standardized set of instructions that musical devices use to talk to each other. When you press a key on a MIDI keyboard, it doesn't send audio — it sends a digital message that says "Note On, C4, velocity 90." A synthesizer, drum machine, or software instrument receives that message and decides how to respond. MIDI carries performance information (what notes, at what timing, at what velocity) but no sound itself. This distinction — MIDI as control data, not audio — is the foundation of understanding how modern music production works.

What you'll learn: What MIDI is and isn't, how MIDI messages work, the difference between MIDI and audio, MIDI controllers and what to look for, how MIDI works in your DAW, MIDI clock and synchronization, and the creative possibilities that understanding MIDI unlocks.

A Brief History — Why MIDI Exists Beginner

Before MIDI, electronic synthesizers from different manufacturers couldn't communicate with each other. A Moog synthesizer couldn't control a Roland drum machine. A Yamaha keyboard couldn't trigger a Sequential Circuits synthesizer. Every manufacturer used proprietary control voltages and gate signals with no standardization. This meant live performance with multiple synthesizers required extensive manual programming for each performance and made studio integration of multiple instruments extremely complex.

MIDI was developed collaboratively by Roland, Yamaha, Korg, and Sequential Circuits and publicly announced at the January 1983 NAMM show, where a Roland Jupiter-6 and a Sequential Circuits Prophet-600 were demonstrated playing together in real time — an unprecedented achievement that immediately transformed electronic music production. The MIDI 1.0 specification was simple, elegant, and deliberately open — any manufacturer could implement it without licensing fees, which drove rapid universal adoption.

MIDI 1.0 remained essentially unchanged for 40 years. In 2020, the MIDI Association released MIDI 2.0, which dramatically expands resolution (more precise velocity, pitch, and control data), adds bidirectional communication, and addresses limitations of the original standard. MIDI 2.0 is backwards-compatible and is beginning to appear in DAWs and hardware in 2024–2026.

How MIDI Messages Work Beginner

MIDI transmits digital messages — small packets of data — that describe musical events. The three most common message types are:

Note On: Sent when a key is pressed or a drum pad is struck. Contains three values: the MIDI channel (1–16, allowing up to 16 separate instruments on one MIDI connection), the note number (0–127, where 60 = middle C), and velocity (0–127, representing how hard the key was struck). A velocity of 0 is equivalent to a Note Off message.

Note Off: Sent when a key is released. Contains channel, note number, and release velocity (often ignored by receiving instruments).

Control Change (CC): Continuous control messages sent when knobs, faders, or wheels are moved. CC 1 is the modulation wheel. CC 7 is channel volume. CC 11 is expression. CC 64 is the sustain pedal. There are 128 CC numbers available per channel, allowing complex expressive control of synthesizer parameters in real time.

Other message types include: Program Change (selecting presets), Pitch Bend (wheel data with 14-bit resolution for smooth pitch curves), Aftertouch (pressure on keys after they're pressed), and system messages including MIDI Clock (for tempo synchronization).

MIDI vs Audio — The Critical Distinction Beginner

This is the most important conceptual distinction for producers new to MIDI. Audio is actual sound — the waveform data that your speakers convert back into pressure waves. MIDI is performance instructions — notes, timing, and control data — that tell a sound source what to play. MIDI itself makes no sound.

When you record MIDI in your DAW, you're recording a sequence of instructions. When you play it back, those instructions are sent to a software instrument (a virtual synthesizer, drum machine, or sample library), which interprets them and generates audio in real time. This means: you can change the sound of a recorded MIDI performance at any time, simply by changing which instrument is playing it. A piano performance recorded as MIDI can be instantly reassigned to play a string quartet, an organ, a synth bass, or anything else. The performance instructions are permanent; the sound they produce is flexible.

Audio recordings are fixed once captured — the waveform contains the actual sound of the original performance. You can process and edit audio, but you can't change what instrument made it without re-recording. MIDI's flexibility is one of the most powerful advantages of modern digital production over traditional recording.

MIDI Controllers — Your Physical Interface Beginner

A MIDI controller is a hardware device that generates MIDI data in response to physical interaction. The most common type is a keyboard controller — a piano-style keyboard that sends Note On, Note Off, and velocity data when keys are pressed. These range from 25-key mini controllers for portable production to 88-key full-size weighted keyboards for pianists who need realistic key action.

Pad controllers (Akai MPC, Native Instruments Maschine, Ableton Push) have velocity-sensitive pads for drum programming and triggering samples. Grid controllers (Ableton Push, Novation Launchpad) have matrices of buttons for triggering clips and patterns in Ableton's Session View. MIDI wind controllers (Akai EWI) send breath and fingering data for realistic woodwind performance. All of these send MIDI data — no audio, just control messages.

When choosing a MIDI controller, the most important considerations are: key count and key action (weighted keys for pianists, light synth-action for quick programming), pad sensitivity for drum programming, included software (most controllers bundle with useful DAW integrations), and the quality of the included MIDI implementation (some controllers send limited CC data, others are fully programmable).

MIDI in Your DAW Intermediate

Every major DAW handles MIDI through a similar architecture. MIDI tracks receive MIDI input and route it to an instrument plug-in that generates audio. The MIDI editor (Piano Roll in most DAWs) displays recorded MIDI as colored blocks on a grid — the horizontal position represents time, the vertical position represents pitch, and the width represents note duration.

In the Piano Roll, you can: draw new notes manually, move existing notes to change their pitch or timing, adjust note velocity by changing the height of velocity bars below each note, quantize timing to correct sloppy playing, and edit any parameter in extreme detail. MIDI editing is non-destructive — changes to MIDI data don't affect the original recorded data in the way that audio editing does.

MIDI Quantize is one of the most used but most misused MIDI features. Quantizing snaps MIDI notes to a grid (eighth notes, sixteenth notes, etc.) to correct timing. 100% quantization removes all human timing nuance and can make performances sound robotic. Many producers use 50–75% quantization, which corrects obvious errors while preserving some of the natural timing feel. Swing quantization adds a groove by delaying every other grid position slightly, recreating the feel of a jazz drummer's swing triplets or an MPC's signature groove.

MIDI Channels Explained

MIDI transmits on up to 16 independent channels simultaneously. Think of channels as separate lanes — channel 1 can carry drum data, channel 2 bass data, channel 3 melody data. Each channel can control a different instrument. Most DAW workflows manage this automatically (each MIDI track sends on its own channel), but understanding channels is essential when working with external hardware synthesizers or complex routing setups.

Creative MIDI Applications Intermediate

MIDI arpeggiators take held chord notes and play them sequentially in patterns — up, down, up-down, random — at a synchronized rate. Pressing and holding three notes of a chord can produce a running eight-note melodic pattern. Most synthesizers and DAWs include arpeggiators; they're one of the fastest ways to generate melodic movement from minimal input.

MIDI effects process MIDI data before it reaches the instrument — transposing notes, adding chords below each note, delaying notes by rhythmic amounts, or adding random pitch variations for humanization. Ableton Live's MIDI effect rack is particularly powerful for creative MIDI processing.

CC automation — recording Control Change messages in real time — is how producers create dynamic filter sweeps, volume fades, modulation changes, and complex evolving synthesizer textures through automated parameter changes rather than static settings.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is MIDI?

MIDI (Musical Instrument Digital Interface) is a communication protocol that allows electronic instruments, computers, and audio software to send and receive performance data. Unlike audio, MIDI doesn't carry sound — it carries instructions: which note was played, how hard it was struck, how long it was held, and other performance parameters. A MIDI note message contains the note number (pitch), velocity (how hard it was played), and timing — the instrument receiving the MIDI interprets these and produces the actual sound.

What is the difference between MIDI and audio?

Audio is recorded sound — a waveform representing actual acoustic or electronic vibrations. Once recorded, audio is fixed: you can edit its level, timing, and some pitch characteristics, but you can't change the instrument or the underlying notes. MIDI is performance data — instructions that can be sent to any instrument. You can change the tempo, key, velocity, timing, and instrument of a MIDI performance at any time without quality loss. MIDI is infinitely editable; audio is a fixed recording of a moment.

What is a MIDI controller?

A MIDI controller is a hardware device that generates MIDI data when you interact with it — without producing sound itself. Piano-style keyboards, drum pads, wind controllers, and guitar-style controllers are all MIDI controllers. When you press a key, the controller sends a MIDI note-on message with note number and velocity to your DAW or hardware synthesizer, which then generates the sound. MIDI controllers give physical, expressive control over software instruments.

What is MIDI velocity?

MIDI velocity represents how hard a key, pad, or button was pressed — on a scale of 0 to 127. Velocity 1 is the softest; velocity 127 is the hardest. Instruments respond to velocity by changing volume, brightness, timbre, or other parameters. A piano played at velocity 30 sounds soft and quiet; at velocity 110 it sounds loud and bright. Velocity humanises MIDI performances — consistently uniform velocity (all notes at 100) sounds mechanical; varied velocity sounds natural and expressive.

What are MIDI channels?

MIDI has 16 channels, each of which can carry independent performance data simultaneously over a single cable or connection. Each MIDI channel can be assigned to a different instrument — channel 1 plays piano, channel 2 plays bass, channel 10 traditionally plays drums. This allows a single MIDI connection to control 16 separate instruments simultaneously. In modern DAW production, MIDI channels are less critical because each software instrument track handles its own MIDI routing.

What is a MIDI note number?

MIDI note numbers are integers from 0 to 127 representing pitches across the musical range. Middle C is MIDI note 60. Each semitone is one MIDI note number — C# above middle C is 61, D is 62, and so on. The standard 88-key piano ranges from MIDI notes 21 (A0) to 108 (C8). MIDI note numbers allow any pitch to be specified precisely as a number, making MIDI entirely language-independent and universally compatible across all software and hardware.

What is MIDI CC (Control Change)?

MIDI CC (Control Change) messages are a type of MIDI data beyond note on/off — they control continuous parameters like modulation wheel position (CC1), expression (CC11), sustain pedal (CC64), pan position (CC10), and volume (CC7). Each CC number from 0-127 represents a different parameter. When you move a modulation wheel, your controller sends CC1 messages with values from 0-127 representing the wheel's position. CC messages allow real-time, continuous control of instrument and effect parameters.

Do I need a MIDI controller to make music?

No — MIDI can be drawn in using your DAW's piano roll editor with a mouse or trackpad. Many professional producers work primarily with drawn MIDI rather than performed MIDI. However, a MIDI controller makes the process more musical and expressive: playing notes feels more natural than clicking them, velocity is captured in real time, and physical interaction with hardware encourages more intuitive creative decisions. For beginners, a basic 25-key MIDI keyboard is a worthwhile investment even if piano skills are limited.
