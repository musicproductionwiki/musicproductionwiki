What Is Midi

Quick Answer — Updated May 2026

MIDI (Musical Instrument Digital Interface) is a protocol that lets electronic instruments, controllers, and software communicate by sending performance data — notes, velocity, timing, and control changes — rather than audio. A MIDI signal contains no sound itself; it tells a synthesizer or plugin what to play, and that instrument generates the actual audio. It is the foundational language of every DAW, software synthesizer, and digital music workflow.

Updated May 2026

MIDI — Musical Instrument Digital Interface — is the foundational language that allows electronic music instruments, computers, and software to communicate with each other. Without MIDI, modern music production as we know it would not exist. Every software synthesizer, every drum machine plugin, every virtual instrument you use in your DAW responds to MIDI. Every MIDI keyboard, every pad controller, every arpeggiator, every DAW piano roll — all of it speaks MIDI.

MIDI Is Performance Data, Not Audio

The most important thing to understand about MIDI is what it isn't: MIDI is not audio. A MIDI signal contains no sound whatsoever. It is a stream of digital instructions that tells a sound-generating device what to do.

Think of sheet music. A score tells a performer which notes to play, how long to hold them, how loudly to play them. But the sheet music itself makes no sound — that depends entirely on which instrument performs it. MIDI works exactly the same way. A MIDI clip in your DAW might say: note 60 (middle C) starts at 0:00, plays at velocity 84, holds for 0.5 seconds. That same clip routed to a piano plugin sounds like a piano. Routed to a synthesizer, it sounds like a synth. Routed to a string library, it sounds like strings.

This flexibility is MIDI's superpower. You can compose an entire production using MIDI, then change every sound without changing a single note — just reassign the plugins. You can transpose a melody by selecting all notes and shifting them. You can fix timing without re-recording. None of this is possible with recorded audio. For a deeper comparison, see our guide on MIDI vs audio explained.

A Brief History of MIDI

MIDI was developed in 1981–1982 by a coalition of electronic instrument manufacturers — Roland, Korg, Oberheim, Sequential Circuits, and others — who recognized that a universal communication standard would benefit the entire industry. Before MIDI, electronic instruments from different manufacturers couldn't talk to each other.

The MIDI 1.0 specification was finalized in 1983. The first public demonstration was at the 1983 NAMM show, where a Roland JP-6 communicated with a Sequential Circuits Prophet-600 in real time — two competing products from rival companies, linked by a single cable. The crowd was astonished. MIDI 1.0 has remained essentially unchanged since that year, which is a remarkable feat of engineering longevity. A MIDI keyboard from 1985 plugged into a modern DAW still works.

Core MIDI Messages: Notes, Velocity, and Channels

MIDI communicates through structured messages. The most fundamental are Note On and Note Off — telling an instrument to start or stop a note. Every note message also carries a velocity value from 1 to 127, representing how hard the key was pressed. Higher velocity typically produces a louder, brighter, or more intense sound, and it is one of the most critical parameters for making programmed performances feel human.

MIDI uses 16 channels, numbered 1–16, allowing a single MIDI connection to control up to 16 independent instruments simultaneously. Channel 10 has historically been reserved for drums by the General MIDI convention. In a DAW, channels let you route specific MIDI data to specific tracks or instruments with precision. Learning how to use MIDI in your DAW is one of the highest-leverage skills a producer can develop.

MIDI CC: Controlling Parameters Over Time

Beyond notes, MIDI Control Change (CC) messages let you control any parameter on an instrument or plugin — modulation, filter cutoff, volume, panning, sustain pedal state, and more. Automating CC values over time in your DAW creates evolving, expressive performances: filter sweeps, vibrato builds, volume swells. Most MIDI controllers expose knobs and faders that transmit CC data in real time, making live automation feel natural and musical.

Producer Tip: Over-quantizing MIDI kills the feel of a performance. Use partial quantization (50–70%) to nudge notes toward the grid while preserving human timing variation. Most DAWs offer swing and groove settings that add rhythmic life back to fully quantized patterns.

MIDI Controllers and Connection Types

A MIDI controller is any hardware device that generates MIDI data. Keyboard controllers generate MIDI when you press keys. Pad controllers generate MIDI when you hit pads. Wind controllers, guitar MIDI systems, drum pads, and foot pedals can all generate MIDI. None of them produce sound on their own — they send data to an instrument or plugin that generates audio. If you're choosing between input styles, our comparison of MIDI keyboard vs pad controller breaks down which suits different production workflows.

MIDI connections come in three main types: the classic 5-pin DIN cable (the original hardware standard), USB (the most common modern connection, carrying MIDI directly to your computer), and Bluetooth LE MIDI (wireless, supported on iOS and increasingly on desktop DAWs). USB has become the default for studio use — most modern controllers are class-compliant, meaning no driver installation is needed.

MPE and MIDI 2.0: The Future of MIDI

MPE (MIDI Polyphonic Expression) extends standard MIDI to allow per-note expression. In standard MIDI, pitch bend affects all notes on a channel simultaneously. MPE assigns each note its own channel, so it can have independent pitch bend, pressure, and slide data. This means you can bend one note while holding others stable — mimicking the expressive capability of guitar or violin. MPE is supported by controllers like the Roli Seaboard and LinnStrument, and by a growing list of plugins and DAWs.

MIDI 2.0 was ratified in 2020 and is gradually being adopted by manufacturers and software developers. It addresses the core limitation of MIDI 1.0 — the 7-bit resolution of most parameters (128 steps) — expanding to 32-bit resolution for ultra-fine control. MIDI 2.0 also adds bidirectional communication, allowing devices to negotiate capabilities with each other automatically. Full DAW and hardware adoption is ongoing as of 2026, but the foundation is set for the next era of expressive music production.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ What is MIDI in music production?
MIDI (Musical Instrument Digital Interface) is a protocol for communicating musical performance data — notes, velocity, timing, and control changes — between electronic instruments, computers, and software. A MIDI signal contains no audio; it contains instructions that tell a sound-generating device what to play, and that device produces the actual sound.
FAQ What is the difference between MIDI and audio?
Audio is actual sound data — a recording of real sound waves stored as digital information. MIDI is performance data — instructions about what notes to play, at what velocity, for how long. Audio sounds the same every time; MIDI can be routed to any instrument or plugin, completely changing the sound without re-recording anything.
FAQ What is a MIDI controller?
A MIDI controller is a hardware device that generates MIDI data — keyboards, pad controllers, wind controllers, and foot pedals are all examples. None of them produce sound on their own; they send MIDI data to an instrument, plugin, or DAW that generates the actual audio.
FAQ What is MIDI velocity?
MIDI velocity is how hard or fast a note is played, measured from 1 (very quiet) to 127 (maximum force). Most instruments use velocity to control dynamics — a higher velocity typically produces a louder, brighter, or more intense sound, and varied velocity is essential for making programmed performances feel human.
FAQ What are MIDI channels?
MIDI has 16 channels, numbered 1–16. Each channel can carry independent MIDI data, allowing one MIDI connection to control up to 16 different instruments simultaneously. Channel 10 has historically been reserved for drums by the General MIDI convention.
FAQ What is MIDI CC (Control Change)?
MIDI Control Change (CC) messages control parameters other than note data — CC1 is the modulation wheel, CC7 is channel volume, CC10 is panning, CC64 is the sustain pedal, and CC74 often controls filter cutoff. Automating CC values in your DAW creates evolving, expressive performances.
FAQ What is MIDI quantization?
MIDI quantization moves the timing of MIDI notes to align with a musical grid of subdivisions such as quarter notes or 16th notes. Full quantization makes performances perfectly in time; partial quantization nudges notes toward the grid while preserving some human timing variation. Over-quantizing can destroy the natural feel of a performance.
FAQ What is MPE (MIDI Polyphonic Expression)?
MPE is an extension of MIDI that allows per-note expression — each individual note can have its own pitch bend, pressure, and slide data independently. Standard MIDI pitch bend affects all notes on a channel at once, while MPE lets you bend one note while others remain stable, supported by controllers like the Roli Seaboard and LinnStrument.
