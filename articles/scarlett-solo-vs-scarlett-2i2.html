Focusrite Scarlett Solo vs 2i2: Which Interface Is Right for You?

Updated May 2026
MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026
Quick Answer

Scarlett 2i2 ($169) for most home studio producers — two mic preamp inputs, independent headphone and monitor outputs, and a software bundle that includes Ableton Live Lite and a professional plugin collection make it the most complete entry-level interface package available. Scarlett Solo ($119) only if you record one source at a time exclusively and the $50 price difference genuinely matters to your budget. The Solo's single mic preamp input, single headphone output, and lack of MIDI I/O are real limitations that most producers encounter within months of starting to record. The 2i2 is the better long-term investment at only $50 more.

At a glance — key differences: Solo: 1 mic input, 1 instrument input, 1 headphone output · 2i2: 2 mic inputs, 2 instrument inputs, 2 headphone outputs with independent volume · Both: USB-C, 24-bit/192kHz, Air mode, same preamp quality, same software bundle

What Both Interfaces Share

Before examining the differences, it is worth establishing what the Solo and 2i2 have in common — because they share the most important qualities of a professional recording interface, and the choice between them is about feature set rather than fundamental quality.

Preamp quality: Both the Solo and 2i2 use Focusrite's fourth-generation preamp design — the same preamp circuit regardless of which model you choose. The preamp quality has been well-received by engineers and producers since the Scarlett 4th Gen launched: low self-noise (approximately -129.5dBu EIN), clean and transparent gain, and a 56dB gain range that handles most condenser and dynamic microphone combinations without requiring an inline preamp booster (with the exception of very low-output dynamic microphones like the Shure SM7B).

AD/DA conversion: Both interfaces use the same analog-to-digital and digital-to-analog converters — 24-bit depth, sampling rates up to 192kHz. The conversion quality at this price point is professional — clean, accurate, with a measured dynamic range that exceeds the requirements of any recording application at the home studio level.

Air mode: Both include Focusrite's Air mode — an analog circuit that emulates the high-frequency transformer character of Focusrite's vintage ISA microphone preamplifier. With Air mode engaged, the preamp has a gentle presence lift in the upper midrange and high frequencies that adds brightness and detail to vocal and instrument recordings. Air mode is a legitimate addition rather than a marketing feature — the difference is audible and is often flattering on vocals and acoustic instruments.

USB-C connection: Both use USB-C for connection and power — no separate power supply required. USB-C provides bus power that keeps the setup clean and cable-efficient. Both are class-compliant on Mac and require the Focusrite driver installation on Windows.

Software bundle: Both ship with the same software package — Ableton Live Lite (a limited but fully functional version of Ableton Live), the Focusrite plugin collection (including a Pro-Q 3 limited version, compressor, and other tools), and access to Splice's sample library. This bundle represents significant value — Ableton Live Lite alone is a professional production environment for producers who are starting out and do not yet need the full Live feature set.

The Critical Differences

Microphone inputs — the most important practical difference: The Solo has one XLR microphone input (Combo jack that also accepts a line-level signal). The 2i2 has two XLR microphone inputs with independent gain control. This difference determines what recording scenarios each interface supports.

With the Solo: you can record one microphone at a time. A single vocal, a single instrument through a microphone. You cannot simultaneously record a vocal and an acoustic guitar with two microphones. You cannot record a stereo source with two matched microphones. You cannot record a room microphone alongside a close microphone on the same instrument.

With the 2i2: you can record two simultaneous microphone sources. Vocal plus guitar simultaneously. Two overheads on a drum kit. Stereo microphone pair on a piano. Interview with two microphones. Any two-microphone recording scenario is immediately possible without additional equipment.

Many producers believe they will only ever need one input at a time — and many of them are right for the first several months. The limitation typically surfaces when a producer wants to record a live performance with guitar and vocal simultaneously, or when they explore stereo microphone placement for acoustic instruments. At that point, upgrading from a Solo to a 2i2 costs $50 in addition to the Solo's original price — effectively paying $219 for what could have been $169 from the start.

Instrument inputs: The Solo has one instrument (Hi-Z) input on the front panel. The 2i2 has two instrument inputs. For recording guitar or bass directly, the 2i2 allows two instruments simultaneously — useful for recording two guitarists at once, or for routing a bass direct alongside a guitar direct without patching cables.

Headphone outputs: The Solo has one headphone output. The 2i2 has two independent headphone outputs, each with its own volume control. The two headphone outputs allow: a vocalist and a guitarist each monitoring through their own headphones during a simultaneous take, two producers or engineers listening through the same session without sharing a single headphone output, or using one headphone as a cue mix for recording while the other serves as a monitoring reference without the same level control.

MIDI I/O: Neither the Solo nor the 2i2 includes MIDI input or output connectors. This is a notable omission at both price points — producers who want to connect hardware synthesizers, drum machines, or MIDI controllers via traditional 5-pin MIDI rather than USB require a separate MIDI interface or an interface with built-in MIDI. The SSL 2+ ($229) includes MIDI I/O at a comparable price point and is worth considering if MIDI connectivity is important to your hardware setup.

When the Solo Is the Right Choice

The Scarlett Solo is the appropriate choice in a narrow but genuine set of circumstances. If you record exclusively one source at a time and have no intention of expanding to two-microphone setups: a vocalist who does not play instruments, a podcaster recording solo, a producer who records only guitar direct with no simultaneous microphone use, and a producer on a tight budget where the $50 difference is genuinely meaningful rather than marginally inconvenient. In these specific situations, the Solo's limitation is not a limitation — it is simply unused capability that you would pay for unnecessarily with the 2i2.

For everyone else — which is most producers — the 2i2 is the better purchase. The two-input limitation of the Solo surfaces earlier than most people expect, and the cost of upgrading is $169 (purchasing the 2i2 outright) rather than $50 (the price difference), because the Solo has limited resale value that rarely covers a meaningful fraction of the 2i2's cost.

Setup, Drivers, and Latency

Both the Solo and 2i2 use the same driver architecture and have the same setup process. On Mac, both are class-compliant — plug in the USB-C cable, and macOS recognizes the interface immediately without driver installation. On Windows, the Focusrite Control application and ASIO driver must be installed for low-latency recording performance. The ASIO driver is available from Focusrite's website and installs cleanly on Windows 10 and 11 without the driver conflicts that plagued some older audio interfaces.

Buffer size and latency: both interfaces perform comparably at equivalent buffer sizes. At 64 samples buffer size (the lowest practical setting for most systems), round-trip latency on a modern Windows PC with the Focusrite ASIO driver is approximately 5–8ms — acceptable for tracking with monitoring through the DAW. For tracking with direct monitoring (listening to the microphone signal directly from the interface without going through the DAW), the latency is hardware-level and essentially imperceptible regardless of buffer size settings. The Mix button on the front panel blends the direct hardware signal with the DAW playback for direct monitoring — the same control exists on both Solo and 2i2.

The Focusrite Control software (the companion application for both interfaces) adds value beyond driver installation. It provides: metering of input and output levels, loopback channel configuration for recording system audio alongside microphone input, and configuration of the interface's routing settings. The interface works without Focusrite Control installed (the hardware defaults are appropriate for most recording scenarios), but the software is useful for producers who need custom routing configurations.

The Software Bundle — What's Actually Included

Both the Solo and 2i2 ship with the same software bundle, and this bundle significantly adds to the value of either interface at its price point. Understanding what is included helps set appropriate expectations.

Ableton Live Lite: A functional, limited version of Ableton Live — the industry-standard electronic music production DAW. Live Lite is limited to 8 tracks, 4 sends, and a reduced set of effects and instruments compared to the full Live Standard ($449) and Live Suite ($749). For producers who are new to DAW-based production and want to explore Ableton's workflow before committing to the full purchase, Live Lite is a genuine starting environment. Tracks and projects created in Live Lite open in the full Live versions — if you upgrade, your work carries forward.

Pro Tools Intro: An introductory version of Avid's Pro Tools — the professional recording industry standard DAW used in commercial studios worldwide. Pro Tools Intro allows up to 16 tracks and access to the core Pro Tools recording and editing environment. For producers who want to learn the workflow used in professional recording studios without purchasing Pro Tools Artist ($99/year) or Pro Tools Studio ($299/year), this is a useful starting point.

Plugin collection: The Focusrite bundle includes time-limited or full versions of several professional plugins — typically including versions of Focusrite's own EQ and compressor tools, a selection of third-party plugins, and access to additional content through Focusrite's partnerships. The specific plugin content has changed across generations of the Scarlett series; check Focusrite's current bundle page for the exact current contents at the time of purchase.

Splice trial: Access to Splice's sample library — one of the largest commercially available loop and sample databases in the industry — for a trial period. Splice subscription ($7.99–$17.99/month after the trial) gives ongoing access to millions of samples, loops, and one-shot sounds organized by genre, instrument, and key/tempo.

Common Questions

Can I record a podcast with two voices through the Scarlett Solo? No. The Solo has one microphone input. Two people speaking simultaneously requires two microphone inputs — either the 2i2 or a larger interface. This is the most frequently cited limitation that surprises Solo purchasers who intended to use it for podcast or interview recording.

Can I connect a MIDI keyboard to either interface? Yes, via USB. Both the Solo and 2i2 have a 4-port USB hub that allows connecting USB MIDI devices — keyboards, controllers, pad controllers — directly to the interface without using your computer's USB ports. Traditional 5-pin MIDI (the DIN connector used on older hardware synthesizers and some professional equipment) requires a separate USB-MIDI adapter or an interface with built-in MIDI I/O, which neither Scarlett includes.

Is the preamp good enough to record professional-quality vocals? Yes. The Scarlett 4th Gen preamp is professional quality at its price point — low noise, transparent gain, and adequate headroom for all standard recording applications. The limitation on professional vocal recording quality at the Scarlett price point is the room acoustics and microphone choice, not the interface preamp. Upgrading from a Scarlett to a more expensive interface produces audible improvement only in controlled listening comparisons — for practical recording, the Scarlett's preamp is not the bottleneck.

Does the 2i2 have a better preamp than the Solo? No. Both the Solo and 2i2 use the identical preamp circuit. There is no preamp quality difference between them — only the input count, output count, and form factor differ.

Alternatives Beyond the Scarlett Range

SSL 2+ ($229): The most compelling alternative to the 2i2 for producers who need MIDI I/O. The SSL 2+ includes MIDI input and output connections — traditional 5-pin MIDI for connecting hardware synthesizers and controllers — plus SSL's Legacy 4K circuit mode which adds a subtle analog warmth character. The $60 premium over the 2i2 is justified specifically by the MIDI connectivity and the SSL preamp character.

Audient iD4 MkII ($199): A well-regarded alternative with class-leading preamp quality at the entry price point. Single mic input (same as Solo) but superior preamp transparency compared to the Scarlett 4th Gen for critical recording applications. The Audient's preamp is frequently cited as a step above the Focusrite in analytical comparisons, though both are professional quality in practical use.

Universal Audio Volt 2 ($199): Two inputs with a built-in "Vintage" mode that adds analog-style harmonic saturation to recordings. The Volt 2 sits between the Scarlett 2i2 and the SSL 2+ in price and provides a character option that the Scarlett's transparent preamp does not. Worth considering for producers who specifically want analog-colored recording character from their interface.
