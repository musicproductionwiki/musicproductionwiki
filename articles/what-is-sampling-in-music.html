What Is Sampling In Music

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 · 10 min read · Verified May 7, 2026
Quick Answer — Updated May 2026

Sampling is the practice of taking a portion of an existing recording — a drum groove, a vocal phrase, a chord stab, or a single instrument hit — and reusing it in a new composition. It is the creative foundation of hip-hop, lo-fi, and house music. Using samples from copyrighted recordings requires legal clearance; royalty-free sample packs and live replays are the legal alternatives most producers use.

Updated May 2026

Sampling is one of the most important creative techniques in modern music production. It is the reason hip-hop exists as a genre, why lo-fi beats sound the way they do, why house music was born from disco records, and why some of the most recognizable pop hooks of the last 40 years were built on someone else's musical idea. If you have ever dropped a drum loop into a project or triggered a one-shot kick from a sample pack, you have sampled. Understanding what sampling is, how it works technically, and the legal framework around it is essential knowledge for any producer.

What Is a Sample?

A sample is any recorded audio taken from its original context and used in a new piece of music. It can be as small as a single drum hit lasting 50 milliseconds or as large as an 8-bar instrumental loop. What defines it as a sample is not its length — it's the fact that it originated as a separate recording and has been incorporated into something new.

Samples come from two main sources: existing commercial recordings (songs, albums, film scores) and purpose-built sample packs created specifically for producers. The legal and creative considerations differ dramatically between these two sources, but the technical process of working with them in a DAW is identical. The term "sample" is also used to describe the individual recordings inside a virtual instrument — a piano VST plays back short audio recordings of a real piano at every pitch and velocity. That usage is technically correct but distinct from the creative production technique this article covers.

A Brief History of Sampling

Sampling as a deliberate creative technique emerged in the late 1970s with the Bronx DJ culture that gave birth to hip-hop. DJs like Kool Herc discovered that isolating and repeating the "break" — the percussion-only section of a funk or soul record — created an irresistible rhythmic foundation for MCs and dancers. Two turntables and a mixer became the first sampling instrument.

The Mellotron, popular in the 1960s and 70s, was an earlier hardware device that played back tape recordings of orchestral instruments via keyboard — technically a sampling instrument decades before the term existed. The digital sampler arrived in the early 1980s with hardware units like the E-mu Emulator (1981) and the Akai MPC60 (1988), which allowed producers to record, pitch, and loop any audio in real time. By the late 1980s, landmark albums like Public Enemy's It Takes a Nation of Millions to Hold Us Back and De La Soul's 3 Feet High and Rising demonstrated what was possible when producers treated the entire recorded music catalog as raw material. A series of court cases in the early 1990s established that sampling without permission was copyright infringement — creating the clearance industry that exists today.

Types of Sampling

The sampling workflow: source recording → isolate clip → process → finished track.

Sample TypeDescriptionCommon Use
LoopSeamlessly repeating audio segmentDrum grooves, chord beds, bass lines
One-ShotSingle, non-repeating hit or noteCustom drum kits, stabs, FX
ChopSliced recording rearranged into new patternHip-hop flips, melodic chops
FlipPitched and time-stretched transformationSoul samples, lo-fi beats

Loop-based sampling is the fastest way to build a track. A loop ends where it began so that when it cycles, there is no audible gap or click. Royalty-free loop libraries from companies like Splice, Loopmasters, and Noiiz provide thousands of professionally recorded loops tagged by BPM and key so they can be combined in your DAW without tuning or timing issues. For a deep dive into building loop-based beats from scratch, see how to make a beat.

One-shots are single, non-repeating samples — a single kick, a chord stab, a vocal syllable. They are the building blocks of custom drum kits and are triggered in real time via a pad controller or piano roll. Chopping involves slicing a sampled recording into individual segments — notes, syllables, drum hits, or phrases — and rearranging those segments into a new rhythmic or melodic pattern. Chopping is fundamental to hip-hop production and creates the pitched, stutter, and flip effects that define the genre. This technique is also central to lo-fi beat production.

Sampling in Modern DAWs

Modern DAWs have made sampling accessible to anyone with a laptop. FL Studio's step sequencer and pattern-based workflow make it particularly intuitive for building sample-based beats. Ableton Live's Simpler and Sampler instruments are powerful tools for chopping and manipulating samples in real time — see the Ableton Live beginner's guide for a full walkthrough. Logic Pro's Quick Sampler offers fast drag-and-drop sample loading and slicing. If you are choosing a DAW specifically for sample-based hip-hop work, read our comparison of the best DAWs for hip-hop production.

Key Distinction: A royalty-free sample is not the same as a free sample. Royalty-free means you pay once (or subscribe) and do not owe additional royalties per use. You still need a valid license. Always check the specific terms of the sample pack or platform before releasing music commercially.

The Legal Framework: Clearing Samples

Sampling a copyrighted recording without permission is copyright infringement. To use a sample legally, you typically need to clear two separate rights: the master recording rights (owned by the record label or artist who made the recording) and the publishing rights (owned by the songwriter or their publisher). Both must be licensed independently. Sample clearance costs range from a few hundred dollars for an obscure track to tens of thousands — or a percentage of royalties — for a recognizable hit. Many clearance requests are simply denied. For a full breakdown of how rights and royalties work, see how music royalties work.

The practical alternatives most producers use are: royalty-free sample packs, replaying the part yourself on a real instrument (which sidesteps the master rights issue but may still implicate publishing if the underlying composition is protected), or sampling recordings in the public domain (generally recordings made before 1928 in the US, though this continues to shift with legislation).

Finding Legal Samples

The safest and most practical source of samples for most producers is a royalty-free sample pack platform. Splice, Loopmasters, and Noiiz offer subscription-based access to hundreds of thousands of loops and one-shots cleared for commercial use. If you want to build and sell your own sample packs, the process is covered in detail in our guide to how to make your first sample pack. Always verify that a sample pack's license explicitly covers commercial release — some packs restrict use to non-commercial or personal projects only.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ What is sampling in music?
Sampling is the practice of taking a portion of a recorded sound — a drum hit, a vocal phrase, a chord progression, or an entire section of a song — and reusing it in a new musical composition. Samples can be looped, chopped, pitched, filtered, and layered to form the basis of an entirely new track.
FAQ Is sampling legal?
Sampling copyrighted recordings without permission is not legal. To use a sample legally, you typically need to clear two rights: the master recording rights (owned by the record label or artist) and the publishing rights (owned by the songwriter or publisher). Alternatively, producers use royalty-free sample packs, replay samples live, or sample recordings in the public domain.
FAQ What is a sample pack?
A sample pack is a collection of royalty-free audio files — drums, loops, one-shots, vocal phrases, instrument hits, and textures — that producers can legally use in their music without clearing rights separately. Sample packs are sold or offered via subscription by companies like Splice, Loopmasters, and Noiiz.
FAQ What is the difference between a loop and a one-shot sample?
A loop is a sample designed to repeat seamlessly — a drum groove, a chord progression, a bass riff. A one-shot is a single, non-repeating sample — a single drum hit, a chord stab, or a vocal syllable. Loops form the rhythmic or harmonic bed of a track; one-shots are used to build custom kits and arrangements from scratch.
FAQ What is chopping a sample?
Chopping is the process of slicing a sampled audio recording into individual segments — usually individual notes, syllables, drum hits, or phrases — and rearranging those segments into a new rhythmic or melodic pattern. Chopping is fundamental to hip-hop production and creates the pitched, stutter, and flip effects common in sample-based beats.
FAQ What does it mean to clear a sample?
Clearing a sample means obtaining legal permission from the rights holders to use a portion of their recording in your own work. This involves contacting both the master rights holder (usually the record label) and the publishing rights holder (usually the songwriter's publisher) and negotiating a licensing fee and royalty split. Sample clearance can cost anywhere from a few hundred dollars to tens of thousands depending on the track's popularity.
FAQ What is a royalty-free sample?
A royalty-free sample is a sound recording licensed for use in music production without requiring ongoing royalty payments per use. You typically pay once or access via subscription and can use the sample in your music without further fees. Royalty-free does not mean free — it means you don't owe royalties each time the music is played or sold.
FAQ What DAW is best for sample-based production?
FL Studio, Ableton Live, and Logic Pro are all excellent for sample-based production. FL Studio's step sequencer and pattern-based workflow make it intuitive for building sample-based beats. Ableton Live's Simpler and Sampler instruments are powerful for chopping and manipulating samples. Logic Pro's Quick Sampler offers fast drag-and-drop sample loading and slicing.
