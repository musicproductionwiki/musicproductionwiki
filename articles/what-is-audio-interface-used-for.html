What Is an Audio Interface Used For? Complete Guide

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2025 12 min read

An audio interface is the hardware bridge between your microphones, instruments, and headphones and your computer. It converts analogue audio signals — the sound waves captured by microphones or produced by guitars and keyboards — into digital data your computer can process, and converts digital audio back into analogue signals for your speakers and headphones. Without an audio interface, professional-quality recording and monitoring in a home studio is not possible.

What we'll cover: What an audio interface does technically, why the built-in soundcard isn't sufficient for recording, what phantom power is and why it matters, inputs and outputs explained, latency and why it matters, what specifications to look for, and which interface is right for different setups.

What an Audio Interface Does

An audio interface performs two core functions: analogue-to-digital conversion (ADC) and digital-to-analogue conversion (DAC). When you sing into a microphone connected to the interface, the interface's microphone preamp amplifies the signal to a usable level, then the ADC converts it from a continuous analogue signal to a stream of digital samples — typically 44,100 or 48,000 times per second (the sample rate). This digital stream goes into your computer via USB, Thunderbolt, or another connection, where your DAW records and processes it.

When you play back audio from your DAW, the process reverses: the DAC converts the digital stream back to an analogue signal, which the interface sends to your studio monitors or headphones. The quality of both conversions — how accurately the ADC captures the input and how accurately the DAC reproduces the output — directly determines the quality of your recordings and playback.

Why Your Computer's Built-In Soundcard Isn't Enough

Every laptop and desktop computer includes a built-in audio chip that handles basic audio functions. For listening to music, watching videos, and video calls, the built-in audio is adequate. For professional recording and music production, it fails on three critical dimensions.

Microphone preamp quality: Built-in audio chips have basic microphone inputs designed for the headset microphones used in video calls. These preamps have high noise floors, limited gain, and poor frequency response. Plugging a professional microphone into a laptop's headphone jack produces noisy, thin recordings regardless of microphone quality.

Conversion quality: The ADC and DAC quality in consumer audio chips prioritises cost over performance. The noise floor (the level of background hiss and hum introduced by the conversion process) is significantly higher than in dedicated audio interfaces. At typical recording levels, this noise floor is audible and contaminates the recording.

Latency: Latency is the delay between a sound entering the interface and that sound being heard through your headphones. Built-in audio has high latency — typically 20–100 milliseconds — because it's routed through generic operating system audio drivers not optimised for low-latency performance. At 50 ms latency, a singer monitoring through headphones hears their voice noticeably delayed from when they're actually singing, making it impossible to perform accurately. Dedicated audio interfaces with ASIO drivers (Windows) or CoreAudio (Mac) achieve latencies of 1–5 ms, which is imperceptible.

Phantom Power Explained

Phantom power (48V) is the DC voltage that condenser microphones require to operate. Condenser microphones — the type used for studio vocals, acoustic instruments, and most professional recording applications — contain active electronics that need power to function. This power is delivered through the microphone cable at 48 volts, which is why it's called phantom power — it rides invisibly on the same cables that carry the audio signal.

Every audio interface intended for professional use provides phantom power, switchable per channel or globally. Dynamic microphones don't require phantom power and are not damaged by it (with rare exceptions). Ribbon microphones should not receive phantom power unless specifically designed for it — phantom power can damage vintage ribbon microphones. Always check before engaging 48V.

Inputs and Outputs Explained

Mic/line combo inputs: The most common input type on home studio interfaces. XLR connections accept microphone signals (with phantom power for condensers). TRS or TS quarter-inch connections accept line-level signals (from keyboards, synthesizers, audio mixers) or instrument-level signals (guitars, basses) when the channel is set to instrument/hi-Z mode. Combo inputs handle both XLR and quarter-inch in a single socket.

Monitor outputs: Quarter-inch TRS balanced outputs that connect to studio monitors (active speakers). Balanced connections (TRS or XLR) reduce interference and noise over longer cable runs. The interface's monitor volume knob controls the level sent to the monitors independently from your DAW's software level.

Headphone outputs: Quarter-inch headphone outputs with independent volume control. Higher-end interfaces include multiple headphone outputs with separate mixes — allowing a vocalist in the booth to hear a different mix than the engineer at the desk.

MIDI I/O: Some interfaces include 5-pin DIN MIDI inputs and outputs for connecting to hardware synthesizers, drum machines, and MIDI controllers that don't have USB. Less common on basic two-input interfaces, more common on interfaces with four or more inputs.

Understanding Latency

Latency in an audio interface context refers to the round-trip time: the delay from sound entering the interface's input to that sound being heard through the interface's output. This path goes: microphone → interface ADC → computer → DAW processing → computer → interface DAC → headphones. Each step adds delay.

Buffer size is the main latency control in your DAW's audio settings. A large buffer (512 or 1024 samples) reduces CPU load but increases latency — appropriate for mixing but not for tracking (recording while monitoring). A small buffer (64 or 128 samples) increases CPU load but reduces latency — appropriate for real-time monitoring while recording.

Direct monitoring — a feature on most interfaces — bypasses the computer entirely for the monitoring signal. The interface routes the input signal directly to the headphone output before the computer processes it, achieving near-zero latency monitoring. The trade-off: you hear the dry, unprocessed signal rather than the DAW-processed signal with effects applied.

Key Specifications to Understand

Dynamic range (dB): The ratio between the loudest and quietest signals the interface can handle without distortion or noise. Higher is better. Professional interfaces achieve 115–125 dB dynamic range. Budget interfaces typically achieve 100–110 dB. The difference is audible on quiet recordings — higher dynamic range means a lower noise floor and more headroom.

Maximum gain (dB): How much amplification the preamp can provide. More gain allows recording quieter microphones (particularly passive dynamic microphones like the SM7B) without noise. 60 dB is adequate for most condenser microphones. 65–70 dB is needed for low-output dynamics and ribbons.

Sample rate and bit depth: Most modern interfaces support 24-bit/192 kHz. Recording at 24-bit/44.1 kHz or 48 kHz is standard for music production — higher sample rates are used for audio-for-picture work and archiving but don't meaningfully improve most music production results.

Connection type: USB-C is the current standard, replacing USB-A. Thunderbolt offers lower latency and higher bandwidth for multi-channel interfaces. The connection type matters more for larger interfaces with many channels — two-input USB-C interfaces provide sufficient bandwidth for any typical home studio application.
