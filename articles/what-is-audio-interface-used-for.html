What Is an Audio Interface Used For? The Complete Explanation

First published May 2026
MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
May 2026
Quick Answer

An audio interface converts analog signals (microphones, guitars, keyboards) into digital audio your computer can record, and converts your DAW's digital output back to analog for your monitors and headphones. You need one to record anything with a microphone or instrument, to get low-latency monitoring while tracking, and to get significantly better sound quality than a laptop's built-in audio. The interface is the first and last thing the audio signal touches -- its quality defines the ceiling of your recording quality before any plugins or processing.

Analog to Digital Conversion

Your microphone and instruments produce analog signals -- continuous electrical voltages that represent sound waves. Your computer and DAW work entirely in digital audio -- discrete numerical samples taken thousands of times per second. An audio interface performs analog-to-digital (ADC) conversion on the way in and digital-to-analog (DAC) conversion on the way out. The quality of this conversion determines how accurately the digital representation captures the original analog signal. Budget interfaces (Focusrite Scarlett, SSL 2+) perform this conversion with sufficient quality for professional results. Very cheap USB adapters and laptop built-in audio perform it with audible noise and distortion.

Microphone Preamps

Microphones produce very weak electrical signals -- typically between 1 and 100 millivolts. Your DAW needs a signal in the range of 1-2 volts to record at a healthy level. A microphone preamp amplifies the mic signal by 30-60dB (a factor of 30-1000x) before it reaches the ADC. The quality of this amplification -- how much noise it adds, how much it colors the sound, how well it handles different microphone types -- is one of the most audible differences between budget and professional recording gear. A laptop's built-in microphone input has a preamp, but it is designed for voice calls, not music recording. A quality interface preamp is designed for music and the difference is significant.

Monitoring Latency

When you record through a computer, there is an inherent delay between when you sing or play and when you hear yourself through your DAW's output. This round-trip latency -- caused by the time it takes for your computer to process the audio through the DAW and output it -- can range from a few milliseconds (imperceptible) to 30-100ms (severely disruptive to performance). Audio interfaces include direct monitoring capability: routing the input signal directly to the headphone output in hardware, bypassing the computer entirely, so you hear yourself with near-zero latency while the DAW processes in the background. This is why recording vocals and instruments with direct monitoring rather than DAW monitoring is standard practice.

Do You Actually Need One?

You need an audio interface if: you want to record a microphone or instrument at professional quality, you want to use studio monitors (which require balanced line-level connections that laptop headphone outputs do not provide), you want zero-latency monitoring while recording, or you want better audio quality from your DAW output to your headphones. You may not need one if: you produce entirely with software instruments and samples and never record live audio, and you are satisfied with the sound quality of your laptop's headphone output through your headphones for the playback side.
