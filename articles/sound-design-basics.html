Sound Design Basics (2026)

Sound design is the skill that separates producers who build signature sounds from those who scroll through presets. This guide teaches subtractive synthesis from the ground up — oscillators, filters, envelopes, LFOs, and the signal chain that connects them.

The Subtractive Synthesis Signal Chain

Oscillator (generates the raw waveform) → Filter (removes unwanted frequencies, shapes tone) → Amplifier (controls volume over time) → Effects (reverb, chorus, delay). All three main stages are shaped by Envelopes (ADSR) and animated by LFOs (rhythmic modulation). This chain is the foundation of virtually every synthesiser ever made.

What Sound Design Is and Why It Matters

Sound design is the craft of creating sounds intentionally rather than accepting what a preset delivers. A producer who understands sound design opens a synthesiser, hears a preset, and knows exactly which controls to adjust to transform it into the sound they hear in their head. A producer who does not understand sound design scrolls through thousands of presets looking for something close enough to use.

The difference between these two producers is not intelligence or talent — it is knowledge. The synthesiser signal chain follows a small set of logical principles that, once understood, apply to every synthesiser ever made. A producer who understands subtractive synthesis can sit down with any synthesiser — hardware or software, classic or modern — and create sounds purposefully within minutes of learning the interface layout.

The Building Blocks: Waveforms

Every synthesiser begins with a waveform — a repeating pattern of air pressure variation that the human ear perceives as a pitch. The frequency of repetition determines the pitch. The shape of the waveform determines the tonal character.

Sawtooth Wave

The sawtooth wave contains every harmonic — the fundamental frequency plus harmonics at every integer multiple above it — at decreasing amplitudes. It sounds bright, buzzy, and rich. Sawtooth waves are the starting point for most leads, brass stabs, strings, and basses. Almost everything that sounds "like a synth" starts with a sawtooth waveform that has been filtered and shaped.

Square Wave

The square wave contains only odd harmonics — the fundamental frequency plus harmonics at the third, fifth, seventh harmonic, etc. — producing a hollow, reedy quality associated with woodwind instruments, the classic Minimoog lead, and vintage video game sounds. Narrowing the square wave into a pulse wave (where the positive portion of the cycle is shorter than the negative portion) creates progressively thinner, more nasal timbres.

Sine Wave

The sine wave contains only the fundamental frequency — no harmonics. It is a pure tone, the simplest possible waveform. On its own, it sounds like a tuning fork. In synthesis, sine waves are used for sub-bass frequencies (where harmonics are unnecessary and potentially problematic on small speakers) and as modulation signals. The 808 kick drum's characteristic rumbling sustain is generated by a sine wave or near-sine oscillator with a pitch envelope that drops rapidly after the initial hit.

Noise

White noise contains all frequencies simultaneously at equal amplitude — it sounds like radio static. In synthesis, noise is used for percussion sounds (the hiss of a hi-hat, the crack component of a snare), breath textures, and atmospheric pads. Filtering white noise — running it through a resonant bandpass filter — creates pitched, swooshing effects. A heavily filtered noise burst with a sharp volume envelope creates a convincing snare transient.

The Filter: Sculpting Tone

The filter is the most expressive component of the subtractive synthesiser. It removes frequencies from the oscillator signal, sculpting what remains into a desired tonal shape. The interaction between the filter cutoff frequency, the resonance, and the filter envelope creates the characteristic expressiveness of synthesiser sounds.

Low-Pass Filter (LPF)

The low-pass filter allows frequencies below the cutoff frequency to pass through and removes frequencies above it. This is the most common filter type in synthesis. With the cutoff fully open (maximum frequency), the full harmonic content of the oscillator passes through — the sound is bright and buzzy. Closing the cutoff (lowering the frequency) progressively removes high-frequency harmonics, making the sound darker, warmer, and less bright. Fully closing the low-pass filter on a sawtooth wave produces a dark, muffled sine-like tone. Opening it progressively brightens the sound.

Resonance (Q)

The resonance control boosts frequencies immediately around the cutoff frequency, creating a peak or emphasis at the cutoff point. Moderate resonance creates the characteristic "squelch" of synthesiser filter sounds — the frequencies at the cutoff frequency are louder than surrounding frequencies, creating a peaky, focused tonal character. Maximum resonance causes the filter to self-oscillate — the resonance feedback loop becomes loud enough that the filter produces its own pitched tone independent of the oscillator input. This phenomenon is the basis of specific synthesis techniques and produces distinctive, harmonic-rich sounds.

High-Pass Filter (HPF)

The high-pass filter allows frequencies above the cutoff to pass and removes frequencies below it. The inverse of the low-pass filter, it is used in synthesis for creating thin, bright textures, for removing low-end from elements that should not carry bass, and for the characteristic sweeping sounds of progressive house and trance production where a high-pass filter sweep reveals the full frequency content of a track progressively.

Envelopes: Shaping Sound Over Time

A static filter cutoff produces a static tonal character — the sound's brightness does not change from the beginning of a note to its end. Envelopes allow parameters to change over time in response to note trigger and release events. The ADSR envelope is the universal standard for this time-varying control.

Attack

Attack controls the time it takes a parameter to rise from zero to its maximum value after a note is pressed. A short attack on the amplitude envelope produces a percussive sound that hits immediately — like a plucked string or struck drum. A long attack produces a slow swell that rises gradually — like a string section bowing gently into a chord. The same attack principle applies to the filter: a short filter envelope attack opens the filter immediately, a long attack sweeps it slowly from dark to bright over several seconds.

Decay

Decay controls the time it takes the parameter to fall from its maximum value to the sustain level after the attack peak. A short decay on the filter envelope produces the characteristic wah-like sweep of classic funk synth sounds — the filter opens fully on note attack and closes quickly to a darker sustain. A long decay creates a slower, more gradual filter closing.

Sustain

Sustain is not a time parameter — it is a level parameter. It sets the level at which the parameter holds while the note remains pressed, after the attack and decay phases have completed. An amplitude sustain of 100% means the sound stays at full volume while a key is held. An amplitude sustain of 0% means the sound fades to silence during the decay phase regardless of whether the key is held — creating a percussive sound that dies away even with a sustained key press. Setting the filter envelope's sustain to 0% produces a sound that sweeps from bright to dark immediately after being triggered, regardless of how long the note is held.

Release

Release controls the time the parameter takes to fall from its sustain level to zero after the key is released. A short release on the amplitude envelope produces a sound that cuts off immediately when the key is lifted — appropriate for punchy bass sounds and staccato leads. A long release produces a gradual fade after the key is released — appropriate for pads, strings, and reverberant textures where a smooth tail is desirable.

The LFO: Creating Movement

The Low Frequency Oscillator runs at frequencies below the audible range — typically 0.1 Hz to 20 Hz — producing a repeating control signal rather than an audible pitch. Routing an LFO to a synthesiser parameter creates rhythmic, cyclical variation in that parameter. This movement is what makes synthesiser sounds feel alive rather than static.

LFO to pitch: creates vibrato — the pitch wobbles rhythmically, mimicking the natural vibrato of a vocalist or string player. The rate of the LFO sets the speed of the vibrato. The depth sets the amount of pitch variation. LFO to filter cutoff: creates a filter sweep that repeats rhythmically — the classic "wah-wah" effect applied to synthesiser sounds, and the basis of auto-filter effects. LFO to amplitude: creates tremolo — the volume pulses rhythmically, like a tremolo guitar effect or vintage organ tremolo. LFO synced to tempo: creates rhythmic variations — a filter sweep that opens and closes in time with the song's BPM, gate effects where the volume cuts rhythmically at a set subdivision of the beat.

Synthesis in Practice: Building Sounds

A Classic Pad Sound

Oscillator: two sawtooth waves, detuned slightly against each other (3-5 cents) to create beating between the waveforms. Filter: low-pass at 60-70% open, resonance low, filter envelope with long attack (0.5-1s), long decay (1-2s), sustain at 70%, long release (2-4s). Amplitude envelope: long attack (0.5-1s) matching the filter, sustain at 100%, long release (2-4s). LFO: sine wave at 0.3-0.8 Hz routed to filter cutoff at moderate depth. Effects: light chorus, generous reverb, moderate delay. This produces the classic evolving pad sound of ambient and cinematic music.

A Simple Bass Sound

Oscillator: single sawtooth wave, or a sawtooth layer with a sub-octave sine. Filter: low-pass at 40-55% open, resonance at 20-30%, filter envelope with very short attack (0-5ms), medium decay (100-300ms), sustain at 30-40%, short release. Amplitude envelope: very short attack, medium release. This produces a warm, punchy bass with the classic subtractive synthesis character — bright attack that decays to a darker sustained tone.

A Lead Sound

Oscillator: sawtooth wave, optionally with unison doubling (two voices detuned) for width. Filter: high cutoff (fully open or near-open), resonance at 15-25%. Amplitude envelope: very short attack, medium-long sustain at 100%, medium release. Portamento (glide) at a fast setting for pitch slides between notes. LFO: slow sine wave (0.5-1 Hz) routed to pitch at low depth (a few cents) for subtle vibrato. This produces a classic analogue-style lead that sits naturally in a mix and responds expressively to playing dynamics.

Recommended Synthesisers for Learning Sound Design

Vital (free) — the best starting point for beginners in 2026. A wavetable synthesiser with exceptional visual feedback that shows exactly what each control does to the waveform in real time. Its interface makes synthesis principles immediately visible rather than abstract. The free version contains everything needed to learn synthesis fundamentals.

Serum — the industry standard wavetable synthesiser for electronic music production. Used on a significant proportion of commercially released EDM, pop, and hip-hop. Its wavetable editor, effects chain, and modulation matrix provide professional-grade sound design capability. Worth investing in once you understand the basics.

Every major DAW includes synthesisers that are fully capable for learning: Ableton's Analog (subtractive synthesis), Logic Pro's Retro Synth (multi-mode synthesis including subtractive, FM, and wavetable), FL Studio's 3xOsc (simple subtractive), GarageBand's built-in synths. Start with whatever is already in your DAW before purchasing additional synthesisers.

Practical Exercises

Exercise 1 — Beginner: The Init Patch Exploration

Open any synthesiser and initialise it to a blank preset (usually called "Init" or "Default"). You should hear a simple sawtooth or sine wave at full volume. Now: move the filter cutoff from fully closed to fully open and listen to the change. Move the resonance up to maximum and listen to the self-oscillation. Set an ADSR envelope on the filter with a long attack and hear the sweep. Set the same ADSR on the amplitude and hear the slow fade in. This 15-minute exercise teaches the fundamental controls of every synthesiser through direct experience.

Exercise 2 — Intermediate: Recreate Three Presets from Scratch

Choose three presets from your synthesiser — a pad, a bass, and a lead. Listen carefully to each. Initialise the synth, then attempt to recreate each preset using only the controls you have learned: oscillator waveform, filter cutoff and resonance, filter envelope, amplitude envelope, and LFO. You will not get an exact match — that is not the goal. The goal is understanding which controls produce which sonic characteristics. After each attempt, compare your version to the original and identify exactly which settings differ.

Exercise 3 — Advanced: Design Three Original Sounds for a Track

Work on a production session where you have a specific sonic need — a bass that hits at a particular frequency, a pad with a specific emotional character, a lead that cuts through a specific arrangement. Design each sound from an initialised patch, using only synthesis controls rather than browsing presets. The sound you need for a specific musical context is the most powerful creative brief possible — it gives you a clear target and teaches you to translate musical intent into synthesis parameter decisions.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is sound design in music production?

Sound design is the process of creating and shaping sounds using synthesisers, samplers, and audio processing. Understanding sound design gives producers the ability to create signature sounds and realise specific tonal ideas rather than searching through preset libraries.

What is subtractive synthesis?

Subtractive synthesis starts with a harmonically rich waveform and uses filters to remove unwanted frequencies, shaping what remains into the desired sound. The classic synthesisers — Moog, Roland TB-303, Roland Juno, Sequential Prophet — use subtractive synthesis as their primary sound generation method.

What is an oscillator in a synthesiser?

An oscillator is the sound source — it generates the raw waveform. Common waveforms: sine (pure tone, no harmonics), sawtooth (bright, rich, all harmonics — most common for leads and basses), square (hollow, nasal quality, odd harmonics only), triangle (softer than sawtooth), and noise (for percussion and effects).

What is an ADSR envelope?

An ADSR envelope shapes how a parameter changes over time: Attack (time to reach maximum level after key press), Decay (time to fall from maximum to sustain level), Sustain (level held while key is pressed), Release (time to fade to silence after key release). Applied to amplitude, ADSR creates the fundamental shape of a sound's volume over time.

What does a filter do in a synthesiser?

A filter removes specific frequency ranges from the oscillator signal. A low-pass filter removes frequencies above the cutoff, making the sound darker as the cutoff is lowered. Resonance boosts frequencies around the cutoff, creating the classic synthesiser squelch. The filter is the most expressive component of a subtractive synthesiser.

What is an LFO in synthesis?

A Low Frequency Oscillator runs below audible frequency and modulates other parameters rhythmically. LFO to pitch creates vibrato. LFO to filter creates rhythmic filter sweeps. LFO to amplitude creates tremolo. LFOs make static sounds feel alive and animated.

What synthesiser should a beginner start with?

Vital (free) is the most recommended starting point — a wavetable synthesiser with exceptional visual feedback showing what each control does in real time. Serum is the industry standard for electronic music once you understand fundamentals. Start with whatever synthesisers are already in your DAW before purchasing new ones.

What is the difference between monophonic and polyphonic synthesis?

Monophonic synthesisers play only one note at a time — associated with leads, basses, and melodic phrases. Polyphonic synthesisers play multiple notes simultaneously, allowing chords. Polyphony count determines how many simultaneous notes the synthesiser can produce before older notes cut off.
