Sound Design Basics: A Complete Beginner's Guide

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2025 15 min read

Sound design is the practice of creating and shaping sounds using synthesizers, samplers, and audio processing tools. Every distinctive sound in modern music — the bass drop, the pad filling the chorus, the lead synth cutting through the mix — was designed by someone who understood how synthesis works. Learning the fundamentals of sound design opens up a world of sonic possibility and transforms you from a producer who uses presets into one who creates them.

What we'll cover: How sound works and why it matters for synthesis, subtractive synthesis explained from first principles, oscillators, filters, envelopes, and LFOs in depth, wavetable synthesis, FM synthesis, sampling as a sound design tool, and practical exercises for developing your sound design ear.

How Sound Works: The Foundation

Sound is vibration — pressure waves moving through air at different frequencies and amplitudes. The frequency of the vibration determines pitch: 440 Hz is concert A. The amplitude determines loudness. The shape of the waveform — how the pressure changes over time within each cycle — determines timbre, the quality that makes a violin sound different from a clarinet playing the same pitch at the same volume.

Synthesizers generate and shape these waveforms electronically. Different synthesis methods generate waveforms in different ways: subtractive synthesis starts with harmonically rich waveforms and removes frequency content with filters; additive synthesis builds complex waveforms by adding together simple sine waves; FM synthesis generates complex timbres by using one oscillator to modulate the frequency of another; wavetable synthesis plays back pre-recorded single-cycle waveforms and scans through collections of them.

Understanding the sonic character of basic waveforms is the starting point for all synthesis. A sine wave produces a pure tone with a single fundamental frequency and no harmonics — it sounds like a flute or a soft whistle. A sawtooth wave contains the fundamental and all harmonics in descending amplitude — it sounds bright and buzzy, the classic analogue synth sound. A square wave contains only odd harmonics — it sounds hollow and woody, like a clarinet. A triangle wave contains fewer harmonics than the sawtooth — warmer and softer, between a sine and a sawtooth.

Subtractive Synthesis: The Most Common Starting Point

Subtractive synthesis is the most widely used synthesis method and the starting point for most sound designers. The signal path: one or more oscillators generate a harmonically rich waveform, a filter removes frequency content to shape the timbre, an amplifier controls the output level, and envelopes and LFOs modulate these elements over time.

The simplicity of subtractive synthesis is deceptive — its parameter space is large and the interaction between components is complex. But the architecture is learnable, and once understood, it applies to most hardware and software synths you'll encounter: Minimoog, Juno-106, Prophet-5, Moog Subsequent, Arturia MiniBrute, and their software emulations all share the same fundamental architecture.

Oscillators: The Sound Source

Oscillators are the primary sound generators in a synthesizer. Each oscillator produces a periodic waveform at a controllable frequency. Most subtractive synths include two or three oscillators, allowing layering, detuning (setting oscillators to slightly different pitches for a chorus-like thickening), and mixing different waveforms together.

Waveform selection: The choice of waveform determines the harmonic content and basic character of the oscillator's output. Start with a sawtooth for most analogue sounds — it contains the richest harmonic content and gives the filter the most material to shape. Use a square wave for hollow, woodwind-like timbres. Use a sine wave when you want a pure, harmonic-free tone (kick drum sine sub, bass sub, flute).

Pitch and tuning: Oscillators are tuned in semitones (coarse tuning) and cents (fine tuning, 1/100th of a semitone). When using two oscillators, detune the second oscillator slightly (3–8 cents) for a chorus effect that thickens the sound without being obviously out of tune. Detuning both oscillators upward and downward from the fundamental spreads them symmetrically.

Oscillator sync: Hard sync resets oscillator 2's waveform to the beginning of its cycle each time oscillator 1 completes a cycle. When oscillator 2 is tuned higher than oscillator 1, this produces a distinctive, aggressive harmonic character — the "sync sweep" sound. Sweeping oscillator 2's pitch while in sync produces the classic sync sweep effect heard throughout 1980s pop and electronic music.

Pulse width modulation: Square waves have a variable duty cycle — the ratio of the wave's high portion to its total period. At 50% duty cycle it's a standard square wave. Varying the duty cycle (pulse width) changes the harmonic content and the sound's timbre. Modulating the pulse width with an LFO produces a subtle chorus-like movement with a richer character than static detuning.

Filters: Shaping the Timbre

The filter is the most expressive element in subtractive synthesis. It removes frequency content from the oscillator's output, shaping the timbre from the starting waveform toward the target sound. Filter movement — controlled by envelopes and LFOs — creates the dynamic timbral evolution that makes synthesizer sounds feel alive rather than static.

Filter types: A low-pass filter (LPF) allows frequencies below the cutoff frequency to pass while attenuating frequencies above it. This is the standard subtractive synthesis filter — the bright sawtooth waveform becomes warmer and darker as the cutoff frequency is lowered. A high-pass filter (HPF) does the opposite — removes low frequencies and allows highs to pass. A band-pass filter allows a frequency band around the cutoff to pass while attenuating both below and above. A notch filter removes a narrow frequency band — the opposite of band-pass.

Cutoff frequency: The frequency at which the filter begins to attenuate. On a low-pass filter, raising the cutoff makes the sound brighter (more high-frequency content passes through). Lowering it makes the sound darker. The cutoff is typically the most used real-time performance control on a subtractive synthesizer.

Resonance: Amplifies the frequency content around the cutoff frequency, creating a peak in the frequency response at the cutoff point. Low resonance (0–30%) adds subtle character and emphasis to the cutoff region. Medium resonance (30–70%) produces the classic "filter sweep" sound as the cutoff moves — the resonant peak becomes audible and sweeps through the frequency spectrum. High resonance (70–100%) produces self-oscillation in many filters — the filter generates a sine wave at its cutoff frequency regardless of the input signal. This sine wave can be tuned to pitch and used as an additional oscillator.

Filter character: Different filter designs have distinct characters even when set to the same cutoff and resonance. The Moog ladder filter has a warm, smooth low-pass character with a musical resonance that never becomes harsh. The Roland SEM state variable filter has a more open, transparent quality. The Korg MS-20 filter is aggressive and distinctive. Software synthesizers model these classic filter designs — Moog's character in Minimoog emulations, Roland's character in Juno emulations. The filter is often the most sonically distinctive component of a synthesizer's sound.

Envelopes: Shaping Sound Over Time

An envelope is a control signal that changes over time in a defined shape. In subtractive synthesis, the most important envelope is the amplitude envelope — it controls how the loudness of a note changes from the moment it's triggered to the moment it ends. A second envelope typically controls the filter cutoff, creating timbral evolution over the same period.

The standard ADSR envelope has four stages: Attack, Decay, Sustain, and Release.

Attack: The time it takes for the envelope to rise from zero to its peak level after the note is triggered. Short attack (0–10 ms) produces an immediate, punchy onset — percussive sounds, plucked strings, aggressive leads. Long attack (100 ms–2 seconds) produces a gradual swell — pads, string section swells, ambient sounds that fade in.

Decay: After reaching its peak, the envelope falls to the sustain level over the decay time. Short decay combined with zero sustain produces a percussive shape — the sound reaches maximum and immediately begins falling. A longer decay allows the sound to hold briefly at maximum before settling into the sustain level.

Sustain: The level at which the envelope holds as long as the note is held down. Unlike attack and decay (which are time values), sustain is a level value (0–100%). Sustain at 100% means the sound holds at maximum level. Sustain at 0% means the sound decays completely — the note triggers a percussive shape regardless of how long you hold the key.

Release: The time it takes for the envelope to fall from the sustain level to zero after the note is released. Short release (0–20 ms) produces an abrupt cutoff — the note stops immediately when the key is released. Long release (500 ms–5 seconds) produces a smooth fade — the sound continues and gradually disappears after the key is lifted, like the reverb tail of an acoustic instrument.

Filter envelope: The filter envelope modulates the filter cutoff frequency over the same ADSR shape. With a positive envelope amount, the filter opens (cutoff rises) during the attack and decays back toward the initial cutoff — the classic "vowel-like" filter movement heard on bass sounds, synth leads, and plucked sounds. The amount of filter envelope modulation determines how much the filter opens — a large amount creates dramatic brightness changes; a small amount adds subtle movement.

LFOs: Cyclic Modulation

An LFO (Low-Frequency Oscillator) generates a periodic waveform at a frequency too low to be heard as pitch — typically 0.01 Hz to 20 Hz. Instead, it's used as a modulation source that varies other parameters cyclically over time.

LFO destinations: Pitch — produces vibrato, the pitch oscillation used by vocalists and string players. Filter cutoff — produces a rhythmic filter sweep ("wah" effect at slow rates, timbral pulsing at faster rates). Amplitude — produces tremolo, the amplitude oscillation heard in certain guitar amplifiers and vintage keyboard instruments. Pan — moves the sound across the stereo field cyclically. Pulse width — produces pulse width modulation, the classic chorus-like synth movement.

LFO rate: Slow rates (0.1–2 Hz) produce gradual, perceptible modulation. Medium rates (2–8 Hz) produce vibrato and tremolo speeds. Fast rates (8–20 Hz) produce more extreme timbral effects — rapidly pulsing filters and pitch for special effects rather than musical performance. Syncing the LFO rate to the project tempo (1/4, 1/8, 1/16 note) produces rhythmically locked modulation that feels intentional rather than random.

LFO shape: A sine wave LFO produces smooth, symmetrical modulation. A triangle is similar but with linear rather than curved transitions. A sawtooth produces a one-direction sweep that resets — useful for rhythmic filter sweeps that always move in one direction. A square wave alternates between two extreme values — useful for hard timbral switching effects. A sample-and-hold (random) shape jumps to random values at a controlled rate — useful for R2-D2-style random pitch effects and unpredictable filter movement.

Wavetable Synthesis

Wavetable synthesis uses pre-recorded single-cycle waveforms (wavetables) as the oscillator source instead of mathematically generated waveforms. A wavetable is a collection of many different single-cycle waveforms organised into a sequence. Scanning through the wavetable — moving from one waveform to the next — produces continuous timbral evolution as the oscillator's character changes.

Serum (Xfer Records), Vital (free), Surge XT (free), and Massive X (Native Instruments) are the most widely used wavetable synthesizers. These instruments allow importing custom wavetables, drawing waveforms by hand, and scanning through factory and user wavetables for distinctive timbral movement. The sound design range of wavetable synthesis significantly exceeds standard subtractive synthesis — wavetables can contain timbres that no mathematical oscillator waveform can produce.

FM Synthesis

FM (Frequency Modulation) synthesis generates complex timbres by using one oscillator (the modulator) to modulate the frequency of another (the carrier). The ratio between the carrier and modulator frequencies, and the modulation depth (how much the modulator affects the carrier's frequency), determine the resulting harmonic content. At low modulation depths, FM adds subtle harmonic richness. At high modulation depths, it produces inharmonic, metallic, or bell-like timbres unlike anything possible with subtractive synthesis.

The Yamaha DX7 (1983) made FM synthesis famous — its electric piano, bass, and bell presets defined the sound of 1980s pop and remain influential. Modern FM synthesizers include Ableton Operator, Native Instruments FM8, and the free Dexed (DX7 emulation). FM synthesis is notoriously difficult to program intuitively but rewards study with a unique and distinctive sonic range.

Practical Sound Design Exercises

Exercise 1 — Build a bass sound from scratch: Start with a sawtooth oscillator. Set the amplitude envelope to short attack (2 ms), short decay (100 ms), sustain at 60%, and medium release (200 ms). Set a second envelope to control the filter cutoff with moderate attack (5 ms), short decay (80 ms), sustain at 30%, and same release as the amplitude envelope. Set the initial filter cutoff at 300 Hz, and set the filter envelope amount to moderate. Play notes in the bass register and adjust the filter cutoff and envelope amounts until you have a moving bass sound. This is the fundamental architecture of most analogue bass sounds.

Exercise 2 — Build a pad: Use two detuned sawtooth oscillators (oscillator 2 detuned +5 cents). Set amplitude envelope to long attack (800 ms), no decay, full sustain, long release (1.5 seconds). Open the filter cutoff to 70%. Add an LFO on the filter cutoff at a slow rate (0.3 Hz) with a sine wave and low depth. The long attack creates the swell, the LFO creates gentle movement. This is the architecture of the vast majority of analogue pad sounds.

Exercise 3 — Identify synthesis types by ear: Listen to three tracks and identify elements that sound like subtractive synthesis (bright sawtooth with filter sweeps), wavetable synthesis (complex evolving timbres with digital character), and FM synthesis (metallic, bell-like, or classic DX7 electric piano sounds). Ear training for synthesis types accelerates learning — once you can hear what architecture produced a sound, you know where to start recreating it.
