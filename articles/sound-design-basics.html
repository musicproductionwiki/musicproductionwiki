Sound Design Basics: How Synthesis Actually Works

Every sound you have ever heard from a synthesizer was built from the same basic components — oscillators, filters, envelopes, and LFOs. Understanding what each component does transforms you from a preset browser into a sound designer.

MP The Music Production Wiki Team Updated April 2026 12 min read Beginner

The moment you stop using presets and start understanding what the knobs actually do is one of the most significant transitions in a producer's development. Presets are starting points — useful, sometimes excellent, but fundamentally borrowed. Sounds you build from scratch are yours — expressions of your specific aesthetic that cannot be replicated by anyone who hasn't spent the same time learning the same synthesizer. This guide covers the building blocks of subtractive synthesis — the most common synthesis method and the foundation for understanding virtually every synthesizer you will ever use.

The Signal Flow: How a Synthesizer Makes Sound

Every subtractive synthesizer follows the same basic signal flow, even though the interface and controls look different across different instruments. Understanding this flow gives you a mental model that applies across every synthesizer you ever use.

The signal starts at the oscillator — the sound generator. The oscillator produces a raw waveform at a pitch determined by the MIDI note being played. The waveform is harmonically rich — it contains a fundamental frequency and a series of overtones — and the specific harmonic content of the waveform determines its tonal character.

The oscillator's output passes through the filter — a circuit that selectively removes certain frequencies from the signal. A low-pass filter removes high frequencies, leaving only low frequencies. A high-pass filter removes low frequencies. A band-pass filter removes both high and low frequencies, leaving only a band in the middle. The filter is the primary tonal shaping tool in subtractive synthesis — you start with a harmonically rich oscillator waveform and subtract frequencies to arrive at the sound you want.

The filtered signal passes through the amplifier — the circuit that controls the overall volume of the sound. The amplifier's level is typically controlled by an envelope, creating the volume shape of the sound over time.

Envelopes control how parameters change over time when a note is triggered. The volume envelope shapes how the sound's amplitude evolves from the moment a key is pressed to the moment it stops. LFOs (Low Frequency Oscillators) add modulation — periodic variation — to parameters, creating vibrato, tremolo, filter sweeps, and rhythmic movement.

Oscillator Waveforms: The Starting Point of Every Sound

The oscillator's waveform is the raw material that everything else shapes. Different waveforms have different harmonic content — different amounts of each overtone — which gives them different tonal characters.

Sine wave: The simplest waveform — a pure fundamental frequency with no overtones. A sine wave sounds smooth, pure, and somewhat hollow. It lacks the richness and complexity of other waveforms, which makes it useful for sub-bass sounds (where overtones would conflict with the kick drum) and for additive synthesis where you build complex tones from multiple sine waves. A pure sine wave on its own rarely sounds interesting as a lead or pad sound because it lacks harmonic content for the filter to sculpt.

Sawtooth wave: The richest common waveform — contains a fundamental and all overtones (both odd and even harmonics) in decreasing amplitude. The result is a bright, buzzy, harmonically dense sound that responds dramatically to filter cutoff changes. The sawtooth wave is the most commonly used oscillator waveform in synthesis because its harmonic richness gives the filter maximum material to work with. Pad sounds, lead sounds, and bass sounds all commonly start with sawtooth waves.

Square wave: Contains a fundamental and only odd harmonics — a harmonic series that creates a hollow, reedy quality reminiscent of woodwind instruments. The pulse width of the square wave — how wide the positive portion of the cycle is relative to the negative — determines its specific harmonic content. A 50% pulse width is a perfect square wave. Other pulse widths create asymmetric waveforms with different harmonic content. Pulse width modulation — continuously varying the pulse width using an LFO — creates the animated, chorusing character associated with classic synthesizer sounds.

Triangle wave: Contains a fundamental and only odd harmonics, similar to the square wave, but with harmonics that decrease in amplitude faster than the square wave. The result is a softer, flute-like tone with less harmonic richness than the square or sawtooth. Triangle waves are useful for softer lead sounds, flute simulations, and as a modulation source rather than a primary oscillator.

Noise: White noise contains all frequencies at equal amplitude — it sounds like radio static. Pink noise has more energy in the low frequencies. Both are used in synthesis as components of percussion sounds (adding the breath and hiss of cymbals and snare drums) and as sources of randomization in modulation chains.

The Filter: The Heart of Subtractive Synthesis

The filter is the most expressive component of a subtractive synthesizer. Its two primary controls — cutoff frequency and resonance — determine the character of the sound more than any other parameter.

Cutoff frequency determines which frequencies pass through the filter and which are removed. In a low-pass filter — the most common filter type — frequencies below the cutoff pass through and frequencies above are attenuated. Turn the cutoff down and the sound becomes darker, removing the high-frequency overtones. Turn it up and the sound becomes brighter, allowing more of the oscillator's harmonic content through. Automating or modulating the cutoff frequency over time creates the filter sweeps that are central to electronic music production — the sound of a synthesizer opening up or closing down as the music evolves.

Resonance (also called emphasis or Q) boosts the frequencies at the cutoff frequency, creating a peak or emphasis at that point in the frequency spectrum. At moderate resonance settings, this adds a characteristic coloration to the filtered sound — a singing, nasal quality that responds to cutoff changes with increased expressiveness. At high resonance settings, the filter begins to self-oscillate — generating its own sine wave at the cutoff frequency independently of the oscillator. Self-oscillation turns the filter into its own sine wave oscillator, useful for sound effects, bass sounds, and specialized tonal applications.

Envelopes: Shaping Sound Over Time

An envelope controls how a parameter changes over time in response to a note being triggered and released. The standard envelope — called an ADSR envelope — has four stages: Attack, Decay, Sustain, and Release.

Attack is the time it takes for the parameter to reach its maximum value after a note is triggered. A fast attack on the volume envelope (close to 0ms) means the sound begins at full volume immediately when a key is pressed — like a drum hit or a plucked string. A slow attack means the sound gradually builds from silence to full volume — like a string section swelling or a pad evolving.

Decay is the time it takes for the parameter to fall from its maximum value to the sustain level after the attack phase completes. A fast decay on the volume envelope gives the sound a percussive quality — it hits hard and falls quickly. A slow decay gives the sound a sustained quality — it stays near its peak level for longer before settling to the sustain level.

Sustain is the level at which the parameter holds while a note is held. Unlike attack, decay, and release — which are time values — sustain is a level value. While you hold a key, the sound maintains its volume at the sustain level. Release the key and the sound begins the release phase.

Release is the time it takes for the parameter to fall from the sustain level to zero after a note is released. A fast release means the sound cuts off abruptly when you lift your finger. A slow release means the sound fades gradually — like reverb on an acoustic piano or the sustain of a string section after the bow lifts.

Envelopes can be applied to any parameter — not just volume. Applying an envelope to the filter cutoff creates sounds that evolve tonally over time — starting dark and opening brightly, or starting at full brightness and slowly darkening. This filter envelope is one of the most commonly used techniques in synthesis and one of the primary tools for creating sounds that feel alive and dynamic rather than static.

LFOs: Adding Movement and Modulation

An LFO — Low Frequency Oscillator — is an oscillator that runs at frequencies below the audible range (typically below 20Hz) and is used to modulate — periodically vary — other parameters. Where envelopes shape parameters in response to note triggers, LFOs shape parameters continuously and periodically — creating the sense of movement, animation, and life that distinguishes interesting synthesizer sounds from static ones.

An LFO applied to pitch creates vibrato — the periodic pitch variation that singers and string players use expressively. An LFO applied to volume creates tremolo — the periodic amplitude variation used in guitar effects and Rhodes electric pianos. An LFO applied to filter cutoff creates a filter wobble — the animated, evolving quality of many electronic music sounds. An LFO applied to pulse width creates the pulse width modulation described earlier — the chorusing, animated quality of classic synthesizer pad sounds.

LFO rate — the speed of the modulation — and LFO depth — the amount by which the modulated parameter varies — are the primary controls. Rate can often be synced to the project tempo, creating modulation that moves in rhythmic relationship to the music rather than independently of it. Tempo-synced LFOs are essential for creating rhythmic filter wobbles, sidechaining effects, and other modulation that needs to align precisely with the beat.

The Sound Design Exercise That Teaches Everything

Open any synthesizer. Start from an initialized patch — all settings reset to default. Play a note and listen to the raw oscillator. Now slowly turn down the filter cutoff while holding the note. Listen to the harmonics disappearing as you remove them. Now add resonance and sweep the cutoff again. Now apply an envelope to the filter with a fast attack, medium decay, and low sustain — the sound will have a characteristic pluck at the start that evolves into a darker sustain. Now add an LFO to the filter cutoff at a slow rate — the sound begins to breathe. In fifteen minutes you have covered the fundamental techniques of subtractive synthesis by doing rather than reading. That experimentation, repeated across different synthesizers with different starting points, is how sound designers build their vocabulary.
