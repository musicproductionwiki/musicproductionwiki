Sound Design Basics: Your First Steps into Synthesis

First published May 2026 · Updated May 2026
MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 Beginner → Advanced
Quick Answer

Sound design is the process of creating sounds from scratch using synthesizers, rather than using pre-recorded samples. A synthesizer generates sound using oscillators (which produce the initial waveform), filters (which shape the tone), amplifiers (which control the volume), and modulators (like envelopes and LFOs that make the sound change over time). Understanding these four components — oscillators, filters, amplifiers, and modulators — gives you the ability to create virtually any electronic sound.

Oscillators — Where Sound Starts Beginner

An oscillator generates a repeating waveform at a specific frequency (pitch). Different waveform shapes have different harmonic content and sound completely different even at the same pitch:

Sine wave: A pure tone with no harmonics — just the fundamental frequency. Sounds smooth and clean, like a tuning fork or flute tone. Fundamental for bass sub frequencies and smooth pads.

Sawtooth wave: Contains all harmonics (both odd and even), producing a bright, rich, buzzy tone. The most common starting point for bass sounds, brass emulations, and lead synths. Most of the warm pad sounds in electronic music history start with sawtooth waves processed through filters and effects.

Square wave: Contains only odd harmonics, producing a hollow, woody, clarinet-like tone. Classic for bass sounds and leads in chiptune and lo-fi contexts.

Triangle wave: Similar to a sine wave with additional odd harmonics — warmer than a square wave but richer than a sine. Good for mellow pads and flute-like leads.

Noise: Not a periodic waveform — random frequencies producing a "hiss" sound. Used in percussion synthesis (snare drum synthesis uses white or pink noise filtered by the amp envelope), textural elements, and rhythmic noise effects.

Filters — Shaping the Tone Beginner

Synthesizer filters remove frequencies from the oscillator's output. The most common type is the low-pass filter (LPF), which passes low frequencies and removes high frequencies above the cutoff frequency. This is the fundamental sound-shaping tool of analog synthesis — it's what gives analog synths their warmth and character.

Cutoff frequency determines which frequencies are removed. Low cutoff = dark, muffled tone. High cutoff = bright, full-spectrum tone. Sweeping the cutoff from low to high is the classic "filter sweep" effect fundamental to electronic music.

Resonance (also called Q or emphasis) boosts the frequencies right at the cutoff point, creating a peaked, nasal quality. High resonance values create a whistle-like resonant peak that's useful for specific tonal effects. At maximum resonance on many filter designs, the filter self-oscillates, producing a sine wave at the cutoff frequency — a technique used for specific bass and effects sounds.

High-pass filter (HPF) removes low frequencies below the cutoff. Used to thin out a sound and remove low-end weight.

Band-pass filter (BPF) passes a narrow band of frequencies and removes everything above and below. Creates nasal, telephone-quality tones useful for specific effect and creative applications.

The ADSR Envelope — Shaping Volume Over Time Beginner

The ADSR envelope is the most fundamental modulation tool in synthesis. It controls how the amplitude of a sound changes over time when a note is triggered. ADSR stands for: Attack, Decay, Sustain, Release.

Attack: How long it takes for the sound to reach its maximum volume after a note is triggered. Short attack = immediate sound (percussion, plucks). Long attack = slow fade-in (pad swells, ambient textures).

Decay: After reaching maximum volume, how long it takes to fall to the Sustain level. A fast decay creates a pluck or ping character. A slow decay creates a more gradual settling.

Sustain: Not a time value — a level. This is how loud the sound is while a note is held. High sustain = the sound stays near its peak as long as the key is held. Zero sustain = the sound decays completely before the note is released (like a piano note or a plucked string).

Release: After releasing the note, how long the sound takes to fade to silence. Short release = abrupt cut. Long release = gradual fade (typical of pad and string sounds).

The ADSR envelope applied to the filter cutoff (rather than the amplitude) creates a filter envelope — a classic sound design technique. A fast filter attack and short filter decay opens the filter briefly when a note is triggered and then closes it, creating the characteristic "vowel sweep" of funk bass sounds and many lead synth sounds.

LFO — The Modulation Engine Intermediate

LFO stands for Low Frequency Oscillator — an oscillator running so slowly that its frequency is below the range of hearing (below 20Hz). Instead of generating audible sound, the LFO generates a slow waveshape that modulates (moves) other parameters over time.

An LFO applied to pitch creates vibrato — the subtle pitch wavering of a string player or vocalist. An LFO applied to amplitude creates tremolo — a rhythmic volume pulsation. An LFO applied to the filter cutoff creates a wah-wah effect — the filter opens and closes rhythmically. An LFO applied to the stereo position creates auto-panning.

The LFO rate determines how fast the modulation occurs. Slow LFOs (below 1Hz) create gradual, almost imperceptible changes that add life to static sounds without obvious movement. Fast LFOs (5–20Hz) create obvious rhythmic effects. Syncing the LFO rate to the tempo of the track (common in most modern synthesizers) ensures the modulation feels musical rather than random.

Building Your First Sound Beginner

A simple exercise that teaches every major concept: build a basic bass sound from scratch.

  1. Start with initialized (blank) settings on any subtractive synthesizer.
  2. Set one oscillator to a sawtooth wave at pitch C2 (or the lowest range your synth offers).
  3. Set the filter to low-pass with cutoff at about 30% (fairly closed, dark tone). Set resonance at about 20%.
  4. Set ADSR on the amp envelope: Attack 0, Decay 100ms, Sustain 60%, Release 100ms. You now have a sound with a fast attack, a slight settle, and a sustained body.
  5. Play a note. You now have a simple bass sound.
  6. Now apply the filter envelope: Attack 0, Decay 200ms, Sustain 0%, Release 0. Set the filter envelope amount to about 40%. Now the filter opens briefly when you trigger the note (creating a bright attack transient) and closes quickly to the cutoff position. This is what gives bass sounds that characteristic "punch."
  7. Experiment: change the oscillator to a square wave. Change the filter cutoff. Adjust the filter envelope amount. Each change teaches you what each control does in a real sound.
