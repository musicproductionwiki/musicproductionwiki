How to Make Techno

MP

To make techno, start with a punchy kick drum at 120-135 BPM, layer a rolling bassline, add hypnotic synth loops and percussion patterns, then arrange in 8 or 16-bar sections with gradual transitions. Focus on driving rhythm, repetition with subtle variation, and creating tension through filters, effects, and strategic breakdowns.

Techno remains one of electronic music's most enduring and innovative genres, spanning from Detroit's original machine funk to Berlin's industrial minimalism and everything in between. Whether you're drawn to the deep, hypnotic grooves of dub techno, the aggressive energy of peak-time bangers, or the experimental territories of avant-garde techno, understanding the fundamental production techniques will give you the foundation to express your unique sonic vision.

This comprehensive guide walks through every stage of techno production, from establishing your rhythmic foundation to mastering the subtle details that separate professional tracks from bedroom demos. Updated May 2026, this article reflects current production standards while honoring techno's rich history and core principles.

Understanding Techno Fundamentals and Genre Context

Before diving into production techniques, it's essential to understand what defines techno as a genre. Techno emerged in Detroit during the mid-1980s through pioneers like Juan Atkins, Derrick May, and Kevin Saunderson, who fused electronic instrumentation with funk, electro, and European synth-pop influences. The genre emphasizes repetition, mechanical precision, futuristic aesthetics, and the primacy of rhythm over melody.

The typical techno track operates between 120-135 BPM, though subgenres vary considerably. Minimal techno might sit at 125 BPM with sparse arrangements, while peak-time techno often pushes 130-135 BPM with dense, driving energy. Dub techno and ambient techno frequently work at slower tempos around 120-125 BPM, prioritizing atmosphere and space.

Techno's defining characteristic is its relationship with repetition and subtle variation. Unlike genres that rely on dramatic chord changes or verse-chorus structures, techno builds hypnotic momentum through gradual evolution. A single eight-bar loop might extend across several minutes, with producers introducing microscopic changes—filter sweeps, percussion additions, subtle modulations—that transform the listener's perception without disrupting the groove.

The genre also emphasizes functional dance floor effectiveness. Professional techno producers consider how tracks will sound on large club systems, how they'll mix with other records, and how they'll affect dancers over extended periods. This practical consideration influences every production decision from frequency balance to arrangement structure.

Modern techno production benefits from both analog and digital tools. While classic hardware like the Roland TR-909, TR-808, TB-303, and various analog synthesizers defined early techno's sound, contemporary producers achieve equally authentic results using software emulations, sample libraries, and digital synthesis. The key lies in understanding the sonic principles rather than fetishizing specific equipment.

Building the Foundation: Kick Drum and Bassline

The kick drum forms techno's absolute foundation. Unlike genres where kicks provide periodic punctuation, techno's four-on-the-floor pattern makes the kick both timekeeper and primary musical element. Your kick must be powerful, consistent, and capable of driving dancers for hours without listener fatigue.

Start by selecting or designing a kick with appropriate character for your subgenre. Peak-time techno typically uses punchy kicks with pronounced attack transients and substantial sub-bass content extending below 60 Hz. The attack should hit hard between 80-120 Hz where human hearing perceives punch, while sub-bass rumble fills the lower spectrum. Minimal and dub techno often favor softer, rounder kicks with less aggressive attacks and more sustained low-frequency content.

Many producers layer multiple kick samples to achieve optimal results. A common technique combines a transient-rich top kick (providing click and attack) with a sub-focused bottom kick (providing weight and power). Process these separately—compress the top kick heavily for consistency, apply subtle saturation for harmonics, and use EQ to carve specific frequency roles for each layer. The top kick might occupy 80-500 Hz with a sharp transient, while the sub kick dominates 30-80 Hz with longer sustain.

Kick drum synthesis offers precise control over every parameter. Many techno producers use dedicated kick synthesizers like Sonic Academy's Kick 2 or sample-based tools like Ableton's Sampler with pitched sine waves. A basic synthesized kick starts with a sine wave oscillator that pitches down rapidly from around 150-200 Hz to 40-60 Hz over 50-200 milliseconds. Add a noise burst or transient layer for attack definition, apply an amplitude envelope with fast attack and adjustable decay/sustain, then process with saturation and compression.

The bassline works in symbiotic relationship with the kick. In techno, bass rarely follows complex melodic progressions—instead, it provides rolling, hypnotic movement that locks with the kick's rhythm. The classic techno bassline sits slightly off-beat, creating syncopated interplay with the four-on-the-floor kick pattern.

For bass sound design, techno typically employs subtractive synthesis with simple waveforms. A sawtooth or square wave filtered through a low-pass resonant filter creates the characteristic acidic, growling bass tone associated with the Roland TB-303 and its countless software emulations. Set your filter cutoff around 200-800 Hz initially, add moderate resonance (30-50%), and apply filter envelope modulation with fast attack and medium decay to create the distinctive "squelch" that defines acid techno basslines.

Alternative bass approaches include sine wave sub-bass (common in minimal techno for pure low-frequency power without harmonics), FM synthesis (for digital, metallic tones in industrial techno), and resampled/processed bass sounds. Regardless of sound source, ensure your bassline occupies a complementary frequency range to your kick—if your kick dominates 40-80 Hz, tune your bassline's fundamental frequency above 80 Hz and use EQ to create separation.

Programming techno basslines requires understanding syncopation and groove. Rather than playing straight eighth or sixteenth notes, introduce rhythmic variation through off-beat placements, rests, and subtle timing shifts. A typical pattern might hit on the "and" of beat 1, the downbeat of beat 2, skip beat 3 entirely, then hit the "and" of beat 4. Vary note lengths—some notes sustain while others are staccato. Use velocity variation to create dynamic movement, and consider applying subtle swing (typically 8-15%) to add human feel to programmed patterns.

The relationship between kick and bass determines your track's fundamental groove. Many producers use sidechain compression to duck the bass slightly whenever the kick hits, creating rhythmic pumping that enhances the groove and prevents frequency masking. Set your compressor with fast attack (0.1-1 ms), medium-fast release (50-150 ms), and moderate ratio (3:1 to 6:1). Apply just enough gain reduction (3-6 dB) to create perceptible ducking without destroying the bass's sustain.

Percussion and Rhythm Design

Beyond kick and bass, techno's percussive elements create the rhythmic complexity and textural detail that define each track's character. The hi-hat pattern serves as primary timekeeping alongside the kick, while additional percussion—claps, snares, shakers, toms, and various synthetic sounds—builds rhythmic interest and forward momentum.

Hi-hats in techno typically follow sixteenth-note patterns with velocity variation creating dynamic movement. Rather than playing all hi-hats at equal velocity, create groove by accenting certain hits while keeping others quiet. A common pattern accents every fourth sixteenth note (creating implied eighth-note pulse) with additional accents on syncopated positions. Many producers program closed hi-hats on most sixteenth notes with occasional open hi-hats on eighth or quarter notes for variation.

The classic Roland TR-909 remains the hi-hat reference standard in techno, offering crisp, metallic tones that cut through dense mixes. Software emulations like AudioRealism's Drum Machine (ADM) or D16 Group's Nepheton provide authentic 909 sounds, while sample libraries offer thousands of variations. Process hi-hats with high-pass filtering (removing content below 200-400 Hz), subtle saturation for harmonic enhancement, and compression for consistency.

Claps and snares provide backbeat emphasis, typically hitting on beats 2 and 4 in traditional patterns. However, techno frequently subverts this expectation—you might place claps on off-beats, use multiple layered claps with different timings for a "rolling" effect, or omit them entirely in minimal arrangements. When layering claps, combine samples with different tonal characteristics: one for transient snap, another for body and sustain, perhaps a third for room ambience. Apply reverb (particularly plate or hall algorithms with 1.5-2.5 second decay) to create the spacious clap sound characteristic of many techno productions.

Percussion loops and one-shots add textural variety and rhythmic complexity. Shakers, tambourines, congas, timbales, woodblocks, and countless synthesized percussion sounds populate techno tracks, each serving specific roles. Shakers and tambourines add high-frequency shimmer and continuous motion. Toms and timbales provide melodic percussion hits during transitions. Rim shots, claves, and woodblocks offer sharp transient accents that punctuate specific rhythmic moments.

When programming percussion, consider polyrhythmic relationships and implied rhythms. Rather than simply adding more sounds playing the same pattern, create interlocking rhythms where different elements accent different subdivisions. For example, your hi-hats might emphasize sixteenth notes, shakers play dotted eighth notes, and a clave pattern hits every three eighth notes—the mathematical interaction creates subtle complexity that rewards repeated listening.

Rhythm Programming Pro Tip: Create humanization and groove by applying subtle randomization to percussion timing, velocity, and pitch. Shift individual hits forward or backward by 5-20 milliseconds, vary velocities within 10-20% ranges, and detune similar percussion sounds by 5-10 cents. These micro-variations prevent machine-gun repetition while maintaining electronic precision.

Percussion processing significantly impacts the final sound. Compression glues elements together and creates punch—use parallel compression to maintain transients while adding sustain and body. Send percussion to a heavily compressed bus (10:1 ratio, fast attack, medium release, driving 10-15 dB of gain reduction), then blend this crushed signal under your dry percussion for density without losing definition.

Saturation and distortion add harmonic content and presence. Subtle tape saturation warms digital percussion, while aggressive distortion transforms sterile samples into gritty, characterful sounds. Try different saturation algorithms—tube saturation for smooth warmth, transformer saturation for midrange punch, tape saturation for gentle compression and harmonic color, or bit-crushing for digital degradation effects common in industrial techno.

Percussion placement in the stereo field creates width and separation. Keep kick, snare, and primary bass elements centered for mono compatibility and low-frequency focus. Pan hi-hats slightly off-center (15-30% left or right), place shakers and tambourines wider (40-60%), and position accent percussion across the stereo field for spatial interest. Use the Haas effect—delaying one channel by 10-30 milliseconds—to create width from mono percussion sources without phase issues.

Synthesis and Sound Design for Techno

Synthesized elements—pads, leads, stabs, and textural sounds—provide melodic and harmonic content in techno while maintaining the genre's characteristic hypnotic repetition. Unlike pop or trance with memorable hooks, techno synth parts often function as textural layers that evolve gradually over extended periods.

Subtractive synthesis forms the foundation of classic techno sound design. This approach starts with harmonically rich waveforms (sawtooth, square, triangle) and uses filters to remove frequencies, shaping the sound's character. A typical techno pad might combine detuned sawtooth oscillators passed through a low-pass filter with moderate resonance, then processed with chorus and reverb. Keep note patterns simple—single sustained notes, two-note oscillations, or simple arpeggios work better than complex melodies.

The filter sweep remains techno's most iconic sound design technique. Automate your filter cutoff frequency over 8, 16, or 32 bars, slowly opening from closed (removing high frequencies) to open (allowing full harmonic spectrum). This gradual transformation creates tension and release, building energy that payoffs when the filter fully opens. Enhance sweeps by simultaneously increasing resonance as cutoff increases, creating the characteristic resonant peak that emphasizes specific frequencies as the sweep progresses.

FM synthesis offers digital, metallic, and complex tones that complement subtractive synthesis's analog warmth. Frequency modulation creates inharmonic overtones perfect for bell-like stabs, digital bass sounds, and aggressive lead tones. Native Instruments' FM8, Ableton's Operator, and numerous other software FM synths provide accessible FM programming. Start with simple two-operator patches—use operator 1 as carrier (heard sound) and operator 2 as modulator (affecting carrier's timbre), then adjust modulation amount and modulator frequency ratio to explore tonal variations.

Wavetable synthesis has become increasingly central to modern techno production. Unlike traditional oscillators generating static waveforms, wavetable synthesis morphs between different waveforms stored in tables, creating evolving timbres. Xfer Serum, Native Instruments' Massive X, and Ableton's Wavetable excel at this approach. Create movement by slowly scanning through wavetable positions using LFOs or envelopes, generating continuously evolving textures without manual automation.

Techno's hypnotic quality comes from creating movement within repetition. Rather than writing new melodies every 8 bars, design sounds that evolve through internal modulation. Route LFOs to filter cutoff, oscillator pitch, wavetable position, or effect parameters. Use slow LFO rates (1/4 note to 4 bars) for gradual evolution, faster rates (1/16 note to 1/4 note) for rhythmic wobbles and gating effects. Experiment with different LFO waveforms—sine waves for smooth modulation, triangle for linear sweeps, square for stepped changes, and random for unpredictable variation.

Layering multiple synth sounds creates depth and richness. Combine a sub-focused bass layer with a midrange harmonic layer and a high-frequency sparkle layer, each occupying distinct frequency ranges. Process layers independently—compress the bass layer heavily for consistency, add saturation to the midrange layer for warmth, and apply stereo widening to the high layer for spatial width. This approach creates full-spectrum sounds that maintain clarity in busy mixes.

Resampling transforms synthesized sounds into unique sample-based material. Create a synth sound with heavy modulation or effect processing, record several minutes of output, then chop interesting sections into new samples. This technique captures happy accidents and creates sounds impossible to recreate with standard programming. Granular synthesis plugins like Granulator in Ableton Live or Output's Portal can further mangle resampled material, generating abstract textures perfect for experimental techno.

Arrangement and Track Structure

Techno arrangement differs fundamentally from pop song structure. Rather than verse-chorus alternation, techno builds through gradual addition and subtraction of elements over extended periods. A typical techno track runs 6-8 minutes, allowing DJs adequate mixing time and giving arrangements space to breathe and evolve naturally.

The standard techno arrangement follows a loose template: intro (16-32 bars), buildup 1 (16-32 bars), main section 1 (32-64 bars), breakdown (16-32 bars), buildup 2 (16-32 bars), main section 2/peak (32-64 bars), outro (16-32 bars). However, successful techno producers treat this as flexible guideline rather than rigid formula, adjusting based on the track's specific energy and intended use.

Start arrangements with DJ-friendly intros. The first 16-32 bars should establish the groove with drums and percussion, allowing DJs to beatmatch and begin their mix before your track's distinctive elements enter. Begin with kick and hi-hats, gradually adding percussion elements every 4-8 bars. Keep intros relatively sparse—save your main bass, synth hooks, and unique elements for after the 32-bar mark.

The buildup creates anticipation through layering, filtering, and rising energy. Add elements progressively—introduce the bassline, bring in a filtered synth pad, add a rolling percussion loop. Use filter automation to create tension, slowly opening low-pass filters or closing high-pass filters. Layer white noise sweeps, apply rising pitch bends, or program drum fills in the final 4-8 bars before the main section drops. The buildup's final moments should create maximum anticipation, making the drop to the main section feel powerful and satisfying.

Main sections present your track's core groove at full intensity. All primary elements—kick, bass, main synth parts, percussion—play together, creating the hypnotic, driving energy that defines the track's character. While maintaining core groove consistency, introduce subtle variations every 8-16 bars: bring percussion elements in and out, apply filter sweeps to sustained sounds, add transitional effects, or introduce secondary melodic elements. These micro-changes maintain interest without disrupting the hypnotic flow.

Breakdowns provide contrast and create opportunities for dramatic re-entries. Reduce arrangement density by removing elements—typically starting with the kick and bass, leaving atmospheric pads, reverb tails, and sparse percussion. Breakdowns might last 16-32 bars, giving dancers momentary rest while building anticipation for the next drop. Apply creative effects during breakdowns: reverse cymbal sweeps, delay feedback buildups, granular textures, or abstract processing that prepares listeners for the groove's return.

The second main section or peak typically adds new elements or intensifies existing ones. You might introduce a new synth line, add a secondary bassline, increase percussion density, or bring in vocal samples. This section represents your track's highest energy point—the moment that justifies the journey through previous sections. Many techno tracks peak around the 4-5 minute mark, then gradually reduce intensity during the outro.

Outros mirror intros in DJ functionality, providing 16-32 bars of sparse, mixable material. Remove distinctive elements early in the outro, leaving kick, hi-hats, and basic percussion. This allows the next DJ to begin their mix using your track's steady rhythm as foundation. Some producers end with just the kick drum for the final 8-16 bars, creating maximum mixing flexibility.

Consider energy management across the entire arrangement. Visualize your track's energy curve—intensity should generally rise from intro through peak, then fall during the outro, but include moments of tension and release along the way. Use breakdowns as energy valleys that make subsequent peaks feel more powerful. Avoid maintaining maximum intensity for too long; even aggressive peak-time techno benefits from dynamic variation that prevents listener fatigue.

Work in 8 and 16-bar blocks, which align with DJ mixing patterns and create natural phrase structures. When adding or removing elements, do so at these boundaries. However, don't let rigid 8-bar patterns make your arrangement predictable—occasionally extend sections to 12 or 20 bars, or introduce elements at unexpected moments to surprise experienced listeners familiar with standard structures.

Mixing and Mastering Techno

Mixing techno requires balancing power with clarity, ensuring your track sounds massive on club systems while maintaining definition across all elements. The mixing process begins during production—good sound selection and arrangement make mixing considerably easier—but dedicated mixing attention transforms decent tracks into professional productions.

Start with gain staging to prevent clipping and maintain headroom. Set your master fader around -6 dB, giving space for mastering processing. Adjust individual channel faders so nothing clips and your master peaks around -6 to -10 dBFS. Many producers mix at lower levels (around -18 dBFS on the master) to prevent digital distortion and ear fatigue, then push levels higher during mastering.

Frequency balancing ensures no range dominates inappropriately. Use a spectrum analyzer to visualize frequency distribution—techno typically emphasizes lows (30-250 Hz) and highs (8-20 kHz) with slightly reduced midrange. However, don't mix with your eyes; use the analyzer to identify problems (excessive mud around 200-400 Hz, harsh resonances in the 2-5 kHz range) but trust your ears for creative decisions.

Apply subtractive EQ before additive EQ. Remove problematic frequencies first—cut buildup around 200-300 Hz that clouds mixes, notch harsh resonances in the 2-5 kHz range, roll off unnecessary sub-bass below 30 Hz. Then make additive boosts to enhance desired characteristics, though often subtractive EQ alone achieves better results than excessive boosting. When boosting, use moderate Q values (0.7-1.5) to avoid unnatural filtering, and rarely boost more than 3-6 dB at any frequency.

Create separation through EQ carving. If your kick dominates 50-80 Hz and your bass occupies 80-150 Hz, use EQ to emphasize these distinct ranges while cutting each element in the other's territory. Cut bass around 50-70 Hz to make room for kick fundamentals, and cut kick around 120-150 Hz to clarify bass note definition. This complementary EQ approach creates powerful low-end without frequency masking.

Compression controls dynamics and adds punch. Apply gentle compression on individual elements (2-3:1 ratio, medium attack 10-30 ms, medium release 100-250 ms, 3-4 dB gain reduction) to even out levels without destroying transients. Use parallel compression on drum busses and synth groups, blending heavily compressed signals with dry versions for density and power while maintaining dynamic expression. Experiment with different compressor types—VCA compression for transparent control, FET compression for aggressive coloration, optical compression for smooth, musical compression.

Sidechain compression creates the pumping groove central to most techno. Beyond ducking bass with the kick, try sidechaining pads, synths, and even percussion to create rhythmic movement synchronized with the kick pattern. Adjust attack and release times to taste—fast attack (0.1-1 ms) creates immediate ducking, while slower attack (5-20 ms) allows some transient through. Release times between 50-200 ms typically work well, automatically adjusting to your tempo.

Saturation and harmonic enhancement add warmth, character, and perceived loudness. Apply subtle saturation on most channels—a few percent of any saturation algorithm adds harmonic richness and helps sounds cut through dense mixes. Different saturation types serve different purposes: tape saturation for analog warmth, tube saturation for smooth even-order harmonics, transformer saturation for punch and aggressive coloration. Mixing with saturation techniques can dramatically improve your techno production quality.

Reverb and delay create space and depth. Use reverb to place elements in three-dimensional space—close (short reverb with early reflections), medium (1.5-2.5 second plate or hall reverb), or distant (long ambient reverb 3+ seconds). Keep kick and bass relatively dry to maintain low-frequency clarity. Apply reverb to claps, snares, synths, and percussion, often using different reverb instances for different depth zones. Consider using high-pass filters on reverb returns to prevent low-frequency muddiness.

Delay serves both rhythmic and spatial purposes. Use ping-pong delay for stereo width, dub delay for spacious trails, and filtered delay for textural interest. Common techno delay settings include eighth-note and dotted-eighth-note patterns, often with 20-50% feedback for multiple repeats. Automate delay feedback or filter cutoff during transitions for dramatic buildups where delays spiral into feedback chaos before cutting clean at the drop.

Reference your mix against professional techno releases in similar styles. Import reference tracks into your project and A/B compare at matched levels (commercial releases are typically 6-10 dB louder, so adjust accordingly). Listen critically to how your low-end compares—is your kick as punchy? Does your bass have similar weight? Check high-frequency balance, stereo width, and overall density. Note differences, then adjust your mix to achieve similar characteristics while maintaining your track's unique identity.

The mastering process finalizes your track for distribution and club play. While professional mastering engineers bring specialized expertise and acoustic environments, understanding basic mastering principles helps you create competitive self-mastered tracks or communicate effectively with mastering professionals. Mastering typically involves EQ for tonal balance, compression for glue and consistency, limiting for loudness, and final quality control.

Mastering EQ addresses the complete frequency spectrum with subtle adjustments. You might apply a gentle high-pass filter at 25-30 Hz to remove subsonic rumble, boost 30-50 Hz by 0.5-1 dB for sub-bass weight, cut 200-300 Hz by 0.5-1 dB if the mix sounds muddy, boost 8-12 kHz by 0.5-1.5 dB for air and sparkle. Use linear-phase EQ for transparent processing or minimum-phase EQ for analog character—both approaches work depending on material and preference.

Multiband compression controls dynamics differently across frequency ranges. You might compress lows (20-150 Hz) with 3:1 ratio and medium attack/release to control kick and bass consistency, compress midrange (150 Hz-5 kHz) gently with 2:1 ratio, and compress highs (5-20 kHz) with 2:1 ratio to control harsh transients. Multiband compression requires careful adjustment—excessive processing creates unnatural pumping and lifeless dynamics.

Mastering limiting pushes final loudness while preventing clipping. Target integrated loudness around -9 to -14 LUFS for techno, depending on style and platform requirements. Aggressive peak-time techno might hit -9 LUFS, while deeper, more dynamic styles work better around -12 LUFS. Use true-peak limiting with oversampling to prevent intersample peaks that cause distortion during format conversion. Set your limiter ceiling to -0.3 to -1.0 dBTP (decibels true peak), allowing safety margin for encoding.

Export final masters at maximum quality settings. Use 24-bit depth at your project sample rate (typically 44.1 or 48 kHz), with dithering only if downsampling from higher bit depths. Export both WAV and high-quality MP3 (320 kbps) or AAC formats. Listen to your final master on multiple systems—studio monitors, headphones, car stereo, phone speakers, laptop speakers—ensuring it sounds good across all playback contexts. Pay special attention to how your track sounds on club systems or large speakers, as this represents techno's primary listening environment.

Practical Exercises

Beginner Exercise

Build Your First Techno Loop

Create an 8-bar loop with just kick, bass, and hi-hats. Set your tempo to 126 BPM, program a four-on-the-floor kick pattern, add a simple rolling bassline using 3-5 notes in a minor scale, and program sixteenth-note hi-hats with velocity variation. Focus on getting the kick punchy and making the bass groove with the kick using sidechain compression.

Intermediate Exercise

Design an Evolving Pad Sound

Create a 32-bar pad progression that transforms continuously using filter and LFO modulation. Use subtractive synthesis with detuned sawtooth oscillators, route an LFO to filter cutoff with a 16-bar cycle time, and automate filter resonance to increase over the 32 bars. Add effects processing with reverb and subtle chorus, then record and listen to how the sound evolves organically through internal modulation rather than note changes.

Advanced Exercise

Complete Arrangement with Dynamic Energy Curve

Produce a 7-minute techno track with clearly defined arrangement sections and deliberate energy management. Map your entire arrangement before adding details—mark intro, buildups, main sections, breakdown, peak, and outro with precise bar counts. Create tension and release through strategic element addition/removal and filter automation. Reference a professional track in your target style, analyzing exactly when elements enter/exit and how energy evolves, then apply similar structural principles to your original composition while developing your unique sound.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ What BPM should I use for techno?
Most techno sits between 120-135 BPM. Deep and dub techno typically works at 120-125 BPM, minimal techno around 125-128 BPM, and peak-time/hard techno at 130-135 BPM or higher. Choose your tempo based on the energy level and subgenre you're producing.
FAQ Do I need hardware synthesizers to make authentic techno?
No, software synthesizers and sample libraries can produce equally authentic techno sounds. While hardware like the Roland TR-909, TR-808, and TB-303 defined classic techno, modern software emulations and digital tools capture these sounds accurately. Focus on understanding synthesis principles and sound design techniques rather than specific equipment.
FAQ How loud should I master my techno tracks?
Target integrated loudness between -9 and -14 LUFS depending on your style and distribution platform. Aggressive peak-time techno typically hits -9 to -11 LUFS, while deeper, more dynamic styles work better at -12 to -14 LUFS. Always maintain at least -0.3 dBTP true peak ceiling to prevent intersample clipping.
FAQ What's the best way to create movement in repetitive techno loops?
Use LFO modulation, filter sweeps, gradual element addition/subtraction, velocity variation, and automation of effect parameters. Route slow LFOs to filter cutoff, wavetable position, or effect wet/dry. Automate filters over 8-32 bar cycles. Introduce subtle variations every 8-16 bars through percussion changes or melodic embellishments without disrupting the core groove.
FAQ How do I make my kick drum punch through the mix?
Use EQ to emphasize the kick's punch frequency (80-120 Hz) and attack click (2-6 kHz), apply moderate compression for consistency, and use sidechain compression to duck competing elements. Layer multiple kick samples if needed—one for sub-bass, one for punch, one for click. Ensure the kick is properly tuned to your track's key to avoid frequency conflicts with the bassline.
FAQ What makes a good techno bassline?
Good techno basslines are rhythmically syncopated, tonally simple, and lock tightly with the kick drum. Focus on groove and rhythm rather than complex melodies. Use off-beat placements, vary note lengths, apply velocity variation, and create movement through filter modulation. The TB-303 acid bass sound—sawtooth wave through resonant low-pass filter—remains the archetypal techno bassline.
FAQ How long should my techno track be?
Standard techno tracks run 6-8 minutes to provide DJs adequate mixing time. Include 16-32 bar intros and outros with just drums for DJ mixing. While shorter versions work for digital streaming, full-length versions are essential for club and DJ use. Some experimental or live-oriented techno tracks extend beyond 8 minutes with continuous evolution.
FAQ Should I use swing or quantize everything to the grid?
Most techno uses tight quantization with minimal swing for mechanical, precise rhythms. However, subtle swing (8-15%) can add groove to percussion and hi-hats. Experiment with slight timing variations—some producers manually shift certain hits by 5-10 milliseconds for human feel without sacrificing electronic precision. The choice depends on whether you want rigid, machine-like grooves or slightly looser, more organic rhythms.
