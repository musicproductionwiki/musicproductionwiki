What Is Mixing

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 · 8 min read · Verified May 7, 2026
Quick Answer — Updated May 2026

Mixing is the process of blending all individual tracks in a song — drums, bass, guitars, vocals, synths — into a single polished stereo file. The mix engineer uses level balancing, EQ, compression, reverb, delay, and automation to make every element sound clear, balanced, and emotionally powerful together. The result is a stereo mix file, which is then passed to a mastering engineer for final distribution preparation.

Updated May 2026

When a song is recorded or produced, every instrument and vocal lives on its own separate track inside a DAW (digital audio workstation). The kick drum is on one track. The snare is on another. The lead vocal, the bass, the guitars, the synths — each has its own channel, recorded at different times, often in different spaces, by different people.

Mixing is the process of taking all those individual pieces and blending them into a single, cohesive listening experience. A great mix makes every element easy to hear and feel, removes clutter and harshness, creates a sense of depth and space, and delivers the emotional intent of the song.

Mixing vs. Mastering: What's the Difference?

These two terms are often confused by beginners, but they describe entirely different stages of music production.

Stage What It Involves Input Output
Mixing Balancing and processing individual tracks within a song Multitrack session (stems, audio files) Stereo mix (WAV or AIFF)
Mastering Optimising the finished stereo mix for distribution Stereo mix file Distribution-ready master (WAV, MP3, DDP)

Mastering is applied after mixing. The mastering engineer receives your completed stereo mix and processes it as a whole — applying subtle EQ, multiband compression, limiting, and loudness optimisation to prepare the track for streaming, CD, vinyl, or broadcast. A common mistake is attempting to master before the mix is finished. Mastering cannot fix a bad mix — it can only optimise a good one.

What Mix Engineers Do

A mix engineer's job is part technical, part creative, and entirely in service of the song. They receive a session — either the full DAW project or a set of exported stems — and work through every element methodically. Their core responsibilities include:

  • Gain staging — setting appropriate levels across all tracks so the signal chain remains clean and headroom is preserved throughout
  • Level balancing — setting the relative volume of every track so the most important elements (usually lead vocal, kick, and bass) sit at the front without drowning out supporting elements
  • EQ — cutting unwanted frequencies (mud, harshness, rumble) and boosting character frequencies to help each element occupy its own space in the frequency spectrum
  • Compression — controlling dynamics on individual tracks and buses so performances feel consistent without losing energy
  • Reverb and delay — adding depth, space, and cohesion so all tracks feel like they exist in the same acoustic environment
  • Panning — placing elements in the stereo field (left, centre, right) to create width and separation
  • Automation — programming level, pan, and effect changes over time so the mix breathes, builds, and responds to the arrangement dynamically
  • Bus and mix bus processing — applying compression and EQ to groups of tracks (drum bus, vocal bus, mix bus) to glue elements together

Understanding Mix Signal Flow

Every track passes through individual processing, combines on the mix bus, then outputs as a stereo file.

Core Mixing Tools

You don't need expensive gear to start mixing. Every major DAW includes the fundamental tools required. The essentials are:

  • EQ (Equaliser) — shapes the tonal balance of each track. See the complete mixing EQ guide for frequency-by-frequency breakdowns.
  • Compressor — controls dynamic range, adds punch and consistency. Critical on drums, vocals, and bass.
  • Reverb — adds space and depth. Learn how to use reverb in a mix without washing out your tracks.
  • Delay — creates rhythmic echoes that add dimension and movement.
  • Limiter — prevents the mix bus from clipping during mixdown.
  • Metering plugins — spectrum analysers, loudness meters (LUFS), and phase correlation meters guide objective decisions.

Beyond plugins, experienced engineers rely on reference tracks — commercially released songs in a similar style — to check their mix against, and mono compatibility checking to ensure the mix translates on phones, laptops, and club PA systems.

Pro tip: Before reaching for any plugin, build your rough mix using faders and panning only. A balanced rough mix with no processing reveals arrangement problems early and gives your plugins less work to do. Many professional engineers spend 30–60 minutes on a static fader balance before touching EQ or compression.

The Mixing Process, Step by Step

While every engineer develops their own workflow, a reliable mixing sequence looks like this:

  1. Organise and colour-code your session — group drums, bass, guitars, keys, vocals, and FX into logical buses
  2. Gain stage every track — aim for peaks around −18 dBFS on individual tracks before any plugin processing
  3. Build a static balance — set levels and panning with no processing until the song sounds roughly correct
  4. Process individual tracks — apply EQ, compression, and saturation per channel
  5. Add depth and space — use send effects for reverb and delay to keep your mix bus clean
  6. Bus processing — compress and EQ drum, bass, and vocal buses to glue each group
  7. Mix bus processing — apply light glue compression and EQ on the stereo output
  8. Automate — ride vocal levels, automate send amounts, build energy into drops and choruses using DAW automation
  9. Reference and revise — check against reference tracks, check in mono, check on different playback systems

Can You Mix Your Own Music?

Yes — and most producers do, at least at the demo or independent release stage. Modern DAWs include every tool needed: EQ, compression, reverb, delay, metering, and automation. The challenge is developing your ear to make objective decisions about your own work, which takes consistent practice.

Using reference tracks is the single most effective habit for self-mixing producers. Load a commercially mixed track you admire into your session and A/B your mix against it regularly. This exposes tonal imbalances, level issues, and width problems that are easy to miss when you've been listening to the same session for hours.

For a structured approach to developing these skills, the beginner's guide to mixing music covers workflow, ear training, and common mistakes to avoid. If you're specifically working on vocals, the dedicated vocal mixing guide goes deep on EQ curves, compression settings, and de-essing.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ What is mixing in music production?
Mixing is the process of blending all the individual recorded tracks in a song — drums, bass, guitars, vocals, synths — into a single cohesive stereo file. The mix engineer adjusts levels, panning, EQ, compression, reverb, and other effects to make every element sit together clearly and emotionally.
FAQ What is the difference between mixing and mastering?
Mixing is the process of balancing and processing individual tracks within a song. Mastering is the final step applied to the finished stereo mix — it optimises loudness, tonal balance, and prepares the track for distribution across streaming platforms, vinyl, and broadcast.
FAQ What does a mix engineer do?
A mix engineer receives the individual stems or tracks from a recording session and uses level balancing, EQ, compression, reverb, delay, and automation to blend them into a polished stereo mix that serves the song emotionally and sonically.
FAQ Can I mix my own music?
Yes. Most modern DAWs include all the tools needed to mix your own music — EQ, compression, reverb, and automation. Self-mixing takes time to learn, but with reference tracks and critical listening practice, you can achieve professional results.
FAQ What tools are used in mixing?
The core mixing tools are a DAW, equaliser (EQ), compressor, reverb, delay, limiter, and metering plugins. Mix engineers also use reference tracks, mono checking, and spectrum analysers to guide their decisions.
FAQ What is a stem in mixing?
A stem is a submix of a group of related tracks — for example, all drum tracks mixed down to a single stereo drum stem, or all vocal tracks combined into a vocal stem. Stems make it easier to send sessions between engineers and allow flexible reworking of a mix.
FAQ What is gain staging in mixing?
Gain staging is the practice of setting the input and output levels of each track and plugin in your signal chain to avoid clipping and distortion while maintaining headroom. Proper gain staging ensures your mix stays clean from the first plugin to the master bus.
FAQ What is automation in mixing?
Automation is the ability to program changes to any mix parameter — volume, panning, EQ, effects sends — over time, so they happen automatically during playback. Automation allows precise control over dynamics and emotion throughout a song.
