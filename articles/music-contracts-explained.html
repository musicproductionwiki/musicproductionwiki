Music Contracts Explained: What Every Producer Needs to Know

First published May 2026
MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
May 2026
Legal disclaimer: This article provides general educational information about music contracts. It is not legal advice. Always consult a qualified music attorney before signing any contract.
Quick Answer

Most music career problems involving money come from a contract not understood before signing, or no contract when there should have been one. The most important contracts for producers and artists: co-writing agreements (who owns what percentage of each song), producer agreements (royalty percentage and credit), recording agreements (what the label owns and what you get back), and sync licensing agreements (what the client can do with your music).

Co-Writing Agreements

The most common contractual problem: collaborators who agreed on splits verbally, never put them in writing, and cannot agree on what was said when money arrives. Every co-writing session that produces a completed song should produce a written agreement signed by all parties before the song is released. Key terms: ownership percentage of the composition per co-writer, artist credit, and what happens if one writer assigns publishing rights to a publisher (other writers should have approval rights over co-publishing partners). Even a short email with all parties' explicit confirmation is better than nothing.

Producer Agreements

When a producer creates a beat or production for an artist, the terms define who owns what for the life of the copyright. Without a written agreement, the default legal position is murky. Key terms: does the producer retain the beat or does the artist purchase full ownership? Does the producer share in composition copyright for melodic and harmonic contributions? Producer royalty: on major label releases, producers typically receive 3-5% of master recording royalties. Production credit: "Produced by [Name]" in credits is a contractual right -- do not assume it will be provided without specifying it explicitly. Exclusivity: can the producer sell or license the same beat to other artists, or is it exclusive to this release?

Key Contract Terms

Term: How long the contract lasts. Watch for terms renewable by the label but not by you. Territory: What geographic regions the contract covers. Reversion: When do your rights come back? Publishing deals should have reversion clauses returning your copyright if the publisher fails minimum activity thresholds. 360 deal: Label takes a percentage of all revenue streams -- not just recorded music but also touring, merchandise, endorsements, and publishing. Recoupment: Label advances are loans against future royalties. You receive nothing until the advance is fully recouped from your royalty earnings at your royalty rate (15-25%), not the label's rate (100%).

Before Signing

Never sign a music contract without having it reviewed by a qualified music attorney. Consultation costs $200-500 per hour. Red flags requiring immediate legal consultation: any contract assigning copyright permanently, 360 deal terms above 15%, contract terms longer than 3 years, reversion clauses absent from publishing agreements, and any offer pressured with an expiration deadline.
