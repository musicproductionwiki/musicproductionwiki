Music Contracts Explained: Every Deal Type Every Artist Needs to Know

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2025 10 min read
Legal Disclaimer

This article provides general educational information about music industry contracts. It is not legal advice. Always have a qualified entertainment attorney review any contract before signing.

Music industry contracts determine who owns what, who gets paid what, and who controls your music for how long. Signing without understanding is one of the most damaging mistakes an artist can make — and the terms are often not negotiable once signed. This guide explains every major contract type in plain language, including what the key terms mean and what to watch out for in each.

What we'll cover: Record deals (major and indie), publishing agreements, management contracts, booking agent agreements, sync licensing contracts, producer agreements, and the key clauses that appear across all of them.

Record Deals

A record deal is a contract between an artist and a record label where the label funds recording, distribution, and marketing in exchange for ownership (or partial ownership) of the master recordings and a share of the revenue generated by those recordings.

Traditional major label deal: The label owns the master recordings, funds recording costs as a recoupable advance (the artist pays this back from royalties before receiving any royalty payments), and pays the artist a royalty rate of 15–25% of net receipts. The label controls marketing, distribution, release timing, and often creative direction. The artist receives no royalty payments until the advance is fully recouped. Given typical advance sizes and royalty rates, recoupment can take years even for commercially successful albums.

360 deal: A modern major label structure where the label takes a percentage of all revenue streams — not just recording royalties but also touring, merchandise, sync licensing, and publishing — in exchange for larger advances and broader support. The percentage the label takes from non-recording revenue is typically 15–30%. Artists with strong existing brands in all these categories should be particularly cautious about 360 deals.

Independent label deal: Indie deals vary enormously but often involve better royalty rates (50% or higher), artist ownership or reversion of masters after a period, and less corporate creative control. The trade-off is smaller marketing budgets and less distribution infrastructure.

Key terms to watch: Recoupment structure (what costs are recoupable and at what rate), term length and options (how many albums the label can require, how long they control your recordings), creative control clauses, audit rights (your ability to verify the label's royalty accounting), and reversion clauses (when and how you can get your masters back).

Publishing Agreements

A publishing deal is a contract between a songwriter and a music publisher where the publisher administers and promotes the songwriter's compositions in exchange for a share of publishing revenue. Publishing revenue includes mechanical royalties, performance royalties, sync licensing fees, and print licensing.

Traditional publishing deal: The publisher typically takes 50% of the publisher's share of royalties (25% of total royalties, since the other 50% of the publisher's share belongs to the songwriter regardless). The publisher actively pitches songs for sync placements, manages licensing, and handles global collection. The songwriting catalog is assigned to the publisher for the contract term, which may extend for years after the contract ends.

Admin deal: The publisher handles administration (collection, licensing, registration) for a smaller percentage (10–25%) without owning the catalog. The songwriter retains ownership and more control. Better for established songwriters who want collection efficiency without giving up catalog ownership.

Co-publishing deal: The publisher and songwriter jointly own the copyrights, splitting the publisher's share (25% each of total revenue). The songwriter retains their writer's share (50%). Better terms than a full publishing deal but still involves giving up catalog ownership.

Key terms to watch: Retention period (how long after the contract ends the publisher retains rights), reversion clauses, minimum commitment requirements (how many songs you must deliver), advances and recoupment, and territory coverage.

Management Contracts

A management contract engages a personal manager to guide the artist's career in exchange for a commission on all or most of the artist's income. Managers handle booking coordination, label negotiations, team building, and career strategy. They are paid whether or not their specific work generated the income.

Standard commission: 15–20% of gross income in most markets, though rates vary. The commission typically applies to all music-related income: touring, recording royalties, publishing, merchandise, endorsements, and sync. Some managers exclude certain income streams (income from pre-existing deals, income from deals the manager didn't negotiate).

Key terms to watch: Sunset clause (how long the manager continues receiving commission on deals made during the contract period after the relationship ends — can be years), scope of commission (what income is included), post-term provisions, exclusivity, and territory.

The sunset clause is the most misunderstood and potentially costly management contract provision. If you sign a deal with a manager, they may continue collecting commission on that deal for 2–5 years after your management relationship ends — even if you fired them. Negotiate sunset provisions carefully.

Booking Agent Agreements

A booking agent secures live performance engagements for the artist in exchange for a commission, typically 10–15% of the performance fee. Unlike managers, booking agents focus specifically on live performance rather than overall career management.

Booking agent contracts are typically shorter and simpler than management contracts. Key terms: commission rate, territory, exclusivity (whether you can use multiple agents in different territories), and term length. Unlike management deals, booking agent commissions are generally limited to performances the agent actually books — there's less exposure to the long sunset clauses common in management contracts.

Sync Licensing Contracts

A sync license grants permission to use a piece of music in a specific audiovisual production — film, TV, advertisement, video game, or online content. Every sync deal licenses two things separately: the synchronisation right (from the music publisher or songwriter) and the master license (from the label or recording artist who owns the master).

Sync fees vary enormously based on the production type, the prominence of the music use, the term of the license, and the territory. A prominent use in a major studio film earns significantly more than a background use in an independent short film. Key terms: one-time fee vs. backend royalties, term (how long the production can use the music), territory (global or specific regions), exclusivity, and whether the sync fee is in addition to or instead of backend performance royalties.

Producer Agreements

A producer agreement between a recording artist and a music producer sets out the producer's fee and royalty for producing a track or album. Producers typically receive an upfront fee (ranging from hundreds to tens of thousands of dollars) plus a royalty of 2–5 points (percentage points of the recording royalty) on net receipts from the produced recordings.

Key terms: which royalty base the producer's points are calculated on (some labels pay producer royalties from a lesser base), whether producer royalties are paid from the first sale or only after the artist recoup (the industry standard is after artist recoups, though this is negotiable), and ownership of the underlying musical elements the producer contributes.

Key Clauses That Appear Across All Music Contracts

Territory: The geographic scope of the agreement. "Worldwide" is common for major deals; independent deals sometimes restrict to specific territories.

Term: How long the contract lasts. Watch for option periods — the contract may be for one album with the label holding options for additional albums, each extending the term.

Exclusivity: Whether you can do similar work with others during the contract period. Management and label deals are almost always exclusive; some licensing deals are non-exclusive.

Audit rights: Your ability to have an accountant examine the other party's books to verify royalty calculations. Essential in any deal involving royalty payments. Typically allowed once per year with advance notice.

Reversion: Conditions under which rights revert to you — if the label fails to release recordings within a specified period, if the contract is breached, or after a specified time period. Hard to negotiate from a position of weakness but worth attempting.

The most important advice for any music contract: have a qualified entertainment attorney review it before signing. The cost of attorney review ($300–1,000 depending on contract complexity) is trivial compared to the multi-year, potentially million-dollar implications of signing unfavorable terms.
