Music Sync Licensing: How to Get Your Music Placed in Film, TV, and Advertising

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated April 2026 13 min read Intermediate

Every piece of music you hear in a film, television show, commercial, or video game is there because someone — a music supervisor, a director, a brand creative director — listened to it, decided it served their project, and licensed it. That licensing process generates a fee that goes to the rights holders of the music — the artist who recorded it and the songwriter who wrote it. For independent artists with catalog that suits visual media, sync licensing represents one of the highest per-use revenue opportunities in the music industry. Understanding how to pursue it effectively is a career-level skill worth developing carefully.

What Sync Licensing Actually Is

A sync license — short for synchronization license — grants permission to use a piece of music synchronized with visual media. Every time music appears in a film, television show, commercial, video game, YouTube video, or any other audiovisual content, a sync license has been granted by the music's rights holders.

Sync licensing involves two separate rights that must both be cleared. The master license — from whoever owns the recording — covers the use of the specific recorded version. The sync license — from whoever owns the composition — covers the use of the underlying song. For independent artists who write and record their own music and own both their masters and their publishing, clearing both rights is a single negotiation with themselves. For more complex ownership situations — co-writers, label deals, sampled material — clearing both rights requires negotiating with multiple parties, which is why music supervisors strongly prefer music with clean, clear, uncomplicated ownership.

Who Music Supervisors Are and What They Do

Music supervisors are the professionals responsible for selecting, licensing, and clearing music for visual media. They work on film productions, television shows, advertising campaigns, video game development, and streaming platform original content. A music supervisor's job is to find music that serves the creative vision of the project — emotionally, tonally, culturally — and then negotiate the licenses required to use it within the project's budget.

Music supervisors listen to enormous quantities of music — hundreds of submissions per week for busy supervisors working on major projects. They develop strong aesthetic opinions and clear criteria for what they are looking for. Understanding how they think and what they need from the music they license is the most important context for positioning your catalog for sync placement.

What music supervisors consistently say they need: music that is emotionally evocative without being lyrically specific. Lyrics that describe a specific story or reference specific people, places, or events are harder to place than lyrics that express universal emotions. A song about missing someone is placeable in a hundred scenes. A song that names a specific person, place, or situation is useful in far fewer. Instrumental music is often the most flexible — it carries emotional content without the specificity of lyrics, making it usable across a wider range of scenes and contexts.

The Types of Sync Placements and What They Pay

Sync fees vary enormously based on the type of placement, the profile of the project, the territory of the license, and the duration of use. Understanding the range helps set realistic expectations and prioritize which opportunities to pursue.

Major film placements command the highest fees — typically $15,000 to $100,000 or more for both master and sync rights combined, depending on the prominence of the placement (background versus featured), the territory (worldwide versus specific markets), and the profile of the production. A song featured in the opening credits of a major studio film can command fees in the six figures. A background placement in an independent film might pay $500 to $2,000.

Television placements range widely based on network versus streaming, cable versus broadcast, and the prominence of the placement. A network television placement on a major drama — prominently featured in an emotional scene — might pay $5,000 to $30,000. A background placement in a lower-rated cable show might pay $500 to $2,000. Streaming platforms like Netflix and HBO pay mid-range fees — typically $3,000 to $15,000 for featured placements — but offer the advantage of global distribution without territory-specific licensing complications.

Advertising placements are the highest-value sync opportunity per placement. National advertising campaigns for major brands regularly pay $50,000 to $500,000 or more for the use of a track, with fees depending on the media buy (national television broadcast versus digital-only), the duration of the campaign, and the profile of the brand. Even regional advertising campaigns pay fees that dwarf streaming income for most independent artists.

Video games pay fees that vary based on the prominence of the music in the game — background ambient music pays less than featured tracks on the game's main menu or key story moments. Major AAA game placements can pay $5,000 to $30,000. Indie game placements might pay $500 to $3,000.

YouTube and digital content creators represent the most accessible entry point for independent artists. Sync licensing libraries — Musicbed, Artlist, Epidemic Sound, Musicshake — license music from independent artists non-exclusively and pay fees each time a creator licenses a track. Per-placement fees are lower than the categories above — typically $50 to $500 per license — but volume of placements through major libraries can generate meaningful income passively and builds a track record of licensed placements that supports higher-value opportunities.

Building a Sync-Ready Catalog

Not all music is equally sync-ready. Building a catalog that music supervisors can use requires understanding what characteristics make music placeable and intentionally developing those characteristics in your work.

Clear ownership is the foundation. Music supervisors cannot use music with complicated or unclear ownership. Sampled music that has not been cleared. Music with co-writers who cannot be located. Music owned partly by a label that has not provided sync clearance. Any of these situations can make music impossible to license regardless of its quality. Independent artists who own their masters and publishing cleanly — no samples, clear co-writer agreements, properly registered copyrights — have a significant advantage in the sync market over artists with complicated ownership structures.

Stems and alternatives add value. Many sync placements require edits of specific length, versions without certain instruments, or alternatives to the original track. Producers who deliver stems — separate audio files for each element of the track (drums, bass, melody, vocals) — alongside their finished track give music supervisors the flexibility to create the version the scene requires. Vocal and instrumental versions of every track double the placement opportunities immediately.

Emotional specificity matters more than technical complexity. Music that evokes a clear, specific emotion — joy, tension, melancholy, triumph — is more placeable than music that is technically impressive but emotionally ambiguous. Music supervisors are looking for sounds that serve specific emotional moments in their project. Music that creates a clear, immediate emotional response when heard gives them more options than music that requires careful listening to appreciate.

Genre versatility through catalog breadth. A producer with a diverse catalog — different tempos, different moods, different instrumentation — has more opportunities than a producer with a stylistically narrow catalog. Building catalog intentionally across emotional and sonic ranges, rather than staying within a single aesthetic, increases the number of placement opportunities available across the range of projects seeking music.

How to Submit Music to Music Supervisors

There are multiple paths to connecting with music supervisors, ranging from passive (placing music in sync libraries) to active (direct outreach and relationship building).

Sync licensing libraries are the most accessible entry point. Companies like Musicbed, Artlist, Epidemic Sound, Musicshake, and Pond5 license music from independent artists and make it searchable by music supervisors and content creators. The vetting process for acceptance varies — some libraries accept music from any artist, others curate their catalog carefully. The advantage: passive placement opportunities without requiring relationships or active outreach. The disadvantage: lower fees per placement and non-exclusive arrangements that may limit what you can do with the music in other contexts.

Music licensing agencies pitch music on behalf of artists to music supervisors, representing a catalog and actively seeking placement opportunities. In exchange for their services, agencies typically take 25-50% of sync fees. For artists with catalog that suits high-value commercial placements, the fees generated through an agency can significantly exceed what the artist would generate through passive library placements, even after the agency's commission.

Direct outreach to music supervisors is the most relationship-intensive approach and the one with the highest potential upside. Music supervisors who trust a specific artist's or producer's aesthetic judgment will return to them for multiple projects — a relationship that generates repeated sync income from a single connection. Building these relationships requires knowing which supervisors work on which projects, understanding their aesthetic preferences, and making contact through legitimate channels — industry events, mutual connections, or carefully crafted cold outreach — rather than spam submissions.

The Sync Catalog Audit

Go through your existing catalog and ask three questions about each track: Does it have clean ownership with no samples and clear co-writer agreements? Does it evoke a specific, clear emotion within the first fifteen seconds? Does it exist in both a vocal and instrumental version? Tracks that meet all three criteria are immediately sync-ready. Tracks that fail any of these criteria need work before they can be actively pitched. Most independent artists discover that their sync-ready catalog is significantly smaller than their total catalog — which is the most useful thing to know before pursuing sync opportunities.
