How to Make a Beat: The Complete Beginner's Guide

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated April 2026 13 min read Beginner

Every producer remembers their first beat. The excitement of hearing the kick and snare lock together for the first time. The moment a melody appears over a drum pattern and something clicks emotionally — this thing I built from nothing sounds like music. That experience is available to anyone willing to learn the fundamentals. This guide takes you from opening your DAW for the first time through the complete process of building a finished beat — one step at a time, with the understanding that comes from having made thousands of them.

Step One: Choose Your BPM and Lock In the Tempo

Every beat starts with a tempo decision — the BPM (beats per minute) that determines how fast or slow the music feels. Tempo is not just a technical setting. It is a creative decision that shapes the emotional character of everything built on top of it. A beat at 70 BPM feels heavy, slow, and contemplative. A beat at 140 BPM feels urgent, energetic, and driving. Choose your tempo based on the feeling you want the music to create, not based on what is convenient or what the default setting was when you opened your DAW.

Common BPM ranges by genre as a starting reference: trap and dark hip-hop typically runs 130-145 BPM (though trap's half-time feel means the perceived tempo is often 65-72 BPM). Classic boom-bap hip-hop runs 85-95 BPM. Drill runs 140-150 BPM. Lo-fi hip-hop runs 70-90 BPM. House music runs 120-130 BPM. Techno runs 130-150 BPM. These are ranges, not rules — some of the most interesting music is made at tempos that defy genre convention.

Set your BPM before you program a single note. Everything you build will be locked to this tempo, and changing it later shifts every element of the beat in ways that require correction. Make the decision once and commit to it.

Step Two: Build the Foundation — Kick and Snare

The kick drum and snare drum are the rhythmic foundation of almost every genre of popular music. The kick provides the low-end pulse — the heartbeat. The snare provides the accent — the crack that tells you where beat two and beat four are. Getting the relationship between kick and snare right is the first mixing challenge every beat maker faces and the one that determines whether a beat feels locked or loose.

The most common beginner mistake: programming the kick and snare in isolation and wondering why the beat doesn't groove. The kick and snare groove in relationship to each other. A kick on beat one means nothing without understanding what beat two (where the snare falls in most genres) does in response. Think of them as a conversation, not as two separate elements.

A simple starting point that works across most genres: kick on beats one and three (the first and third quarter notes of a four-beat bar), snare on beats two and four. This is the most common drum pattern in popular music because it creates a rhythmic tension and release that the human body responds to instinctively. From this foundation, add complexity — additional kicks on the offbeat, snare variations, ghost notes — rather than starting from complexity and trying to find the groove afterward.

Sound selection matters as much as pattern programming. A kick drum with too much low-end mud clashes with the bass. A snare with no crack gets buried in the mix. Spend time choosing sounds that work together before you spend time programming patterns. The best pattern programmed with wrong sounds sounds worse than a simple pattern with sounds that lock together perfectly.

Step Three: Add the Hi-Hats and Percussion

Hi-hats and percussion add rhythmic detail and energy above the kick-snare foundation. They fill the space between the main beats, create movement, and give the beat its rhythmic character. The difference between a beat that feels mechanical and one that feels alive is usually in the hi-hat programming — specifically in the velocity variation, timing variation, and pattern complexity that separates programmed drums from played ones.

Velocity variation is the single most important element of realistic-feeling hi-hat programming. In a real drum performance, no two hi-hat hits are exactly the same velocity — the drummer's hand moves slightly differently with each stroke. Programmed hi-hats at uniform velocity sound robotic because they lack this natural variation. In your DAW's piano roll, select all your hi-hat notes and randomize their velocities within a range — varying them by 15-30% creates a human feel without making the pattern inconsistent.

Timing variation — called humanization in most DAWs — applies slight timing offsets to individual notes, moving them slightly ahead of or behind the exact grid position. Combined with velocity variation, timing humanization transforms mechanically programmed drums into something that feels performed rather than sequenced. Apply it subtly — too much makes the drums feel sloppy rather than human.

For trap and modern hip-hop specifically: the rolling hi-hat pattern — rapid sixteenth-note or thirty-second note hi-hat sequences with velocity variation creating a sense of acceleration and rhythm — is a defining characteristic of the genre. Program these patterns with aggressive velocity variation (some hits very quiet, others very loud) and occasional timing offsets to create the rolling, breathing quality that distinguishes trap hi-hats from mechanical sequencing.

Step Four: The 808 Bass — The Heartbeat of Modern Beats

The 808 bass — derived from the Roland TR-808 drum machine's bass drum sound but used in modern production as a pitched, sustained bass element — is the defining low-frequency element of trap, hip-hop, and a wide range of electronic music. Understanding how to tune, program, and mix 808s is one of the most important skills in contemporary beat making.

808s must be tuned to the key of the beat. An out-of-tune 808 is the most common technical problem in amateur beats and the one that makes a beat sound immediately amateur. Load your 808 sample into a sampler plugin, play the note that corresponds to its fundamental pitch, and tune it to match the root note of your melody or chord progression. If you don't know the key of your beat yet, establish the key before programming 808s rather than after.

808 slides — the pitch glide between successive 808 notes — are one of the most expressive elements of trap production. In your piano roll, the slide is typically programmed by having two consecutive 808 notes overlap slightly, triggering the synth's portamento or glide function. The amount of pitch slide, the speed of the transition, and the notes being connected all determine the emotional character of the slide. Study the 808 work of producers you admire and identify how they use slides — the answer will teach you more about melodic bass production than any tutorial.

Step Five: Add Melody — The Emotional Core

The melody is what makes a beat memorable. Drums establish rhythm. Bass establishes low-end. Melody establishes emotion — the feeling that makes a listener want to hear a track again. Most beat makers underinvest in melody development, treating it as something to layer on top of drums rather than as the primary emotional element that everything else should serve.

Choose your key before you play a single melody note. Most DAWs allow you to set a scale in the piano roll — showing you which notes are in key and which are not — which removes the technical barrier of music theory and lets you focus on the musical decision of which notes feel right. Work in a scale that fits the emotional character you want: minor scales (natural minor, harmonic minor, phrygian) feel dark, melancholic, or tense. Major scales feel bright, hopeful, or joyful. Modal scales (dorian, lydian, mixolydian) feel somewhere between — more complex and ambiguous emotionally.

The most common beginner mistake with melody: playing too many notes. A three-note melody repeated with rhythmic variation over four bars is more memorable and more effective than a technically complex melody that tries to say too many things. The producers whose beats get placed on major records are masters of melodic economy — they say what needs to be said and stop. Study the top melodic elements in beats you admire and count the notes. You will almost always find fewer than you expected.

Step Six: Arrangement — Taking It from Loop to Song

The most common failure point for beginner beat makers is not the beat itself — it is the inability to turn the beat into a finished arrangement. A one-bar loop that sounds incredible as a loop often sounds monotonous over two minutes. Professional beat makers do not just make loops — they arrange music.

The basic arrangement structure for a beat: intro (establish the mood with reduced elements), verse (main beat with space for a vocal or instrument), pre-chorus (build tension by adding elements), chorus (full beat, all elements, maximum energy), bridge or breakdown (strip back to create contrast), outro (fade or resolve). This structure is not mandatory — many great beats depart from it significantly — but understanding it gives you a framework to work from rather than staring at an empty arrangement window.

The most effective arrangement technique for beat makers: mute and unmute elements to create the sense of build and release rather than adding entirely new sounds. A beat that has the full drum kit, bass, melody, and atmosphere from bar one cannot build anywhere — everything is already there. Start sparse. Add elements. Remove elements. The contrast between a stripped verse and a full chorus creates emotional impact that a consistently full arrangement cannot replicate.

The Finish Rate Rule

The single most important habit a developing beat maker can build is finishing beats. Not perfecting them — finishing them. A beat that is 80% of what you envisioned and exported as a complete file teaches you more than a beat that is 95% perfect and has been sitting open in your DAW for three weeks waiting for that final 5%. The producers who develop fastest are the ones who finish the most beats, learn from each one, and move to the next. Set a rule: every beat you start, you finish within 48 hours. The quality of your hundredth finished beat will shock you compared to your first.
