How to Make a Beat: Complete Beginner's Guide

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2025 20 min read

Making a beat is the entry point for most music producers. The process — building a rhythm section, adding a bassline, layering melodic elements, and shaping an arrangement — is the same whether you're making hip-hop, trap, house, or pop. This guide walks through the complete process from the first kick drum to a finished, export-ready beat, with practical settings and real workflow advice at every step.

What we'll cover: Choosing your tempo and key, programming drums step by step, building a bassline, writing melodies and chords, arranging your beat into a full structure, mixing fundamentals, and exporting for distribution or sending to artists.

Setup: Tempo, Key, and Time Signature

Before any sound is made, three decisions shape everything that follows: tempo, key, and time signature.

Tempo (BPM): Tempo sets the energy and genre context of the beat immediately. Common tempo ranges by genre: hip-hop 70–100 BPM, trap 130–160 BPM (often with a half-time feel, so the kick and snare feel like 65–80 BPM), lo-fi hip-hop 70–90 BPM, house 120–130 BPM, drum and bass 160–180 BPM, pop 100–130 BPM, R&B 60–90 BPM. If you're not sure, 90 BPM is a versatile starting point for hip-hop and R&B. 140 BPM works for most trap.

Key: The key determines the tonal centre of the beat — which notes sound "home" and which create tension. Minor keys (natural minor, harmonic minor) produce the dark, emotional quality associated with hip-hop, trap, and much electronic music. Major keys produce brighter, more uplifting sounds. Common minor keys for beats: A minor, C minor, D minor, G minor (all comfortable ranges for most synthesizers and sample packs). Set the key in your DAW before programming any melodic content — most DAWs display the key's notes in the piano roll to guide programming.

Time signature: Standard 4/4 time (4 beats per bar, quarter note gets one beat) is the default for almost all beat-making genres. Every DAW defaults to 4/4. Change it only if you're intentionally working in 3/4 (waltz time, some jazz), 5/4 (progressive), or other signatures.

Programming Drums: The Foundation

Drums define the genre, tempo feel, and groove of the beat. Everything else is built on top of the drum pattern.

Load your drum sounds: In your DAW, create a drum track (MIDI track with a drum plugin) and load drum sounds. Options: your DAW's built-in drum instrument (Logic's Drum Machine Designer, Ableton's Drum Rack, FL Studio's FPC), a third-party drum plugin (Battery, BeatMaker 3), or individual samples triggered from a sampler. Most producers use samples — drum sounds from sample packs or their own recorded sounds. Load kick, snare, closed hi-hat, open hi-hat, clap, and crash as starting sounds.

Create the basic drum pattern: Open the step sequencer or piano roll for your drum instrument. Program a basic 4-bar drum pattern:

For hip-hop/trap: Kick on beat 1 and beat 3 (steps 1 and 9 in a 16-step grid). Snare on beat 2 and beat 4 (steps 5 and 13). Closed hi-hat on every 8th note (steps 1, 3, 5, 7, 9, 11, 13, 15). This is the foundation — every variation builds from here.

For house/electronic: Kick on every beat (steps 1, 5, 9, 13) — the "four on the floor" kick. Clap on beats 2 and 4. Hi-hat on every 16th note or in a distinctive pattern. Open hi-hat on the "and" of beat 2 and beat 4.

Add variation and humanisation: A perfectly quantised, equal-velocity pattern sounds mechanical. Add variation in two ways: velocity variation (make some hi-hat hits quieter — 60–70% velocity for ghost notes, 90–100% for accented hits) and rhythmic variation (add a snare on the "and" of beat 4 in the last bar, add an extra kick on beat 3.5). In most DAWs you can also add slight timing randomisation — a 5–15 ms random timing variation applied to each hit creates a human feel without audible sloppiness.

Layer your sounds: Professional drum sounds are rarely single samples. Layer a punchy attack sample over a deeper sub-kick for kick drums with both click and weight. Layer a sharp clap over the snare for additional snap. Layer different hi-hat samples at different pitches for a more complex texture. Keep layers phase-coherent — trigger them simultaneously and ensure they don't cancel each other.

Process the drums: Apply a high-pass filter to the overhead/cymbal channel (cut below 200 Hz to remove low-frequency buildup from cymbals). Apply compression to the kick with a fast attack (2–5 ms), medium release (50–100 ms), and ratio 4:1 for punch and control. Apply a transient shaper to the snare (increase attack, decrease sustain) to sharpen the snap. Apply a drum bus compressor at a low ratio (2:1) with moderate gain reduction (3–4 dB) to glue the kit together.

Building the Bassline

The bassline provides harmonic foundation and low-end energy. It works with the kick drum to define the beat's rhythmic feel and tonal centre.

Bass sound selection: Match the bass sound character to the genre. Trap uses 808 bass — a long, pitched bass drum sample that slides between notes. Hip-hop often uses sampled bass (from vinyl records, bass guitars, or keyboard basses). Electronic music uses synthesized bass (subtractive synth with low filter cutoff, or FM bass). Load the appropriate type for your genre.

Programming the bassline: The bassline typically follows the chord progression of the beat — it plays the root note of each chord, changing when the chord changes. Start with root notes only before adding passing notes or rhythmic variation. A simple bassline pattern: one sustained note per bar on beat 1, changing to match the chord on each new bar. This alone establishes harmonic direction. Add rhythmic movement by shortening some notes and adding fills at bar endings once the root movement is established.

808 bass programming (trap): 808 bass is programmed as long notes in the piano roll. The key is tuning — the 808 must be pitched to the key of the beat. Identify the root note of your sample (most 808 sample packs are labelled with the root note) and program notes in the piano roll at the correct pitch. Use pitch slides (portamento) between notes for the characteristic trap bass slide. Sustain 808 notes long enough to fill the space between hits — typically 1–2 beats.

Bass mixing: High-pass the bass above 40 Hz to remove sub-bass rumble. Apply compression to control dynamics (4:1 ratio, medium attack preserving transient, auto release). Add gentle saturation to create harmonic content above the fundamental — this makes the bass audible on small speakers that can't reproduce sub-bass. Sidechain compress the bass to the kick drum so the bass momentarily ducks each time the kick hits, keeping the low end clean and preventing the kick and bass from fighting for the same space.

Writing Melodies and Chords

Melody and harmony distinguish memorable beats from generic ones. Even simple melodic elements — a 4-note lead, a basic chord progression — create the emotional identity that makes listeners connect with a beat.

Chord progressions: Start with a simple 4-chord progression that loops every 4 bars. In A minor, a classic progression: Am – F – C – G (i – VI – III – VII). In C minor: Cm – Ab – Eb – Bb. These progressions appear in thousands of successful records because they work emotionally and melodically. Program each chord in the piano roll with 3 notes (root, third, fifth) and let them sustain for one bar each. This gives you a harmonic framework to write melodies over.

Lead melody: Write a melody that fits the chord progression. Start on the root note of the first chord, move by steps or small leaps (avoid large jumps initially), and end phrases on chord tones (notes that are part of the current chord). A 4-bar melody that repeats works well as the basis of a beat. Keep it simple — the most effective beats often have melodies that are minimal enough to leave space for a rapper or singer. Use the pentatonic scale (in A minor: A, C, D, E, G) for melodies that naturally avoid clashing with the chords.

Counter-melody and texture: Add a second melodic element as a counter-melody — a complementary line that plays in the spaces of the main melody, creating a conversation between the two. Add pads (sustained chords with slow attack) to fill the harmonic space with texture without adding rhythmic activity. Layer risers (upward sweeps) and downlifters (downward sweeps) at section transitions for dramatic effect.

Sample flips: Many beat-makers build melodies entirely from samples — taking a snippet of an existing recording, chopping it, and rearranging the pieces into a new melody. If working with samples, ensure you're using royalty-free or properly licensed material. Sample packs designed for this purpose are widely available and eliminate copyright concerns. The technique of chopping, pitching, and rearranging samples produces a distinctive texture that synthesized sounds can't replicate.

Arranging Your Beat

Arrangement transforms a loop into a full beat with beginning, middle, and end — the structure that makes a beat suitable for a full song rather than just a looped demo.

Standard beat structure: Intro (4–8 bars, stripped back — drums only or drums + bass), Verse (16–32 bars, full arrangement), Pre-chorus or bridge (8 bars, adding or removing elements), Chorus (16–32 bars, fullest arrangement with most energy), Outro (4–8 bars, elements dropping out). This structure is a template — vary it based on the specific beat's needs.

Building tension and release: Tension is created by removing elements (a bar with only hi-hats before the full kit returns), adding dissonance (a sus4 chord before the resolution), or by introducing a riser. Release is the return of the full arrangement after tension. The most impactful moment in any beat is the drop — the return of the full arrangement after a stripped-back section. Engineer your arrangement to maximise this contrast.

8-bar block principle: Most beat arrangement works in 8-bar blocks. A verse is typically 16 bars (two 8-bar blocks). A chorus is 16 bars. Changes happen at 8-bar intervals. This structure is immediately legible to rappers and singers who will write to the beat — they can feel where phrases should start and end. Work in 8-bar blocks unless you have a specific reason to break the convention.

Automation: Volume automation between sections (a slight level increase in the chorus creates energy without adding new elements), filter automation on synth pads (opening the filter cutoff in the chorus adds brightness), reverb send automation (more reverb on transitions, less in the verses for intimacy) — these subtle automations make the arrangement feel produced rather than static.

Mixing the Beat

A well-arranged beat with poor mixing sounds amateur. A few focused mixing decisions elevate the sound significantly without requiring advanced mixing expertise.

Gain staging: Before adding any processing, set all your channel fader levels so the mix bus meter peaks at approximately -6 dBFS. If the mix bus is hitting 0 dBFS or clipping, turn down the individual channels rather than the master fader. Proper gain staging gives your master bus compressor and limiter clean input to work with.

High-pass filter everything: Apply a high-pass filter to every track that doesn't need sub-bass content. Pads, leads, hi-hats, and melodic elements all accumulate low-frequency content that muddies the mix without contributing useful information. High-pass at 80–150 Hz on most melodic tracks, 200–400 Hz on hi-hats and cymbals. This clears the low end for the kick and bass to occupy cleanly.

Frequency separation: Ensure the kick drum and bass don't occupy the exact same frequency range at the same time. The kick provides the transient punch (centered around 60–80 Hz and 4–5 kHz for click). The bass provides the sustained tonal content (fundamental below 200 Hz). Use sidechain compression so the bass ducks when the kick hits, or separate their frequency content using EQ (boost the kick's sub at 60 Hz, cut the bass at 60 Hz and vice versa for the upper harmonics).

Panning: Centre the kick, bass, snare, and lead vocal (if present). Pan hi-hats slightly left or right (not hard pan). Pan melodic elements to create width — chords left and right, counter-melody opposite the lead. Double any melodic elements and pan one left and one right for width.

Loudness target: Apply a limiter on the master bus with a ceiling of -0.3 to -1 dBTP. For beats intended for rap vocals, target -9 to -11 LUFS integrated — louder than streaming targets because the beat will be mixed with vocals in the final mix session. For finished beats ready for streaming, target -14 LUFS to match Spotify's normalisation target.

Exporting and Sending Your Beat

Export format depends on the destination. For sending to artists: WAV at 24-bit, 44.1 kHz, stereo. This is the universal professional format — lossless, compatible with every DAW, and standard for professional collaboration. For streaming distribution: WAV at 24-bit/44.1 kHz is what distributors want. They encode to MP3 or AAC themselves.

When sending beats to artists for consideration (non-exclusive leasing), include a watermark — spoken tag over the music (most producers say their name every 16 bars). Remove the watermark when the beat is licensed. Export the tagged version for streaming demos and the clean version for the license purchase.

Export stems — individual tracks or groups (drum stem, bass stem, melody stem) — when artists request them for mixing or remixing. Most professional beat-selling arrangements include stem delivery as part of the exclusive licensing fee.
