Behringer Truth B2031A Review

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 · 16 min read · Verified May 7, 2026
Quick Answer — Updated May 2026

The Behringer Truth B2031A is an 8.75-inch two-way active near-field monitor that delivers surprisingly capable performance for its price bracket, making it a legitimate option for budget-conscious home studio producers. Its bi-amplified design, relatively flat frequency response, and EQ adjustment controls give it an edge over purely entry-level alternatives, though its low-end extension and stereo imaging fall short of mid-tier competitors like the Yamaha HS8 or Adam Audio T8V.

Updated May 2026 — The Behringer Truth B2031A has been a staple of the budget studio monitor conversation for over two decades. Originally introduced as Behringer's answer to the Mackie HR824 — a monitor that cost three to four times more — the B2031A aimed to democratize near-field reference listening for home producers, project studios, and entry-level commercial facilities. In 2026, the B2031A still occupies a unique position: it is one of the few budget monitors offering a genuine bi-amplified, two-way design with onboard EQ shelving controls at its price point.

This review is written from the perspective of working producers and engineers who need actionable intelligence, not vague praise or dismissal. We'll cover the B2031A's actual frequency response behavior, its strengths and weaknesses across different production contexts, how it compares to direct competitors, and exactly who should — and who should not — be spending money on it.

Technical Specifications and Build Design

The Behringer Truth B2031A uses an 8.75-inch woven Kevlar woofer paired with a 1-inch aluminum dome tweeter in a front-ported, magnetically shielded enclosure. The bi-amplified system delivers 75W to the woofer and 35W to the tweeter for a combined 110W RMS output — a substantial figure for the price tier.

Frequency response is rated at 52Hz–20kHz (±3dB), which is a realistic spec for a monitor of this cabinet size. The crossover point sits at 2.2kHz, which is well-chosen for keeping the demanding upper-midrange out of the woofer's territory while giving the tweeter enough bandwidth to handle detail and transient information. The maximum SPL is rated at 113dB, meaning you can push the monitors harder than most bedroom producers will ever need.

Build quality is functional rather than premium. The cabinet uses medium-density fiberboard (MDF) with a black vinyl wrap finish. The front baffle is plastic — a cost-cutting measure that can introduce minor resonances at high SPL compared to the die-cast aluminum baffles found on monitors like the Genelec 8020. That said, the cabinet feels solid in practice, and the drivers are well-secured. The rear panel layout is logical: volume knob, EQ trim pots, and the three input options (XLR, TRS, and RCA) are clearly organized, making setup quick in any studio scenario.

The onboard EQ section is worth highlighting. A ±4dB shelving cut or boost at 90Hz allows you to compensate for bass buildup from room placement — useful when the B2031A is sitting on a desk near a wall. The matching ±4dB shelf at 10kHz lets you tame harshness or add a touch of air depending on your room's high-frequency absorption. These are not surgical controls, but in practice they make a meaningful difference to the usable accuracy of the monitor in untreated or semi-treated spaces.

Sound Character and Frequency Response in Practice

The B2031A has a characteristically forward midrange with a slightly bright tendency in the 3–6kHz region. This forwardness can be an asset for vocal clarity and mix translation checking, but it requires calibration of expectations — mixes that sound slightly dull on the B2031A often translate more warmly on consumer playback systems, which is a useful mixing reference dynamic.

The low end is present and reasonably controlled down to around 55–60Hz in most rooms. Below that, the front port takes over, and you'll notice a gentle rolloff rather than a hard cliff. Bass-heavy genres like trap, drill, and house music will benefit from supplementing the B2031A with a subwoofer or cross-referencing with headphones for the 40Hz and below range. If you regularly produce music with significant sub-bass content, be aware that the B2031A will not give you reliable information about what is happening below 50Hz — this is a real limitation.

Stereo imaging is adequate but not exceptional. The aluminum dome tweeter provides reasonable horizontal dispersion, giving a workable sweet spot of approximately 30–35 degrees. However, imaging precision in the upper midrange — particularly the 4–8kHz range where width, depth cues, and reverb tails live — is noticeably less defined compared to monitors using waveguide-controlled tweeters like the Adam Audio T-series with its Air Motion Transformer (AMT) ribbon. For producers doing detailed spatial mixing, this is a genuine consideration.

The Kevlar woofer handles transients cleanly. Kick drum punch, plucked bass notes, and percussive synth stabs all come through with reasonable definition. Where the B2031A shows strain is on dense, complex material — a fully-loaded mix with heavy reverb, dense low-end, and layered midrange content can feel slightly cluttered compared to more resolving monitors. This is partly an amplifier headroom issue and partly a cabinet resonance behavior at high SPLs.

Producer Tip: Set your B2031A's LF shelf to −2dB if the monitors are placed within 18 inches of a rear wall, and leave the HF shelf flat unless your room has substantial high-frequency absorption (thick foam panels or heavy curtains). This starting point will get you closer to a flat response before you even open a room correction tool. Running a free tool like REW (Room EQ Wizard) with a calibrated measurement microphone — even a budget Dayton Audio EMM-6 — will reveal your specific room's interaction with the monitor and let you make informed corrections.

Mixing Performance Across Production Genres

Understanding how a specific monitor performs across different production contexts is essential for evaluating its real-world usefulness. The B2031A's sound character creates predictable strengths and limitations across genres.

Hip-hop and Trap: The B2031A handles the upper-midrange clarity of hi-hats, snares, and vocal presence well. The low-end limitation is the key issue here — 808 sub-bass content below 50Hz will be underrepresented, so always cross-reference on headphones. If you are learning how to craft trap 808s, the B2031A is acceptable for shaping the upper harmonics and punch of the 808, but you will need headphone verification for the true sub-bass weight.

Electronic Music and House: The forward midrange helps with synth clarity and filter sweep definition, but sub-bass again requires supplementation. For mixing electronic music, the monitor's 52Hz floor means you have reasonable reference down to around 55Hz in practice — enough to make informed decisions about kick drum body and bass line fundamentals, but not sub-bass content.

Rock and Live Instruments: This is arguably the B2031A's strongest genre. Guitar presence, drum overheads, and vocal midrange all translate naturally on these monitors. If you regularly record and mix rock or acoustic material, the B2031A gives you a reliable window into the 200Hz–8kHz range where most of the critical information in those mixes lives. Check out our guide on mixing a rock band for context on where near-field monitor accuracy matters most.

Podcasting and Voice-Over: The bright midrange character makes the B2031A quite useful as a monitoring tool during recording sessions. Voice presence, sibilance, and room noise are all clearly audible, which is helpful for real-time recording decisions. See our podcast recording guide for additional monitoring recommendations in voice contexts.

Ambient and Cinematic: The limited sub-bass and average stereo imaging make the B2031A less suitable for cinematic and ambient production, where the deep end and spatial precision are primary tools. For these genres, upgrading to a monitor with better low-end extension and imaging — or using the B2031A alongside reference headphones — is the practical solution.

Competitor Comparison: How Does the B2031A Stack Up?

Positioning the B2031A correctly in the market requires an honest comparison against its direct competitors. In 2026, the most relevant alternatives in the same or adjacent price range include the Yamaha HS5, the Presonus Eris E8 XT, the KRK Rokit 8 G4, and the Adam Audio T7V.

vs. Yamaha HS5: The HS5 is a 5-inch monitor with a leaner low end but arguably better imaging and a more neutral midrange than the B2031A. The HS5 is the more accurate reference tool for producers who mix in treated rooms, while the B2031A's larger woofer gives it more usable low-frequency information for untreated rooms where the HS5 can sound thin. The HS5 is a better mixing reference; the B2031A is a better monitoring tool when acoustic treatment is minimal.

vs. Presonus Eris E8 XT: The Eris E8 XT is a close competitor to the B2031A. The E8 XT has a wider sweet spot due to its waveguide-controlled tweeter, and its EQ section is slightly more flexible with mid-frequency control in addition to high and low shelving. For the small price premium the E8 XT typically commands, it offers meaningfully better stereo imaging. If budget allows even a modest stretch, the E8 XT is worth considering over the B2031A.

vs. KRK Rokit 8 G4: The KRK Rokit 8 G4 has a famously bass-heavy character with a built-in 25-band DSP EQ. The Rokit's onboard correction is impressive as a feature, but its default voicing flatters low-end content in a way that can encourage over-lean mixes. The B2031A is actually a more neutral starting point than the Rokit 8 G4, though the Rokit with its DSP fully engaged and properly calibrated can be the better tool for experienced engineers who know what they are adjusting.

vs. Adam Audio T7V: The Adam Audio T7V is a step above the B2031A in almost every technical category — tighter low end, dramatically better stereo imaging from the AMT ribbon tweeter, and a more controlled midrange. The T7V typically costs around 30–40% more per unit, which is a significant outlay for a stereo pair. If your budget can reach the T7V, it is a substantially better mixing tool. If it cannot, the B2031A is a credible alternative.

Setup, Placement, and Room Interaction

Placement discipline matters more with budget monitors than with high-end ones, because budget monitors are less likely to have sophisticated acoustic correction systems to compensate for poor positioning. The B2031A's front port means that you can place it closer to rear walls than rear-ported monitors without creating extreme bass buildup — but wall proximity still affects the low-frequency balance, which is why the onboard LF shelving EQ exists.

Ideal placement follows the equilateral triangle rule: the two monitors and the listening position form an equilateral triangle, with each monitor toed inward approximately 15–30 degrees toward the listening position. The tweeter should be at ear height when seated. Monitor isolation pads or stands are strongly recommended — placing the B2031A directly on a desk couples cabinet vibrations to the desk surface, creating resonance artifacts in the 80–150Hz range that will affect your low-mid perception. A pair of foam isolation pads (IsoAcoustics Iso-Pad or similar budget equivalents) costs relatively little and makes a measurable difference.

Because the B2031A does not include any onboard digital room correction (no Sonarworks integration, no built-in measurement microphone protocol), pairing it with software room correction tools is advisable for serious mixing. Sonarworks SoundID Reference provides calibration profiles that can substantially flatten the in-room response, and the investment in that software is often more impactful for mixing quality than upgrading to a slightly better monitor in the same budget tier.

The three input options — XLR, TRS balanced, and RCA unbalanced — make the B2031A flexible in terms of what audio interfaces or mixers it can connect to. Always use the balanced XLR or TRS connections when available; the unbalanced RCA input is more susceptible to interference and hum, particularly in setups with multiple pieces of powered equipment. Verdict: Who Should Buy the Behringer Truth B2031A?

The Behringer Truth B2031A typically retails for approximately $299 per unit ($598 for a stereo pair), though street pricing has varied over the monitor's production life and discounts are common. At this price, the value proposition depends heavily on what you are comparing it to and what your production workflow demands.

The B2031A is a legitimate option for producers in the following situations: you are setting up a first home studio on a constrained budget, you are primarily working in rock, acoustic, or voice-based genres where sub-bass accuracy is less critical, or you need a secondary reference monitor pair for a B-room or secondary mixing position. Its bi-amplified design and flexible EQ controls genuinely set it apart from the lowest-tier passive or single-amplifier competitors.

The B2031A is not the right choice if you produce bass-heavy electronic music professionally and need reliable sub-bass information, if you are doing critical stereo imaging work for spatial or immersive formats, or if your budget can reach the Adam Audio T7V or Yamaha HS8 territory. For producers who find themselves in those situations, the marginal cost increase to reach a genuinely better monitor tier is worth the investment.

One often-overlooked use case for the B2031A is as a complementary second pair of reference monitors in a studio that already has a primary high-end pair. At its price point, having the B2031A as a secondary consumer-adjacent reference — something that reveals how a mix will behave on a mid-quality playback system — has genuine practical value. Many professional engineers use a rough consumer reference monitor alongside their main pair precisely to check translation.

For producers building out their first complete studio environment, understanding how monitors fit into the broader signal chain is essential. Our home recording studio setup guide covers the full equipment chain from interface to monitors to acoustic treatment in a structured way that will help you make decisions about where to invest relative to your specific goals.

When you move into actual mixing work, remember that no monitor — regardless of price — replaces the habit of checking mixes on multiple playback systems. The B2031A as part of a multi-reference approach (monitor pair, headphones, phone speaker, car stereo) is far more useful than the B2031A as your sole reference tool. Understanding how to make music that translates across systems is a skill that complements good monitoring equipment at every budget level.

In summary: the Behringer Truth B2031A is a competent, honest monitor for its price range. Its limitations are real but predictable. Its strengths — power output, EQ flexibility, and bi-amplified design — are genuine. Buy it knowing what it is, set it up correctly, and it will serve you well within its documented capabilities.

Practical Exercises

Beginner Exercise

Reference Monitoring Orientation

Set up your B2031A pair in the equilateral triangle position at ear height, with the LF shelf trimmed to −2dB if placed within 18 inches of a rear wall. Play five commercially released tracks from your target genre and listen for one hour without touching any controls — the goal is to internalize how known, well-mixed material sounds on this specific monitor so you have a mental reference baseline before mixing your own work.

Intermediate Exercise

Cross-Reference Translation Check

Take a mix you have been working on and listen to it consecutively on your B2031A pair, a pair of studio headphones, and a consumer Bluetooth speaker. Write down specific frequency observations — what sounds different on each system, particularly in the sub-bass and stereo width — and use those notes to identify where your B2031A is giving you reliable information versus where it requires supplementation. Repeat this process with three different mixes until the translation patterns become predictable.

Advanced Exercise

Room Correction and Calibration Workflow

Download REW (Room EQ Wizard) and use a calibrated measurement microphone to take a sweep measurement of your B2031A's in-room frequency response at your mixing position. Identify the three most significant peaks and dips below 300Hz, then use the B2031A's onboard LF shelving EQ in combination with a software correction tool (Sonarworks SoundID Reference or equivalent) to achieve a response within ±3dB from 60Hz to 10kHz at the listening position. Document the settings and re-measure to verify the correction.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ What is the Behringer Truth B2031A best used for?
The B2031A is best suited for mixing rock, acoustic, vocal, and pop content where sub-bass accuracy is less critical, as well as for use as a secondary reference monitor pair alongside a primary high-end system. Its reliable midrange performance and flexible EQ make it a strong choice for untreated or semi-treated home studio environments.
FAQ Does the Behringer B2031A have enough bass for hip-hop and trap production?
The B2031A's rated low-frequency extension of 52Hz is borderline for hip-hop and trap, where critical 808 sub-bass content often lives between 30–50Hz. Producers in these genres should cross-reference with studio headphones for sub-bass decisions, or add a dedicated subwoofer to the monitoring chain.
FAQ How does the B2031A compare to the Adam Audio T7V?
The Adam Audio T7V offers significantly better stereo imaging thanks to its Air Motion Transformer ribbon tweeter, a tighter and more extended low end, and a more controlled midrange. The T7V costs approximately 30–40% more per unit, but delivers a genuinely superior mixing experience for producers who can afford the upgrade.
FAQ Should I use the XLR or TRS input on the B2031A?
Either the XLR or TRS balanced inputs are preferable — both carry a balanced signal that rejects common-mode noise and interference. Avoid the RCA unbalanced input unless you have no alternative, as it is more susceptible to ground hum and electromagnetic interference from other studio equipment.
FAQ What EQ settings should I start with on the B2031A?
A good starting point is to set the LF shelving EQ to −2dB if the monitors are within 18 inches of a rear wall, and leave the HF shelving EQ flat. From there, use a measurement tool like REW with a calibrated microphone to identify your room's specific acoustic behavior and make further adjustments based on actual data.
FAQ Is the Behringer B2031A good for mixing in an untreated room?
The B2031A's onboard LF and HF shelving EQ controls give it better adaptability to untreated rooms than monitors with no correction options, and its front-ported design reduces rear-wall bass buildup. However, no monitor performs accurately in a severely untreated room — minimal acoustic treatment (bass traps in corners, absorption at first reflection points) will always improve the mixing environment regardless of monitor quality.
FAQ Can the B2031A be used as a single mono monitor?
Technically yes, but a single mono monitor is not recommended for mixing because you lose stereo imaging information entirely. The B2031A is sold as a single unit, so some producers purchase one initially, but for any serious mixing work you need a matched stereo pair.
FAQ How does the Behringer B2031A compare to the KRK Rokit 8 G4?
The KRK Rokit 8 G4 has a more bass-heavy character and includes a 25-band DSP EQ for correction, but its default voicing can encourage over-lean low-end decisions in mixes. The B2031A starts from a more neutral point and is simpler to dial in for accurate reference work, making it arguably the more honest tool for producers who are still calibrating their mixing ears.
