Compression Ratio Explained

What compression ratio means, how it interacts with threshold and other parameters, and exactly which ratios to use on every instrument in your mix.

By MusicProductionWiki • Updated May 2026 • ~2,800 words
Quick Answer: Compression ratio controls how much the compressor reduces gain above the threshold. A 4:1 ratio means for every 4 dB above the threshold, the output only increases by 1 dB. Low ratios (2:1–4:1) are transparent and musical. High ratios (8:1–20:1) are aggressive and audible. Infinity:1 is a limiter. Ratio only matters in combination with threshold — always set both together.

What Is Compression Ratio?

Compression ratio is one of the four core parameters of a compressor, alongside threshold, attack, and release. It controls how aggressively the compressor reduces the level of a signal once it crosses the threshold.

The ratio is expressed as X:1, where X is the number of dB the input must rise above the threshold for the output to rise by 1 dB.

  • 2:1 ratio: if the signal rises 8 dB above the threshold, the output rises 4 dB. Gentle, barely perceptible.
  • 4:1 ratio: if the signal rises 8 dB above the threshold, the output rises 2 dB. Musical and controlled.
  • 8:1 ratio: if the signal rises 8 dB above the threshold, the output rises 1 dB. Aggressive, often audible.
  • Infinity:1 ratio: no matter how far the signal exceeds the threshold, the output stays clamped at the threshold. This is a limiter.

Crucially: ratio only applies above the threshold. Signal below the threshold passes through unchanged regardless of the ratio setting. This is why ratio and threshold must always be set together — a very high ratio with the threshold set above the signal's peaks creates zero compression.

How Ratio Interacts with Other Parameters

Ratio and Threshold Together

High threshold plus high ratio: the compressor only engages on the loudest peaks, but compresses them heavily — used for peak limiting and transient control. Low threshold plus low ratio: the compressor engages consistently throughout the performance but applies gentle, smooth compression — used for transparent levelling and glue. Low threshold plus high ratio: heavy, constant compression that produces a dense, squashed sound — intentional in parallel compression, usually too aggressive as primary compression.

Ratio and Attack

A slow attack lets the initial transient pass through uncompressed before the compressor engages — preserving punch and snap. A fast attack engages on the transient itself, softening it. High ratio plus slow attack gives drums and bass their characteristic tightness while keeping the attack snappy. High ratio plus fast attack applies immediate, full compression including the transient — works for electronic sounds and heavy limiting, less natural on acoustic sources.

Ratio and Release

Short release causes audible pumping — the compressor rapidly releases and re-engages, creating rhythmic breathing. This is intentional in sidechain compression (EDM ducking) and parallel drum bus compression, but destructive on vocals and guitars. Longer release produces smoother, more natural compression but can reduce average level if the signal is consistently active.

Low vs High Ratios

Low Ratios (2:1 – 4:1): Transparent and Musical

Low ratios produce compression that is felt rather than heard. Dynamic range is reduced and performances become more consistent, but the compressor itself remains invisible. This is the standard for vocal and instrument compression in professional mixing. Low ratios are forgiving: even if the threshold is set lower than intended, the gentle ratio keeps the compression subtle and musical rather than audible.

Mid Ratios (4:1 – 8:1): Punchy and Controlled

Mid ratios are the workhorse range for drums, bass, and harder genres. They provide enough compression to add punch and density without obviously squashing the signal. A kick drum compressor at 6:1 with slow attack and medium release sounds tighter and more controlled than a dry kick — the compressor reduces the tail while the transient punches through. Audible to a trained ear but serving the music rather than drawing attention to itself.

High Ratios (8:1 – 20:1): Aggressive and Intentional

High ratios are used when the compression effect itself is the creative goal. Parallel compression — crushing a drum bus at 20:1 and blending the crushed signal with the original — is one of the most common applications. The crushed signal adds density, grit, and sustain without removing natural dynamics from the main signal. High ratios on rap vocals in hip-hop or drill create a dense, upfront quality that is part of the genre's sonic identity.

Soft Knee vs Hard Knee

Hard knee: The full ratio engages immediately as the signal crosses the threshold. Abrupt, precise, more audible at moderate settings. Common in peak-control applications and limiting.

Soft knee: The ratio gradually increases as the signal approaches and crosses the threshold. Smooth and transparent — preferred for vocals, acoustic instruments, and any situation where the compression should be inaudible. Most modern digital compressors offer an adjustable knee.

Reading the Gain Reduction Meter

The gain reduction (GR) meter shows how much the compressor is reducing the signal level in real time. This is one of the most important visual tools in mixing:

  • 0 dB GR: No compression. Either the signal is below threshold or ratio/threshold needs adjustment.
  • 1–3 dB GR: Gentle glue. Appropriate for bus compression and transparent vocal control.
  • 3–6 dB GR: Moderate, musical. Common range for vocals, bass, and individual drums.
  • 6–10 dB GR: Heavy. Can sound great on drums deliberately; risks destroying vocal dynamics.
  • 10+ dB GR: Extreme. Almost certainly audible as an effect. Use intentionally or reduce the threshold.

A common beginner error: setting a very high ratio but leaving the threshold so high that the GR meter shows 0 dB. The compressor is doing nothing. Set the threshold until the GR meter shows the desired gain reduction on the loudest peaks, then choose the ratio that delivers the right character for that amount of reduction.

Practical Exercises

Beginner: Ratio A/B Test

Place a compressor on a vocal track. Set the threshold so the GR meter shows 6 dB of gain reduction on the loudest phrases. Cycle through the following ratios and listen carefully: 2:1, 4:1, 8:1, 20:1. The vocal becomes progressively more controlled and dense with each increase. At 20:1 the compression is clearly audible; at 2:1 it is subtle. Most professional vocal compression lives between 2:1 and 6:1. This exercise trains your ear to hear what different ratios actually sound like in practice — which no amount of reading can replace.

Intermediate: Parallel Compression Ratio Experiment

On a drum bus, set up two parallel compressors. On the primary path, use 2:1 with 2–3 dB of gain reduction — gentle glue. On the parallel path, set 20:1 with the threshold very low — maximum squash. Blend the parallel path in quietly until the drums become denser and heavier without losing natural dynamics. This is the parallel compression technique used on countless professional records. The extreme ratio is only usable because it is blended with the natural signal — the combination achieves what neither signal could achieve alone.

Advanced: Multi-Stage Vocal Compression

Apply two compressors in series on a vocal track. First: 4:1, threshold for 3–4 dB GR on the loudest phrases, medium attack, auto release. This handles the large dynamic swings. Second: 2:1, threshold set to engage on the average level of the post-first-compressor signal, slow attack, medium release, 1–2 dB GR. This catches smaller inconsistencies the first compressor missed. Compare the dual-stage result to a single compressor at equivalent total gain reduction. The dual-stage version will sound more natural — each stage doing less work with fewer artefacts than one compressor working twice as hard.

FAQ — Compression Ratio Explained

What does compression ratio mean?

Compression ratio controls how aggressively a compressor reduces the level of a signal once it crosses the threshold. A 4:1 ratio means for every 4 dB the signal goes above the threshold, the output rises by only 1 dB. Higher ratios mean more compression. A ratio of infinity:1 is a limiter.

What is a good compression ratio for vocals?

2:1 to 4:1 is the standard range for lead vocals. This controls dynamics without making the compression audible. For aggressive styles like trap or hip-hop, 6:1 to 8:1 can work. Avoid ratios above 8:1 on vocals unless you want the squashed sound deliberately.

What compression ratio should I use for drums?

For kick and snare: 4:1 to 8:1 with slow attack and medium release gives punch and control. For parallel drum bus compression: 8:1 to 20:1 mixed at low levels adds density without removing natural dynamics from the main signal.

What is the difference between a compressor and a limiter?

A limiter is a compressor with a very high ratio — typically 10:1 or above, often infinity:1. A compressor reduces gain proportionally above the threshold; a limiter prevents the signal from exceeding the threshold at all. Limiters are used for peak control in mastering and broadcast.

Does a higher compression ratio mean more compression?

Yes, in general — but ratio alone does not determine how much compression occurs. The threshold is equally important. A high ratio with a threshold set above the signal's peaks creates no compression. Ratio and threshold always work together.

What is a soft knee vs hard knee in compression?

A hard knee applies the full ratio immediately as the signal crosses the threshold. A soft knee gradually increases the ratio as the signal approaches and crosses the threshold, creating a smoother, more natural-sounding transition preferred for vocals and acoustic instruments.

What compression ratio is used for limiting?

Limiters typically use ratios of 10:1 and above. At infinity:1, no signal above the threshold passes — the output is completely clamped. Practical mastering limiters use 20:1 to 100:1 with very fast attack times for transparent peak control.

Can I use the same compression ratio on every instrument?

No. Different instruments have different dynamic ranges. Percussive instruments tolerate higher ratios. Sustained instruments like pads and strings often need lower ratios to preserve natural movement. Choose ratio based on how much dynamic control is needed and whether you want the compression to be audible.
