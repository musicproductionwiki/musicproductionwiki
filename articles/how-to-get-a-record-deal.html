How to Get a Record Deal in 2025: What Labels Actually Look For

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2025 10 min read
Quick Answer

In 2025, labels sign artists who have already built something — streaming numbers, social media engagement, a live fanbase, or compelling commercial momentum. The traditional model of labels discovering raw talent and developing it from scratch is largely gone. Build your audience independently first, make the label offer a logical next step rather than a starting point, and understand clearly what you're giving up before signing anything.

The record deal remains one of the most sought-after and most misunderstood things in the music industry. The romantic version — a label A&R rep spots your talent, signs you, and launches your career — still exists but represents a small fraction of how signings actually happen. Understanding how labels actually make signing decisions gives you a realistic roadmap and helps you decide whether a record deal is the right goal for your situation.

What we'll cover: What labels look for before signing an artist, the role of streaming data and social proof, how to get in front of A&R, the difference between major and indie label approaches, what signing actually means for your career, and whether a record deal is the right move for you.

What Labels Actually Look For

Labels are businesses that invest in artists they believe can generate returns. The investment is real — recording, marketing, touring support, team building — and the return timeline is 2–5 years. Labels are risk-averse by necessity, which means they look for evidence that an artist can succeed commercially before committing resources.

Streaming momentum: Monthly Spotify listeners, save rates, editorial playlist placements, and stream velocity (how quickly streams are growing) are primary signals. Labels track Spotify for Artists data, Chartmetric, and similar analytics platforms to identify artists on upward trajectories. An artist with 50,000 monthly listeners and a 50% month-over-month growth rate gets more attention than an artist with 200,000 listeners on a flat or declining trajectory.

Social media presence and engagement: Follower counts matter less than engagement rates. An artist with 20,000 TikTok followers and 15% engagement rate signals a more committed audience than one with 200,000 followers and 0.5% engagement. Labels assess whether social media audiences translate to streaming, ticket sales, and merchandise purchases.

Commercial viability of the music: Does it fit a genre with commercial momentum? Is there a clear audience for it? Does it sound like something that could receive radio, sync, or playlist placement? Labels think about where the music fits in the market, not just whether it's good.

Live performance capability: The ability to headline shows, build ticket sales, and deliver compelling live performances remains important, particularly at mid-tier and above. Labels investing in artist development want to know the artist can sustain a touring career.

The Role of Streaming Data

Streaming data has become the primary objective signal in A&R decision-making. Before streaming, A&R relied more heavily on subjective taste, industry connections, and live show attendance. Now, every label has access to real-time data on what's performing, who's listening where, and what audiences are engaging with.

The practical implication: your Spotify for Artists dashboard is your pitch deck. Before approaching any label, ensure your streaming data tells a compelling story — growing listener numbers, high save rates (saves indicate genuine intent, not passive listening), playlist placement history, and geographic concentration in markets the label cares about.

Labels also track Shazam identifications, TikTok sound usage (songs used as TikTok sounds by non-fans), YouTube views and retention rates, and radio airplay in regional markets. Chartmetric and Soundcharts aggregate this data, and A&R departments use these platforms actively.

How to Get in Front of A&R

A&R (Artists and Repertoire) are the label employees responsible for discovering and signing new artists. Reaching them directly is harder than it was a decade ago — most A&R contacts are flooded with unsolicited submissions — but it's not impossible.

Music attorney introduction: The most reliable path. Entertainment attorneys have ongoing relationships with label A&R departments. An attorney who vouches for an artist gets their submissions listened to. This path requires finding and engaging a music attorney, which is itself a relationship-building exercise.

Manager introduction: A well-connected manager with existing label relationships can get submissions in front of A&R. This is one of the primary reasons signing with a manager before approaching labels makes strategic sense.

Industry showcases and conferences: SXSW, CMJ, Music Connection's events, and genre-specific conferences attract A&R. Performing at these events and building relationships with industry attendees is a legitimate path to label attention, though it requires significant investment in the right event selection and performance quality.

Direct online outreach: Lower success rate but occasionally effective when your streaming data is compelling enough that the numbers speak for themselves. Research specific A&R contacts at labels relevant to your genre (industry databases, LinkedIn, Twitter/X). Send a brief, professional email with a Spotify link and concise streaming data highlights. Do not send unsolicited attachments.

Major vs. Independent Label Approaches

Major labels (Universal, Sony, Warner, and their subsidiaries) offer larger marketing budgets, wider distribution reach, and more established sync and radio relationships. They also take more control, typically own the masters, and sign artists to more restrictive terms. Major label A&R are more selective and require higher demonstrated commercial traction before engaging.

Independent labels vary enormously — from well-funded independents with genuine marketing capability to small operations with limited resources beyond distribution. Independent deals often have better royalty rates, more artist control, and sometimes master ownership or reversion. The trade-off is smaller investment and more limited infrastructure.

The rise of distributor services like DistroKid, TuneCore, and especially distribution + marketing services like UnitedMasters and AWAL has created a middle path between true independence and label signing — distribution deals with some marketing support and better royalty rates than traditional labels, without giving up master ownership.

What Signing Actually Means

A record deal is a business partnership with significant long-term implications. The label invests money (your advance, recording costs, marketing budget) and expects a return. The mechanisms for that return — master ownership, high recoupment thresholds, multiple album options — are structured in the label's favor.

In a typical major label deal: the advance is recoupable (you pay it back from royalties before receiving any payments), the label owns the masters for the term and often beyond, the royalty rate of 15–25% means the label keeps 75–85% of recording revenue, and the label holds options on additional albums that extend the relationship for years.

An artist who sells 500,000 albums may never recoup a large advance and therefore never receive royalty checks beyond the initial advance, while the label profits significantly. Understanding this arithmetic before signing prevents the common experience of commercially successful artists who feel they never made money from their label deal.

Do You Actually Need a Record Deal?

This is the most important question, and the honest answer in 2025 is: many artists don't. Independent artists on distribution platforms like DistroKid retain 100% of their master royalties, maintain full creative control, and build careers at their own pace. Artists like Chance the Rapper and Macklemore demonstrated early that major commercial success was achievable without traditional label deals.

A record deal makes sense when: you need investment you cannot generate independently (recording, touring, marketing at scale), you want access to the label's team and infrastructure for a specific career stage, or the specific deal terms on offer are genuinely favorable and strategic rather than exploitative.

A record deal does not make sense when: you're early in your career and would be signing from a weak negotiating position, the terms require giving up master ownership without commensurate investment and support, or you can achieve your goals independently at your current trajectory.
