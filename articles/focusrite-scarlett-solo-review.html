Focusrite Scarlett Solo Review

First published May 2026 · Updated May 2026
MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2026 Beginner → Advanced
Quick Answer — Verdict

The Focusrite Scarlett Solo (4th Gen) is the best entry-level audio interface available in 2026. At $119, it delivers clean preamps, excellent converter quality, a solid software bundle, and bulletproof reliability. For home studio recording of vocals, guitars, and keyboards, it's the right choice at this price. The only reason to look elsewhere at this budget is if you need more than two inputs — the Solo gives you one mic input and one instrument input, nothing more.

Quick specs: 1 mic preamp · 1 instrument input · 2 outputs · 24-bit/192kHz · USB-C · Bus-powered · Mac & Windows · $119 street price

Who This Is For Beginner

The Scarlett Solo is purpose-built for the solo home studio producer: one person recording one thing at a time. A vocalist who tracks over beats. A singer-songwriter recording acoustic guitar and voice (separately). A beatmaker who needs to record a keyboard or guitar sample. A podcaster recording a single-microphone show. If that's you, the Solo is likely the right interface and you don't need to keep reading reviews — buy it, plug it in, and make music.

If you need to record two people simultaneously, a drum kit, or multiple inputs at once, look at the Scarlett 2i2 (two mic inputs, $169) or the Scarlett 4i4 (four inputs, $219) instead. The Solo's single preamp is its defining limitation, and it's one that can't be worked around.

Sound Quality Intermediate

The 4th Generation Scarlett Solo's preamp is a genuine improvement over previous generations. Focusrite introduced "Air" mode in the 3rd Gen — a high-frequency enhancement that simulates the transformer-coupled character of their ISA studio preamps — and refined it in the 4th Gen. With Air engaged, condenser microphones pick up an additional open, present quality in the upper midrange and high frequencies that makes recordings sound more "studio" and less "bedroom." It's subtle but consistently flattering on vocals and acoustic instruments.

The converters run at up to 24-bit/192kHz, though recording at 44.1kHz or 48kHz/24-bit is the practical choice for most productions. At these settings, the noise floor is impressively low — the equivalent input noise (EIN) specification has improved in the 4th Gen to the point where even lower-output dynamic microphones like the Shure SM7B can be used without a dedicated preamp booster, though a Cloudlifter still helps.

Objectively: the Scarlett Solo does not sound like a $2,000 preamp. It sounds clean, accurate, and neutral — exactly what a transparent entry-level interface should sound like. It will not add warmth, character, or color to your recordings. What goes in comes out accurately. For most home studio applications, that's the correct behavior.

Build Quality

The 4th Gen Solo continues Focusrite's shift to a slightly redesigned chassis with a cleaner front panel layout. The gain knob has satisfying resistance and doesn't feel cheap. The 48V phantom power button is now a dedicated toggle rather than a shared function button, which eliminates the accidental phantom power activation that plagued earlier generations. Bus-powered via USB-C means one cable handles both data and power — no separate power supply needed.

The interface has survived countless drops, coffee incidents, and touring conditions in home studios worldwide. It's not built like a tank, but it's built well enough that failure is uncommon. Focusrite's warranty and customer support are consistently rated among the best in the interface category.

Software Bundle

The Scarlett Solo includes Focusrite's "Hitmaker Expansion" software bundle: Ableton Live Lite, Pro Tools Artist (3-month trial), Antares Auto-Tune Access, a collection of Focusrite plugins, and an expanding library of third-party plugin trials. Ableton Live Lite alone has genuine production value for a beginner — it's a fully functional DAW with limitations on track count and plugin use, sufficient for learning and early projects before upgrading. The total retail value of the bundle exceeds the cost of the interface itself.

Pros and Cons

Pros: Best preamp quality at the sub-$150 price point. Air mode genuinely improves condenser recordings. Bus-powered USB-C. Excellent software bundle. No-driver plug-and-play on modern Mac and Windows. Focusrite's reputation for reliability and support. 4th Gen improvements to gain range make it more usable with dynamic microphones.

Cons: Only one mic preamp — can't simultaneously record two microphones. No MIDI I/O. No hardware monitoring mix control (only direct monitoring toggle). The instrument input can only handle one source at a time. Headphone amp is functional but not exceptional — audiophile headphone users may want a dedicated amp.

Three Alternatives

At the same price — Audient EVO 4 ($119): Technically competitive with the Scarlett Solo on preamp quality. The EVO 4's "Smartgain" automatic gain setting feature is genuinely useful for beginners. The interface is smaller and has a slightly different gain structure that some users prefer. Personal preference between these two is the primary decision factor — both are excellent.

Step up — Focusrite Scarlett 2i2 ($169): Two mic preamps instead of one. If there's any chance you'll want to record two sources simultaneously — even occasionally — the $50 additional investment is straightforwardly worth it. The 2i2 uses the same converters and preamps as the Solo.

Professional step — Universal Audio Volt 1 ($119): UA's entry-level interface includes a "Vintage" mode that adds subtle analog-style harmonic saturation to the preamp signal. Genuinely different character from the Scarlett. Worth auditioning if you want color rather than transparency in your preamp.
