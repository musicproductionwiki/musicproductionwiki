Music Metadata Explained: What It Is and Why It Determines If You Get Paid

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated April 2026 11 min read Beginner

Hundreds of millions of dollars in music royalties go uncollected every year. The primary reason is not fraud, not platform dishonesty, and not record label malfeasance — it is missing or incorrect metadata. When a song is released without the correct songwriter credits, the publishing royalties generated by that song have nowhere to go. When a recording is released without a proper ISRC code, it cannot be accurately tracked across platforms. When an artist name is spelled differently across different releases, the streaming algorithms cannot connect them into a single artist profile. Metadata is not administrative paperwork. It is the infrastructure that makes getting paid possible.

What Metadata Is

Metadata is structured information attached to a piece of music that identifies it, describes it, and establishes ownership. Think of it as the digital equivalent of the information printed on a vinyl record label — artist name, song title, album, catalog number, publisher, songwriter — except that digital metadata travels with the file everywhere it goes and is used by streaming platforms, collection societies, and sync licensing databases to track usage and route royalties.

Every piece of music that exists in the digital ecosystem has metadata attached to it. Some of that metadata was entered carefully and completely. Some of it was entered hastily and incorrectly. The difference between those two scenarios determines whether the rights holders associated with that music receive the royalties they are owed.

The Two Types of Music Rights and Why They Both Need Metadata

Before explaining the specific metadata fields, you need to understand that every piece of music involves two separate sets of rights — and each requires its own metadata infrastructure to generate and collect royalties correctly.

Master rights belong to whoever owns the recording — the specific audio file that is the finished song. For most independent artists, that is the artist themselves. Master royalties are generated when the recording is streamed, downloaded, or used in sync (film, TV, advertising). Your distributor collects master royalties on your behalf and pays them to you based on your streaming and download numbers.

Publishing rights belong to whoever owns the composition — the underlying melody, chord progression, and lyrics, independent of any specific recording of them. Publishing royalties are generated when the composition is streamed (mechanical royalties), performed publicly (performance royalties), or used in sync. Publishing royalties are collected by Performing Rights Organizations (PROs) like ASCAP, BMI, and SESAC in the United States, and by equivalent organizations in other countries. Your distributor typically does not collect publishing royalties automatically — you must register separately.

The metadata that routes master royalties and the metadata that routes publishing royalties overlap but are not identical. Getting both right requires attention to different fields and different registration processes.

The Essential Metadata Fields Every Release Needs

ISRC — International Standard Recording Code. The ISRC is a unique twelve-character identifier for a specific recording. Every recording — every version, every mix, every release — should have its own ISRC. ISRCs are used by streaming platforms to track streams of a specific recording across services, by collection societies to route royalties, and by sync licensing databases to identify recordings. Your distributor generates ISRCs automatically if you do not already have them. If you are re-releasing a recording that previously had an ISRC, use the same ISRC to maintain streaming history continuity.

ISWC — International Standard Musical Work Code. The ISWC is the composition equivalent of the ISRC — a unique identifier for the underlying musical work rather than the recording. ISWCs are assigned by your PRO when you register a composition. They are used to track publishing royalties across territories and platforms. Not all distributors collect ISWCs, but registering your compositions with a PRO assigns them automatically.

UPC — Universal Product Code. The UPC is a barcode identifier for the release as a product — the album, EP, or single as a whole rather than individual tracks. Your distributor generates this automatically. It is used by retail and streaming platforms to identify your release as a distinct product in their catalog.

Artist name. The exact spelling of your artist name as you want it to appear on all platforms. Inconsistency in artist name spelling — even a single capitalization difference — can create separate artist profiles on streaming platforms, splitting your listener data and making it harder for fans to find your complete catalog. Check the spelling against your existing releases before every submission.

Song title. The exact title of the track. Do not include featured artist information in the title field — use the dedicated featured artist field your distributor provides. Do not use ALL CAPS unless it is your deliberate stylistic choice. Do not include version information (Radio Edit, Extended Mix, Acoustic Version) in the main title field if your distributor provides a separate version field.

Songwriter credits. The legal names of every person who contributed to writing the composition — melody, lyrics, and in some genre contexts, the beat or track. This is the most frequently incomplete metadata field and the one whose omission costs artists the most money. If a co-writer's name is missing from the songwriter credits, the publishing royalties attributed to their share have nowhere to go. Enter every songwriter's legal name — not their stage name — exactly as it appears in their PRO registration.

Publisher information. The publishing entity associated with each songwriter's share of the composition. For independent songwriters who have not signed with a traditional music publisher, this is typically either their own publishing entity (which they can establish by registering as a publisher with their PRO) or their PRO affiliation. Leaving this field blank does not prevent royalties from being generated — it prevents them from being collected correctly.

Producer credits. The producer of the recording — the person or persons responsible for creating the beat, track, or musical arrangement. Producer credits are increasingly recognized in streaming platform metadata and are used by some platforms to surface producer information to listeners. They are also relevant to producer royalty agreements and backend points in professional contexts.

Genre. The primary genre of the track. Streaming platforms use genre metadata as a signal for algorithmic playlist placement and listener recommendation. Choose the most accurate genre available — not the genre you aspire to, but the genre the track actually is. Misclassified genre metadata results in the track being surfaced to the wrong audience.

Language. The primary language of the lyrics. Used by streaming platforms for regional recommendation and playlist curation. If the track is instrumental, mark it as such.

Explicit content flag. Mark accurately. Unmarked explicit content can result in removal from family-friendly playlists and may violate distributor and platform terms of service. Incorrectly marked clean content frustrates listeners who discover a track labeled explicit that contains no explicit language.

Why Metadata Errors Are So Costly

Metadata errors compound over time in ways that are difficult and sometimes impossible to fully correct retroactively. A song released with a misspelled songwriter name generates publishing royalties that are held by the collection society as unmatched — they exist, but they have no verified owner to pay them to. Correcting the error after the fact requires filing a correction with your PRO and potentially your distributor, a process that can take months and may not recover royalties already distributed incorrectly.

A song released with the wrong artist name creates a separate artist profile on streaming platforms. Merging profiles requires contacting platform support — a process that Spotify and Apple Music accommodate but that takes time, requires documentation, and may not perfectly consolidate historical streaming data.

A song released without an ISRC cannot be accurately tracked across platforms. Streaming data for that recording may be fragmented across multiple entries in the platform's database, making it impossible to get accurate streaming counts or verify royalty payments against actual usage.

None of these problems are catastrophic in isolation. All of them are entirely preventable by spending fifteen minutes checking metadata carefully before every release submission.

The Metadata Checklist

Before submitting any release to your distributor, run through this checklist. Every field should be confirmed, not assumed.

Artist name — check spelling against existing releases. Song title — correct capitalization, no featured artist information embedded. Featured artists — entered in the dedicated field. ISRC — assigned or confirmed from previous release. Songwriter credits — every contributor's legal name included. Publisher information — your PRO affiliation or publishing entity. Producer credits — entered where the platform supports them. Genre — accurate to the actual track. Language — correct or marked instrumental. Explicit flag — accurately marked. Album artwork — minimum 3000x3000 pixels, no URLs or pricing information embedded. Release date — set with enough lead time for editorial playlist pitching if applicable.

This checklist takes five minutes to complete. The problems it prevents can take months to fix. There is no reasonable argument for skipping it.

Register With a PRO Before Your First Release

If you have not yet registered with a Performing Rights Organization — ASCAP, BMI, or SESAC in the United States — do it before you release your next piece of music. Registration is free for songwriters with ASCAP and BMI. Once registered, register every song you release. The performance and mechanical royalties generated by your music from the moment of release are only collectible if your compositions are registered. Royalties generated before registration are gone. There is no retroactive collection for unregistered works in most territories. Register now.
