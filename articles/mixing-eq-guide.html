Mixing EQ Guide: How to EQ a Mix Professionally

A deep dive into how to use EQ in a mix — from cleaning up individual tracks to shaping the overall tonal balance of your finished song.

By MusicProductionWiki • Updated May 2026 • ~4,100 words
Quick Answer: EQ in a mix serves two purposes: corrective (removing problems — mud, harshness, resonances) and creative (shaping character — warmth, presence, air). The most impactful single move is high-passing every non-bass track at 80–120 Hz. Work subtractive first: cut problems before boosting anything. Always EQ with the full mix playing, never in solo.

Why EQ Is the Foundation of Mixing

Every instrument in a recording occupies space in the frequency spectrum — from the deep sub-bass of a kick drum to the shimmering air of a cymbal. When you record multiple instruments simultaneously, they all occupy this space together. The problem is that many instruments share similar frequency ranges. A bass guitar and a kick drum both live in the 60–200 Hz region. A piano and a guitar both compete in the 200–2,000 Hz range. A vocalist and a lead guitar both need space in the 1–5 kHz presence range.

Without EQ, these instruments mask each other — when they play simultaneously, one makes the other harder to hear. EQ is the tool that creates frequency separation: giving each instrument its own lane in the frequency spectrum so all elements can be heard clearly when playing together.

EQ also shapes the emotional character of a mix. A mix with too much low-mid energy sounds warm but muddy. Too much presence sounds harsh and fatiguing. Too much high-frequency energy sounds bright and thin. EQ is how you balance these qualities across the full mix to achieve the tonal character you want.

The EQ Fundamentals Every Mixer Needs

Filter Types

Every parametric EQ includes the same basic filter types. Knowing what each does lets you choose the right tool for each job.

  • High-pass filter (HPF): Removes frequencies below the cutoff point. Used on almost every track to eliminate sub-bass rumble and mud.
  • Low-pass filter (LPF): Removes frequencies above the cutoff. Used to tame bright, harsh elements or to create low-fi filtered effects.
  • Bell curve (peak/dip): A boost or cut centred on a frequency, with a bell-shaped curve. The most versatile filter — used for everything from corrective cuts to creative boosts.
  • High shelf: Boosts or cuts all frequencies above the shelf point. A common choice for adding air (gentle boost above 10 kHz) or cutting harshness (gentle cut above 8 kHz).
  • Low shelf: Boosts or cuts all frequencies below the shelf point. Used to add warmth or reduce boominess.
  • Notch filter: A very narrow bell cut — used to surgically remove a specific resonance, hum, or feedback frequency.

EQ Parameters

For bell curve filters, three parameters determine the shape of the EQ move:

  • Frequency: Where the EQ acts — which frequency is being boosted or cut.
  • Gain: How much boost or cut is applied, in dB. Most mixing EQ moves are in the range of 1–6 dB. Larger moves indicate a fundamental recording or arrangement problem.
  • Q (bandwidth): How wide or narrow the EQ curve is. Low Q = wide, gentle curve affecting a broad range. High Q = narrow, surgical cut affecting a small range. Wide boosts and narrow cuts are a common starting approach.

Step 1 — Gain Staging Before EQ

Before touching any EQ, set appropriate gain levels throughout the mix. Individual tracks should peak around -18 dBFS (leaving 18 dB of headroom from the peak to 0 dBFS). The mix bus should have at least 6 dB of headroom before any limiting.

This matters for EQ because EQ changes the level of a signal. A significant boost (say, +6 dB at 3 kHz on a vocal) raises the output level of that track. If the track is already hitting close to 0 dBFS, that boost will cause clipping. Good gain staging gives you room to apply EQ without running into ceiling problems. It also keeps your compressors and saturators operating in their optimal range.

Step 2 — Corrective EQ: The High-Pass Filter Pass

Before making any tonal decisions, do a high-pass filter pass on every track. Open each channel and apply a high-pass filter. The cutoff frequency depends on the instrument:

  • Kick drum: no high-pass, or a very low one at 20 Hz to remove infrasonic content
  • Bass guitar / 808: no high-pass, or at 30–40 Hz
  • Electric guitar: 80–100 Hz
  • Acoustic guitar: 80–120 Hz
  • Piano: 80 Hz (unless playing bass voicings)
  • Lead vocals: 100–120 Hz
  • Background vocals: 150–200 Hz
  • Hi-hats, cymbals: 200–300 Hz
  • Pads, ambient layers: 100–200 Hz depending on content

This step alone will make most mixes immediately cleaner, less muddy, and more controlled. The energy you remove with high-pass filters is not musical content — it's handling noise, room rumble, cable hum, and low-frequency buildup that accumulates in the mix bus and reduces headroom without adding any audible quality.

Step 3 — Corrective EQ: Removing Problems

With high-pass filters applied, listen to the mix and identify problem frequencies. Use the sweep technique: insert a bell curve with a high Q and a large boost (+10 to +12 dB). Sweep it slowly from the bottom of the audible range to the top while the mix plays. When you hit a problem frequency — a harsh resonance, a boxy buildup, a muddy thickness — it will become dramatically exaggerated and easy to identify. Once found, switch the boost to a cut (2–4 dB, moderate Q) and the problem will be attenuated.

Common corrective EQ problems and where they live:

  • Mud / thickness: 200–400 Hz. Cut 2–4 dB across multiple tracks to open the mix.
  • Boxiness / cardboard: 400–800 Hz. Particularly common on guitars, snares, and piano.
  • Nasal quality (vocals): 800–1,200 Hz. A narrow cut here can transform a thin, nasal vocal.
  • Harshness / edginess: 2–5 kHz. Common on distorted guitars, compressed vocals, and cheap mics.
  • Sibilance ('s' sounds in vocals): 6–10 kHz. Target narrowly with a notch cut or de-esser.
  • Brittle / ice-pick: 10–12 kHz. Occurs on thin, over-brightened material.

Step 4 — Compression Between EQ Stages

After corrective EQ, apply compression to control dynamics. Then apply a second round of EQ — this time creative. The reason for this order (EQ → comp → EQ) is:

  • EQ before compression shapes the signal that enters the compressor. Removing mud before compression means the compressor isn't triggered by bass energy that isn't musically meaningful.
  • EQ after compression adds creative character to the compressed signal. Boosts on a compressed track feel tighter and more controlled than boosts on an uncompressed track.

This is not a rigid rule — experienced engineers frequently use only one EQ or reverse the order for specific applications. But for beginners, EQ → comp → EQ is a reliable and educational workflow.

Step 5 — Creative EQ: Building Character

Creative EQ adds the tonal character that makes tracks sound musical, not just clean. These moves add warmth, presence, air, and punch — qualities that make professional mixes feel alive and exciting. Common creative EQ moves:

  • Air boost (vocals and cymbals): A gentle high shelf boost at 12–16 kHz (+1.5 to +3 dB) adds openness and a luxurious, extended high-frequency quality associated with premium analogue gear and expensive microphones. This is one of the most consistent improvements available on well-recorded vocals.
  • Presence boost (vocals, guitars): A bell curve boost at 2–4 kHz (+2 to +4 dB, moderate Q) adds intelligibility and cut-through. Vocals with presence EQ sit forward in the mix without needing to be louder.
  • Low-shelf warmth (bass, kick): A gentle low shelf boost at 60–80 Hz adds physical weight and warmth to kick and bass without adding mud (because mud is in the 200–400 Hz range, not the 60–80 Hz range).
  • Beater click (kick drum): A bell boost at 2–5 kHz adds the click attack of the kick beater — essential for the kick to translate on small speakers where the sub-bass can't be heard.
  • String shimmer (acoustic guitar): A high shelf boost at 8–12 kHz adds sparkle to acoustic guitar that makes it feel in the room with the listener.

Frequency Carving — Creating Space for Every Instrument

One of the most powerful EQ techniques in mixing is frequency carving — deliberately cutting a frequency range in one instrument to make space for another instrument that needs that range more. This is complementary EQ: if the kick drum owns 60–80 Hz, the bass guitar gives up some of that range. If the lead vocal owns 2–4 kHz, the electric guitar cuts back there.

A systematic carving approach for a hip-hop or pop mix:

  • Sub-bass (20–60 Hz): Kick drum and 808 bass only. Everything else: high-pass.
  • Bass (60–200 Hz): Bass guitar / sub-synth. Kick can share the upper part. Guitar, piano, pads: cut gently here.
  • Low-mid (200–500 Hz): The most crowded zone. Every instrument produces some energy here. Cut 2–4 dB on everything that doesn't need warmth — guitars, pads, piano.
  • Mids (500 Hz–2 kHz): Piano and guitar share this range. Give each some space by cutting in the mid-range of the one that matters less in the arrangement at each section.
  • Presence (2–5 kHz): Vocals dominate. Cut the electric guitar and piano back in this range slightly when the vocal is singing. Automate this cut to open up in instrumental sections.
  • Air (10+ kHz): Vocal, overhead cymbals, and synthesiser high-frequency content. Wide, open range with enough room for multiple elements without conflict.

EQ on the Mix Bus

Mix bus EQ is applied to the master fader — the output that all individual tracks route through. The goal is gentle, overall tonal shaping rather than corrective work (which should be done at the track level). Common mix bus EQ moves:

  • Very low high-pass at 20–30 Hz (12 dB/oct) to remove sub-sonic energy that causes intermodulation distortion.
  • Small cut at 200–400 Hz (1–2 dB) if the overall mix sounds muddy after track-level EQ work.
  • Small high shelf boost at 10–16 kHz (1–2 dB) to add overall air and sheen — especially effective on well-mixed pop and hip-hop.
  • A Pultec-style move: simultaneously boost and cut the same low frequency (both moves on 60 Hz, for example). The boost adds warmth, the cut adds punch. Together they create a sound more natural than either alone.

Keep mix bus EQ gentle. If you're making cuts and boosts of more than 3 dB on the mix bus, you have track-level problems that should be addressed there.

Common EQ Mistakes in Mixing

Even experienced producers make these errors. Knowing them helps you avoid them:

  • EQ-ing in solo: Every EQ decision needs to be made with the full mix playing. Soloing a track changes how your ears perceive its frequency content dramatically.
  • Over-boosting: Beginners often reach for boosts when cuts are the answer. Large boosts create phase issues, increase distortion, and can make a track louder without making it better.
  • Ignoring the low end: On consumer headphones and laptop speakers, bass is rarely audible. Mixes made on these playback systems often have too much or too little bass. Always check mixes on multiple playback systems.
  • Static EQ on dynamic material: A vocalist moves toward and away from the mic, changes their tone between verses, and has different intensity in different sections. Static EQ applied once doesn't respond to these changes. Dynamic EQ (which applies cuts and boosts only when signal crosses a threshold) or automation is more appropriate for live performances.
  • Matching EQ settings from tutorials: Tutorials use specific vocal recordings, specific microphones, specific rooms. Their EQ settings will not work on your material. Use tutorials to understand the technique, then apply your own judgment to your own recordings.

Practical Exercises

Beginner: The Three-Move Mix Cleanup

Open any multitrack session. Apply three moves only across the entire session: (1) High-pass every non-bass track at 80–100 Hz. (2) Cut 2–3 dB at 300–400 Hz on every instrument track (not the lead vocal). (3) Add a +2 dB high shelf at 12 kHz on the lead vocal. Export the mix before and after. Listen on headphones and on phone speakers. Note what changed and why. This exercise demonstrates the power of three focused, principled EQ moves versus many small random adjustments.

Intermediate: Frequency Carving Exercise

Take a session with at least four elements (kick, bass, guitar or synth, lead vocal). Identify what frequency range each element is most important in. Then: cut the guitar/synth at 60–80 Hz to give that range entirely to the bass and kick. Cut the guitar/synth at 2–4 kHz to make space for the vocal's presence. Boost the vocal at 3 kHz to take advantage of the space you just created. Listen to how the four elements become more distinguishable without any change in volume. This is frequency carving — the most powerful mixing technique beyond gain staging.

Advanced: Dynamic EQ on Vocals

Load a dynamic EQ (FabFilter Pro-Q 4 in dynamic mode, or a dedicated dynamic EQ like Neutron's) on your lead vocal. Set a dynamic cut at the harsh frequency you identified on the vocal (typically 3–5 kHz). Set the threshold so the cut only activates when the vocal gets loud or harsh — not during quiet phrases. Listen to how the vocal retains warmth and intimacy on quiet lines while harshness is controlled on loud, intense sections. This is dynamic EQ thinking: treating the same frequency differently depending on how the performer is using it at each moment. Compare to a static EQ at the same frequency — the dynamic version will sound more natural and less processed.

FAQ — Mixing EQ Guide

What is the most important EQ move in mixing?

High-passing every track that doesn't need low end. Set a high-pass filter on everything except kick and bass at 80–120 Hz. This is the single highest-impact EQ move and should be applied to virtually every track.

Should you EQ in solo or in context?

Always EQ in context — with the full mix playing. A track that sounds perfect in solo often sounds wrong in the mix. EQ decisions need to be made based on how elements interact with each other.

What is frequency masking and how do I fix it?

Frequency masking occurs when two instruments share the same frequency range, causing one to become inaudible when both play simultaneously. Fix it by giving each instrument its own frequency slot — cut the range in the instrument that needs it less.

What is mid/side EQ and when should I use it?

Mid/side EQ lets you EQ the center of the stereo image and the sides independently. It's used in mastering to add brightness to wide elements without affecting the center. In mixing, it's useful on reverb returns and stereo instruments.

How much EQ is too much?

If you're making cuts or boosts of 6 dB or more, the recording or arrangement may have a problem that EQ can't fully fix. Large EQ moves often create phase issues and artefacts. Start by asking whether better source material or arrangement would fix the problem before reaching for extreme EQ.

What is dynamic EQ?

Dynamic EQ combines EQ and compression — it applies EQ cuts or boosts only when the signal crosses a threshold. It only attenuates harsh frequencies when they become problematic, rather than permanently cutting. Dynamic EQ is increasingly preferred for vocals because it responds to the performance rather than applying a fixed treatment.

How do I EQ the mix bus?

Mix bus EQ should be gentle and corrective. Common moves: a very low high-pass at 20–30 Hz; a small cut at 200–400 Hz if the mix is muddy; a small high shelf boost at 10–16 kHz for air. Avoid dramatic mix bus EQ — fix problems at the track level first.

Linear phase vs minimum phase EQ — which should I use?

Minimum phase EQ is transparent at moderate settings and preferred for individual tracks. Linear phase EQ adds no phase shift and is better suited for mastering and mix bus where phase coherence across the full spectrum matters most.
