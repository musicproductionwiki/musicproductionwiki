Beyerdynamic DT 990 Pro Review

MP
The Music Production Wiki Team
Updated May 2025 10 min read
Quick Verdict

The DT 990 Pro is an excellent open-back headphone for mixing reference and critical listening at a competitive price point. Its wide soundstage and detailed high frequencies make it genuinely useful for mixing decisions. The elevated treble response is a known characteristic that requires acclimatisation — experienced engineers use it to catch sibilance and high-frequency harshness that closed-back headphones can mask. Not suitable for tracking or any situation requiring isolation.

Beyerdynamic's DT 990 Pro has been in continuous production since 1985, which in the headphone market is an extraordinary run. While competitors have cycled through multiple generations of redesign, the DT 990 Pro's fundamental acoustic design has remained largely consistent — a testament to how well the original engineering held up, and to the loyal professional user base that kept buying it decade after decade.

This review covers the DT 990 Pro specifically in the context of music production and mixing — what it does well for studio work, where its frequency response quirks help and hurt, and whether it belongs in your monitoring setup.

What we'll cover: Build quality and comfort, the open-back design and what it means practically, frequency response characteristics, soundstage and imaging, mixing use cases, comparisons with key competitors, and who the DT 990 Pro is actually for.

The 250-ohm impedance is the version most commonly found in professional studio contexts. Beyerdynamic also makes 32-ohm and 80-ohm versions — the 32-ohm is intended for portable devices, the 80-ohm splits the difference. For DAW-based production from an audio interface, the 250-ohm version is the standard recommendation and what this review covers.

Build Quality and Comfort

The DT 990 Pro is built to a standard that justifies its professional label. The headband is steel with a padded underside, the earcups rotate on metal yokes, and the plastic used throughout feels dense and durable rather than cheap. Beyerdynamic sells replacement parts — earpads, headband padding, cables — individually, making the DT 990 Pro genuinely repairable rather than disposable. Producers who bought DT 990 Pros a decade ago are still using them with replaced pads and headbands, not new headphones.

Comfort is a more complicated story. The velour earpads are soft and breathable — significantly more comfortable over long sessions than the leatherette pads found on many closed-back studio headphones. The clamping force is firm, particularly when new, which some users find tiring over multi-hour sessions. This typically loosens over several weeks of use. The coiled cable is heavy and can create some neck strain if you move around the studio — it's optimised for sitting at a desk rather than moving between positions.

At 250 grams, the DT 990 Pro is light enough that weight is not a comfort concern. The headband adjustment is simple and secure. These are headphones designed for long studio sessions, and aside from the clamping force issue (which resolves with time), they deliver on that design intent.

The Open-Back Design

Open-back headphones have perforated or mesh earcup backs that allow air and sound to pass through. This creates a more natural, spacious sound with better stereo imaging and a larger perceived soundstage than closed-back designs — but it also means sound leaks out of the headphones and environmental noise leaks in. The DT 990 Pro offers essentially no passive isolation.

For mixing and critical listening in a quiet studio environment, this is not a problem — it's a feature. The open-back design allows the headphone to breathe acoustically, reducing the artificial bass buildup and sense of confinement that closed-back headphones can introduce. Mixing decisions made on open-back headphones tend to translate better to speaker playback because the listening experience is more similar to speaker listening than closed-back headphones provide.

For tracking — recording a vocalist or instrumentalist who is wearing headphones while recording — open-back headphones are unsuitable. The sound leakage bleeds into microphones and the lack of isolation means the performer hears everything in the room. Use closed-back headphones for tracking. The DT 990 Pro is a mixing and listening tool, not a tracking tool.

Frequency Response: The Treble Question

The DT 990 Pro's frequency response is the defining characteristic of the headphone and the source of most of the debate about it. The low end is extended and slightly elevated, providing a sense of weight and fullness. The midrange is generally neutral. The high frequencies — from around 8 kHz upward — have a pronounced peak that makes the headphone sound noticeably bright compared to a flat reference target.

This elevated treble response divides opinion along experience lines. Producers new to the DT 990 Pro often find it fatiguing or harsh. Experienced engineers who use it regularly learn to compensate for it — they know that if a mix sounds slightly dark or veiled on the DT 990 Pro, it may actually be well-balanced on speakers. More practically, the elevated treble makes sibilance, harsh consonants, and high-frequency distortion easier to identify. Problems that sit in the 6–12 kHz range, which can be masked by warmer-sounding headphones, are immediately apparent on the DT 990 Pros.

Whether you find this useful or annoying depends on how you use headphones in your mix process. Engineers who use headphones as a cross-reference alongside speakers — checking specific problem areas rather than mixing exclusively on headphones — tend to appreciate what the DT 990 Pro's character reveals. Engineers who mix primarily on headphones may find a flatter, more neutral-sounding option (the Sennheiser HD 600 series, or the AKG K702) easier to work on for extended sessions.

Soundstage and Imaging

The DT 990 Pro's soundstage is one of its genuine strengths and a key reason experienced engineers choose it. The open-back design combined with the 45 mm drivers creates a wide, spacious presentation that makes stereo positioning more discernible than many closed-back alternatives at similar price points. Elements that appear mono or congealed in the center on a closed-back headphone separate clearly on the DT 990 Pro.

Imaging — the precision of the perceived position of individual sounds within the stereo field — is accurate. Panning decisions, reverb tail placement, and the width of individual elements are all easier to evaluate than on headphones with a narrower soundstage. This makes the DT 990 Pro particularly useful for checking stereo width, identifying mono compatibility issues, and evaluating spatial processing like reverb and delay.

Mixing Use Cases

The DT 990 Pro earns its place in professional studios primarily as a cross-reference tool rather than a sole mixing medium. Using it to check mixes that were primarily built on speakers allows engineers to catch issues that speakers in a problematic room might mask — certain high-frequency problems, specific stereo imaging inconsistencies, and low-frequency muddiness that the room acoustics are concealing.

It works particularly well for: checking vocal sibilance and consonant harshness (the elevated treble makes these impossible to miss), evaluating stereo width and panning decisions, identifying resonant frequencies that disappear on speakers due to room modes, and checking mix translations before sending to clients or submitting for distribution.

Key Comparisons

vs. Sony MDR-7506: The MDR-7506 is closed-back, significantly cheaper (~$99), and more portable. Its frequency response is similarly bright but with a different character — more forward in the upper mids, less extended in the low end. The DT 990 Pro's soundstage is substantially wider and its build quality is superior. For pure studio use on a desk, the DT 990 Pro is the better reference tool. For versatility including tracking use, the MDR-7506's closed-back design is a practical advantage.

vs. Sennheiser HD 600: The HD 600 (~$299) has a more neutral frequency response — less treble elevation, more natural tonality — and is widely considered the more accurate reference tool. The DT 990 Pro's elevated treble makes it more revealing of high-frequency problems but harder to listen to for long sessions. The HD 600 is easier to mix on for extended periods. Both are excellent; the choice depends on whether you want revealing brightness or neutral accuracy.

vs. AKG K702: Similar price range, similarly wide soundstage, similarly analytical character. The K702 has a slightly more forward upper midrange. Choice between the two is largely personal preference — both serve the same mixing reference use case effectively.

Verdict

The Beyerdynamic DT 990 Pro at ~$149–179 represents excellent value for an open-back studio reference headphone. Its build quality justifies decades of use with user-replaceable parts. Its soundstage and imaging are genuine mixing tools. The elevated treble response is a characteristic that experienced engineers find useful for catching high-frequency problems — but it requires acclimatisation and is not suitable for all mixing approaches or all ears.

Buy the DT 990 Pro if you want an open-back reference headphone for cross-checking mixes, can handle the treble character, and value repairability and long-term durability. Consider alternatives if you prefer a flatter, more neutral response (HD 600) or need a closed-back design for tracking or isolation (MDR-7506, DT 770 Pro).

Score: 8.6/10
