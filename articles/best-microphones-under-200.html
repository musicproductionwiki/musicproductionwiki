Updated May 2026 — Finding a genuinely great microphone under $200 has never been easier, but the sheer number of options makes choosing the right one legitimately confusing. Marketing copy is almost uniformly dishonest at this price tier, and spec sheets rarely tell you what you actually need to know. This guide cuts through all of that. Every recommendation here has been evaluated against real-world recording scenarios: untreated bedroom studios, semi-treated home setups, live rooms, and everything in between.

Whether you're recording lead vocals for a hip-hop track, miking an acoustic guitar for a singer-songwriter EP, capturing room ambience for a film score, or setting up a podcast rig, the right microphone makes an enormous difference — and the wrong one wastes both time and money. Let's get specific.

Before You Buy: No microphone sounds good in a bad acoustic environment. If you're recording in an untreated room with hard walls and parallel surfaces, a sensitive large-diaphragm condenser will capture every reflection and make your recordings sound like they were made in a bathroom — regardless of the mic's quality. Before spending $200 on a mic, spend $30 on moving blankets or foam panels, or read our guide to home studio acoustic treatment to understand what you actually need. A dynamic mic in an untreated room will almost always beat a condenser in the same space.

Understanding Mic Types: Condenser vs. Dynamic vs. Ribbon

Before diving into individual picks, you need to understand the fundamental differences between microphone types — because choosing the wrong type for your application is far more damaging to your sound than choosing a budget option over a premium one.

Large-Diaphragm Condensers (LDC) are the workhorses of studio vocal recording. They use a thin electrically-charged diaphragm and require 48V phantom power from your audio interface or mixer. LDCs are extremely sensitive, capturing fine detail and extended high-frequency response — which is both their greatest strength and their biggest weakness in home studios. They capture everything: the warmth and presence of a great vocal performance, but also every keyboard clack, HVAC hum, neighbor's lawnmower, and room reflection. In a treated room, they're unbeatable at this price tier.

Small-Diaphragm Condensers (SDC) use a smaller capsule and excel at capturing transient-rich sources: acoustic guitars, percussion, piano, and overhead drum miking. They have a more consistent polar pattern across the frequency spectrum compared to LDCs, meaning off-axis coloration is more controlled. The Rode M5 and Audio-Technica AT4041 are classics in this category; for under $200, matching pairs are available.

Dynamic Microphones work on electromagnetic induction — a coil attached to a diaphragm moves through a magnetic field. They're far less sensitive than condensers, require no phantom power, and are significantly more resistant to handling noise, humidity, and physical abuse. This makes them ideal for untreated rooms, live recording, loud sources (guitar amps, drum kits), and broadcast/podcast applications. The tradeoff is typically reduced high-frequency extension and transient response compared to condensers.

USB Microphones integrate a built-in audio interface and connect directly to your computer via USB. They're ideal for podcasters, streamers, and producers who need a fast, simple setup without purchasing a separate interface. The audio quality of modern USB mics has closed significantly on comparable XLR options, though you lose the flexibility to integrate them into a larger studio chain.

For a deeper exploration of this topic, our dedicated guide on condenser vs. dynamic microphones covers polar patterns, SPL handling, self-noise specifications, and application-specific recommendations in detail.

Top Picks: Detailed Breakdown

1. Audio-Technica AT2020 — Best All-Round Budget Condenser

The AT2020 has been a home studio staple for nearly two decades, and in 2026 it still earns that reputation by delivering consistent, honest performance at a price that leaves budget for the rest of your setup. Its fixed cardioid polar pattern offers excellent off-axis rejection, and the large diaphragm capsule provides a smooth, relatively flat frequency response with a gentle presence peak around 7–12 kHz that adds intelligibility to vocals without sounding harsh.

Self-noise sits at 20 dB-A — acceptable for studio recording, though not class-leading. Maximum SPL handling of 144 dB (with the 10 dB pad engaged) means it can handle louder acoustic sources including guitar amps at a distance. The build quality is excellent for the price: the all-metal body feels solid and the integrated stand mount is functional, though a proper shock mount is a worthwhile $20–$30 addition.

What it sounds like: Slightly forward midrange, clean low end, present but not hyped high frequencies. Works well on male and female vocals, acoustic guitars, and as a room mic. Sounds particularly good on rap and hip-hop vocals where forward mids translate well through earbuds and phone speakers. Does not flatter every voice — singers with harsh sibilance or excessive brightness may prefer the warmer sE Electronics X1 S.

Pairing advice: The AT2020 outputs a moderate signal level and works well with entry-level interfaces like the Focusrite Scarlett Solo. You won't need a heavy preamp gain stage. Pair with a basic pop filter (a $10–$15 nylon mesh filter is fine) and position the vocalist 6–8 inches from the capsule for optimal proximity effect without excessive plosives.

Pros: Proven track record, excellent value-to-quality ratio, widely available, handles loud sources, consistent manufacturing quality.
Cons: Self-noise is moderate (20 dB-A), only cardioid polar pattern, doesn't come with shock mount.

2. Rode NT1 5th Generation — Best Overall Quality Under $200

The 5th generation NT1, released in 2022 and still the benchmark for sub-$200 condenser performance in 2026, is a genuinely remarkable microphone. Its self-noise specification of 4 dB-A is extraordinary — lower than many microphones costing three times as much, including the AKG C414 XLII. This makes it the go-to choice for quiet acoustic sources, delicate singers, and anyone recording in a treated room who wants to capture maximum detail.

The 5th Gen added a USB-C output alongside the traditional XLR, giving you genuine flexibility: use it as a traditional studio condenser through your interface, or plug it directly into your laptop for fast tracking sessions. Both outputs work simultaneously, meaning you can monitor through USB while recording through XLR — a genuinely useful workflow feature. The built-in ADC for the USB path runs at 24-bit/192kHz, which is overkill but means there's no quality ceiling.

What it sounds like: Clean, open, and detailed with a subtle warmth in the low-mids that prevents the clarity from sounding clinical. The presence peak is gentler than the AT2020, making it more flattering on brighter voices. Extended low-frequency response down to 20 Hz captures room fundamentals and instrument body. This is the mic I'd recommend for singer-songwriters recording acoustic performances, vocalists with strong technique in a treated room, and anyone recording anything where detail matters.

Real-world caveat: The NT1's ultra-low self-noise and high sensitivity mean it will ruthlessly expose any room acoustic problems. In an untreated bedroom, it will sound worse than an SM58. This is not a mic for compromised recording environments.

Pros: Industry-leading 4 dB-A self-noise, dual XLR/USB-C, exceptional detail, 10-year warranty from Rode.
Cons: Expensive at the top of this budget range, sensitivity to room acoustics, requires a quiet preamp to realize its potential.

3. Shure SM58 — Best Dynamic for Vocals

If you're recording vocals in an untreated space — which describes the majority of home producers — the SM58 is the most reliable choice on this entire list. Shure's cardioid dynamic has been on more professional recordings than any other microphone in history, and its continued relevance in 2026 is a testament to the soundness of its fundamental design. The built-in spherical wind and pop filter, the carefully tuned presence peak at around 5–10 kHz, and the tight cardioid polar pattern with significant rear rejection make it uniquely forgiving of imperfect environments.

The SM58 rejects room noise and handling vibrations that would ruin a condenser recording. Its frequency response rolls off sharply below 80 Hz (useful built-in high-pass behavior) and extends to 15 kHz — not the 20 kHz you'd get from a condenser, but sufficient for virtually all vocal and instrument recording. The lack of high self-noise (dynamic mics don't have this spec in the same meaningful way) means recordings are clean at the noise floor.

Use it close: The SM58 rewards proximity. Work it at 2–4 inches from the source for maximum proximity effect and rear rejection. The closer positioning also means you capture more direct sound relative to room reflections — a natural acoustic advantage in untreated spaces.

Limitation to acknowledge: The SM58 needs gain. Entry-level interfaces with mediocre preamps may struggle to drive it to a healthy recording level without adding noise. The Focusrite Scarlett Solo and 2i2 Gen 4 handles this fine; older or cheaper interfaces may not. If you're using a budget interface with low gain headroom, consider the SM7dB instead.

Pros: Bullet-proof build quality, exceptional room rejection, no phantom power required, extremely versatile, proven on millions of recordings.
Cons: Requires decent preamp gain, limited high-frequency extension vs. condensers, no included shock mount.

4. Shure SM7dB — Best Dynamic Under $200 for Modern Producers

The SM7dB is Shure's evolution of the legendary SM7B, redesigned to incorporate an internal preamp that adds 18 or 28 dB of clean gain directly at the mic capsule. This solves the SM7B's biggest real-world limitation — the need for a high-gain, low-noise preamp — while delivering the same characteristic warm, present broadcast sound that made the SM7B a studio standard for vocals, podcasting, and voice-over.

At $199, the SM7dB sits at the very top of this budget bracket, but it represents exceptional value given what you're getting: the same capsule and acoustic design as the SM7B ($399+), with the added convenience of not needing a Cloudlifter or a premium preamp to drive it properly. The selectable high-pass filter (with a steeper, more aggressive option than the original SM7B) and presence boost switch provide real mixing utility at the mic level.

Who this is for: Podcasters, broadcasters, voice-over artists, rappers and vocalists recording in untreated rooms, and anyone who wants the SM7B sound without the preamp tax. The SM7dB is also excellent for electric guitar cabs miked at distance and for recording spoken word in challenging acoustic environments.

Pros: Built-in preamp solves gain requirements, warm broadcast character, excellent room rejection, high-pass and presence boost switches.
Cons: At the very limit of the $200 budget, heavier and bulkier than most mics, cardioid only.

5. sE Electronics X1 S — Best Budget Condenser for Versatility

The sE Electronics X1 S is arguably the most underrated microphone under $200. sE is a serious manufacturer — they make capsules for many other brands and have a genuine engineering heritage — and the X1 S reflects that with a 1-inch condenser capsule that produces a warm, flattering sound that works well on sources where the AT2020 might sound slightly aggressive.

Self-noise of 16 dB-A beats the AT2020 by 4 dB, and the included RF-X Reflection Filter (a mini acoustic baffle that clips to the microphone) is a genuinely useful accessory for untreated rooms — it's not a substitute for proper treatment, but it does reduce rear reflections in modest environments. The X1 S package includes a shock mount and pop filter, which makes the total cost-of-ownership argument even stronger.

What it sounds like: Warmer and smoother than the AT2020, with a softer presence peak. Excellent on female vocals, acoustic instruments, and piano. Works well on voice-over where the neutral-to-warm character sounds natural through laptop speakers and podcast headphones. Slightly less forward in the mix than the AT2020 — some producers find this requires more EQ work on hip-hop vocals.

Pros: Warm, flattering character, lower self-noise than AT2020, includes shock mount and pop filter, reputable manufacturer.
Cons: Less presence peak may require more post-EQ, reflection filter accessory is limited in very live rooms.

6. Blue Yeti X — Best USB Microphone

The Blue Yeti X remains the benchmark USB microphone in 2026 for producers and content creators who want professional-quality audio without the complexity of an audio interface setup. Its four selectable polar patterns (cardioid, bidirectional, omnidirectional, stereo) make it adaptable to an unusual range of recording scenarios for a single microphone, and the integrated headphone output with zero-latency monitoring means you can hear yourself in real time during recording.

The Yeti X features a multi-capsule array of four condenser elements, processed internally to produce the selectable patterns. In cardioid mode, it produces clean, present audio that works well for voiceovers, podcast recording, streaming commentary, and even acoustic instrument spot miking. The LED metering on the front is a useful visual feedback tool for gain-staging without a DAW meter visible.

Realistic limitations: The Yeti X's internal ADC and processing chain, while excellent, is not equivalent to a dedicated audio interface + XLR condenser at the same price point. For music production where you want maximum fidelity and the ability to process gain staging at every point in the chain, the AT2020 + Focusrite Scarlett Solo combination provides more routing flexibility. The Yeti X is the right choice when simplicity, portability, and fast setup are priorities.

Pros: No interface required, four polar patterns, integrated headphone monitoring, excellent ease of use, built for content creators.

8. Rode M3 — Best Small-Diaphragm Condenser for Acoustic Sources

The Rode M3 is the only small-diaphragm condenser on this list, and it fills an important gap: if you're primarily recording acoustic guitar, piano, violin, or percussion, a well-positioned SDC often beats an LDC at any price. The M3's response is extremely consistent off-axis, its transient accuracy is excellent, and it provides a natural, uncolored capture of acoustic instruments that allows more precise EQ in post.

A unique feature is its ability to run on a 9V battery when phantom power is unavailable — genuinely useful for field recording and mobile setups. The frequency response extends from 40 Hz to 20 kHz with a very flat midrange and clean high-frequency extension. Self-noise of 24 dB-A is the highest on this list, meaning it's not ideal for very quiet sources (a whispered voice, for example), but for acoustic guitar and instruments with natural output levels, the noise floor is inaudible in practice.

Recording tip: For acoustic guitar, try the M3 aimed at the 12th fret from about 8 inches away for a balanced capture that avoids the harsh brightness of the soundhole and the thin, low-output response of aiming at the neck. Matching Your Microphone to Your Use Case

The most common mistake budget microphone buyers make is optimizing for the wrong use case. Here's a direct, scenario-by-scenario guide to which microphone to buy:

Recording hip-hop or trap vocals in a bedroom with no acoustic treatment: Shure SM7dB (first choice) or SM58 (budget option). The dynamic microphone's natural room rejection will produce cleaner recordings than any condenser in this environment. Position at 3–4 inches, apply a high-pass filter at 100 Hz in your DAW, and use a fast compressor to tame dynamics. Check our guide on recording vocals at home for a complete workflow.

Recording singer-songwriter vocals and acoustic guitar in a semi-treated room: Rode NT1 5th Gen for vocals (best detail at this price), Rode M3 or AT2020 for guitar. The NT1's low self-noise captures the natural breathiness and dynamic range of acoustic performances without adding noise. If you want a single mic for both, the AT2020 handles both competently, or the sE X1 S for a warmer character.

Podcasting or voice-over with no interface: Blue Yeti X. The multi-pattern design handles solo recording (cardioid), two-person in-person recording (bidirectional), and conference-style group recording (omnidirectional). The integrated monitoring and gain control means zero additional purchases.

Recording acoustic instruments: guitar, violin, piano, percussion: Rode M3 as a primary small-diaphragm option, or the AT2020 if you already own it. For piano, try a pair of AT2020s in XY configuration — a matched stereo pair under $200 total is achievable and will produce surprisingly professional results.

Recording electric guitar amp: Shure SM58 or SM7dB. Dynamic mics are the standard choice for close-miking guitar cabs (the SM57, not on this list, is the canonical choice — but the SM58's capsule is very similar and produces excellent results). Position at 1–3 inches from the speaker cone, angled between the center and edge of the speaker to balance brightness and body.

First microphone with zero other gear: Samson Q2U. Plug it in, record, and upgrade the chain gradually. It gives you an immediate working setup without the $100+ interface purchase.

Audio Interface Pairing: Getting the Most from Your Microphone

A microphone is only as good as the signal chain it's connected to. Budget microphones in particular benefit significantly from clean, low-noise preamps that provide adequate gain without adding hiss or coloration. Here's what you need to know about pairing your sub-$200 microphone with the right interface.

XLR condenser microphones need 48V phantom power — confirm your interface provides this before purchasing. Every modern interface does, but some older or very cheap models have unreliable phantom power that can underpower condensers, resulting in thin, distorted, or noise-prone results.

For dynamic microphones like the SM58, preamp gain headroom matters. The SM58 has an output sensitivity of approximately -54.5 dBV/Pa, which is relatively low. You'll need 50–60 dB of clean gain from your preamp to reach a healthy recording level of -18 to -12 dBFS. Budget interfaces like the Behringer UMC22 often top out at 40–45 dB of gain — not quite enough. The Focusrite Scarlett Solo Gen 4 provides 69 dB of gain, which is sufficient. The Focusrite Scarlett 2i2 Gen 4 is the recommended pairing for most XLR microphones on this list: clean preamps, 69 dB gain range, solid phantom power, and a street price around $180 that fits the overall budget.

For the SM7dB specifically, the internal preamp means any interface with an XLR input will work — even budget options — because the mic handles its own gain requirements. This makes the SM7dB unusually interface-agnostic.

The Rode NT1 5th Gen's dual XLR/USB-C functionality means you can bypass the interface entirely for tracking sessions, then switch to XLR for critical recording. This flexibility makes it an ideal choice for producers who mix between laptop recording setups and studio configurations. For deeper guidance on building out your interface setup, our best audio interfaces for beginners guide covers the full market at every price point.

One final pairing consideration: microphone placement hardware. Every XLR microphone on this list benefits from a proper boom arm or stand rather than a desktop stand. Boom arms eliminate desk vibration transmission (footsteps, keyboard typing, mouse clicks) that desktop stands pick up directly. For under $30, the Rode PSA1+ is a reliable option; the InnoGear and TONOR boom arms are functional at $15–$20 for budget setups.

Acoustic Treatment: The Multiplier Effect on Microphone Performance

This section exists because it's the most important factor most budget microphone buyers ignore. The $99 you spend on a microphone will be undermined by a $0 investment in acoustic treatment. Conversely, modest acoustic treatment — even DIY solutions — can make a $50 dynamic microphone sound significantly better than a $500 condenser in an untreated room.

The primary acoustic problem in home recording spaces is flutter echo and early reflections: sound from the microphone's source bouncing off parallel walls, hard floors, and flat ceilings, arriving at the microphone capsule 5–30 milliseconds after the direct signal. This creates a comb-filtering effect that sounds like a metallic resonance or "honky" coloration in your recordings — the telltale sign of an untreated room.

The fastest acoustic improvement for under $50:

  • Three moving blankets hung on walls behind and to the sides of the recording position: approximately $30–$40 total from hardware stores. These won't treat the room properly, but they'll absorb mid-to-high frequency reflections and noticeably improve vocal recordings.
  • Record in a closet. Seriously. A clothes-filled closet is one of the best acoustic environments in most homes because the clothing acts as distributed broadband absorption. The SM58 or AT2020 in a well-stocked closet will produce very usable recordings.
  • Use a reflection filter on your condenser mic. The sE Electronics Reflexion Filter and similar products clip to the microphone stand and absorb reflections from behind the capsule — they help meaningfully with rear-coming reflections but don't address room modes or floor/ceiling bounce.

For a systematic approach to treating your recording space, our dedicated home studio acoustic treatment guide covers bass traps, broadband absorption panels, diffusion placement, and budget DIY builds in comprehensive detail.

The practical takeaway: if your recording environment is completely untreated and you can't change that, prioritize a dynamic microphone (SM58, SM7dB) over a condenser. If you can achieve even basic treatment, the condenser options on this list will reward that investment significantly.

What to Avoid: Red Flags and Budget Mic Traps

The sub-$200 microphone market is littered with products that look professional (large bodies, gold-plated capsule grilles, heavy knurled finishes) but measure and sound like toys. Here's what to watch for:

Unspecified or suspiciously high sensitivity ratings: Some budget condensers claim extremely high sensitivity to make their gain look impressive, but high sensitivity combined with poor capsule manufacturing means you're amplifying both signal and noise equally. A mic that claims -20 dBV/Pa sensitivity but doesn't specify self-noise is a red flag.

No phantom power requirement on a condenser: True condenser microphones require 48V phantom power. Some budget "condensers" are actually back-electret designs that use battery-style internal biasing. These can work fine, but they're not true condensers and their performance ceiling is lower. Knowing the difference helps you evaluate claims accurately.

Generic capsule rebranding: A significant portion of sub-$50 condenser microphones on Amazon and AliExpress use identical Chinese-manufactured capsules in different housings. The AT9934 capsule, for example, appears in dozens of differently-branded microphones with wildly different claimed specifications. If a microphone doesn't come from a manufacturer with a documented engineering history (Shure, Rode, Audio-Technica, sE Electronics, AKG, Blue/Logitech), scrutinize claims carefully.

USB mics with no headphone monitoring: If you're buying a USB microphone for real-time recording, the absence of a headphone output means you'll experience latency between singing/speaking and hearing yourself — this negatively affects performance. Always check for direct monitoring capability.

Mics marketed primarily on aesthetics: Microphones with RGB lighting, chrome finishes, and LED displays aimed at streamers are often engineered primarily for appearance. Some are excellent (certain HyperX and HyperX Quadcast models measure well); many are not. The Blue Yeti X included in this list performs; most similarly-marketed competitors don't.

Practical Exercises

Beginner Exercise

A/B Test Your Room vs. a Treated Space

Record a 30-second vocal take in your normal recording space, then recreate the same take inside a large closet filled with clothing. Compare the two recordings in your DAW — zoom into the waveform and listen for the smearing and coloration caused by room reflections in the untreated space. This exercise builds direct auditory awareness of how acoustic environment affects microphone performance before you spend money on gear.

Intermediate Exercise

Microphone Placement Mapping for Acoustic Guitar

Using any condenser microphone, record the same acoustic guitar chord progression from five different positions: directly at the soundhole, at the 12th fret from 6 inches, at the 12th fret from 12 inches, over the shoulder aimed down at the body, and about 3 feet away for a room sound. Layer these five recordings in your DAW and listen to how dramatically placement changes the character of the instrument — then blend two of them to create a stereo-width effect using pan and phase alignment.

Advanced Exercise

Signal Chain Noise Floor Analysis

Set up your XLR condenser microphone through your audio interface with the gain at maximum, cap the mic input (or point it at your treated wall in silence), and record 60 seconds of silence. Import to your DAW, zoom in at maximum waveform scale, and analyze the noise floor using a spectrum analyzer plugin — identify your dominant noise source (self-noise, preamp hiss, ground hum, or RF interference) and its frequency content. Then optimize gain staging by reducing interface gain and compensating with makeup gain in the DAW to find the minimum noise floor achievable with your specific signal chain.

Frequently Asked Questions

FAQ Do I need an audio interface to use a microphone under $200?
Not necessarily — USB microphones like the Blue Yeti X connect directly to your computer. However, XLR microphones (AT2020, Rode NT1, SM58, etc.) require an audio interface or mixer with XLR inputs and phantom power. For most music production workflows, an XLR mic plus a budget interface like the Focusrite Scarlett Solo provides more flexibility and better audio quality than a USB-only solution.
FAQ Is the Rode NT1 5th Gen worth the extra cost over the AT2020?
Yes, if you're in a treated recording space. The NT1 5th Gen's 4 dB-A self-noise (vs. 20 dB-A on the AT2020) means substantially cleaner recordings of quiet sources, and its dual XLR/USB-C connectivity adds genuine workflow flexibility. In an untreated room, the self-noise advantage is masked by room noise and the AT2020 represents better value.
FAQ Can I record professional-quality music with a microphone under $200?
Absolutely — many commercially released albums feature prominent performances recorded on budget microphones when the acoustic environment, microphone technique, and signal chain are properly optimized. The AT2020 and Rode NT1 in a treated space with a clean preamp can produce recordings indistinguishable from those made on mics costing five times as much.
FAQ What is self-noise and why does it matter for microphone selection?
Self-noise (also called equivalent noise level, or EIN) is the electrical noise a microphone's capsule and electronics generate internally, measured in dB-A. Lower numbers are better. For loud sources (vocals at normal distance, loud instruments), self-noise above 20 dB-A is rarely audible in practice. For quiet sources (acoustic instruments recorded from a distance, quiet vocalists, room ambience), lower self-noise — like the Rode NT1's 4 dB-A — makes a meaningful difference in recording cleanliness.
FAQ Which microphone is best for recording rap vocals in a bedroom?
The Shure SM7dB is the top recommendation for rap vocals in an untreated bedroom — its dynamic capsule rejects room reflections, its built-in preamp solves gain requirements, and its warm, present character translates well through the earbuds and phone speakers that most rap listeners use. The SM58 is a more affordable alternative with similar acoustic properties.
FAQ Does the type of polar pattern matter for home studio recording?
Yes, significantly. Cardioid polar patterns (used by all mics on this list) are ideal for home studios because they reject sound from the rear, minimizing room noise and reflections. Omnidirectional patterns pick up equally from all directions — useful for natural acoustic recordings in excellent rooms but disastrous in untreated spaces. For most home producers, cardioid is the correct choice.
FAQ Should I buy a condenser or dynamic mic as my first microphone?
If you record in an untreated room and primarily record vocals, buy a dynamic (SM58 or SM7dB). If you have basic acoustic treatment and record diverse sources including acoustic instruments, buy a condenser (AT2020 or sE X1 S). If you have no interface and need the simplest setup, buy the Samson Q2U (dynamic with USB output) or Blue Yeti X (condenser USB).
FAQ What accessories do I need to buy alongside a budget microphone?
For XLR mics: an audio interface (e.g., Focusrite Scarlett Solo, ~$120), an XLR cable (~$10–15), a mic stand or boom arm (~$20–30), and a pop filter (~$10–15). For condenser mics, a shock mount is also recommended (many come included or cost $20–30 separately). For USB mics, no accessories are required — just a USB cable (usually included).
