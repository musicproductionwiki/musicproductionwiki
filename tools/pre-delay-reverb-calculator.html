Reverb Type Encyclopedia
Plate+
Metal plate with contact mics. Dense, smooth tail, naturally bright. Best for vocals, snare, strings. EMT 140 is the gold standard — more records than any other reverb.
Spring+
Mechanical coil. Bouncy, slightly metallic wobble. Best for guitar amp use, vintage vocals, lo-fi. Integral to surf music, rockabilly, and classic reggae.
Room+
Small space simulation. Natural, tight, subtle placement. Best for drums, acoustic instruments. You want the listener to feel the source is in a space, not hear "reverb".
Hall+
Concert hall. Long, lush, spacious. Best for orchestral instruments, cinematic pads, R&B vocals with long pre-delay.
Chamber+
Tiled physical room. Dense but natural. Best for vocals in vintage pop/rock, snare. The Capitol Studios chambers are the most famous examples.
Shimmer+
Algorithmic with pitch-shifted feedback. Ethereal, rising, otherworldly. Best for ambient pads, cinematic atmosphere. Sigur Rós / Bon Iver production aesthetic.
Convolution+
Impulse response of a real space. Exact replica of a real room. Best for acoustic instruments needing realistic placement.
Gated+
Reverb tail cut off abruptly by a gate. Deliberate, unnatural, iconic. The Phil Collins / 80s snare sound. Currently used in retrowave and vaporwave production.
Parameter Guide
Pre-delay
0ms = source IN the space. 20–60ms = source in front of the space. Critical for vocals — preserves intelligibility.
Decay / RT60
How long until reverb fades 60 dB. Match to tempo: reverb tail crossing into the next bar = rhythmic clutter.
Diffusion
High = smooth, even tail. Low = individual early reflections audible. High for pads. Low for drums (adds character).
Modulation
Prevents static-sounding tails. Under 0.5 Hz rate, under 20% depth as a starting point.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is the purpose of pre-delay on a reverb?

Pre-delay creates a gap between the dry signal and the start of the reverb tail. This preserves the attack and intelligibility of the source while still placing it in a space. Without pre-delay, reverb begins immediately and can smear transients — especially on vocals and snares.

How long should my reverb tail be?

The tail should clear before the next strong beat. At 120 BPM, a bar is 2,000ms — a tail longer than 1,500ms risks bleeding into the next phrase. Shorter tails (300–800ms) work well for tight, rhythmic reverb. Longer tails (1.5–4s) work for ambient pads and slow ballads.

What is the Phil Collins gated reverb technique?

The famous In the Air Tonight snare sound uses a long room or hall reverb with a noise gate set to cut the tail short. The gate closes quickly after the snare hit, leaving only the attack of the reverb. The result is a massive, punchy transient without a long wash. The tail length sets how quickly the gate closes.
